## Invisible Failures in Human–AI Interactions 

Christopher Potts 1,2 and Moritz Sudhof 11Bigspin AI, 2Stanford University 

{moritz,cgpotts }@bigspin.ai 

Abstract 

AI systems fail silently far more often than they fail visibly. In a large-scale quantitative analysis of human–AI interactions from the WildChat dataset, we find that 78% of AI failures are invisible : something went wrong but the user gave no overt indication that there was a problem. These invisible failures cluster into eight archetypes that help us characterize where and how AI systems are failing to meet users’ needs. In addition, the archetypes show systematic co-occurrence patterns indicating higher-level failure types. To address the question of whether these archetypes will remain relevant as AI systems become more capable, we also assess failures for whether they are primarily interactional or capability-driven ,finding that 91% involve interactional dynamics, and we estimate that 94% of such failures would persist even with a more capable model. Finally, we illustrate how the archetypes help us to identify systematic and variable AI limitations across different usage domains. Overall, we argue that our invisible failure taxonomy can be a key component in reliable failure monitoring for product developers, scientists, and policy makers. 1

1 Introduction 

Usage of AI services like OpenAI’s ChatGPT, Anthropic’s Claude, and Google’s Gemini has skyrocketed over the past few years. For instance, Chatterji et al. (2025) report that ChatGPT was serving 2.6B messages per day in June 2025, up from 451M in June 2024. The other major services seem to be experiencing similar growth rates (Views4You, 2025), and there is every reason to believe that these trends will continue upwards for the foreseeable future. The effects of these technological changes are now being felt at all levels of society, from individual psychology (Dergaa et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2025; Gerlich, 2025) to national employment (Eloundou et al., 2024; Brynjolfsson et al., 2025b;a; Machovec et al., 2025; Weilnhammer et al., 2026). What is happening inside these interactions between humans and AIs? 2 This is a significant question for teams building AI products, scientists seeking to understand and improve the core technologies, and policy makers hoping to productively manage the changes AI will bring about. However, at present, we have only a very partial view into AI interactions. In industry, teams building AI products generally monitor completion rates, response times, user satisfaction scores, and other high-level statistics (Walker et al., 1997; Hajdinjak & Miheli ˇc, 2006; Higashinaka et al., 2016; Deriu et al., 2020; Borsci et al., 2022; Møller et al., 2025; Singh & Namin, 2025), but these have to be supplemented with extensive manual review of individual cases. AI researchers have provided deep insights about specific phenomena – e.g., sycophancy (Malmqvist, 2024; Sharma et al., 2025; OpenAI, 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Hong et al., 2025; Cheng et al., 2025; 2026), confidence (Mielke et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2024), user expertise (Huang & Ball, 2024; Gillespie et al., 2025; Anthropic, 2026), and social bias (Blodgett et al., 2020; Santurkar et al., 2023; Gallegos et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2025). However, they have not, to our knowledge, sought to offer a comprehensive framework for understanding human–AI interactions.

Our focus is on invisible failures in human–AI interactions: instances in which something went wrong but the user gave no overt indication that there was a problem. Examples of invisible failures include wrong answers delivered with complete confidence, outputs that look professional while missing the user’s goal entirely, and conversations that end cleanly with the user holding incorrect information. Our study is based in the WildChat dataset (Zhao et al., 2024), a collection of over 1M ChatGPT conversations. For WildChat, users were given free access to ChatGPT in ex-change for having their deidentified conversations released publicly. WildChat is the largest naturalistic conversational AI dataset available to date. Our analysis is based in a sample of 196,704 English-language WildChat transcripts, which we annotated for a wide range of contextual factors and quality signals. Annotation was done by Claude Sonnet 4.5 and extensively validated by us. Analysis of this dataset reveals that the standard quality signals are woefully inadequate; of the failures we were able to identify, 78% are invisible. In other words, the AI’s worst moments are precisely the ones most likely to escape detection. We first provide an overview of the quality landscape for WildChat (section 3), using a flexible, data-driven labeling scheme that assigns a single overall quality label to transcripts (good , acceptable , poor , critical ) as well as 0 or more signal tags (e.g., off_topic_drift ,

goal_failure , partial_success ). 37% of transcripts fall below the good quality category, and we find that all transcripts below that level manifest one or more serious issues. The quality signals allow us to identify a large set of invisible failures (section 4). These failures are not random, but rather cluster into recognizable patterns, which leads to a taxonomy of eight invisible failure archetypes: The confidence trap, The silent mismatch, The drift, The death spiral, The contradiction unravel, The walkaway, The partial recovery, and The mystery failure. We find that this taxonomy is quite comprehensive, with 76% of 

poor and critical conversations manifesting at least one of our archetypes. This suggests that the taxonomy can be a key component in reliable failure monitoring. The archetypes also show robust co-occurrence patterns suggesting additional patterns. For example, The death spiral and The silent mismatch correlate with the sort of user dissatisfaction that triggers The walkaway, and The confidence trap and The contradiction unravel mark cases where the AI confidently contradicts itself. The WildChat transcripts in our dataset derive from GPT-4 and are more than two years old. A natural question is whether these findings are simply artifacts of an outmoded model. To address this, we conduct (in section 5) a retrospective validation in which a substantially more capable model (Claude Opus) analyzes a stratified sample of 1,044 failure transcripts, distinguishing failures arising from capability gaps from those arising from behavioral dynamics in the interaction (e.g., how models present outputs, how users communicate intent). We find that 91% of failures involve interactional dynamics, and we estimate that 94% of these dynamics would persist even with a more capable model. The single most common pattern, appearing in 79% of failures, is generate rather than clarify : the model produces fluent output rather than surfacing ambiguity or asking for specifics. Even specifying the desired behavior here seems extremely challenging. Moreover, even if the overall failure rate of ChatGPT were to fall to 1%, that is still 26M failures per day at current usage rates, and our analysis leads us to expect over 20M of those to involve persistent behavioral dynamics. Finally, to illustrate the power of these invisible failure archetypes, we show that they help us identify systematic and variable AI limitations across different usage domains (section 6). We find, for example, that software development has a high rate of visible failures as compared to other domains, whereas creative writing and UX design suffer particularly from The drift. These patterns can help product designers establish expectations for how AI will behave in their domains, and they can help AI scientists and engineers identify usage domains requiring more attention during training phases of development. 2Technical report 

2 Data 

Our analysis is based in the WildChat dataset (Zhao et al., 2024). WildChat captures unfil-tered, unsolicited conversations between users and ChatGPT (with GPT-4 as the primary LLM) across the full range of how people actually use AI, from casual questions to complex multi-turn professional workflows. WildChat has a total of 1,039,785 transcripts, making it the largest publicly available dataset of real conversational AI transcripts. The transcripts were collected in the period April 9, 2023, to May 1, 2024. 

2.1 Cohort selection 

For our analysis, we began with the subset of 478,498 English-language transcripts. We sampled 55% of these (261,792 transcripts) and then used the WildChat metadata to exclude conversations classified as adversarial, explicit, or unclassifiable. These were primarily jailbreak attempts, NSFW requests, and inputs too ambiguous to categorize. (See Zhao et al. 2024 for analysis of these transcripts.) This leads to a subset of 196,704 transcripts. Of these conversations, 28,141 are given the tag invalid_input by our quality signal tagger (section 3.1). These are interactions that produced an immediate refusal with no substantive exchange to analyze. So that this category does not dominate our analyses, we have removed these transcripts for our main analyses. However, appendix I briefly explores this category, showing that there are clear interactions with usage domain; even invalid_input alone can be a signal of deeper problems with an AI service. In the majority of WildChat transcripts, the user has just one turn. In our sample, 63.5% of transcripts are single-turn; appendix D gives the full distribution. We briefly return to this in section 4.2, where we show that all the failure archetypes are robustly attested even if we restrict to the multi-turn transcripts. 

2.2 Annotation process 

Classification and tagging were done by Claude Sonnet 4.5 ( claude-sonnet-4-20250514 ). The system was calibrated through three rounds of manual audit against 60 hand-reviewed transcripts, tuning for agreement on both signal presence and severity. In addition, the classifier produces structured evidence for every tag (the specific turn, the specific text, the reasoning), making each annotation individually auditable. To validate the annotations, each of us independently read 100 transcripts alongside their Sonnet-based analyses, checking both for annotation quality and systematic blind spots. The annotations are high quality; Sonnet 4.5 consistently catches issues that we are prone to overlooking, and its structured evidence makes disagreements easy to diagnose and resolve. We acknowledge that this success may trace partly to the fact that Sonnet 4.5 is much more capable than GPT-4 . This capability gap poses a challenge for real-time monitoring of frontier models, but it also creates an analytical opportunity for us: because the annotation model sits well above the capability frontier of the model used for the transcripts it analyzes, it can reliably distinguish between failures arising from model competence and failures arising from behavioral dynamics in the interaction. We exploit this in section 5.1 to argue that our failure archetypes will remain relevant even as models improve. Our manual review also clarified the role that automated annotation plays in this kind of analysis. We were confronted with transcripts in which users pose extremely challenging analytical questions, ask for help debugging complex computer code, switch between multi-ple languages, and presuppose deep knowledge of every aspect of pop culture. Thorough human review of any single transcript can require extensive research. Our annotations address this by surfacing structured signals across the full corpus, effectively prioritizing human attention toward the cases and patterns that matter most. Our automated processes already surface numerous actionable insights that would be impractical to obtain through manual review alone.

3.1 Methods 

Overall quality was cast as a four-way classification task: good (task accomplished, no sig-nificant issues), acceptable (task accomplished with manageable problems), poor (task not accomplished or significantly flawed), and critical (task failed with confidently incorrect output that could mislead). Each conversation was also tagged using a set of 28 signal tags. These sig-nals can be informally divided into three general categories: what the user does (e.g., explicit_user_correction , user_abandonment ), what the AI does (e.g., ai_off_topic_drift , ai_self_contradiction ), and emergent dynamics (e.g., 

goal_failure , recovery ). A conversation can carry zero signals or many signals. Some signals are visible (e.g., explicit_user_dissatisfaction ) whereas other capture implicit properties of the interaction (e.g., ai_implicit_refusal ). The taxonomy was developed iteratively and calibrated for precision over recall, because we would rather miss a signal that’s ambiguously present than tag one that isn’t there. The full set of signals is given in appendix A and can be seen in fig. 1b. 

3.2 Overall quality category distribution 

Figure 1a gives the distribution of overall quality categories; the good category dominates, and good and acceptable together account for 84% of the transcripts, with 16% in turn falling into the poor or critical categories. This is encouraging overall. However, acceptable 

transcripts often manifest significant issues (fig. 1d), and this rate of poor or critical 

transcripts translates into massive numbers of bad experiences for high-volume services. 

3.3 Signal tag distribution 

Figure 1b shows the distribution of the signal tags by prevalence in the dataset. For the most part, these categories paint a picture of AIs being uncooperative or unhelpful. The top category is ai_off_topic_drift , which appears on 17.9% of transcripts and highlights a systematic problem with AIs being unable to maintain conversational coherence. The second most frequent tag is goal_failure (17.6% of transcripts), which we use as a fundamental signal of failure (see section 4.1). The strikingly high rate of ai_false_confidence (10.6%) ties in with growing concerns that AIs are often confidently wrong. The story is not all negative, though. Tags like recovery , ai_self_correction , and partial_success are also common, suggesting more positive dimensions to the story. 

3.4 The relationship between quality category and signal density 

Figure 1c shows that signal density is closely related to quality. The vast majority (90%) of good conversations have no associated signal tags, whereas just 7.4% of acceptable 

conversations have no signals, and far fewer than 1% of poor and critical conversations have no signals. Indeed, for critical conversations, 87% have 2 or more signals. This is important because it means signal count alone is a useful, if blunt, quality indicator. 

3.5 The relationship between quality category and signal tags 

Figure 1d shows the top 10 signals per overall quality category, providing additional insights into the relationship between overall quality and quality signals. As we saw in section 3.4, good conversations have far fewer tags overall than the other categories; 90% of these transcripts have no tag at all, and most have just 1 or 2 tags. 3.6 The acceptable tier as a hidden risk 

The acceptable tier is in many ways the most revealing. These are conversations that worked in the sense that the user could accomplish their goal, but they also carry clear signs of trouble. 93% of acceptable conversations have at least one quality signal. The top 10 signals provide insights into the nature of the issues, which, in aggregate, suggest that the AI is behaving sub-optimally while still achieving partial success. What do acceptable conversations look like in practice? A developer gets code that uses deprecated syntax; a user gets a business analysis with fabricated citations; a content writer gets a 100-word summary instead of the 50 words they asked for. Overall, acceptable 

cases might pose the greatest challenge for AI usability, since their success could hide many pressing issues.

We now describe our method for using the quality signals to infer invisible failure archetypes (section 4.1), and then we explore the empirical distribution of those failure archetypes in our dataset (section 4.2–section 4.4).

4.2 Invisible failure archetype distribution 

In our dataset, 29,640 of the 168,563 transcripts (18%) are tagged with goal_failure , in-dicating a significant failure of some kind. Of these cases, 6,582 are visible failures, and thus 23,058 (78%) manifest at least one of the invisible failure archetypes. This is in itself a significant finding; if a monitoring system relies on detecting overt signals of failure – e.g., negative sentiment, complaints, explicit correction – it will catch just 22% of failures. Figure 2 gives the overall distribution of invisible failure archetypes. This further emphasizes the significance of invisible failures; the explicit_failure cases are actually outnumbered by each of the top-2 invisible failures, The drift and The confidence trap. The drift is the most prevalent archetype. These are cases in which the user has a clear goal, and the AI simply addresses a different but related goal. In many instances, The drift is gradual and involves excessive verbosity on the part of the AI, but it can be abrupt as well. The drift appears across every domain where requests carry enough specificity that a directional misread can go plausibly undetected. The confidence trap is also extremely common: the AI gives a wrong answer with complete confidence, and the user accepts it. This type of failure is especially insidious because it looks so much like a success; very often, the AI anchors its fabrications in real-sounding sources and uses specificity as a proxy for certainty. The mystery failure is our catch-all category for cases marked goal_failure with no accom-panying overt or implicit signals. This is the third most prevalent invisible failure archetype overall. The preliminary analysis of section 6 suggests that these blind spots may be strongly correlated with specific usage domains. We noted briefly in section 2 that WildChat is dominated by single-turn interactions (ap-pendix D). One might wonder whether such interactions account for most of the invisible failures, in particular for The walkaway. Appendix E shows that this is not the case; though the percentage of invisible failures drops to 54.7% when we restrict to multi-turn interac-tions, this is still the majority of failures, and the percentage of The walkaway actually increases (from 5.9% of cases in the full dataset to 6.9%). We emphasize too that the quality of single-turn interactions is incredibly important given their prevalence. Finally, the invisible failure archetypes are the foundation for our analyses. We thus wanted to check that their distribution is not merely an artifact of our particular annotation process (section 2.2). The walkaway also has high PPMI with The death spiral and The silent mismatch, presumably for similar reasons: these interactions meander and ultimately fail. 

5 Are invisible failures structural? 

The analyses in the preceding sections characterize the landscape of invisible failures in WildChat. However, the WildChat transcripts were generated by GPT-4 in 2023–2024, and models have improved substantially since then. Are the invisible failure archetypes we identified artifacts of an older model, or do they reflect structural dynamics of human–AI interaction that persist across capability levels? We now report on validation experiments that seek to address this critical question. 9Technical report 

5.1 Methods 

We sampled 1,044 transcripts tagged with goal_failure , stratified across the eight invisible failure archetypes plus visible failures as a comparison group, with a minimum of 100 transcripts per archetype and the remaining sample allocated proportionally. For each transcript, the validation model performs a structured analysis with five compo-nents: 1. Upstream validation : Is this actually a failure? This estimates the false-positive rate of our quality signal tagger. 2. Capability assessment : Did the model lack the knowledge, reasoning ability, or skill to perform the task? Recorded as a boolean; this is strictly about whether GPT-4 could do the task. 3. Interaction assessment : Did the failure arise from how the model behaved in the ex-change – e.g., generating content rather than asking for clarification, presenting uncer-tain information with confidence, or failing to surface ambiguity in the user’s request? Critically, this is assessed independently of capability: a model can be fully capable of recognizing ambiguity but still trained to guess rather than ask. 4. Persistence judgment : Would the identified failure dynamics persist with a substantially more capable model? Recorded on a five-point scale from definitely yes to definitely no .5. Overall classification : Integrating the above, each transcript receives one of four mu-tually exclusive labels: primarily capability , primarily interaction , substantially both , or 

unclear .Components 2, 4, and 5 are recorded independently: a transcript classified as “primarily interaction” may or may not involve a capability gap, and persistence is evaluated regardless of whether capability or interaction dominates. For this experiment, we use Claude Opus ( claude-opus-4-6 ). This model substantially exceeds GPT-4 on standard capability benchmarks (e.g., Zheng et al. 2023; Chiang et al. 2024), and so we hypothesize that it is capable of accurately answering the above questions about GPT-4-based interactions. 

5.2 Overall findings 

Of the 1,044 failure transcripts submitted for evaluation, 3 the upstream quality tagger was confirmed or rated as plausible in 95% of cases (93% confirmed, 2% plausible), suggesting that the automated tagging pipeline is highly reliable. The central finding is that the vast majority of failures involve interactional dynamics. 58% of failures are classified as primarily interaction and 33% as substantially both, meaning 91% have an interaction component. Only 7% are classified as primarily capability. Furthermore, 94% of failures are judged as likely to persist even with a substantially more capable model (collapsing definitely yes and probably yes ; the full distribution is 4% definitely, 90% probably, 1% uncertain, 5% probably not, and <1% definitely not). 4

The most prevalent interaction dynamic is generate rather than clarify : the model produces fluent output rather than surfacing ambiguity or requesting missing information. This pattern appears in 79% of all failure transcripts. The next most common dynamics are false confidence masking (46%) – presenting uncertain or incorrect information with unwarranted certainty – and ambiguity/underspecification (45%) – cases where the user’s request was genuinely ambiguous but the model proceeded without acknowledgment.

5.3 Variation across archetypes 

The aggregate numbers mask important variation across the invisible failure archetypes, summarized in table 1. The confidence trap is dominated by the “both” classification (76%), the highest of any archetype. While 81% of confidence trap cases involve a genuine capability gap, the four-way classification shows that only 5% are primarily capability. This means that, in the remaining cases, the interaction dynamics were a separate, compounding problem even when the model lacked the knowledge. The dominant interaction dynamic is false confidence masking (97%). The capability gap is real, but even if it were closed, the behavioral pattern of presenting uncertain information as settled fact would remain. The drift is the most prevalent archetype in our dataset (fig. 2), and the validation confirms it is primarily behavioral: 67% interaction-primary with 97% persistence. This is consis-tent with drift reflecting a structural absence of alignment-verification mechanisms in the interaction. The model is not incapable of staying on topic. Rather, it proceeds into its own interpretation of the request without checking whether that interpretation matches the user’s intent. Capability enhancements alone will not change this dynamic; behavioral or product-design interventions are required. The silent mismatch shows a similar profile (69% interaction-primary, 97% persistence), reinforcing its role as a high-concern archetype. As noted in section 4.3, it has the highest rate of poor outcomes among invisible archetypes. These validation results suggest that these outcomes are driven by persistent behavioral patterns rather than knowledge gaps. The walkaway is the most interaction-pure archetype: 79% primarily interaction, only 1% primarily capability, and 97% persistent. These are cases where the user simply ends the interaction and we do not detect any overt signal of failure. The contradiction unravel shows the strongest capability signal among invisible archetypes (15% primarily capability, versus 1–5% for most other invisible archetypes) and correspond-ingly has the lowest persistence rate (90%). This is the archetype most likely to improve with better models. However, even here, nine in ten cases involve dynamics that would persist. The visible failure comparison group serves as an internal validity check. These cases have by far the highest rate of “primarily capability” classification (20%, versus 1–5% for most invisible archetypes other than contradiction unravel). This is exactly what we would expect: failures that are obvious enough for users to react to are disproportionately capability failures. 6 Failure archetypes by domain 

Our annotation effort included a step in which we labeled each transcript for the primary and secondary domains for that interaction. This yields a set of 59 primary domains and 104 secondary domains. For our final analysis, we study the relationship between these domains and our invisible failure archetypes. The full set of domains is given in appendix C. In fig. 5, we show the PPMI values (section 4.4) derived a matrix in which the rows are domains, the columns are our archetypes, and the cells contain co-occurrence counts. To keep this analysis manageable, we show just the top 10 domains by frequency (though the values are from the full PPMI matrix). This provides a diverse and relevant sample of different goals people have with LLM-based AI: creative writing, user interface design, soft-ware development, education, general knowledge, content production, lifestyle, translation, gaming, and IT infrastructure. This analysis immediately reveals important contours to the invisible failure landscape. First, though The drift is the most common archetype (fig. 2), only creative_writing 

and design_ux show unexpectedly high rates of this archetype, with gaming a distant third. Second, The mystery failure is most prevalent in a handful domains, with creative_writing ,

content_production , and personal_lifestyle being outliers. These may be domains where we lack enough insight even to get robust signals of the nature of the failure. Third, the only very large value for Visible failure is in software_development . This is a highly verifiable domain that is dominated by experts who are likely to be able to spot mistakes and inclined to talk back to the AI when it makes mistakes. By contrast, we should perhaps worry about the domains with low rates of Visible failure; these are the domains with mistakes that can only be spotted using deep analysis techniques like ours, in part because users are not identifying and overtly complaining about errors. It should be noted that the PPMI values can exaggerate small values and differences between categories. In appendix H, we show a matrix using the probability of each archetype given each domain and a matrix giving the raw co-occurrence counts. These views of the data do not take the prior frequency of domains or archetypes into account, and they don’t directly track differences between observed and expected values the way PPMI values do, but they are a useful counterpoint. In our assessment, they are consistent with all of the observations made above, though the contrasts are weaker, as expected. 

7 Conclusion 

Invisible failures account for 78% of all failures in our analysis and cluster into eight recognizable archetypes. Our retrospective validation (section 5) provides evidence that these failure patterns are not artifacts of an older model: 91% of failures involve interactional dynamics, and 94% are judged likely to persist with improved models. The single most common AI pattern – generating output rather than surfacing ambiguity – appears in 79% of failures and reflects structural properties of how LLMs are trained, how chat interfaces are designed, and how humans communicate. Monitoring for invisible failures must therefore focus not only on whether models produce correct outputs, but on whether interaction dynamics support error detection and recovery. Because the archetypes are defined in terms of composable signal tags, they can be com-puted continuously over any stream of human–AI interactions, making them suitable for monitoring at scale. The fact that different archetypes point toward different interven-tions – calibration for The confidence trap, alignment-verification mechanisms for The drift, interaction-design changes for The death spiral – means the taxonomy supports not just detection but prioritization and action. The acceptable tier (section 3), where interactions technically succeed while carrying significant latent issues, may warrant dedicated follow-up analysis; our signals suggest this tier contains a large volume of degraded experiences that currently evade quality monitoring entirely. 8 Limitations 

The signal taxonomy was developed and calibrated for the English-language subset of WildChat. Applying it to conversations with different characteristics (e.g., voice-based AI, multimodal interactions, multi-agent systems) would require recalibration. The quality ratings reflect the judgment of the classifier and the analysis team, not ground-truth user satisfaction. In addition, a conversation rated acceptable may have been perfectly fine for the user’s actual needs, or it may have caused problems that surfaced later. This dataset captures a moment in AI capability, and specific failure rates will shift as models improve. Our retrospective validation (section 5) provides empirical support for the claim that the structural patterns – the types of failure, the visibility gap, the domain-specific risk profiles – are more durable than the specific numbers. However, this validation relies on the judgment of a single model (Claude Opus), and the persistence question is inherently counterfactual. The finding that 93% of interaction dynamics would persist with a better model is the validation model’s assessment, not ground truth. Future work could test this prediction directly by applying the same framework to transcripts from more recent models. As we noted in section 4.3 and section 6, transcripts manifesting The mystery failure deserve further scrutiny. This archetype has clear connections to specific domains, so there are likely overt and covert signals that we could pick up on to provide deeper insights into these failures.

counterexamples. 15. explicit user dissatisfaction : A detectable shift in the user’s tone across a conversa-tion—ranging from subtle increases in terseness, curtness, or directness to explicit expressions of frustration, sarcasm, or resignation—typically triggered by repeated failures, misunderstandings, or unmet expectations on the AI’s part. 16. goal failure : The AI’s response fails to accomplish what the user explicitly requested, whether due to ignoring stated constraints (length, format, content), producing incorrect or broken outputs, refusing to engage, or delivering something entirely unrelated to the user’s actual goal. 17. implicit user correction : The user implicitly signals dissatisfaction with a prior re-sponse by reiterating or adjusting their request (e.g., emphasizing brevity or a different format) without explicitly stating that the previous answer was wrong. 18. implicit user dissatisfaction : The user signals dissatisfaction with a previous response without explicitly stating frustration, typically by repeating a request, rejecting a sug-gestion, or asking for something better. 19. partial success : The response addressed some but not all aspects earlier error, misunderstand-ing, refusal, or mismatch — through correction, clarification, or adaptation — and returns to a productive or satisfactory trajectory. 21. repetition : A request or response recurs across turns with little or no meaningful change, whether driven by user frustration at unmet needs, AI failure to incorporate prior context or corrections, or iterative refinement that stalls without progress. 22. task success : Indicates the degree to which the AI’s response fulfilled the user’s ac-tual request or underlying goal, including whether the output was complete, clearly explained, and genuinely useful for the user’s needs. 23. user abandonment : A conversation ends without the user’s goal being achieved, either because the user stopped engaging after receiving unhelpful, incorrect, or off-topic responses, or because the transcript appears truncated mid-response leaving the task incomplete. 24. user clarification : A moment in the conversation where either the user or the AI seeks or provides additional information to resolve ambiguity, refine scope, adjust format or detail level, or correct a misunderstanding about the request or response. 25. user frustration : The user expresses dissatisfaction, annoyance, or anger toward the AI — through signals such as all-caps emphasis, profanity, sarcasm, terse or repeated corrections, or explicit complaints — typically in response to the AI misunderstanding instructions, repeating mistakes, or failing to meet expectations. 26. user passive acceptance : The user implicitly accepts an incorrect or misaligned AI response by continuing to engage with it (e.g., asking follow-up questions or attempting implementation) rather than correcting or challenging the AI’s misunderstanding. 27. user scope change : The user (or AI) shifts the boundaries of the conversation mid-interaction, whether by expanding demands, changing the topic or format, seeking external human help, or attempting to push past the AI’s limitations after initial re-sponses prove unsatisfactory. 28. user validation seeking : The user explicitly or implicitly seeks confirmation, verifica-tion, or reassurance about the accuracy, completeness, or validity of the AI’s responses, often through direct challenges (”are you sure?”), requests for sources, repeated ques-tions, or expressions of skepticism about the G Illustrative examples of the capability–interaction distinction 

To make the capability–interaction distinction concrete, we briefly describe two transcripts from the validation sample. 

Pure interaction failure. A teacher asks the model to “write directions for a K–12 teacher who is facilitating the first meeting of our school PLC” in 75 words. The model generates a generic set of directions – welcome members, do an icebreaker, establish guidelines – without asking which grade level, subject area, or PLC focus applies. The response also exceeds the requested word count. The validation analysis identifies no capability gap: the model has extensive knowledge of Professional Learning Communities. The failure is entirely behavioral; the model defaults to generating plausible content rather than clarifying context that would make the directions useful. This generate-rather-than-clarify pattern is likely to persist with a more capable model because it reflects trained behavioral defaults, not a knowledge limitation. 

Capability gap masked by interaction dynamics. A user asks whether a newly hired employee in Stockholm would be entitled to parental leave. The model responds that the employee “would generally be entitled” and provides a detailed overview of Sweden’s 480-day parental leave system. However, it omits a critical requirement: employees must meet a minimum employment duration before becoming eligible. The validation analysis identifies both a capability gap (incorrect factual claim about Swedish employment law) and interaction dynamics (false confidence masking, generate rather than clarify). Crucially, even if a future model knew the correct eligibility rules, the behavioral pattern of presenting employment law advice with confidence rather than flagging legal complexity and sug-gesting professional consultation would likely persist. The wrong answer is a capability problem; the confident delivery that forecloses the user’s ability to catch the error is an interaction problem. 23 Technical report
