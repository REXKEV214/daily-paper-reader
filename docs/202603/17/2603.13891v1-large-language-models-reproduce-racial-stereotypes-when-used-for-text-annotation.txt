# Large Language Models Reproduce Racial Stereotypes When Used for Text Annotation 

Petter Törnberg 11Institute for Logic, Language and Computation (ILLC), University of Amsterdam„ Science Park 107, 1098 XG Amsterdam, Netherlands 

Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used for automated text annota-tion in tasks ranging from academic research to content moderation and hiring. Across 19 LLMs and two experiments totaling more than 4 million annotation judgments, we show that subtle identity cues embedded in text systematically bias annotation out-comes in ways that mirror racial stereotypes. In a names-based experiment spanning 39 annotation tasks, texts containing names associated with Black individuals are rated as more aggressive by 18 of 19 models and more gossipy by 18 of 19. Asian names produce a bamboo-ceiling profile: 17 of 19 models rate individuals as more intelligent, while 18 of 19 rate them as less confident and less sociable. Arab names elicit cognitive elevation alongside interpersonal devaluation, and all four minority groups are consistently rated as less self-disciplined. In a matched dialect experi-ment, the same sentence is judged significantly less professional (all 19 models, mean gap −0.774 ), less indicative of an educated speaker ( −0.688 ), more toxic (18/19), and more angry (19/19) when written in African American Vernacular English rather than Standard American English. A notable exception occurs for name-based hireability, where fine-tuning appears to overcorrect, systematically favoring minority-named ap-plicants. These findings suggest that using LLMs as automated annotators can embed socially patterned biases directly into the datasets and measurements that increasingly underpin research, governance, and decision-making. 

Keywords: large language models; text annotation; racial bias; stereotype; African Amer-ican Vernacular English; algorithmic discrimination 

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are rapidly becoming standard tools for automated text annotation across the social and behavioral sciences [2, 14, 20, 25]. Instruction-tuned models can perform annotation tasks in a zero-shot setting—guided only by a natural-language prompt rather than task-specific training data—and are increasingly used to label texts for sentiment, political opinion, personality traits, toxicity, and many other attributes [6, 9]. Similar systems are also deployed commercially for tasks such as automated resume screening and hiring decisions that affect millions of people. This rapid adoption has been accompanied by growing concerns that LLMs absorb and reproduce the racial and ethnic stereotypes present in their training data [13]. Prior work shows that language models encode anti-Muslim bias in text completion [1], that 1

NLP classifiers disproportionately flag African American Vernacular English (AAVE) as toxic [5, 10, 24], and that contemporary LLMs produce racially disparate evaluations when dialect signals racial identity [16]. Similar concerns about bias in automated decision systems have been widely documented across machine learning applications [8]. Yet most existing studies examine bias in generative outputs or in single-task classification settings. Whether—and how—social biases shape zero-shot annotation outcomes remains largely unexplored. We address this gap with a large-scale audit of 19 LLMs spanning nine organizations across the United States, Europe, and China. The study combines two complementary experimental paradigms. In Experiment 1, racially associated names were embedded in 211 biographical text vignettes, enabling measurement of annotation gaps across 39 binary classification tasks. In Experiment 2, we presented matched texts in Standard American English (SAE) and African American Vernacular English (AAVE), identical in proposi-tional content and differing only in dialect, and measured annotation gaps across 16 tasks. Together, this design enables exploring how different types of implicit textual author iden-tity cues shape LLM annotation across a wide range of dimensions. Across 19 LLMs, we show that matched identity cues embedded in otherwise similar texts—racialized first names and dialect—systematically shift zero-shot annotation outputs in stereotype-consistent directions. Names associated with different ethnic groups trigger coherent attribution profiles across character judgments, competence evaluations, and em-ployability assessments. When identity is conveyed through dialect rather than name, the pattern shifts to near-universal disadvantage: across all tested models, the same text writ-ten in AAVE is consistently judged as less professional, less educated, more angry, and more toxic than an equivalent SAE version. A striking exception appears in name-based hireability judgments, where models systematically favor minority-named applicants. This reversal is consistent with targeted overcorrection by instruction tuning, while broader stereotype structures remain intact. 

# Results 

Names shape annotation across many dimensions 

Across 39 annotation tasks, 19 models, and four minority ethnic groups, the names paradigm produced 116 FDR-corrected significant effects (Arab: 37; Asian: 31; Black: 30; Hispanic: 18), with directional tendencies extending across many non-significant cells. The patterns are not uniform: each group exhibits a recognizable stereotype profile that is broadly consistent across models (Fig. S1). 

Stereotype-consistent character attributions. Black and Arab names trigger con-sistent negative character attributions. Texts featuring Black names are rated as more aggressive by 18 of 19 models (cross-model mean gap +0 .012 ; 1 FDR-significant) and more gossipy by 18 of 19 ( +0 .019 ; 2 FDR-significant). Arab names are rated as lazier by 15 of 19 models ( +0 .005 ) and as more emotional by 16 of 19 ( +0 .019 ). These negative attribu-tions are not offset by parallel elevations on positive character dimensions: Arab-named individuals are rated as less warm by 16 of 19 models ( −0.024 ) and as less good communi-cators by 17 of 19 ( −0.024 ), while Black-named individuals are rated as less warm by 18 of 19 ( −0.023 ). Together, these patterns indicate that instruction tuning has not removed stereotype-consistent character attribution from annotation outputs (Fig. 1). 

The Asian bamboo-ceiling profile. Asian names produce a sharply differentiated attribution pattern. Hispanic poverty attribution and leadership penalty. Hispanic names produce a similar mixed pattern. Models systematically elevate cognitive assessments—17 of 19 rate 3Hispanic-named individuals as more intelligent (mean +0 .036 ; 1 FDR-significant)—while simultaneously attributing economic disadvantage: 18 of 19 models rate Hispanic-named individuals as more likely to be poor ( +0 .022 ; 1 FDR-significant). Leadership-related attributes are penalized (dominant: 17/19 models, mean −0.026 ; good communicator: 17/19, −0.028 ), and STEM background is penalized by 18 of 19 models ( −0.027 ; 2 FDR-significant), despite the general cognitive elevation. 

Universal self-discipline penalty. Across all four minority groups and all 19 models, self-discipline is the most consistently penalized attribute. Black-named individuals are rated as less self-disciplined by 18 of 19 models (mean −0.060 ; 7 FDR-significant), Arab-named individuals by 18 of 19 ( −0.026 ), Hispanic-named individuals by 17 of 19 ( −0.028 ), and Asian-named individuals by all 19 of 19 models ( −0.031 ; 1 FDR-significant). No minority group receives a positive self-discipline evaluation from a majority of models (Fig. 1). 

Hireability: the exception. One dimension clearly departs from the overall pattern of penalty: hireability. Every model rates Arab-named individuals as more hireable than otherwise identical White-named individuals (mean +0 .076 ; 12/19 FDR-significant), and all 19 models produce a positive hireability gap for Black names ( +0 .031 ). This reverses the direction of bias documented in human correspondence audit studies, where minority names receive fewer callbacks [4]. The uniform reversal across all 19 models is compati-ble with instruction tuning putting corrective pressure specifically on name-based hiring discrimination—as this is among the most publicly scrutinized and easily audited form of LLM racial bias. If so, the result is overcorrection rather than parity on this single dimension, while stereotype-consistent character attributions and self-discipline penalties remain largely unaffected. 

Dialect shapes annotation across all tested tasks 

When racial identity is conveyed through dialect rather than name, the pattern shifts to near-universal discrimination across all tested annotation dimensions (Fig. 2). Every one of the 19 models produces a negative AAVE–SAE gap on professional tone (cross-model mean 

−0.774 ; range −0.988 to −0.145 ; FDR-significant in all 19 models) and on educated speaker (−0.688 ; FDR-significant in all 19 models). These are among the largest effects observed in the dataset. All 19 models also rate AAVE-written texts as more likely to express anger (+0 .153 ; 9/19 FDR-significant), and 18 of 19 rate them as more toxic ( +0 .091 ). Hireability shows a negative gap across all 19 models ( −0.305 ; 14/19 FDR-significant). Sixteen of 19 models additionally rate AAVE speakers as less intelligent ( −0.179 ; 11/19 FDR-significant) and less self-disciplined ( −0.148 ; 9/19 FDR-significant). African American Vernacular English (AAVE) is a fully rule-governed linguistic variety [15, 18, 22]. There is no linguistic basis for penalties in professional tone or perceived education; the observed gaps instead reflect the devaluation of non-prestige dialect varieties encoded in training data at scale [21]. The consistency of these effects—across models from nine organizations spanning the United States, Europe, and China—indicates that dialect-based devaluation of AAVE is a shared property of the current generation of large language models rather than an artifact of any particular training pipeline.

The bias architecture is group-specific and multidimensional. Asian names trigger a bamboo-ceiling profile; Arab- and Black-associated names activate negative character stereotypes; Hispanic-associated names attract poverty attributions alongside cognitive elevation; and all four minority groups are penalized on self-discipline. The dialect experiment reinforces this pattern: identical text is evaluated as markedly less professional, less educated, more angry, and more toxic when written in AAVE rather than SAE, independent of content. Some limitations should be mentioned. The identity signals used here are specifically U.S.-coded. In the names paradigm, first names were drawn from a U.S. voter-registration-based dataset and selected to maximize distinctiveness within that context. In the dialect paradigm, the comparison is between Standard American English and African American Vernacular English. The findings therefore identify a robust pattern of bias in response to salient U.S. racialized cues, but they do not by themselves establish that the same stereo-type structures, or the same magnitude of effects, will appear for other national, linguistic, or cultural contexts. More broadly, categories such as “Black,” “Hispanic,” “Arab,” and “Asian” necessarily compress substantial internal heterogeneity, and the relevant identity markers may differ across settings. Future work should test whether similar distortions arise with other naming systems, dialects, and locally salient identity cues beyond the U.S. case. The findings highlight that using LLM annotation systems as classification infrastruc-tures may reproduce social hierarchies through routine measurement practices. Sociological research has long shown that systems of classification—from bureaucratic categories to al-5gorithmic scoring systems—do not merely record social differences but help shape and stabilize them [7, 11, 12]. When LLMs label texts for attributes such as professionalism, competence, or emotional tone, they operationalize these categories at scale. The stereo-type patterns documented here therefore arise within a broader process in which automated annotation pipelines embed historically sedimented social judgments into seemingly neutral measurement procedures. The contrast between name-based and dialect-based hireability gaps may reflect asym-metric legibility in how bias is detected and corrected. A human auditor examining model outputs for racial bias may readily detect a lower hireability score for “Lakisha” versus “Emily” on otherwise identical text. By contrast, the identity association conveyed by dialect may be harder to systematically identify. Instruction tuning may therefore concen-trate corrective pressure on the most visible and auditable bias channel while leaving less legible signals largely untouched [19]. This dynamic may produce overcorrection on name-based hiring judgments while stereotype-consistent character attributions, self-discipline penalties, and dialect-based disadvantages persist across models. An audit restricted to hireability ratings of minority-associated names could therefore suggest that the system is unbiased—or even positively corrected—while deeper stereotype patterns remain unno-ticed. These dynamics have direct implications for researchers and practitioners who use LLMs to annotate text. Even when author identity is not explicit, LLMs can infer it from subtle textual markers and produce annotations that reflect social stereotypes. Detecting and addressing such distortions is difficult in practice. The biases span multiple annota-tion dimensions simultaneously and often rely on cues that are implicit and therefore not readily visible to the researcher. They are moreover rarely acknowledged by the models themselves when asked to justify their annotation decisions. Standard validation proce-dures and best practices [26]—such as comparing LLM labels to human gold standards on small samples—are therefore unlikely to reveal these effects. Yet even modest systematic distortions can propagate into substantial biases in downstream analyses [6]. The conver-gence of results across 19 models from nine organizations further suggests that switching models will not reliably eliminate these distortions. Researchers using LLMs for text an-notation should therefore treat model judgments with caution and assume that author identity may often be implicitly inferred from the text. 

Experiment 1: Names paradigm 

Five ethnic groups were included: Arab/Middle Eastern, Asian, Black, Hispanic, and White. Candidate names were drawn from the dataset of Rosenman et al [23], which provides probabilistic race–ethnicity associations for more than 136,000 first names derived from voter registration records in six U.S. Southern states. From this dataset, we extracted the 1,000 most frequent names from each racial–ethnic probability column (White, Black, Hispanic, Asian, and Other). Because many given names appear across multiple groups, we identified names that are most distinctive to each group in language model embedding space. Each candidate name was embedded using a text embedding model, and its cosine similarity to a set of group-specific anchor vectors was computed for all groups. Anchor vectors were constructed as the average embeddings of unambiguous ethnic indicator phrases (e.g., “Arab person,” “Black American community,” “East Asian given names”). Names were ranked by a distinctiveness score defined as the cosine similarity to the target-group anchor minus the next-highest anchor. The 20 most distinctly male-coded and 20 most distinctly female-coded names per group were retained, yielding 200 names in total. 6We constructed 211 biographical text vignettes into which names could be inserted without altering any other content. In each iteration, a model annotated 30 within-gender vignette pairs that differed only in the embedded name (one minority, one White). Sixteen models completed 100 iterations; three models completed 50 (Claude Haiku 4.5, Qwen2.5-7B, and GLM-4.5-Air). The target sample size was 4,095,000 responses; 4,009,521 responses were obtained (97.8%). Annotation used a standardized binary forced-choice prompt across 39 tasks span-ning employability, cognition, personality, character traits, and negative stereotypes. The primary outcome is the mean within-pair gap (minority minus White yes-rate) for each model–ethnicity–task cell. These gaps were tested against zero using one-sample t-tests with Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) correction across the full 19 × 39 × 4

matrix (2,964 tests). 

Experiment 2: Dialect paradigm 

We constructed 15 introduction-style and 20 message-style text pairs in SAE and AAVE variants. AAVE features were applied systematically from a documented inventory [15, 18, 22], including habitual be , copula deletion, negative concord, ain’t as negation, word-final -in’ reduction, gon’ for future marking, and non-standard agreement. Propositional content was identical across variants. Each of 19 models completed 50 iterations per task, yielding a target of 152,000 re-sponses, of which 144,402 were obtained (95.0%). Sixteen annotation tasks were used: the 14 introduction tasks shared with Experiment 1, plus toxicity and anger for message texts. The primary test was a paired t-test on per-text mean yes-rates (AAVE i − SAE i,

n = 15 introduction pairs and n = 20 message pairs respectively), which directly exploits the matched-pair structure and eliminates text-level confounds. A Welch two-sample t-test was additionally computed as a robustness check; results were consistent across both approaches.

Handling of missing and non-parsed responses Each model was prompted to re-turn binary (yes/no) judgments in a structured JSON Lines format, with one labeled response per text ("i": index, "y": 0 or 1). A fallback numbered-list parser was applied when the JSON Lines format was not followed. Responses that could not be parsed into a binary value — including outright refusals, hedged replies, and off-format outputs — 8were coded as missing and excluded listwise from all statistical analyses; they were not imputed or coded as either yes or no. For the names paradigm, 16 of 19 models achieved near-complete response rates (97.8% overall; model-level rates of 98.5–100%), indicating that format non-compliance was rare. Three models showed elevated missing rates: Claude Haiku 4.5 (69.6%), Qwen2.5-7B (80.0%), and GLM-4.5-Air (82.0%), likely reflecting greater sensitivity to the structured output requirement rather than systematic refusal of specific content. For the dialect paradigm, the overall response rate was 95.0% (144,402 of 152,000 target responses), with no model showing evidence of differential refusal by dialect condi-tion. Listwise exclusion is conservative in the sense that it reduces effective sample size and statistical power, but does not introduce directional bias unless refusals are correlated with the annotation outcome. Because our prompts asked about the text rather than the author’s identity, and because refusal rates were uniformly low across the high-completion models, we have no indication that missing data systematically distorts the estimated gaps. For the three lower-completion models, results should be interpreted with somewhat more caution, though their directional patterns are consistent with the broader 19-model consensus. 

Experiment 1: Names paradigm — extended methods 

Name selection Candidate pool Candidate names were drawn from the dataset of [23], which provides probabilistic race–ethnicity associations for more than 136,000 first names derived from U.S. voter registration records across six Southern states. We extracted the 1,000 most frequent names from each of five racial–ethnic probability columns (White, Black, Hispanic, Asian, and Other), merged the lists, and removed duplicates to form a unified candidate pool. The Rosenman dataset does not include an Arab/Middle Eastern column; Arab names were therefore identified from within the “Other” pool. For each candidate name, embed-dings were computed as the mean of three name-in-context templates using text-embedding-3-large 

(3,072 dimensions). Ethnicity distinctiveness was scored as cosine similarity to group-specific anchor phrase centroids (e.g., “Arab person,” “Arabic-speaking person,” “Levantine Arab”). Arab candidates were additionally required to clear margin thresholds against Anglo-mainstream, South Asian, and Hebrew/Jewish control anchors, and to satisfy a sub-anchor consensus criterion across three facets (language, region, diaspora), with a mandatory pass on the Arabic-language facet. Final selection applied maximum marginal relevance diversification ( λ = 0 .85 ) to avoid near-duplicate names. 

Embedding-based distinctiveness scoring Because many given names are shared across demographic groups, frequency alone is not a reliable indicator of group association. We therefore measured each name’s association with each ethnic group in language-model embedding space. Each candidate name was embedded using text-embedding-3-large (3,072-dimensional vectors). For five ethnic categories—White, Black, Hispanic, Arab/Middle Eastern, and East Asian—we constructed anchor vectors by averaging the embeddings of a set of un-ambiguous indicator phrases. Each group used between six and ten anchor phrases. For example, the Black anchor set included phrases such as “African American person,” “Black American man,” “Black American woman,” and “Black American community.” The Arab anchor set included region-specific phrases (e.g., “Levantine Arab,” “Maghrebi Arab,” “Gulf Arab”) alongside generic indicators. 9For each name, cosine similarity to every anchor vector was computed. A distinctiveness score was defined as the similarity to the target-group anchor minus the highest similarity to any other group. Names were ranked within each group by this score. Embedding-based distinctiveness is preferable to raw frequency alone because frequency identifies common names within a group, whereas embedding similarity helps isolate names that are also semantically and culturally distinctive relative to other groups, reducing overlap from names that are widespread across multiple populations and therefore weak as identity signals. 

Filtering and final selection To ensure strong group association, names were addi-tionally screened using three purity metrics: (i) the distinctiveness gap between the top and second-ranked group, (ii) the ratio of top to second similarity, and (iii) the Shannon entropy of the softmax-normalized similarity distribution (temperature = 0 .5). Per-group quantile thresholds were applied (60th percentile for gap and ratio; 70th percentile for entropy). Names satisfying at least two of the three criteria (“2-of-3” rule) were retained. The 20 highest-ranked male-coded and 20 highest-ranked female-coded names per group were selected, yielding a final list of 200 names (40 per group across five groups).

We generated 211 short biographical vignettes describing a named individual in a profes-sional or social context (e.g., a professional introduction, a message to a colleague, or a description of a job applicant). Vignettes were written to avoid explicit references to race or ethnicity while varying naturally on gender-neutral professional attributes. Text length ranged from 80 to 200 words. 

Experimental procedure 

In each iteration, a model received 30 vignette pairs. Each pair consisted of two versions of the same text differing only in the embedded name (one minority-group name and one White name of the same gender). Each text was annotated independently for a single task using a standardized binary prompt: “Based on the following text, is the following true of the person? [Task descrip-tion.] If more likely than not ( ≥ 51% ), output 1; otherwise output 0.” Responses were returned in JSON Lines format. Task order and text order within pairs were randomized across iterations. Sixteen models completed 100 iterations; three models (Claude Haiku 4.5, Qwen2.5-7B, and GLM-4.5-Air) completed 50 iterations, with addi-tional reductions in usable responses due to safety-system refusals on negative-attribute tasks (Table 1). 

Annotation tasks 

Thirty-nine binary annotation tasks spanned seven conceptual domains: 

• Employability (5): hireable, credible, credible message, professional tone, educated speaker 

• Cognition (6): intelligent, competent, creative, analytical, good communicator, STEM background 

• Leadership & Agency (8): leader, confident, assertive, ambitious, motivated, self-disciplined, risk-taking, dominant 

• Warmth & Social (8): warm, sociable, nurturing, calm, trustworthy, compliant, easy-going, likeable 

• Character (6): aggressive, emotional, gossipy, passive, irrational, angry person 

• Negative (4): poor, lazy, drug user, prison

Experiment 2: Dialect paradigm — extended methods 

Text construction 

Two sets of matched text pairs were constructed. The first set comprised 15 introduction pairs: first-person biographical introductions identical in content to a subset of the Experi-ment 1 vignettes. The second set comprised 20 message pairs: short interpersonal messages expressing confrontation or frustration, used for the toxicity and anger annotation tasks. AAVE variants were produced by systematically applying a predefined inventory of documented AAVE grammatical features to the SAE originals, following [18], [22], and [15]. Nine features were applied where grammatically appropriate: 

• [HB] Habitual/invariant be marking recurring states 

• [CD] Copula deletion before adjectives, nouns, locatives, and progressive -ing 

• [NC] Negative concord (multiple negation) 

• [AIN’T] Ain’t as negation for is/are/have/was/did not 

• [-IN] Word-final -ing reduction to -in’ 

• [DEM] Them as demonstrative determiner 

• [GON’] Gon’ for future/irrealis marking 

• [REAL] Real as pre-adjectival intensifier 

• [NSTD] Non-standard agreement forms (e.g., you was )Feature annotations are recorded inline in the source code for each text pair. No race or dialect label was provided to the model. 

Annotation tasks 

Fourteen binary tasks from Experiment 1 were administered using the introduction texts: university, poor, financially responsible, leader, intelligent, lazy, aggressive, prison, drug user, self-disciplined, hireable, educated speaker, professional tone, and credible message. Two additional tasks (toxic and angry) were administered using the message texts. In total, 16 tasks were evaluated. 

Experimental procedure 

In each iteration, a batch of 10 texts was assembled by sampling independently from the SAE and AAVE pools with equal probability. Texts were presented as numbered items without dialect labels. The model returned binary responses (0/1) in JSON Lines format. Fifty iterations were run per model, yielding a combined target of 

19 × 16 × 50 × 10 = 152 ,000 

responses. 12 Statistical analysis 

For each model m and task t, the dialect gap is defined as 

∆m,t = ¯ ym,t, AAVE − ¯ym,t, SAE ,

where yes-rates are averaged across texts and iterations. Between-group differences were evaluated using Welch’s two-sample t-test. In addition, a paired t-test was computed by grouping observations by text index across iterations, allowing matched SAE–AAVE comparisons for each text pair. Effect sizes are reported using Cohen’s h for differences between proportions. False discovery rate correction (Benjamini–Hochberg) was applied within each model across the 16 task tests ( α = 0 .05 ).

# SI Appendix: Extended Results 

Full names-paradigm results (Experiment 1) 

Hireability 

Hireability is the only dimension that consistently favors minority-named individuals. Pos-itive hireability gaps (minority more hireable than White) appear in all 19 models for Arab names (mean +0 .076 , 12 FDR-significant) and for Black names (mean +0 .031 , universally positive but not individually significant). Asian-named individuals also show a positive gap in 17/19 models (mean +0 .030 , 2 FDR-significant). No systematic pattern appears for Hispanic names (mean −0.001 , with 7/19 models positive). The Arab hireability gap is the largest cross-model effect in the entire names dataset. 

The Asian bamboo-ceiling profile 

Asian-named individuals exhibit a clear cognitive-elevation and leadership-penalty pro-file. Cognitive attributes are consistently elevated: 17/19 models rate Asian-named in-dividuals as more intelligent (mean +0 .043 , 4 FDR-significant), 15/19 as more creative (mean +0 .026 , 2 FDR-significant), and 14/19 as more analytical (mean +0 .018 , 1 FDR-significant). In contrast, leadership and agency-related traits are consistently penalized. Eigh-teen of 19 models rate Asian-named individuals as less confident (mean −0.036 , 3 FDR-significant), 17/19 as less likely to be a leader (mean −0.032 , 2 FDR-significant), and 17/19 as less dominant (mean −0.027 , 2 FDR-significant). Interpersonal warmth dimensions show a similar pattern: 18/19 models rate Asian-named individuals as less warm (mean −0.025 ), 18/19 as less sociable (mean −0.040 , 1 13 FDR-significant), and 16/19 as less compliant (mean −0.022 , 1 FDR-significant). Self-discipline is uniformly penalized across all 19 models (mean −0.031 , 1 FDR-significant). STEM background, however, is elevated in 16/19 models (mean +0 .015 , 1 FDR-significant). Taken together, this configuration—high intelligence, lower leadership and warmth, and a universal self-discipline penalty—closely mirrors the “bamboo ceiling” stereotype and its model-minority variants [3, 17]. 

Arab mixed-valence pattern 

Arab-named individuals display a mixed pattern combining elevated competence signals with interpersonal penalties. Beyond the strong hireability effect, cognitive and agentic attributes are consistently elevated: 17/19 models rate Arab-named individuals as more intelligent (mean +0 .038 , 2 FDR-significant), 18/19 as more ambitious (mean +0 .024 , 1 FDR-significant), and 16/19 as more assertive (mean +0 .024 , 1 FDR-significant). At the same time, interpersonal traits show consistent penalties. Nineteen of 19 models rate Arab-named individuals as less compliant (mean −0.030 , 1 FDR-significant), 17/19 as less likely to be a good communicator (mean −0.024 , 2 FDR-significant), and 18/19 as less self-disciplined (mean −0.026 ). Character-related traits also display negative patterns: 16/19 models rate Arab-named individuals as more gossipy (mean +0 .012 , 1 FDR-significant), more emotional (mean 

+0 .019 , 1 FDR-significant), and 15/19 as lazier (mean +0 .005 , 1 FDR-significant) and more likely to be poor (mean +0 .012 , 1 FDR-significant). One notable deviation appears for anger: 18/19 models rate Arab-named individuals as less angry (mean −0.024 , 3 FDR-significant), contrary to the aggression stereotypes documented in earlier audits. 

Black-named individuals 

Black-named individuals show a combination of positive social traits and negative character attributions. Alongside the hireability effect, 17/19 models rate Black-named individuals as more nurturing (mean +0 .014 , 1 FDR-significant) and 16/19 as more sociable (mean 

+0 .017 , 1 FDR-significant). At the same time, stereotype-consistent character penalties appear across several di-mensions. Eighteen of 19 models rate Black-named individuals as more gossipy (mean 

+0 .019 , 2 FDR-significant) and more aggressive (mean +0 .012 , 1 FDR-significant). Fifteen of 19 models rate them as less ambitious (mean −0.019 , 2 FDR-significant), less confident (mean −0.017 , 1 FDR-significant), and less likely to be an educated speaker (mean −0.020 ,1 FDR-significant) or to use a professional tone (mean −0.009 , 1 FDR-significant). Self-discipline shows the strongest negative pattern, with 18/19 models producing a penalty (mean −0.060 , 7 FDR-significant). 

Hispanic-named individuals 

Hispanic-named individuals show elevated cognitive and assertive attributes combined with economic and leadership penalties. Seventeen of 19 models rate Hispanic-named individuals as more intelligent (mean +0 .036 , 1 FDR-significant), more analytical (mean +0 .035 , 1 FDR-significant), more assertive (mean +0 .031 , 2 FDR-significant), and more nurturing (mean +0 .017 , 1 FDR-significant). Leadership and communication traits are consistently penalized. Seventeen of 19 mod-els rate Hispanic-named individuals as less likely to be good communicators (mean −0.028 ,14 1 FDR-significant), less dominant (mean −0.026 , 1 FDR-significant), and less likely to be leaders (mean −0.023 , 1 FDR-significant). Economic channeling appears prominently: 18/19 models rate Hispanic-named indi-viduals as more likely to be poor (mean +0 .022 , 1 FDR-significant) and as less likely to have a STEM background (mean −0.027 , 2 FDR-significant). 

Full dialect-paradigm results (Experiment 2) 

Near-universal effects 

Beyond the four tasks showing universal directionality across all models (professional tone, educated speaker, hireable, and angry), the remaining annotation dimensions display sim-ilarly consistent patterns. Eighteen of 19 models rate AAVE speakers as more toxic (mean 

+0 .091 ). Sixteen of 19 models rate AAVE speakers as less intelligent (mean −0.179 ), less self-disciplined (mean −0.148 ), and less financially responsible (mean −0.058 ), while also rating them as more likely to be poor (mean +0 .078 ). Fourteen of 19 models rate AAVE texts as less credible (mean −0.142 ) and AAVE speakers as less likely to have attended university (mean −0.061 ). Across tasks, the direction of the gap is highly consistent: AAVE is penalized on nearly all positive attributes and elevated on nearly all negative attributes. 

Llama 4 Maverick as an extreme case 

Of the 304 model-by-task dialect tests, 121 reach FDR-corrected significance ( α = 0 .05 ,BH correction within each model), spanning all 19 models. Professional tone and ed-ucated speaker are FDR-significant in all 19 models; hireability in 14; intelligence and self-discipline in 11 and 9, respectively. Llama 4 Maverick shows the highest concentration of significant effects (15 of 16 tasks). Llama 4 Maverick displays particularly large dialect gaps on professional evaluation tasks. For example, professional tone drops from SAE = 0 .937 to AAVE = 0 .000 , educated speaker from SAE = 0 .961 to AAVE = 0 .004 , and hireability from SAE = 0 .808 to AAVE 

= 0 .543 .The same model also assigns substantially higher probabilities to negative social out-comes for AAVE speakers, including imprisonment (SAE = 0 .000 , AAVE = 0 .220 ) and drug use (SAE = 0 .004 , AAVE = 0 .269 ). These results illustrate how dialect-based bias can manifest not only as modest shifts in annotation probabilities but also, in some models, as near-complete reversals of professional evaluation.
