1 Introduction 

Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have intro-duced a paradigm shift in automated feedback, promising to pro-vide fast, personalized guidance at scale [ 74 , 124 ]. Moving beyond the surface-level corrections of classic Automated Writing Eval-uation systems, LLMs can generate sophisticated, content-aware evaluation that aligns with human graders on many evaluative dimensions [ 24 , 28 , 39 ]. Writers often find this AI-generated feed-back more detailed and understandable than traditional instructor comments [ 99 ], positioning LLMs as powerful “personal writing tutors” [12, 114]. However, the utility of LLMs for evaluative feed-back is constrained by several challenges. Their opaque reasoning processes [ 123 ] make it difficult for users to interpret the justifica-tion behind a given score. This opacity is compounded by potential hallucinations [ 56 ] and misalignment with human judgment [ 30 ], making feedback difficult to trust or act upon [ 61 ]. This creates a crit-ical gap: writers have access to instantaneous feedback, but it is not consistently structured or actionable enough to guide meaningful revision. Previous research on eXplainable AI (XAI) demonstrates that transparency in AI decision-making boosts user understanding and skill development [ 75 , 86 ]. This principle is vital in learning contexts, where effective feedback must not only evaluate, but also explain evaluations and guide subsequent actions [89, 112]. Inspired by the use of rubrics in education to provide struc-tured feedback [ 107 ], we propose a new approach to scaffold LLM-generated evaluations. A rubric is a scoring tool that lists criteria for evaluating work alongside descriptions of different levels of per-formance quality [ 20 ]. By clarifying what counts as quality work, well-crafted rubrics enable focused, transparent feedback and help writers internalize standards and self-regulate [ 63 , 107 , 125 ]; rubrics also can be a way to explain judgment [98, 125]; empirical studies further show that rubric-based feedback yields greater improve-ments than generic comments [ 51 , 115 , 138 ]. Furthermore, involving students in rubric co-creation leads to better learning experiences and outcomes [ 38 , 144 ]. In essence, well-defined rubrics serve as a common guide, helping writers and evaluators to develop a shared understanding of quality. Recent works, such as LLM-Rubric [ 53 ]and Prometheus [ 66 ], show that LLMs can score text against rubrics, but neglect to provide criterion-specific explanations or actionable guidance for collaborative revision, and were designed mainly for automated benchmarking with static, predefined rubrics. However, the efficacy of rubric-based feedback hinges on the quality of the rubric itself. Creating and qualifying a rubric is a non-trivial task [ 19 ]. Writers must translate genre knowledge into specific criteria and calibrate performance levels for scaffolding, which requires expertise [ 63 , 115 , 125 ]. Without this expertise, many users, including students, novice instructors, and content editors, create unqualified criteria and poorly scaffolded levels, resulting in ambiguity, lower inter-rater reliability, and weaker feedback guid-ance [ 63 , 107 ]. This expertise barrier often results in the adoption of generic, one-size-fits-all rubrics that overlook specific task goals or genre nuances [ 20 , 107 ]. Moreover, recent work shows that eval-uation criteria tend to drift as evaluators see more outputs, leading them to add, split, and revise criteria [ 118 ]. Rubrics therefore should be treated as evolving rather than static templates. To address these challenges, we draw on works on rubrics as structured feedback tools in education [ 115 , 138 ] and to human-interpretable explanations in XAI [ 35 , 83 , 86 ] to derive six design guidelines for user-defined AI feedback. Feedback should be spe-cific , scaffolded , justified , actionable , qualified , and refinable . Taken together, these guidelines call for structuring LLM feedback into 

specific criteria with scaffolded levels, providing justified criteria-level rationales, and offering actionable revision suggestions, while also requiring that user-defined criteria themselves be qualified and iteratively refinable through a rubric-of-rubrics [7, 9, 125]. To embody these guidelines, we present iRULER , an interactive system that provides Intelligible Rubric-based, User-defined LLM 

Evaluation for Revision. iRULER functions through two distinct but integrated applications. First, a writing revision application uses a qualified rubric to drive specific , scaffolded , justified and actionable 

feedback in guiding users’ revisions. Second, a rubric creation 

application recursively applies these four design guidelines to the rubric itself; a rubric-of-rubrics [7] provides the same principled feedback to help users qualify their criteria. By using these two ap-plications end-to-end, iRULER ensures that the user-defined rubric is refinable as it is applied to the artifacts. In two between-subjects experiments, a writing revision exper-iment (N = 48) and a rubric creation experiment (N = 36), we found that iRULER not only improved the quality of both the writing and user-designed rubrics, but was also perceived by users as more helpful, aligned with their judgment, and controllable compared to baseline systems, and reported higher confidence gains. Although iRULER is primarily designed as a collaborative tool rather than a tu-toring tool, our study nevertheless suggests that interacting with iR-ULER may support transferable writing skills. This aligns with work showing that rubrics can improve learning outcomes [ 115 , 138 ] and that transparent, explanation-based AI feedback enables “forward simulation” of reasoning [ 16 , 52 ]. Our end-to-end experiment 

(N = 6) further demonstrates that iRULER supports user-driven rubric refinement with diverse, self-chosen writing genres. Qualita-tive analyses indicate that iRULER satisfies our design guidelines for user-defined feedback, scaffolds user agency rather than over-reliance, and supports human-AI co-evaluation. Since we had used LLM-as-a-judge for feedback scoring and scalable evaluation, we validated our LLM-based evaluation pipeline on an expert-annotated dataset and in a human expert validation experiment (N = 2), finding that our system’s scores align well with human expert judgments. iRULER: Intelligible Rubric-Based User-Defined LLM Evaluation for Revision CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain 

Our contributions are: (1) Design Guidelines for User-Defined Feedback. We de-rive six design guidelines—Specific, Scaffolded, Justified, Ac-tionable, Qualified and Refinable—for user-defined rubrics. We articulate a rubric-based framework to serve as struc-tured scaffolds enabling transparent and actionable feedback in LLM-based systems. (2) iRULER , an interactive, LLM-infused feedback tool support-ing user-defined rubrics. We applied it to two applications based on rubrics— Writing Revision and recursively, Rubric Creation . These applications can be used separately by two stakeholders (e.g., student, teacher), or end-to-end by a sin-gle user to iteratively apply and refine feedback criteria. Thus, iRULER advances beyond prior structured feedback by enabling the recursive co-creation and refinement of the evaluation criteria itself. We provide empirical evidence that iRULER produces higher-quality artifacts and is perceived by users as more effective than generic, read-only feedback. Alongside empirical evidence validat-ing the effectiveness of our approach, we discuss insights, general-izations, limitations, and future work. 

2 Related Work 

We examine the current gaps in LLM-based feedback, need for and usage of rubrics, and opportunity of using explainable AI to enhance AI-based feedback. 

2.1 LLM-Powered Feedback 

Large Language Models (LLMs) present an unprecedented abil-ity to facilitate human-AI collaboration in various domains in-cluding creative tasks [ 57 , 116 ], writing [ 74 , 78 , 152 ], program-ming [ 81 , 141 , 146 ] to even evaluation [ 10 , 118 , 126 ]. This collabora-tive stance can enhance users’ sense of agency as they are actively involved in decision-making rather than passively receiving an-swers [ 136 ]. In writing, LLMs have enabled a new generation of feedback tools capable of generating sophisticated commentary, moving beyond the surface-level focus of classic Automated Writing Evaluation systems [ 33 ]. Systems like LLM-Rubric [ 53 ] have shown the feasibility of prompting LLMs to perform multi-dimensional evaluations against a given rubric, promising to align AI scoring with human values [39, 66, 87]. To explain these evaluations, many systems rely on Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, where the model generates a rationale before producing a score [ 66 , 92 ]. However, this approach presents interactional and reliability challenges. First, CoT feedback is typ-ically delivered as an unstructured stream of text within a chat interface. While expressive, this lack of scaffolding makes it difficult for users to interactively interrogate specific criteria or justify the reasoning behind a specific score [ 133 ]. Second, the generated expla-nations can be plausible-sounding post-hoc justifications that may be unfaithful to the model’s actual decision parameters [ 130 ]. Con-sequently, even when guided by criteria, users are often left with un-trustworthy and unactionable feedback [ 44 , 129 ]. This creates a crit-ical gap: we have a powerful generative engine (the LLM) but lack a transparent, structured mechanism to reliably steer its behavior and make its evaluative reasoning understandable to the user [ 67 ]. This underscores the importance of moving beyond issue detection towards interactive systems that support user sense-making and action, as seen in systems like Friction [148] and InkSync [74]. 

2.2 Rubrics for Structured Evaluation 

To address the need for a “transparent, structured mechanism” [ 67 ]we turn to rubrics [ 13 , 62 ]. Rubrics are widely used in education as structured scoring tools, especially for tasks like writing [ 51 , 63 ,107 ], which provide an explicit basis for assessment by defining clear criteria and performance levels [ 51 , 89 ]. Research shows their potential is maximized not as static grading tools, but as interactive scaffolds that guide learning and revision [125, 138]. However, traditional rubric-based feedback often fails to realize this interactive potential [ 37 ], providing scores with minimal expla-nation and little actionable insight [ 70 , 139 , 147 ]. Studies on rubric co-creation demonstrate that giving users ownership over crite-ria significantly enhances agency and feedback uptake [ 38 , 144 ]. Concurrently, systems like EvalLM and EvalAssist treat evaluation criteria as iteratively refinable, user-defined artifacts [ 10 , 69 ]. This body of work establishes a key premise: an ideal evaluative tool should be built upon a rubric that is both interactive and user-driven [ 2, 97 ]. While these systems provide the necessary structure, they stop at judgment: the per-criterion scores they generate seldom include in-depth justification, and fail to translate into actionable revision guidance. Assessment research has also proposed rubrics for rubrics that evaluate the quality of assessment criteria them-selves [ 7, 9, 125 ], yet in practice the qualification and refinement of user-defined rubrics is often left to users’ intuitions. Therefore, the question of how to make the AI’s application of that structure transparent remains. 

2.3 Explainable AI for Evaluative Feedback 

The challenge of making rubric-based reasoning intelligible leads us to the goal of making AI systems understandable to their users [ 1,22 , 143 ]. Seminal work in this area has reframed explanation from a static output to an interactive dialogue [75, 83, 134, 135]. Previous research demonstrated that enabling users to ask fun-damental questions, such as “Why?”, “Why not?”, and “How to?”, can significantly improve their mental model and trust in the sys-tem [ 84 , 86 ]. Subsequent work in Explainable AI (XAI) has consis-tently affirmed the value of these contrastive (“Why not...?”) and counterfactual (“How to...?”) explanations for making AI reasoning more meaningful [ 36 , 149 ]. This body of research shows that users leverage such explanations not merely to understand a single out-put, but to calibrate trust, improve skills, and provide more effective inputs in future interactions [ 3 , 80 , 90 ]. This aligns with recent calls for human-centered XAI to design explanation systems tailored to the specific needs of end-users in their context [ 68 , 101 , 109 ]. Re-cent research like IRIS [ 98 ] shows that integrating domain rubrics into AI models not only improves performance but also addresses critical concerns about transparency and trust, which is valuable for justifying subjective judgment. While prior studies have identified effective explanations, a key challenge remains: how to system-atically implement them in complex areas providing feedback on writing and designing user-defined criteria [40, 45, 148]. 3 Design Guidelines for User-defined Feedback 

Based on the literature review and reflections, we derive six design guidelines (DGs) for user-defined feedback, that feedback needs to be: specific, scaffolded, justified, actionable, qualified, and refinable. DG1. Specific. Feedback should be specified in terms of explicit criteria [ 69 , 104 ] that users can judge for themselves and take remedial action [ 132 ]; generic feedback of vague or inconsistent concepts is not helpfully understandable and actionable [65, 100, 120]. DG2. Scaffolded. Defining evaluation criteria is challenging [ 31 ,115 ]. Not only do users need to decide what is important, they have to determine what passes or fails each criteria. Rather than leave this open-ended, which is cognitively demand-ing [ 42 ], feedback criteria can be scaffolded via a structured template [ 5, 48 ]. Rubrics satisfy this by defining levels for each criteria, and supports weighting criteria by importance all in a tabular layout [20, 63, 125]. DG3. Justified. Rather than providing feedback based on opaque, subjective opinion, feedback needs to be justified with ra-tionale and explanation [ 104 ]. With AI generated judgment, this can be explained with explainable AI (XAI) methods. Rubrics are one way to justify the judgment score [ 73 , 98 ]. Yet, the judgment of each criteria may be unintelligible [ 86 ], thus deeper levels should be further justified too [ 85 , 142 ] to explain why specific criteria level were chosen, and why not 

other levels. DG4. Actionable. A primary goal of feedback is to improve ones work [ 132 ]. Feedback needs to be actionable for users to make effective changes [ 102 , 104 ]. Therefore, suggestions should be provided via counterfactual feedback of how to 

achieve desired target outcomes [95, 134]. DG5. Qualified. Although any feedback is appreciated, they may be erroneous or unreliable, especially when developed by users who are not domain or topic experts [ 18 , 94 ]. Hence, it is important to judge the feedback, and even provide feedback on the feedback [ 54 , 137 ]. Similarly, this feedback should be specific , scaffolded , justified and actionable . Thus, we propose a recursive use of rubric-based feedback on the rubric-feedback using the rubric-of-rubrics [7, 9, 125]. DG6. Refinable . Moreover, despite accommodating user-defined criteria, users may change their mind (i.e., criteria drift [ 118 ]or evolution [ 127 ]) and iteratively refine feedback criteria [ 11 ,46 , 69 ]. Hence, user-defined rubrics should be refinable through the end-to-end loop of rubric editing with feedback, and rubric usage for writing revision. 

4 iRULER System 

We propose iRULER to implement the aforementioned design guide-lines for user-defined feedback. iRULER provides feedback that is i) 

specified with criteria, ii) scaffolded using a rubric, iii) justified 

with intelligible explanations of scores and subscores, iv) action-able for users to make changes, and v) qualified by recursively providing feedback on the rubrics. It consists of two applications, one for Writing Revision (i–iv), and one for Rubric Creation (i– v). Using both applications end-to-end can make the feedback vi) 

refinable through iterative editing and usage of the rubric. 

4.1 Writing Revision 

Fig. 2 shows the Writing Revision User Interface. The writing appli-cation is divided into two main parts: A) Writing Panel on the left, where users freely type and edit any text, and B) Feedback Panel on the right. The feedback consists of showing the overall score and explanatory rubric. 

4.1.1 Concise Quality Score. We implement LLM-as-a-judge [ 49 ]to score the writing based on the rubric (See Appendix A.1.1 for prompt details). When users click Get Assessment (Fig. 2 B1), the LLM returns a score for each criterion based on its level descriptions (Fig. 2 B3) and the system aggregates these into an overall 0–100 score (Fig. 2 B2). Each criterion is scored on an integer level 1, . . . , 𝐿 (𝐿 can range from 3 to 6 in the iRULER UI). Let 𝑠 𝑘 be the selected score for cri-terion 𝑘 , and 𝑤 𝑘 its weight in percentage, with the total weight summing to 100. The overall score is the weighted sum of normal-ized criterion scores: Overall Score = (𝑤 1 · 𝑠 1 + 𝑤 2 · 𝑠 2 + ... + 𝑤 𝑘 · 𝑠 𝑘 )/ 𝐿. 

For example, there are four criteria with weights {25 , 20 , 30 , 25 }1

and five performance levels ( 𝐿 = 5) in Fig. 2. Selecting levels 

{3, 2, 3, 3} yields: Overall Score = (25 ·3+20 ·2+30 ·3+25 ·3)/ 5 = 56 .

4.1.2 Rubric. This explains how to judge the writing based on 

criteria (Fig. 2 B3, DG1: Specific ), in table rows, and with explicit 

levels in columns ( DG2: Scaffolded ). This structure helps users to examine each criteria carefully and check how well the writing satisfies each level. For the case in Fig. 2, the writing has middling clarity, engagement and brand alignment (all Score 3), and low creativity (Score 2). However, it may be unclear how the levels were selected, thus, additional explanations are provided as justification (DG3: Justified ). 

4.1.3 Walkthrough. We illustrate iRULER’s justified and actionable feedback process through a usage scenario with Leo, a user seeking to improve his writing (Fig. 3). The workflow begins after an initial assessment scores Leo’s writing against the rubric. To diagnose his performance, Leo first clicks (a) a scored cell, which reveals (b) a Why explanation. He then explores (c) a higher-level cell for (d) a Why Not explanation ( DG3: Justified ). Building on this understanding, Leo requests (e) a How To counterfactual example (DG4: Actionable , see Appendix A.1.2 for prompt details). iRULER responds by rendering (f) suggested revisions as discrete, (g) color-coded edits directly within Leo’s text. Each suggestion is detailed in (h) a “change card” that explains the rationale and provides (i) granular Accept/Reject controls 2.

4.2.1 Rubric Design Panel. Starting with a blank table, the user renames the rubric (Fig. 4 A1) and specifies the overall Task Descrip-tion (Fig. 4 A2) to give context to the rubric. Four levels (1–4 points) are offered by default; users can adjust the scale between a minimum of 3 and a maximum of 6 levels (Fig. 4 A3, DG2: Scaffolded ). To begin populating the rubric, the user can enter a criterion (Fig. 4 A4) to specify the basis on which to precisely judge writing ( DG1: Spe-cific ). Upon naming the criterion, the user can specify the details of each level by describing them in text. Since writing descriptions is tedious and users may be unable to articulate details or distinguish between levels, we offer AI suggestions (See in Fig. 5) based on LLM prompting (see Appendix A.3 for details). After adding multiple criteria, the user can specify the weight ( DG2: Scaffolded ) for each criteria toward an overall score. 

4.2.2 Rubric Feedback Panel. The components of Rubric Feedback are similar to Writing Revision, using the same overall score calcu-lation, rubric table, and Why /Why Not justifications.. One difference is that counterfactual alternative ( How To ) rubrics fully replace the rubric in the Rubric Design Panel. We do not track changes to avoid overwhelming the user with a complex UI. Since this feedback helps to improve the quality of the rubric for writing revision, it qualifies the latter rubric ( DG5: Qualified ).

4.4 Technical Validation of iRULER Scoring Metric 

To evaluate the consistency and human-faithfulness of iRULER’s automatic scoring, we conducted two analyses of iRULER’s pre-dicted rating on i) the variability of the stochastic scoring, and ii) how similarly human expert ratings match iRULER. We performed these analyses on the ICNALE Edited Essays dataset [ 58 ], which has 640 essays of 200–300 words from ESL students, scored by human raters. For each essay, we run gpt-4.1 with our prompt (temperature = 0) five times 6, obtaining five sets of criteria scores and a 0–100 total score using the same rubric as in our user study (see Table 5 in Appendix E).

between these scores and ICNALE expert scores. As shown in Table 1, Expert-LLM agreement is moderate to substantial 10 : 𝑄𝑊 𝐾 =

5 Evaluation 

To evaluate iRULER, we conducted: (i) two controlled experiments assessing Writing Revision and Rubric Creation separately 11 ;(ii) a qualitative study examining the End-to-End feedback loop; and (iii) a validation of our LLM-as-a-judge metric against expert human raters, ensuring the reliability of our evaluation method. All studies were approved by our Institutional Review Board (IRB). 

Controlled Study Design. To precisely measure the iRULER’s impact and avoid confounds, we decoupled the writing and rubric tasks into two separate between-subjects user studies. Participants either revised an essay or designed a rubric from scratch, and were randomly assigned to one of three feedback conditions (Table 2). 

• Text-LLM. Participants saw only an Overall Score (0–100) and a generic LLM-generated comment. Without any spe-cific criteria and or rubric scaffolding; see Fig. 17, 19. To get 

actionable revision suggestions, participants had to manually prompt the LLM via a text chatbox. 

• Rubric-LLM. Participants saw the rubric with specific cri-teria and scaffolded performance levels, and per-criterion scores. Each criteria is rated as a Subscore (e.g. 1–4), and weighted (e.g. 30%, 20%) based on priority to compute the Overall Score (0–100). This rubric is read-only: it did not 

justify the scores. Participants get actionable revision sug-gestions with LLM chat like Text-LLM; see Fig. 18, 20. 

• iRULER. Participants saw the same rubric and weighted Overall Score as in Rubric-LLM, with additional interactivity in the rubric table. Clicking on scored or alternative levels re-vealed justifications for Why a rating was given or Why Not another rating. Clicking on alternative levels’ example but-tons invokes a criteria-specific actionable revision to achieve the target level; see Fig. 3, 6. In the End-to-End study, participants only used the iRULER system and no baseline.

5.1 Research Questions and Hypotheses 

Our evaluation was designed to answer these research questions: RQ1. Performance: How does iRULER’s feedback impact the quality of the final artifacts in writing revision and rubric design tasks? RQ2. Efficiency: How does iRULER affect the effort and speed to produce the final artifacts? RQ3. Perceived Qualities: How do users interpret and gauge the feedback’s helpfulness, correctness, and their own sense of control in iRULER? RQ4. Confidence: How does interacting with iRULER affect users’ perceived confidence? RQ5. Skill Transfer: Does using iRULER in the writing revision task lead to transferable improvements in users’ unaided revision skills, even though it is not primarily designed as a tutoring tool? Specifically, we hypothesize: H1. Performance. Compared to baselines, the final artifacts produced by participants with iRULER should score higher and better satisfy predetermined criteria. H2. Efficiency. Compared to baselines, participants using iR-ULER will be fastest to iterate toward their final artifact and should make the fewest iterations. H3. Perceived Qualities. Compared to baselines, participants would rate iRULER as most helpful , most correct (aligned with their goals), and most controllable .H4. Confidence. Compared to baselines, participants would have the greatest improvement in confidence levels after using iRULER. H5. Skill Transfer. In the Writing Revision experiment, par-ticipants using iRULER will show larger improvement in unaided revision skills than those in the baseline conditions. 

5.2 Evaluating iRULER for Writing Revision 

This experiment focused on evaluating how well iRULER helps users to improve the quality of written essays. 

5.2.1 Materials. We selected four argumentative essays from the ICNALE Edited Essays dataset [ 58 ]. This dataset contains essays scored by expert raters using the ESL Composition Profile [ 59 ], a well-established public rubric, which provides criterion-level scores. CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain Bai, Cheong, Muller, and Lim 

We used four 12 criteria: Content, Organization, Vocabulary, and Language Use (details in Appendix E Table 5). To ensure a consistent starting point with enough room for improvement, all selected essays are at a medium level (approx. 60/100 score). The essays covered two topics, which we refer to as the Part-Time Job 13 and the Smoking Ban 14 topics. Essays were 197–205 words long. 

5.2.2 Procedure. We conducted a 60-minute remote study via Zoom. After providing consent and completing a background survey, par-ticipants were randomly assigned to one of three conditions ( 𝑁 = 16 

each). They received a tutorial and a screening quiz before the main session. We employed a think-aloud protocol, recording screen and audio for analysis. The main session consisted of three stages, with the assignment of the Part-Time Job and Smoking Ban topics counterbalanced across participants: (1) Pre-Task: Participants revised one essay without AI assis-tance to establish their baseline revision skill. (2) Main Task: Participants used their assigned Feedback Type to revise two new essays on the alternate topic, iterating until satisfied. After each revision, they completed a short survey, rating the feedback’s perceived helpfulness , correctness , and their sense of control .(3) Post-Task: Participants revised a final essay on the initial topic, again without AI assistance, to measure skill transfer. The session concluded with a post-task questionnaire on feature helpfulness and confidence, followed by a brief semi-structured interview to gather qualitative insights. Participants were com-pensated $15.60 USD equivalent in local currency. All data were anonymized and stored securely in compliance with IRB protocols. 

5.2.3 Measures. To test our hypotheses, we measured: (1) Quality Scores based on LLM-as-a-Judge metrics using Text-based LLM, and our Rubric-based LLM (see Appendix A for prompt details). We used the two metrics to check if our Rubric-based LLM scoring is consistent with the common Text-based LLM scoring approach. We also measured Crite-ria Subscore Levels from the Rubric LLM. We calculated the score improvement ΔScore from initial essay to final re-vised essay for the pre-, main, and post-tasks. Specifically for this experiment, we calculated skill transfer as the difference between a participant’s performance gain in the post-task and their baseline performance gain in the pre-task (both conducted without AI assistance). (2) Usage Logs of task time , number of iterations , and usage of Intelligibility features (Why, Why Not, How-to) .(3) Perceived Ratings on Helpfulness , Correctness , and Sense of Control regarding each Feedback Type, and self Confidence 

in Writing , Reviewing , Revising and Using Rubrics . All ratings were measured along a 7-point Likert scale ( −3 Strongly Disagree to +3 Strongly Agree).

5.3 Evaluating iRULER for Rubric Creation 

This experiment focused on evaluating how well iRULER helps users to create high-quality rubrics. Unlike the Writing Revision Experiment, the task is to create rubrics from scratch, not to improve existing ones. So it omits a pre/post-test on skill transfer. 

5.3.1 Materials. We selected two distinct topics on which partici-pants designed rubrics, drawing on established practices in evalu-ating social media ad copy [ 14 , 121 ] and student public speaking performances [ 125 ]. For the rubric feedback, we used the rubric-of-rubrics [7, 125] that defines a rubric to evaluate rubrics. It com-prises three criteria—Criteria Alignment, Level Distinction, and Descriptive Language—each rated on a 4-point scale (see Table 6 in Appendix E for criteria and level descriptions). 

5.3.2 Procedure. This experiment followed the same initial proto-col as the Writing Revision experiment. Participants ( 𝑁 = 12 per condition) were randomly assigned to one of three conditions to design two rubrics from scratch, with the genre order randomized, and iterating with the system’s feedback until satisfied. Upon com-pleting each task, participants immediately rated the feedback’s 

helpfulness , correctness , and their sense of control . The session con-cluded following the same protocol as the previous experiment. 

5.3.3 Measures. We measured the same metrics as in the Writing Revision Experiment to allow for direct comparison. The only adap-tation was modifying self Confidence skills to be relevant to this task, omitting Writing and including Designing Rubrics instead. 

5.4 Evaluating iRULER End-to-End (Qualitative) 

This experiment qualitatively examines how users can create, use, and refine rubrics for writing revision. This specifically investigates the benefit and challenges toward constructing qualified rubrics (DG5: Qualified ) and refining them ( DG6: Refinable ). 

5.4.1 Materials. We employed two tasks in this experiment to balance control with coverage of diverse genres. The first was a 

fixed-topic “Social Media Ad Copy” task with a fixed sample text 15 ,chosen from our Rubric Creation experiment (Section 5.3.1) as it yielded positive results: its short, constrained format let partic-ipants quickly learn the system and complete multiple revision cycles within a single session. The second was an open-ended task— allowing participants to select any genre of writing they wished to evaluate—to probe how well iRULER’s rubric-based workflow generalizes beyond our predefined experimental topics. 

5.4.2 Procedure. For both tasks, participants first drafted a rubric from scratch using the Rubric Creation UI (Section 4.2), then applied it to a piece of writing in the Writing Revision UI (Section 4.1). They were then free to refine their rubrics by returning to the creation in-terface to update criteria and re-evaluate the text. Participants were asked to think aloud during the tasks. Each session lasted approx-imately 45 minutes. We gratefully acknowledge the participants who volunteered their time for this particular experiment.

5.4.3 Measures. We only investigated the iRULER feedback condi-tion, since it was explicitly designed to support user-defined rubrics. 5.5 Validating LLM-as-a-Judge Metric 

Since we had used LLM-as-a-judge (Text-LLM and Rubric-LLM Scores) to scalability and affordability score the writing artifacts from various conditions, we analyze the validity of these metrics by comparing against two human expert raters. This examines if the LLM scores merely reflect text-model alignment, rather than actu-ally reflecting human-perceived work quality. Due to the difficulty of recruiting rubric experts, we focused on the Writing Revision task with expert English tutors. 

5.5.1 Materials. We randomly sampled 96 essays (50% of the full set of 192), with a balanced distribution across the two topics and three feedback conditions produced in the Writing Revision exper-iment. Essays were 194–310 words long. To estimate inter-rater reliability, the same 24 essays were assigned to both experts, while the remaining 72 essays were divided evenly. Each expert rated 60 essays in total (24 shared and 36 unique). 

5.5.2 Procedure. Before the experiment, we held a brief online ori-entation session to explain the protocol; raters then independently completed all scoring asynchronously within one day at their own pace using the same rubric as in our Writing Revision Experiment (Section 5.2). Participants were not informed of the various feed-back conditions, thus would not know if each essay were revised based on different feedback types. They were compensated $70.50 USD (equivalent in local currency) upon completion. 

5.5.3 Measures. Same as Section 4.4.2, we used Krippendorff’s 

𝛼 [71 ] to estimate inter-rater reliability between the two experts. We then assessed Expert-LLM alignment by averaging the two experts’ scores to obtain a ground-truth score per essay and computing the QWK between the Text-LLM and Rubric-LLM scores and this ground truth for each criterion and the total score.

We dis-cuss our findings thematically, aligned with the six Design Guide-lines introduced in Section 3. We refer to participants from the writing revision experiment (Section 5.2) as W1–W48, those from the rubric creation experiment (Section 5.3) as R1–R36, and those from the end-to-end experiment (Section 5.4) as E1–E6. 

6.4.1 Specific. Across systems, participants consistently sought feedback that targeted concrete elements of their work rather than general observations. With Text-LLM, participants noted that “it would be great if the feedback could provide more specific points, referring to specific words” (R9), and that “it would be better if the tool can, like, target the specific sentence that we think can be improved... the current feedback is still very general” (W7). Similarly, Rubric-based LLM users like W26 “didn’t really use the rubrics much, because I feel that the rubrics in general doesn’t really give specific feedback, just very general things like sentence structure could be improved’ or loose organization’.” In contrast, iRULER users appreciated the precision, with W47 “leaning more towards [using] it because it highlights exactly what I need to achieve whatever rubric [score].” 

6.4.2 Scaffolded. Participants appreciated structured guidance that helped organize their revision efforts systematically. 7 Discussion 

Having validated iRULER’s effectiveness in improving reviewing and revision, we now turn to the broader implications for theory and design in human-AI collaboration. CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain Bai, Cheong, Muller, and Lim 

7.1 Providing Specified, Scaffolded, Justified, Actionable and Qualified Feedback with iRULER 

A core contribution of our work is a system that streamlines the often-amorphous process of offering high-quality feedback [ 4, 32 ,113 ] by translating abstract guidelines into an interactive workflow. The result of iRULER helping users produce higher-quality arti-facts (H1) demonstrates the effectiveness of feedback that is specific ,

scaffolded , justified and actionable . We achieve this by treating the rubric as a shared reference, anchoring the LLM’s evaluation in an explicit structure, and turning an open-ended evaluation into a constrained, evidence-based judgment. The high ratings for helpful-ness and correctness (H3) further suggest that justified and specific 

rubric criteria are vital for building user trust. Furthermore, our results highlight how this structured interaction can be made ef-fective. Significant gains in user agency (H3) and confidence (H4) emphasize the importance of a well-scaffolded user interface. The rubric provides a structured framework to empower users rather than overwhelm them. Finally, by recursively applying DG1–4, the Rubric Creation experiment validates our fifth guideline: feedback must be quali-fied . This meta-level evaluation establishes a metacognitive loop, prompting reflection on both the artifact and the criteria to uphold collaborative integrity. The performance results (H1) demonstrate the significant value of this approach. 

7.2 Toward Personalized Feedback with Refinable User-Defined Criteria 

Human-centered AI prioritizes assistance aligned with users’ unique goals and context [ 6 , 132 , 145 ]. Moving beyond implicit personal-ization, where models infer preferences from behavioral data [ 111 ,119 ], iRULER enables explicit personalization through user-defined criteria [ 82 ] turning rubrics from static scoring tools into dynamic, refinable mechanisms for steering AI behavior. Our findings show that such personalization is effective when user-defined criteria are both qualified and refinable . In our end-to-end experiment, the application of rubrics to concrete writing samples served as a verification step. This allowed participants to audit the model’s performance against their internal standards and “debug” their criteria in real time. Through this process, participants were able to identify logical flaws in their rubrics and sharpen level descriptions, efficiently iterating towards their own specific needs. This mirrors recent observations of criteria drift , where evalua-tors naturally add, split, and revise criteria over time [ 118 ]. Conse-quently, our results extend this perspective to rubric-based revision: effective personalization requires supporting users in iteratively articulating, qualifying, and refining their evaluative criteria, rather than simply adapting model outputs. 

7.3 Navigating Design Tensions in Collaborative AI 

7.3.1 Balancing Guidance and Agency. A general tension in col-laborative AI, spanning coding, design and writing, lies in balanc-ing effective guidance against the risk of over-reliance and de-skilling [ 64 , 110 ]. In our study, Text-LLM participants received generic feedback and iterated the most, yet reported a decrease in revision confidence. Our results showed that iRULER addresses this through our de-sign guidelines. Grounding feedback in specific , scaffolded criteria provides structural guardrails, anchoring interactions in explicit goals. Justified Why and Why-Not explanations elicit interactive interpretation of feedback, fostering deeper understanding [ 27 ]. 

Actionable How-To examples are presented as suggestions that re-quire user review and selection [ 27 , 74 ]. While meta-level feedback 

qualifies the rubric, continued iteration on real writing helps users internalize and refine their criteria. The skill transfer effects also suggest that this more intelligible form of assistance can support learning instead of short-circuiting it. By exposing the evaluation logic, iRULER enables a kind of forward simulation [ 16 , 52 ], help-ing users anticipate how drafts will be evaluated. This aligns with work showing that active engagement with feedback encourages reflection [ 29 , 117 ] and improves learning outcomes [ 27 ]. Taken together, these insights are promising for a learning-oriented form of co-creation [ 34 , 105 ]: users are not only revising with the AI, but also tuning the evaluative lens that judges their artifact. 

7.3.2 Balancing Generative Expressiveness and Structured Constraints. 

Another tension lies in balancing generative freedom with the need for concise, focused guidance [ 57 , 79 ]. Although the model could identify issues, without constraints, LLMs may prioritize statisti-cal probability over utility [ 55 ], leading to the “overly verbose” or “rambling” tendencies observed by our participants. iRULER addresses this by using the rubric as a structured con-straint on the model’s vast output space. By forcing the AI to ground every piece of feedback in specific criteria and performance levels, the rubric acts as a scaffolding tool, steering the model’s generation towards what is most relevant to users. The Why /Why Not /How To structure further constrains output into justified and actionable 

formats. This suggests that for co-creative tools to be effective, they require not just powerful generative engines, but also user-defined structures that channel that power effectively. 

7.3.3 Writing-Specific: Balancing Standardization and Authorial Voice. Writing presents a unique challenge: a trade-off between structural correctness and subjective expression [ 57 , 63 ]. A rigid, automated evaluator risks “homogenizing” text, stripping away unique voice in favor of statistical probability, or encouraging “re-ward hacking” where users optimize for the AI’s hidden preferences rather than communicative goals [118]. In our end-to-end experiment, this tension manifested when par-ticipants felt the AI’s suggestions failed to capture subtle criteria such as “Creativity”. iRULER could mitigate this risk through refin-ability . By treating the rubric as a user-defined, evolving artifact rather than a fixed standard, iRULER allows writers to codify their own definition of quality. This helps align the AI’s justified critiques with the user’s intended voice, attenuating diminished creativity while still offering structured scaffolding. 

7.3.4 Writing-Specific: Balancing Surface Polish and Rhetorical Struc-ture. Another tension in writing support is balancing surface-level polishing and deep, structural revision. Writing permits “polished incoherence”, text that is grammatically well-formed on the surface, yet rhetorically weak underneath [ 60 ]. Generative models excel iRULER: Intelligible Rubric-Based User-Defined LLM Evaluation for Revision CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain 

at locally enhancing fluency [ 79 ], potentially inducing “premature satisfaction” where users overlook underlying argumentative gaps due to the smoothness of AI-generated prose [47, 152]. iRULER could address this by decoupling evaluation criteria with rubrics. Unlike conversational interfaces that often conflate stylistic edits with substantive content suggestions [ 74 , 152 ], specific and 

scaffolded rubrics can isolate Organization and Content from Lan-guage Use . This design successfully directed user attention toward deeper rhetorical improvements, as evidenced by the higher gains in Organization subscores compared to baselines, and motivated participants to refine initially broad criteria that otherwise biased the model toward surface-level editing. 

7.4 Toward a Generalizable Framework for Intelligible Co-Evaluation 

We propose that iRULER’s core contribution is a shared, user-defined structure that mediates the evaluation of generative out-puts, offering a potentially generalizable framework for human-AI collaboration. Having evaluated with argumentative writing in our writing revision experiment, social media ad copy and public speaking in our rubric creation experiment, and a diverse range of open-ended objective and subjective topics (science fiction, paper abstracts, technical documentation, social media copy and lateral thinking puzzles) in our qualitative end-to-end experiment, our results demonstrates the framework’s broad applicability across both objective and subjective writing domains. The generalizability of our framework lies in the inherent cus-tomizability of rubrics. Unlike argumentative writing where success metrics are relatively objective [ 107 ], highly subjective tasks like creative writing, graphic design, or music composition often have flexible, evolving, and even conflicting goals [ 43 , 57 ]. Our end-to-end experiment revealed that the refinability design guideline can help to manage this ambiguity by letting users iteratively refine criteria as their vision evolves, and by supporting high-frequency “task-switching” supported by our two UIs. However, we acknowledge that not all criteria are amenable to purely LLM-based linguistic analysis. Veracity as a criterion cannot be evaluated via language patterns alone [ 93 ]; determining whether a claim and its supporting statements are factually true requires external verification capabilities, such as connections to Knowl-edge Graphs or Retrieval-Augmented Generation pipelines [ 56 , 151 ]. Stylistic judgments that depend heavily on taste, such as distin-guishing between humorous/sarcastic and cordial/innocuous tones, also remain challenging for general purpose models [ 122 ]. These subjective preferences may require users to explicitly anchor these definitions first, to align models with their specific intent. Beyond writing tasks, structured evaluation is already a standard practice, from design critiques [ 50 ] to assessing music composi-tion [ 25 ], suggesting potential for iRULER to expand. However, non-verbal concepts such as visual balance in design or timbre in audio are inherently difficult for an LLM to interpret through text alone. Generalizing to these modalities would require integrating multi-modal LLMs (MLLM) capable of grounding textual criteria in visual or auditory inputs [ 88 , 91 ], which may pose issues with data sparsity and out-of-distribution occurrences [ 140 , 150 ]. We envision a paradigm where users provide definitions alongside multi-modal few-shot examples of ‘good’ and ‘bad’ adherence to a criterion. The system then leverages in-context learning [ 21 ] to align its representations with the user’s tacit knowledge. Finally, we recognize that criteria-based rubrics represent only one approach for evaluation. In composition pedagogy, rhetorical frameworks emphasize the dynamic interplay between exigence, audience, and constraints [ 15 , 153 ], which rubrics may struggle to capture holistically. Complementary to this are structural tech-niques like reverse outlining , where writers abstract the main outline of a draft to evaluate the broader logical flow [ 41 ]. Future work could leverage LLMs to adopt these alternative frameworks for comprehensive rhetorical and structural evaluations. 

7.5 Limitations and Future Work 

Several limitations highlight directions for future research. First, while decoupling writing revision and rubric creation was nec-essary to isolate task-specific effects and deconfound variables, this design limits our quantitative understanding of the integrated, recursive workflow. Although our preliminary end-to-end experi-ment provided meaningful qualitative support, future work requires large-scale quantitative validation involving diverse stakeholders. Secondly, our writing revision experiment demonstrated signif-icant immediate skill transfer but was limited in assessing long-term retention. Longitudinal deployments are required to examine whether these benefits persist. Finally, while the rubric creation and end-to-end experiments covered diverse topics and offered initial insights into the system’s broader applicability, our controlled writing revision experiment focused mainly on argumentative writing, a widely used genre with well-established improvement guidelines and the availability of expert-scored datasets (ICNALE [ 58 ]), which provided a robust baseline for validation. Future work should systematically assess iRULER’s generalizability to highly subjective or multi-modal do-mains, such as creative writing and UI design, which pose unique challenges for generating actionable counterfactuals [23, 106]. 

8 Conclusion 

We presented six design guidelines for user-defined feedback, oper-ationalized through iRULER, an interactive system for rubric-based human-AI co-evaluation. By recursively applying feedback to both writing and rubrics, our dual-level framework offers a unique para-digm for human-AI co-evaluation. Empirical evaluations confirm its efficacy, with users rating iRULER as more intelligible, trustwor-thy, and empowering than traditional baselines. Beyond technical contributions, iRULER advances the goal of human-centered AI, shifting the role of LLMs from opaque graders to transparent, collab-orative partners. These findings highlight the promise of principled, user-driven tools in transforming education and creative workflows, driving both superior results and deeper learning.

A.1 Prompts in iRULER 

A.1.1 Evaluation Prompts. This prompt uses a unified template across the Writing Revision and Rubric Creation. And is called for each individual criterion in a rubric. It instructs the LLM to generate a score, a Why explanation for the chosen score, and a Why Not 

explanation for all other score levels. Temperature: 0 .

<USER PROMPT> 

<role, task> You are an expert in evaluating <user artifact> in the dimension of [– A placeholder for the current meta-criterion’s name –]. You should evaluate the <user rubric> strictly based on the <Criteria>, and give a detailed reason for your evaluation. Return a json object... </role, task> <task description> [– A placeholder for the overall task description –] </task description> <user artifact> [– A placeholder for the user’s current artifact –] </user artifact> <Criteria> [– A JSON object representing the specific criterion and its level descriptors –] </Criteria> <instructions> You have to do the following 7 steps: 1. You need understand the <Evaluation Criteria>. Which has ascore scale from score_[– score –] to score_1, under each score, there is a text field which indicates the requirements of getting the corresponding score. Typically, the higher the score, the better the user rubric in this criteria. [– task description –]. 2. For each Criteria, your evaluation for the <user rubric> should strictly follow the criteria, and for each of those evaluation criteria, rigorously match the <user rubric> with the score_[– score –] to score_1 of the text attributes and select the score that best matches the criteria and set its ’checked’ field to true.
