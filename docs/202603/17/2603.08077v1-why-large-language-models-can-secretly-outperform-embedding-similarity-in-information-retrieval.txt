Abstract 

With the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs), new methods in Information Retrieval are available in which relevance is estimated directly through language understanding and reasoning, instead of embedding similarity. We argue that similarity is a short-sighted interpre-tation of relevance, and that LLM-Based Rel-evance Judgment Systems (LLM-RJS) (with reasoning) have potential to outperform Neu-ral Embedding Retrieval Systems (NERS) by overcoming this limitation. Using the TREC-DL 2019 passage retrieval dataset, we compare various LLM-RJS with NERS, but observe no noticeable improvement. Subsequently, we an-alyze the impact of reasoning by comparing LLM-RJS with and without reasoning. We find that human annotations also suffer from short-sightedness, and that false-positives in the reasoning LLM-RJS are primarily mistakes in annotations due to short-sightedness. We con-clude that LLM-RJS do have the ability to ad-dress the short-sightedness limitation in NERS, but that this cannot be evaluated with standard annotated relevance datasets. 

1 Introduction 

Information Retrieval (IR) is the task of finding relevant information in a pool of numerous docu-ments, where a document can be any retrievable unit like a webpage or text fragment. IR meth-ods have constantly been evolving (Kazi et al., 2025). Vector Space Models (Salton, 1965; Salton et al., 1975) use sparse vector representations of term weights, where each weight represents the frequency of the term in the given document. Doc-uments are ordered by relevance to the query, by the means of an ordering function, such as the BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009, Chapter 3.4). Modern IR methods rely on dense vector representations of text, known as “embeddings", which are learned by Neural Networks (Huang et al., 2013). We will query:

2 Related Work 

We describe previous research on NERS and LLM-RJS models. While NERS stem from a long line of existing research in text embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013; Pennington et al., 2014), we specifi-cally focus on embeddings from Neural Networks. 

NERS Neural Embedding Retrieval Systems (NERS) rely on pre-trained language models to create a dense vector representation of text. These language models are Neural Networks trained on some other such as Masked Language Modeling (Devlin et al., 2019) or Contrastive Learning (Gao et al., 2021). The penulatimate layer of the Neural Network therefore has a dense representation of the semantic meaning of the text, capturing properties like synonymy (Karpukhin et al., 2020). NERS models work by computing the embed-ding representation of the query and the document, and evaluating the distance between them, typically through cosine similarity. When a document and a query describe similar concepts they get a higher similarity score. Document relevance is therefore ranked by similarity to the query. We consider this a fundamental limitation of IR systems that rely on embedding similarities. While similarity between query and document is a good heuristic for relevance, it is not exactly the same. When the document answers the question but does not repeat content from the query (as the example in Figure 1), it is not similar, but can still be highly relevant. Even with ever-improving embedding models (Trabelsi et al., 2021), this necessarily gives a limitation of NERS. 

LLM-RJS Large Language Model-Based Rele-vance Judgement Systems (LLM-RJS) instead use the generated answers from an LLM to establish relevance. In this work, the LLM is prompted with query and document, and instructed to quantify how “relevant” the document is in line with the human annotations, though many variations for prompting exist (Arabzadeh and Clarke, 2025). Faggioli et al., 2023 found that LLMs can per-form comparably to humans in identifying irrele-vant information but struggled to consistently rec-ognize relevant pieces of information. In their re-sults GPT-3.5 showed a higher false negative rate than humans. Evaluating the goodness of document relevance assigned by LLMs can be directly compared to human-labeled query-document pairs are used as the “ground truth". However, humans are not con-sistent in rating document relevance (Bailey et al., 2008). To evaluate LLMs fairly, Alaofi et al., 2024 posed the issue as “if LLMs produce labels that agree with humans to the same extent that humans 2Query Passage Rel.

3 Dataset 

TREC (Text REtrieval Conference) datasets are commonly used in the IR field (MacAvaney and Soldaini, 2023; Faggioli et al., 2023; Alaofi et al., 2024). Similarly to Alaofi et al., 2024, we use queries and passages from the test set of the the passage retrieval task dataset of the Deep Learning Track of TREC 2019 (TREC-DL 2019) (Voorhees et al., 2020). TREC-DL 2019 contains triples of form (query, passage, relevance score). The passage corpora of TREC-DL 2019 are de-rived from the MS MARCO dataset. MS MARCO is a large-scale dataset of real web search queries and web text originally sampled from Bing search logs. Queries in MS MARCO are real user queries and passages are extracted from web documents ob-tained via Bing search results. Table 1 gives some examples of (query, passage, relevance) triples. The relevance scores of TREC-DL 2019 are eval-uated by human assessors. The scores (Voorhees et al., 2020, Section 2.2) are integer values using a 4-point scale (0-3) where: 3. Perfectly relevant: The passage is dedicated to the query and contains the exact answer. 2. Highly relevant: The passage has some an-swer for the query, but the answer may be a bit unclear, or hidden amongst extraneous information. 1. Related: The passage seems related to the query but does not answer it. 0. Irrelevant: The passage has nothing to do with the query. For both experiments, the LLMs are instructed to only output the relevance score of the (query, docu-ment) pair. Any response that could not be casted to an integer between 0 and 3 was considered a failed response. All others are successes. For each model, the (query, document) pairs were divided into successes and failures. The intersection of all successful pairs was kept for final analysis.

6 Conclusion 

We proposed that the short-sightedness problem of NERS may be resolved with relevance reasoning LLM-RJS. Similiarity is not always sufficient for relevance. In Experiment 1 we evaluated whether these LLM-RJS outperform NERS on a ranking task, and do not find improvement. In Experiment 2 we added reasoning, and directly compared the LLM-RJS relevance rankings to the human rank-ings. This showed the the LLM-RJS was able to identify more documents as relevant than the hu-man annotations. This suggests that the human annotations suffer from similar short-sightedness as the NERS systems, explaining why the LLM-RJS do not reach a noticeable improvement com-pared to NERS when evaluated on NDCG with short-sighted human annotations as ground truth. 7Limitations The primary limitation to LLM-RJS systems is the added cost compared to embedding models. The price-per-token of top performing LLMs is roughly 10x that of equivalent embedding models. Additionally, for NERS systems imple-mented at scale, each document only needs to be embedded once even for multiple queries, and all document embeddings can be stored in a vector database for efficient retrieval. The LLM-RJS sys-tem demonstrated here requires LLM inference for each (query, document) pair and therefore does not scale well. A solution may be found in CoT re-ranking (Wu et al., 2025), where an embedding model is used to make a first selection of docu-ments that are similar, and then the reasoning-LLM selects the relevant documents from the subset of similar documents. However, this only addresses the problem where similar documents are not rel-evant, but would still fail in cases as the example in Figure 1, where the document is relevant but not similar. Additionally, we should consider that the short-sightedness in human annotations is not universal for all datasets. We have shown cases of short-sightedness in this dataset, but through expert an-notators, instructions that support further research, and more time for annotations it is possible to con-struct relevance annotations that do not suffer from short-sightedness. Since LLM-RJS models do not need large training datasets, efforts can be focused on only creating test sets. 

Future work The presented results suggest, but do not definitively show that LLM-RJS models can outperform human annotations. To establish the performance difference between human rele-vance and LLM-RJS relevance re-annotation may be done where trained annotators compare exist-ing labels and LLM-RJS relevance judgements and establish which is correct. This can be supported by additionally providing the reasoning chain from the LLM-RJS, to provide argumentation for the judgment. We have established in this instance of an In-formation Retrieval a mismatch between similarity and relevance, and show this as a nearsightedness-bottleneck for embeddings. However, for a variety of different Information Retrieval tasks, a variety of retrieval requirements exist beyond ‘relevance’. In tasks further conditions apply such as prefer-ring high-quality documents, while in other tasks specifically similarity might be preferred over rele-vance (e.g. for detecting copyright infringement). With advanced Information Retrieval capabilities of LLM-RJS models, soft requirements can be con-sidered, and there are more possibilities to deviate from ‘similarity’ or requirements that can be quan-tified. Evaluating LLM-RJS against human anno-tations still appears to be the standard (Arabzadeh and Clarke, 2025), and efforts are needed to re-specify what the (sub)tasks of Information Re-trieval problems are, and how LLM-RJS models can be evaluated. In conclusion, we found that the short-sightedness motivation presented in the illustra-tive Figure 1 is a valid limitation of NERS models that can be addressed by LLM-RJS. However, as shown with the examples of short-sighted annota-tions in Table 3, human annotations suffer from the same short-sightedness limitation, they present as false-positives for a confusion matrix in Figure 5. Therefore, under a standard annotated bench-mark evaluation as shown in Figure 3, they do not demonstrably outperform NERS, while they still could be giving better relevance judgments.
