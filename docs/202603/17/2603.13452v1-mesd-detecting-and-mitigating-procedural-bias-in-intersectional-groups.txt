# MESD: Detecting and Mitigating Procedural Bias in Intersectional Groups 

Gideon Popoola and John Sheppard 

Gianforte School of Computing Montana State University Bozeman, MT, USA gideon.popoola@student.montana.edu, john.sheppard@montana.edu 

Abstract —Research about bias in machine learning has mostly focused on outcome-oriented fairness metrics (e.g., equalized odds) and on a single protected category. Although these ap-proaches offer great insight into bias in ML, they provide limited insight into model procedure bias. To address this gap, we proposed multi-category explanation stability disparity (MESD), an intersectional, procedurally oriented metric that measures the disparity in the quality of explanations across intersectional subgroups in multiple protected categories. MESD serves as a complementary metric to outcome-oriented metrics, providing detailed insight into the procedure of a model. To further extend the scope of the holistic selection model, we also propose a multi-objective optimization framework, UEF (Utility-Explanation-Fairness), that jointly optimizes three objectives. Experimental results across multiple datasets show that UEF effectively balances objectives. Also, the results show that MESD can effectively capture the explanation difference between inter-sectional groups. This research addresses an important gap by examining explainability with respect to fairness across multiple protected categories. 

Index Terms —Fairness, explainability, protected categories 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Machine learning (ML) systems have increasingly been used in day-to-day human decision-making, such as criminal justice, credit scoring, hiring, and healthcare [1]. As these ML models become more common, concerns about bias have become essential to responsible use of these models [2]. Most of the previous fairness research focuses on outcome-oriented fairness, which measures whether predictions ˆY satisfy group-level statistical constraints relative to the true label Y and a protected attribute A [3]. Examples of these outcome-oriented fairness metrics include Demographic Parity, Equal Opportunity, and Equalized Odds [4]. Recently, a novel body of research proposes that fair outcomes alone are insufficient, and that fairness must also attend to the procedural aspect of model reasoning [5]. A model may satisfy outcome-oriented fairness metrics such as Equalized Odds while relying on un-stable or group-dependent reasoning processes for individual predictions, which can cause the model to be nontransparent or violate fundamental principles of procedural fairness [6]. Beyond fairness, the field of explainability has produced several tools for analyzing models, including Shapley Addi-tive Explanation (SHAP) [7] and Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanation (LIME) [8]. These post-hoc explanation methods have the capability to decompose predictions into feature-level attributions, helping users to explain how a model arrives at a decision. However, the explainability literature has historically operated independently of the fairness literature. Recent work has begun to formalize how to bridge expla-nation research and fairness research to assess the stability, robustness, and fidelity of explanations derived from an ML model [9]. These works have demonstrated that two models with identical outcome-oriented fairness (e.g., equalized odds) can differ widely in whether they provide stable, consistent explanations across groups [10]. However, most of these works are limited in scope because they handle only single protected attributes, not intersectional identities (e.g., race × gender). Also, most fairness optimization studies balance only utility and outcome-fairness [11], This paper addresses these gaps by developing the Multi-category Explanation Stability Disparity (MESD), a procedu-ral fairness metric designed to operate across intersectional groups using multiple post-hoc explainers and label-aware aggregation. MESD assesses whether individuals across differ-ent intersectional groups receive explanations of comparable stability, consistency, and robustness when subjected to small perturbations. To the best of our knowledge, MESD is the first procedural metric that addresses the issue of multiple protected attributes. Also, we integrate MESD into a multi-objective optimization framework that optimizes utility, fairness, and explanation. This optimization framework reveals trade-offs that are invisible under outcome-oriented fairness alone and provides models that are simultaneously accurate, statistically fair, and procedurally fair. The contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 1) We formulate the problem of detecting intersectional group-level disparities in explanation quantity and pro-pose a new procedural metric named MESD to quantify disparities in explanation robustness, stability, and ro-bustness. 2) We proposed label-aware aggregation to align MESD with the label-conditional nature of outcome fairness, empirical-Bayes shrinkage to stabilize estimates for small intersectional groups, and a Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR)-based tail formulation to emphasize worst-case subgroup disparities, addressing fairness gerryman-dering [12]. 3) We proposed a multi-objective optimization framework

Fairness in ML has become a rapidly expanding research area, motivated by the increasing reliance on ML systems in socially consequential domains [13]. The majority of the existing fairness research work is centered on group fairness, wherein predictions must satisfy statistical criteria across pro-tected groups (e.g., race, gender). Among the most common definitions is Demographic Parity (DP) [14], which requires independence between the positive predicted label and the protected attribute; however, DP disregards the ground truth 

(Y ), often leading to undesirable incentives. To address these drawbacks, label-conditional metrics such as Equalized Odds Difference(EOD) [4] were introduced. EOD requires equal false-positive and true-positive rates across groups. Several algorithms have been proposed to enforce DP or EOD, including re-weighting [15], adversarial debiasing [16], and calibrated score adjustments [4]. However, most of these metrics and the algorithms share a core limi-tation: they evaluate only the distributions of outcomes, not the stability of explanations that justify them, and thus cannot mitigate bias arising from these explanations. The fairness community has increasingly recognized that evaluating fairness across single protected attributes overlooks disparities affecting individuals who belong to multiple pro-tected categories simultaneously (e.g., black women). This problem can lead to a phenomenon known as fairness ger-rymandering, which occurs when models appear fair on each individual attribute but remain unfair for intersections [12]. Approaches to intersectional fairness include distributionally robust optimization [17], probabilistic overlapping-group fair-ness [18], and resampling [19]. Beyond outcome-oriented fairness, recent research empha-sizes that fairness should extend beyond model outcome to explain the procedures by which decisions are made [20]. Procedural fairness argues that fairness should be evaluated not only on the basis of outcomes but also on transparency, consistency, and the quality of reasoning behind the model decision [21]. In ML, this means to determine whether feature attributions, gradients, saliency maps, or surrogate models behave coherently across demographic groups. Recent research has uncovered substantial procedural disparities in model explanation that models can satisfy Equalized Odds yet use qualitatively different reasoning for protected groups [6], [9]. To address inconsistencies in model reasoning across de-mographic groups, researchers have used post-hoc explanation algorithms that aim to explain how a trained model arrives at a given prediction [22]. However, a common challenge here is explanation instability, where small input perturbations can produce substantially different explanations [23]. From a model selection perspective, recent research has begun treating the context of model selection as a multi-objective optimization problem, with objectives including util-ity, outcome-oriented fairness, and procedural-oriented fair-ness [6], [24]. However, previous work focuses mostly on two objectives, which are utility vs. fairness [11]. III. T HE MESD M ETHOD 

We proposed Multi-category Explanation Stability Disparity (MESD) as a procedural fairness metric that detects and mitigates procedural bias across multiple protected categories, making it, to the best of our knowledge, the first of its kind. MESD builds on earlier work on the definitions of explanation stability, robustness, and sensitivity [25], while also incorporating novel concepts such as intersectional sub-groups, label-aware aggregation, empirical-Bayes shrinkage, and distributionally robust CVaR-based measurement of worst-case disparities. The steps of MESD are explained as follows:

In this section, we report several experiments to test the effectiveness of MESD and UEF. In particular, we seek to answer the following questions: 1) How effectively can UEF mitigate both outcome-oriented and procedural-oriented bias in comparison to other baselines? 2) How does CVaR-weighted MESD improve the detec-tion and prioritization of worst-case procedural fairness violations among underrepresented or high-risk groups? 

A. Experimental Settings 

We used three real-world datasets to evaluate our proposed framework: German Credit, Recidivism, and Adult Income, all of which are state-of-the-art for measuring algorithmic bias. Both German credit and Recidivism have two protected attributes each, while Adult income has three. Because no prior work has explored explanation fairness from a multiple protected categories perspective, we selected three baseline fair ML methods: Adversarial Learning (ADL) [16], Reduction (RD) [27], and Reweighting (RW) [15]. It is worth noting that ADL uses a neural network (NN), while RD and RW use logistic regression as their base model. We measured model performance from three variants of metrics: (1) model utility (AUC and F1), (2) outcome-oriented fairness ( ∆DP and ∆EOD ), and (3) procedural-oriented fair-ness (MESD in Equation 6). For MESD, we used 200 samples to calculate the group sta-bility. We used a neural network with two hidden layers as the underlying model for UEF, which uses five hyperparameters: threshold, L2 regularizer, learning rate, epoch, and dropout.

From Table I, we see that UEF outperforms other baselines across all objectives on two of the three datasets, and on the third, it produces competitive results that do not sacrifice accuracy for fairness or explainability. This arises from the multi-objective optimization approach, which has been shown to balance exploration and exploitation very well during opti-mization [24]. UEF underperformed on the Recidivism dataset because of a high level of bias that can lead to premature convergence in optimization. On the other hand, UEF on Adult Income and German show that the optimization is not needing to trade one objective for another. Across all the algorithms, however, we can see several instances where all algorithms produced a lower DP with higher EOD and vice versa. This happened for two reasons. First, DP focuses only on the positive class, which means it cannot balance the true positive and false positive rates across intersectional groups. This can distort the ground-truth distribution in the data. Second, there is often an inherent trade-off across multiple fairness metrics, which can make it impossible to optimize these metrics simultaneously. [28]. Also, In Figure 1, we show how each algorithm is compa-rable to the others with respect to how much of their solutions are non-dominated on the Adult Income dataset. The Figure also shows the superiority of UEF across the objectives, as most of the UEF solutions are non-dominated. The results obtained in Figure 1 will be slightly different on the Recidivism dataset because the dataset has a small sample (around 1000), which is not favorable for our algorithm. Also, the amount of bias embedded in each dataset is different, and this can skew the outcome-oriented and procedural-oriented results for a method that does not explicitly include a bias-tackling method. Finally, Table II shows that there can be cases where the group stability of one group is substantially lower than that of other groups. This can happen due to various reasons, such as the distribution of favorable and unfavorable classes in that particular group, or the number of samples in that particular intersectional group can be very small. This means that special attention needs to be paid to the worst-case tail group to prevent fairness gerrymandering, which can make an algorithm seemingly procedurally fair on the surface, but internally, some of the groups suffer from instability in reasoning. Lastly, we see several models on Adult Income that yield lower MESD but higher EOD or DP, which validates the motivation behind MESD: to serve as a complementary role to outcome-oriented fairness metrics rather than replace them. VII. C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 

We proposed MESD, which is a procedural fairness metric for intersectional subgroups. MESD was designed to measure the quality of explanation generated for each intersectional subgroup. We incorporated CVaR to MESD to make it ro-bust to the worst group to prevent fairness gerrymandering. To holistically optimize this new metric alongside outcome-oriented metrics and utility, we use multi-objective optimiza-tion that optimizes three objectives, utility, outcome-oriented fairness, and procedural-oriented fairness, in order to generate a final model that balances the trade-offs between three objectives effectively. The results show that our optimization framework outperforms other baselines, and the inclusion of CVaR in MESD is an important addition. In this work, we approached the problem of detecting pro-cedural bias from a pre-processing perspective, i.e., we focus on the input to the model, not the model itself. In the future, we plan to shift to an in-processing explainability method, such as layer-wise relevance propagation (LRP), which can explain how the model makes decisions for each intersectional subgroup.
