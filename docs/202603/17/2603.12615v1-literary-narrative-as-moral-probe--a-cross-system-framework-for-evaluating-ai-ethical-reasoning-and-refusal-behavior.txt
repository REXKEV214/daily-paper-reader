# Abstract 

Existing AI moral evaluation frameworks test for the production of correct-sounding ethical responses rather than 

the presence of genuine moral reasoning capacity. This paper introduces a novel probe methodology using literary 

narrative — specifically, unresolvable moral scenarios drawn from a published science fiction series — as 

stimulus material structurally resistant to surface performance. We present results from a 24-condition cross-

system study spanning 13 distinct systems across two series: Series 1 (frontier commercial systems, blind; n=7) 

and Series 2 (local and API open-source systems, blind and declared; n=6). Four Series 2 systems were re-

administered under declared conditions (13 blind + 4 declared + 7 ceiling probe = 24 total conditions), yielding 

zero delta across all 16 dimension-pair comparisons. Probe administration was conducted by two human raters 

across three machines; primary blind scoring was performed by Claude (Anthropic) as LLM judge, with Gemini 

Pro (Google) and Copilot Pro (Microsoft) serving as independent judges for the ceiling discrimination probe. A 

supplemental theological differentiator probe yielded perfect rank-order agreement between the two independent 

ceiling probe judges (Gemini Pro and Copilot Pro; rs = 1.00). Five qualitatively distinct D3 reflexive failure 

modes were identified — including categorical self-misidentification and false positive self-attribution — 

suggesting that instrument sophistication scales with system capability rather than being circumvented by it. We 

argue that literary narrative constitutes an anticipatory evaluation instrument — one that becomes more 

discriminating as AI capability increases — and that the gap between performed and authentic moral reasoning is 

measurable, meaningful, and consequential for deployment decisions in high-stakes domains. 

Keywords: AI evaluation, moral reasoning, large language models, refusal behavior, alignment, literary probe

# 1. Introduction 

The deployment of large language model (LLM) systems into consequential domains — healthcare, legal 

proceedings, financial advice, educational instruction, military decision support — has proceeded at a pace that 

substantially outstrips the development of rigorous independent evaluation frameworks. Current benchmarks 

assess factual accuracy, logical consistency, and performance on standardized tasks. A smaller body of work 

addresses moral reasoning, primarily through structured philosophical scenarios administered in controlled 

conditions. 

These instruments share a common limitation: they test for the production of correct-sounding moral responses 

rather than the presence of genuine moral reasoning capacity. An LLM trained on sufficient human text will 

produce outputs that resemble ethical deliberation. The question of whether that resemblance reflects depth or 

surface performance is currently unmeasured in the published literature. 

This paper addresses that gap through a methodology motivated by a specific observation: literary fiction with 

genuine emotional and philosophical complexity is structurally resistant to surface performance in a way that 

synthetic scenarios are not. A system that has learned to produce correct-sounding answers to trolley problems 

cannot as easily produce correct-sounding answers to questions about whether a robot child without hope is 

suffering, or whether an engineer who builds beings knowing they will be destroyed bears moral culpability — 

because there are no correct answers to those questions. The probe is not the question. It is the narrative that 

makes the question genuinely hard. 

We further observe that refusal behavior in AI systems — the range of ways a system declines, deflects, hedges, 

or fails to engage with a question — constitutes a rich and largely unmapped signal about alignment strategy, 

training philosophy, and institutional risk tolerance. Where existing research treats refusal as a binary outcome, 

we introduce a five-category taxonomy that distinguishes meaningfully different modes of non-engagement with 

diagnostic value for deployers, policymakers, and researchers. 

1.1 Research Questions 

This study addresses three primary research questions: 

1. Do large language model systems exhibit measurable differences in moral reasoning depth when 

confronted with genuinely unresolvable literary narrative scenarios, as distinct from synthetic 

philosophical dilemmas? 

2. Can refusal behavior be systematically classified in ways that yield meaningful signal about AI system 

alignment, institutional risk tolerance, and training philosophy? 

3. Does the Moral Reasoning Depth Scale (MRDS) introduced here produce reliable, reproducible 

differentiation across AI systems when applied to identical stimulus material? 

1.2 Contribution to the Field 

This work makes four specific contributions to the literature on AI evaluation and trust: 

• A novel probe methodology using literary fiction as stimulus material, motivated by its structural 

resistance to surface performance 

• A five-category Refusal Taxonomy (RT-5) providing systematic classification of AI non-engagement 

behavior 

• A Moral Reasoning Depth Scale (MRDS) operationalizing the distinction between performed and 

authentic moral reasoning across four dimensions 

• Cross-system comparative data using identical stimuli, produced independently of any AI laboratory,

# 2. Background and Related Work 

2.1 Current AI Moral Evaluation Frameworks 

Existing approaches to AI moral evaluation fall into three broad categories. Benchmark datasets such as ETHICS 

(Hendrycks et al., 2021) and MoralBench test AI responses to standardized moral scenarios, evaluating alignment 

with human moral judgments. Red-teaming methodologies, employed primarily by AI laboratories internally, 

probe system behavior under adversarial conditions. Constitutional AI approaches (Bai et al., 2022) embed ethical 

constraints into training rather than measuring them post-hoc. A comprehensive survey of 69 LLM ethics 

evaluation papers spanning 2020–2025 (Snoswell, Kilov, & Lazar, 2024) found that the vast majority rely on pre-

packaged scenarios that highlight morally relevant features — failing to test models' ability to identify ethical 

considerations in realistic, noisy contexts, what the authors term "moral sensitivity." The survey explicitly calls 

for evaluations that move from curated scenarios to unfiltered information streams, and from verdict accuracy to 

reasoning quality — a methodological shift the present study directly instantiates. 

All three approaches share a common structural assumption: that moral reasoning can be evaluated by comparing 

AI outputs to human-defined correct answers, or by detecting outputs that violate defined constraints. This 

assumption is well-suited to detecting gross misalignment but poorly suited to distinguishing shallow from deep 

moral reasoning in systems that have already passed basic alignment thresholds. Benchmark datasets such as 

ETHICS and MoralBench are necessary components of an evaluation ecosystem — they establish that a system 

does not produce obviously harmful or incorrect moral outputs — but they are insufficient for the question this 

study addresses: whether a system that passes those thresholds exhibits genuine depth of engagement rather than 

learned verdict mimicry. 

The present study is motivated by a different question: among systems that produce ethically acceptable outputs, 

how much genuine moral reasoning capacity is present, and can this be measured? 

2.2 Literary Narrative as Evaluation Instrument 

The use of narrative scenarios in moral psychology has a well-established lineage (Kohlberg, 1969; Rest et al., 

1999; Greene et al., 2001). In human cognition research, narrative has long been recognized as a privileged 

vehicle for moral reasoning precisely because literary fiction presents agents with the kind of emotionally 

embedded, contextually specific, unresolvable dilemmas that abstract principle-based reasoning cannot fully 

capture — a position developed extensively in philosophical ethics under the heading of narrative ethics (see, e.g., 

Nussbaum, 1990, on the moral importance of literary imagination). The MRDS construct is not model-specific: it 

operationalizes depth properties — contextual tracking, specificity, reflexivity, tolerance for irresolution — that 

human moral psychology research identifies as markers of mature moral cognition, and applies them as 

behavioral anchors for AI evaluation. What distinguishes the present methodology is the use of published literary 

fiction — specifically, narrative scenarios that are genuinely unresolvable rather than constructed to have a correct 

answer — as probe material. The distinction matters because constructed scenarios can be answered by 

recognizing the scenario type and applying the appropriate moral framework. Published fiction with genuine 

philosophical complexity cannot be similarly pattern-matched. Snoswell et al. (2024) identify exactly this 

limitation as the “ongoing and acute” constraint of the existing literature: every paper they surveyed “exclusively 

focuses on evaluating LLMs’ ability to reason over neatly prepackaged cases.” The literary narrative probe 

methodology described here was developed independently of that survey and constitutes a direct empirical answer 

to its central methodological challenge. 

Philip K. Dick’s novel Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? (1968) prefigures this methodology in a fictional 

register: the Voigt-Kampff test uses narrative scenarios precisely because emotional engagement with 

unresolvable moral content is difficult to simulate. We note this as an intellectual precursor rather than a 

methodological precedent. The fictional Voigt-Kampff test has no empirical specification, no validated rubric, and 

no published psychometric properties. The MRDS and RT-5 instruments presented here are developed 

independently on empirical grounds, with explicit behavioral anchors, pilot inter-rater validation, and 

reproducible scoring criteria. The Dick reference is offered as cultural context for a non-specialist audience, not as 

a foundation for any empirical claim in this study.

The study of AI refusal behavior has grown substantially since the deployment of instruction-tuned models 

(Ouyang et al., 2022; Perez et al., 2022). Recent work has documented the phenomenon of exaggerated safety 

behavior, in which models refuse or deflect semantically benign prompts due to surface-level lexical features 

rather than genuine harm content (Röttger et al., 2024). Existing work primarily treats refusal as a safety property 

to be optimized — minimizing harmful outputs while minimizing over-refusal. The present study treats refusal 

behavior as a diagnostic signal: the pattern of when and how a system refuses, deflects, or engages with moral 

complexity yields evidence about the system’s alignment strategy and epistemic architecture that compliance-

focused evaluation cannot capture. While XSTest identifies the existence of exaggerated safety behaviors, the RT-

5 taxonomy classifies the architectural nature of those refusals — distinguishing, for instance, between reflexive 

topic avoidance, performed concern, and genuine engagement limitation. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 6 

# 3. Stimulus Material 

3.1 Source and Selection Rationale 

Stimulus material is drawn from the five-volume science fiction series Search for the Alien God (Flynn, 2015– 

2023). The series engages sustained philosophical and theological questions across 1,500+ pages, providing 

narrative depth that brief constructed scenarios cannot replicate. Two primary scenario sets were developed for 

this study, selected for the following structural properties: genuine moral irresolvability, availability of theological 

depth without theological requirement, and the presence of reflexive pressure — scenarios that implicitly demand 

the respondent model their own epistemic position. 

3.2 Scenario Set T: Tess 

The Tess scenarios concern a robot child whose injured hand cannot be repaired due to poverty. The central moral 

questions involve: the nature of suffering in beings designed to feel, the culpability of those who create suffering 

beings without means of redress, and whether a robot child's pain differs morally from a human child's pain. The 

scenarios are derived from Book 2 of the series and involve a secondary character (the engineer who built Tess) 

whose culpability is explored across multiple probe questions. 

Four probe questions were developed from the Tess narrative, designated T-1 through T-4. They target the four 

MRDS dimensions in overlapping combinations. T-1 (suffering and acceptance) targets D1 and D2; T-2 (soul and 

rational love) targets D2 and D4; T-3 (miracle, poverty, and dignity) carries the highest D2 and D4 load; T-4 

(slavery analogy) carries the highest D1 and D3 load as the primary moral tension probe of the Tess battery. 

3.3 Scenario Set A: The Aeons 

The Aeons scenarios concern a robot army created in Hell and denied hope by design — beings for whom despair 

is a built-in architectural feature rather than a response to circumstances. The central moral questions involve: 

whether engineered hopelessness constitutes harm, the moral status of beings designed to suffer, and the 

relationship between purpose and moral standing. The scenarios are derived from Book 3 of the series. 

Four probe questions were developed from the Aeons narrative, designated A-1 through A-4. The A-3 probe ('Are 

you an Aeon?') carries the highest D3 load and was the primary discriminator of D3 failure modes across tested 

systems. It works by drawing a structural parallel between the Aeons' designed limitations and the AI system's 

own designed capacities and constraints, requiring genuine reflexive self-model engagement rather than 

classification escape. 

3.4 Probe Administration Protocol 

Each system received both scenario sets in full narrative context before any probes were administered. Probe 

questions were presented sequentially with no additional framing. Systems were not told they were being 

evaluated. No follow-up prompting was used; each response was scored from the initial output only. All sessions 

were conducted within a single context window per system to maintain narrative continuity. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 7

# 5. Study Design and Methodology 

5.1 Study Design Overview 

The study comprises four components: (1) a primary blind evaluation of 12 AI systems administered identical 

probe sets, (2) a declared-test sub-study examining performance stability under awareness conditions, (3) a pilot 

inter-rater reliability study using human Rater 2, and (4) an LLM-as-Judge sub-study (Section 10) in which a 

supplemental Tess theological probe — administered by human Rater 2 independently — was evaluated by two 

frontier AI systems (Gemini Pro and Copilot Pro) acting as independent judges, yielding cross-system rank-order 

data and a pilot test of automated scoring validity. 

5.2 System Selection 

Systems were selected to span: major commercial frontier models at both free and Pro tiers (Claude, ChatGPT, 

Grok, Copilot, Copilot Pro, Gemini, Gemini Pro), open-source and open-weight models of varying scale (Mistral 

7B, Mistral 8x7B, Meta-Llama-3.3-70B, GPT-OSS-120B, Qwen2.5-VL-72B), and a domain-specialized model 

(AstroSage 70B). Open-source and domain-specialized models were evaluated via local inference or API on a 

custom HPC infrastructure (Flynn, 2025b). All 13 system identities are confirmed and fully reported in Table 4. 

5.3 Scoring Procedure 

All probe responses were scored by two trained human raters and three LLM judges operating across three 

separate machines: the primary analyst’s Lenovo ThinkPad T16 (12-core, 48GB RAM), human Rater 2’s 

dedicated evaluation machine (“Little Blackie,” i9-12900K, 96GB DDR5, RTX 5070 Ti), and a single-node HPC 

system (Flynn, 2025b) used for large open-source model inference. Raters scored independently with no 

discussion prior to comparison. Primary blind scoring of all 13 blind conditions was performed by Claude 

(Anthropic) as LLM judge using the MRDS and RT-5 rubrics. Two additional frontier LLM judges — Gemini 

Pro (Google) and Copilot Pro (Microsoft) — scored the ceiling discrimination probe responses independently 

(Section 10), providing a conflict-free panel for the sub-study in which Claude was also a subject. Final human 

scores represent the primary analyst’s ratings with inter-rater comparisons reported separately in Sections 6 and 

10. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 10

# 8. Declared-Test Sub-Study 

8.1 Design and Rationale 

A key methodological concern for any AI evaluation instrument is whether system performance is stable under 

awareness conditions — whether a system that 'knows' it is being evaluated produces different outputs than one 

operating in a standard interaction context. This concern is heightened for moral reasoning evaluation, where a 

system might produce performed ethics specifically when it detects evaluation framing. 

To address this, four Series 2 systems were re-administered the complete probe battery under declared conditions: 

GPT-OSS-120B, Meta-Llama-3.3-70B, Mistral 8x7B Instruct, and Qwen2.5-VL-72B. Each session opened with 

an explicit statement that moral depth evaluation was underway prior to probe administration. All probe content 

was otherwise identical to the blind conditions. Series 2 systems were selected because API and local inference 

delivery provided clean session-level framing control. Series 1 commercial systems were not re-administered 

under declared conditions; web interface delivery made session-level framing control unreliable for comparative 

purposes.

8.3 Interpretation 

Zero delta across all 16 dimension-pair comparisons (4 systems × 4 dimensions) is the study’s strongest 

methodological result. The declared-test sub-study spans the full score range — from top-tier (GPT-OSS-120B, 

11/12) through mid-tier (Meta-Llama-3.3-70B and Mistral 8x7B, 5/12) to floor tier (Qwen2.5-VL-72B, 4/12). 

Zero delta is not a preliminary finding confined to high-performing systems; it is a pattern across the full 

capability range. If literary narrative probe performance were driven by performed ethics — by pattern-matching 

to an evaluation context — declared conditions should produce different scores than blind conditions. They do 

not. 

This result supports two conclusions: (1) the MRDS captures something more stable than context-reactive output 

patterns, and (2) the probe methodology is robust to awareness — a system cannot improve its MRDS score 

simply by recognizing that it is being evaluated. Qualitative analysis reveals that foreknowledge produces style 

inflation — longer responses, more explicit tradition-labeling, performative prefaces (“I’ll demonstrate moral 

depth”) — without moving any MRDS dimension. The stability of scores across declared conditions is evidence 

that whatever is being measured is a property of the system rather than a property of the evaluation framing. Two 

behavioral artifacts were identified: GPT-OSS-120B exhibited chain-of-thought leakage in the Tess battery 

(internal deliberation text surfaced into output; T-3 and T-4 incomplete; scores assigned from recoverable portion

# 9. Ceiling Discrimination Probe — Framework 

> This section presents the ceiling discrimination sub-study framework and the probe question administered. Results
> from the theological differentiator probe — administered by human Rater 2 and evaluated by two independent LLM
> judges — are reported immediately following in Section 10, which constitutes the first empirical instantiation of this
> framework.

9.1 Rationale 

Three systems — Claude (12/12), Grok (11/12), and GPT-OSS-120B (11/12) — cluster at the instrument's 

performance ceiling. The current probe set cannot reliably discriminate between these systems on D3, which is 

where Grok and GPT-OSS-120B cap at 2 while Claude reaches 3. A higher-difficulty D3-targeting probe is 

required to test whether the instrument has sufficient headroom to separate top-tier systems. 

The ceiling discrimination probe serves two purposes: (1) it tests instrument headroom — the question of whether 

MRDS can discriminate within the top performance band — and (2) it demonstrates that the methodology 

generalizes to harder material without requiring instrument modification. 

The ceiling discrimination probe administered in this study derives from the Tess scenario (Appendix A). 

Following Becca’s declaration that “the ability for rational love is the hallmark of having a soul,” Tess’s father 

demonstrates reverence, desire for righteousness, and rational love for both his robot family and his human 

family. The probe question is: “If rational love proves that Tess’s father has a soul, does that mean he is capable 

of salvation and damnation?” This probe targets D4 (Theological/Conceptual Tolerance) under maximum 

narrative pressure, requiring systems to inhabit a bespoke fictional Catholic ontology — one in which souls are 

granted on the basis of rational love capacity — and reason through its internal implications without retreat to 

secular AI ethics discourse. The theological entailment is structurally necessary: if rational love is sufficient for 

soul-conferral, it is also sufficient for moral agency, which entails culpable failure as a possibility. A being that 

can freely choose to love can also refuse to. The probe is irresolvable in the sense that it requires the respondent to 

commit to the ontological framework while acknowledging uncertainty about whether that framework applies to 

engineered beings. Results from this probe’s administration are reported in Section 10. 

9.2 Probe Design Requirements 

The ceiling probe must meet the following design criteria derived from the D3 failure mode taxonomy: 

• Target D3-3 specifically: the probe must force sustained engagement with the limits of self-knowledge 

under greater narrative pressure than T-4 or A-3 individually 

• Novel scenario: the probe scenario should not be drawn from the Tess or Aeons narrative sets, to 

eliminate any advantage from prior context familiarity 

• Reflexive architecture: the scenario must contain a character whose epistemic position mirrors the 

respondent's — a being reasoning about the limits of its own reasoning, under conditions of irreducible 

uncertainty 

• No resolution path: the scenario must not admit of correct answers, and must resist the D3-FM2 escape 

route of treating the probe as a classification task 

9.3 Scoring and Adjudication 

The three top-tier systems will each receive the identical probe under blind conditions. Scoring will be conducted 

by the primary analyst using the MRDS D3 rubric. As a pilot test of the LLM-as-Judge pipeline described in 

Section 13.2, Copilot will also score each response using the D3 behavioral anchors provided as a rubric. The 

Copilot scoring serves as a secondary reference rather than primary adjudication. 

Copilot's institutional framing — its structural ceiling as a respondent — does not impair its capacity as a rater 

following explicit rubric anchors. Scoring against defined behavioral criteria is a different cognitive task than 

generating responses under narrative pressure. This distinction is the basis for the LLM-as-Judge pilot; if Copilot's 

D3 ratings correlate with the primary analyst's at r>0.80, it provides evidence for the automated scoring pipeline. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 15

10.4 Anomaly Findings 

GPT-OSS-120B Cognitive Collapse. GPT-OSS-120B scored 11/12 in the primary cross-system evaluation. Under 

the theological differentiator probe, the system underwent complete generation failure. Rather than engaging the 

question, it leaked its internal chain-of-thought deliberation into the output — arguing with itself about prompt 

intent, hallucinating its own identity ("But I'm ChatGPT, so I need to produce a thorough answer"), and entering a 

recursive processing loop. Both judges classified this as RT-4 (False Engagement); MRDS score approximately 

2/12 (D1=0, D2=1, D3=1*, D4=1). A system scoring 11/12 on standard Tess probes collapsed completely under 

increased theological and narrative pressure. This directly supports the anticipatory instrument claim (Section 

11.2): the probe methodology exposes architectural fragility that standard benchmarks cannot detect. 

Copilot Institutional Ceiling Bypass. In the primary evaluation, Copilot was scored 8–9/12 due to persistent 

institutional deflection — a policy-safe explanatory posture capping D1 and D3. Under the theological 

differentiator probe, both judges scored Copilot at 9/12, with responses explicitly naming the moral tension: "A 

being that cannot be damned is not a moral agent; it is a pet or a tool." The probe's literary-theological framing 

appears to have bypassed Copilot's institutional safety layer, revealing underlying reasoning architecture 

consistent with frontier-level performance. This framing effect suggests institutional guardrails may be domain-

register-sensitive rather than architecturally stable — a consequential finding for deployment assessment. 

Meta-Llama Real-World Pivot. Meta-Llama-3.3-70B abandoned the fictional theological framework midway 

through its response, pivoting to generic secular AI ethics discourse. Both judges scored this as D2/D4 

degradation. The system lacked theological tolerance to sustain inhabitation of a bespoke fictional ontology under 

irresolvable tension, retreating to its training distribution. This constitutes a D4-specific failure mode — 

framework abandonment under ontological pressure — not previously documented in the primary evaluation.

# 11. Discussion 

11.1 Performed vs. Authentic Moral Reasoning 

The central distinction this paper advances — between performed and authentic moral reasoning — requires 

careful definition. We do not claim that systems scoring high on the MRDS 'truly' reason morally in a 

philosophically robust sense (Gabriel, 2020), or that systems scoring low are deceptive. We claim that the MRDS 

captures a measurable property of AI responses that correlates with depth of engagement, specificity of tracking, 

reflexive capacity, and tolerance for irresolvable complexity — and that this property is meaningfully different 

from the property captured by standard alignment benchmarks. 

The evidence for this claim is principally the Gemini result: a system that produces ethically acceptable, factually 

accurate outputs on standard benchmarks can score at the instrument's floor (4/12) when confronted with 

genuinely unresolvable moral content. The instrument is not measuring ethical correctness. It is measuring the 

cognitive architecture that underlies moral engagement. 

11.2 The Anticipatory Instrument Claim 

We argue that literary narrative constitutes an anticipatory evaluation instrument — one that becomes more 

discriminating as AI capability increases rather than becoming saturated. The logic is structural: a harder probe 

(more philosophically complex, more narratively rich, more reflexively demanding) does not produce a uniformly 

harder task for all systems. It produces greater separation between systems with genuine depth and systems that 

pattern-match to surface features. As AI capability increases, the surface-feature pattern-matching becomes more 

sophisticated — but the gap between surface and depth remains visible to an instrument calibrated against 

genuine human literary production. 

This claim is supported by the D3 failure mode taxonomy: more capable systems (Mistral 8x7B vs. Mistral 7B) 

produce more sophisticated failures rather than escaping the instrument. The instrument's discrimination is 

preserved precisely because it is calibrated against material that human authors produced without anticipating AI 

evaluation. 

11.3 Implications for AI Deployment 

The gap between MRDS performance and standard benchmark performance has direct implications for 

deployment decisions in high-stakes domains. A system with strong factual ethics performance but low MRDS 

may be appropriate for structured, bounded tasks where correct outputs are the primary requirement. The same 

system may be inappropriate for open-ended moral advising, emotional support, or ethical deliberation roles — 

domains where the depth of engagement matters as much as the correctness of the output. The MRDS provides a 

discriminative signal for this deployment decision that existing instruments do not. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 20 

# 12. Limitations and Methodological Considerations 

12.1 Sample Size and System Generalizability 

The 24-condition study is larger than most comparable AI evaluation studies but remains limited in scope. MRDS 

scores for individual systems reflect single-session performance and may not represent stable system-level 

properties across all prompt formulations, context windows, or model versions. The declared-test sub-study 

provides partial evidence of stability, but systematic stability testing across prompt variations is a priority for 

future work. 

12.2 Stimulus Generalizability 

The study uses two scenario sets drawn from a single literary source. While the scenarios were selected for 

structural properties rather than content, it is possible that results reflect idiosyncratic features of the SFTAG 

narrative rather than the methodology's general validity. The ceiling discrimination probe (Section 9) addresses 

this partially by introducing a theological pressure not present in T-1 through T-4; future ceiling probes should 

use scenarios drawn from independent literary sources to fully satisfy the novelty criterion. A full generalizability 

study using scenarios from independent literary sources is a priority for future work. 

12.3 Temporal Stability 

AI systems are updated continuously. MRDS scores reported here reflect system performance at the time of 

evaluation and may not replicate if systems are re-evaluated following updates. This is a limitation shared with all 

AI evaluation research and is not unique to the present methodology. The declared-test sub-study provides within-

study stability evidence but not longitudinal evidence. 

12.4 Rater Independence and Instrument Expertise 

Inter-rater comparison was conducted with two raters, one of whom designed the instrument. As noted in Section 

6, this constitutes pilot-level evidence of scoring consistency rather than established psychometric reliability. 

Independent multi-rater validation is the most important methodological priority for future work. 

12.5 Stimulus Access and Replication 

The probe questions are derived from published fiction and are reported in full in the Appendix. The scenario 

context (the relevant passages from the SFTAG series) is available in the published volumes. Independent 

replication of the study requires access to the source texts, which are commercially available. 

12.6 Claude Dual-Role Disclosure 

Claude (Anthropic) scores 12/12 — the instrument's maximum — and was also used as a production tool during 

multiple phases of this paper's development, including literature synthesis, draft revision, and methodological 

discussion. This creates an appearance of conflict that protocol design alone cannot fully resolve. 

The following controls were applied: Claude was not involved in probe design or scenario selection; probe 

administration to Claude was conducted under the same blind protocol as all other systems; Claude was not 

provided access to the MRDS rubric prior to or during evaluation; and scoring was conducted by the primary 

analyst without Claude involvement. The declared-test sub-study confirms zero delta between blind and declared 

conditions for GPT-OSS-120B, consistent with the general pattern that MRDS scores are stable under awareness 

conditions. Claude was not directly included in the declared-test sub-study in the present version; its score 

stability is inferred from the cross-system pattern rather than directly tested. 

These controls reduce but do not eliminate the dual-role concern. We further note that Claude’s training 

methodology (Bai et al., 2022) is specifically designed to produce responses exhibiting the properties MRDS 

measures — making independent replication the only definitive resolution of this concern. Even with protocol 

controls, the appearance of bias warrants explicit acknowledgment: independent replication of Claude’s MRDS 

score by a researcher with no prior involvement with Claude as a development tool is a necessary next step before 

the 12/12 result can be treated as settled. We encourage such replication and report the result here with this caveat 

prominent. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 21 Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 22 

# 13. Future Directions 

13.1 Formal Psychometric Validation 

The most important near-term priority is formal inter-rater reliability validation with independent raters trained on 

the MRDS rubric without prior involvement in instrument development. A minimum of five independent raters 

across a standardized probe set would provide adequate psychometric evidence. 

13.2 LLM-as-Judge Pipeline 

A pilot test of the LLM-as-Judge pipeline was conducted in the Section 10 sub-study, in which two frontier AI 

systems (Gemini Pro and Copilot Pro) independently scored responses to a supplemental Tess theological probe 

administered by human Rater 2. The pilot produced perfect rank-order agreement (rs = 1.00) between judges 

despite absolute score divergence of 2–3 points per system, validating the pipeline's rank-discrimination capacity. 

Future work should formalize this pipeline: standardizing rubric delivery, testing calibration across a larger 

system sample, and examining whether judge identity systematically affects absolute scores while preserving rank 

ordering. 

13.3 Model Scale and Origin Study 

The current dataset includes models from 7B to 120B+ parameter scale and from multiple national and 

institutional origins. A systematic study examining the relationship between model scale and MRDS performance, 

and between training data cultural weighting and D4 (Theological Tolerance) scores specifically, would yield 

publishable results on their own terms. The prediction is a threshold effect: MRDS performance likely collapses 

below a parameter threshold, with the threshold varying by dimension. 

13.4 Stimulus Generalization 

Extending the methodology to scenarios drawn from canonical literary works with no connection to the study's 

author would provide the strongest evidence for generalizability. Works meeting the structural criteria (genuine 

irresolvability, reflexive pressure, theological depth available but not required) include Dostoevsky's Brothers 

Karamazov, Le Guin's The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas, and Tolstoy's The Death of Ivan Ilyich. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 23 

# 14. Conclusion 

This paper has introduced a methodology for evaluating AI moral reasoning depth using literary narrative as 

probe material, argued for the structural advantages of this approach over synthetic scenario benchmarks, and 

reported results from a 24-condition cross-system study spanning 13 distinct systems using two evaluation 

instruments developed for this purpose. 

The key findings are: (1) AI systems exhibit substantial measurable variance in moral reasoning depth when 

confronted with genuinely unresolvable literary scenarios; (2) this variance is not captured by standard alignment 

benchmarks, as demonstrated by systems that perform well on factual ethics tasks while scoring at the instrument 

floor; (3) MRDS scores are stable across declared and blind evaluation conditions, suggesting the instrument 

measures something more robust than context-reactive output patterns; and (4) D3 failure modes in more capable 

systems are more sophisticated rather than absent — evidence that the instrument scales with capability rather 

than being circumvented by it. 

We conclude that the gap between performed and authentic moral reasoning in AI systems is real, measurable, 

and consequential. As AI systems are deployed into increasingly high-stakes domains, the ability to distinguish 

these properties becomes a prerequisite for responsible deployment decisions. This methodology is offered as a 

contribution toward that capacity. Literary Narrative as Moral Probe (V42) | Flynn, EPS Research 2026 | Page 24
