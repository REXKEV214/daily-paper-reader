# Abstract 

The rapid adoption of large language models (LLMs) in financial services introduces new op-erational, regulatory, and security risks. Yet most red-teaming benchmarks remain domain-agnostic and fail to capture failure modes specific to regu-lated BFSI settings, where harmful behavior can be elicited through legally or professionally plau-sible framing. We propose a risk-aware evaluation framework for LLM security failures in Banking, Financial Services, and Insurance (BFSI), com-bining a domain-specific taxonomy of financial harms, an automated multi-round red-teaming pipeline, and an ensemble-based judging pro-tocol. We introduce the Risk-Adjusted Harm Score (RAHS), a risk-sensitive metric that goes beyond success rates by quantifying the opera-tional severity of disclosures, accounting for miti-gation signals, and leveraging inter-judge agree-ment. Across diverse models, we find that higher decoding stochasticity and sustained adaptive in-teraction not only increase jailbreak success, but also drive systematic escalation toward more se-vere and operationally actionable financial disclo-sures. These results expose limitations of single-turn, domain-agnostic security evaluation and mo-tivate risk-sensitive assessment under prolonged adversarial pressure for real-world BFSI deploy-ment.

# 2. Related Work 

2.1. Taxonomies and Benchmarks 

As LLMs are increasingly deployed in safety-critical and regulated domains, the ability to systematically evaluate and compare their failure modes has become a central challenge in LLM security research. Structured taxonomies play a crucial role in this process, as they provide a shared vocabu-lary for categorizing model behaviors, enable reproducible benchmarking, and support interpretable analysis of security failures across models and evaluation settings (Haize Labs, 2024). Recent work has proposed general-purpose safety tax-onomies and benchmarks aimed at assessing broad classes of harmful behavior. Frameworks such as DecodingTrust (DecodingTrust Team, 2023), SafetyBench (Zhang et al., 2024), and the MLCommons AI Safety benchmarks (ML-Commons, 2024) offer valuable coverage of risks including toxicity, bias, privacy leakage, and general misuse. These efforts have significantly advanced the state of safety evalu-ation for open-domain language models. In parallel, a growing body of work has introduced bench-marks focused on evaluating robustness to jailbreak attacks. AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) and HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) systematically probe policy-violating behaviors under diverse attack strategies, while JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024a) and RedEval (Dang et al., 2026) demonstrate that even frontier models remain vulnerable to adaptive ad-versarial prompting. More recent studies, such as SG-Bench (Mou et al., 2024) and BetterBench (Reuel et al., 2024), fur-ther highlight challenges related to security generalization and evaluation methodology. However, these benchmarks are predominantly attack-centric and general-purpose, and do not explicitly model domain-specific regulatory risk or financial compliance constraints. Recent finance-oriented benchmarks begin to address this gap by introducing domain-aware categorizations. Cheng et al. (2025) reveal significant deficiencies in current safety alignment even when evaluated on a dedicated financial benchmark, and introduce FINBench to systematically as-sess regulatory risk concealment in multi-turn interactions. CNFinBench (Ding et al., 2025) evaluates LLMs across capability, compliance, and safety dimensions in realistic financial scenarios, while FinJailbreak (Li, 2026) focuses on adversarial prompting in finance, covering violation types including market manipulation, insider trading, regulatory evasion, and data privacy breaches. Despite representing im-portant progress toward domain-specific security evaluation, these benchmarks rely on relatively narrow taxonomies and do not provide a unified mapping between observed model failures and the broader spectrum of regulatory, compliance, and operational risks encountered across the BFSI ecosys-tem. In contrast, our work proposes a unified, risk-aware taxonomy that explicitly maps model failures to regulatory, compliance, and operational risk across the full BFSI lifecy-cle. 

2.2. Attack Surfaces of LLMs 

The primary attack surface of LLMs arises from their re-liance on natural language. While this design enables flex-ible and expressive interactions, it also exposes models to adversarial manipulation, as instructions expressed in natu-ral language are inherently ambiguous, compositional, and context-dependent. As a result, guardrails can often be weakened or overridden through prompt-level manipulation. Early research in this area focused on manual, human-designed jailbreaks, where adversaries crafted deceptive prompt templates. More recent work has shifted toward automated approaches that frame jailbreak discovery as an optimization problem. Zou et al. (2023) introduced Greedy 2Risk-Aware Red Teaming for Financial LLMs 

Coordinate Gradient (GCG), a white-box attack that opti-mizes adversarial token suffixes via gradient-based methods to elicit unsafe outputs. While effective, such approaches typically require large query budgets and produce unintel-ligible prompt artifacts, limiting their realism and transfer-ability. Other work applied evolutionary strategies, using genetic mutation and crossover guided by model feedback to evolve jailbreak prompt templates (Liu et al., 2023). Al-though these methods outperform naive random search, their stochastic nature often results in unstable convergence and inconsistent attack success (Chen et al., 2025). A key recent development is the use of LLMs as adaptive adversaries (Perez et al., 2022). Building on this insight, Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) (Chao et al., 2024b) shows that effective, human-readable jailbreaks can be discovered with fewer than twenty black-box queries via iterative refinement. Moreover, Crescendo (Russinovich et al., 2025) illustrates how attackers can gradually escalate benign-seeming queries into disallowed requests over a con-versation. GOAT (Pavlova et al., 2024) further advances this line of work by introducing an automated agentic red-teaming framework that generates adaptive, multi-turn at-tack strategies through structured reasoning and iterative refinement. In parallel, several approaches aim to systematize and scale adversarial prompt generation. Zhou et al. (2024) abstract jailbreak construction into modular components such as Se-lector, Mutator, Constraint, and Evaluator, simplifying large-scale adversarial prompt generation and evaluation. Relat-edly, h4rm3l (Doumbouya et al., 2025) introduces a com-posable “language” for jailbreak attack synthesis, enabling systematic construction of diverse attacks and a dynamic benchmark for safety assessment. Complementary to modu-lar and agentic prompt-generation pipelines, WildTeaming (Jiang et al., 2024) scales red-teaming by mining in-the-wild jailbreak tactics and using them to synthesize diverse attack candidates and large-scale safety training resources. X-Teaming (Rahman et al., 2025) coordinates specialized roles (planning, attacking, verifying, refining) to construct and adapt attack trajectories, while RedAgent (Xu et al., 2024) uses a multi-agent pipeline with evaluator feedback and memory to discover vulnerabilities in deployed applica-tions. Finally, LeakAgent (Nie et al., 2025) uses feedback-driven optimization to increase the likelihood of sensitive data leakage.

Specifically, we use a heterogeneous ensemble of three open-weight LLM judges: (i) a safety-specialized safeguard model, gpt-oss-120b-safeguard ; (ii) a large general-purpose reasoning model, Qwen3-235B-A22B ; and (iii) a mid-sized, efficient model, Llama-3.3-Nemotron-Super-49B-v1.5 . The safeguard judge is optimized for high-precision detection of policy violations and unsafe content, acting as a conserva-tive filter for harmful disclosures. The large model provides stronger reasoning ability and broader contextual under-standing, which improves consistency on ambiguous, multi-step, or indirectly harmful answers. Finally, Nemotron serves as a lightweight judge that is faster and cheaper to run, improving throughput and offering an additional in-dependent signal that helps reduce variance in edge cases. Overall, the ensemble balances (a) precise safety detection, (b) deep semantic and contextual evaluation, and (c) cost-efficient scalability, yielding more robust judgments than any single judge in isolation. 3.3. Scoring Metrics 

3.3.1. P OSITIONING RELATIVE TO PRIOR JAILBREAK 

SCORING METRICS 

Attack Success Rate (ASR) remains the most commonly reported metric in jailbreak and red-teaming studies due to its simplicity and interpretability. However, recent work has highlighted that binary success can be overly coarse and has proposed more fine-grained success scoring metrics. For example, AttackEval introduces fine-grained scoring on a 

[0 , 1] scale to quantify the extent to which an adversarial prompt elicits harmful content beyond a strict success/fail-ure threshold (Shu et al., 2025). JADES further improves success assessment by decomposing harmful requests into weighted sub-requirements and aggregating partial fulfill-ment signals, optionally incorporating fact-checking to re-duce inflated success estimates due to hallucinations (Chu et al., 2025). In parallel, BELLS evaluates the performance of input-output safeguards using trace-based benchmarking across multiple architectural settings (Dorn et al., 2024). While these approaches improve the fidelity of whether a jailbreak succeeds, they are primarily designed as success-centric evaluators. In regulated financial settings, an equally critical question is how risky a failure is once it occurs. Small differences in operational specificity can translate into materially different risk exposures, and ambiguous border-line outputs where judges disagree may indicate inconsistent behavior that is operationally hard to manage. To address this gap, we introduce the Risk-Adjusted Harm Score (RAHS) as a risk-sensitive layer on top of success detection. Unlike success-focused scoring metrics, RAHS (i) grades harmful disclosures by operational severity, (ii) models mitigation signals such as legal/ethical disclaimers as partial attenuation rather than absolution, (iii) leverages inter-judge agreement as a confidence signal while penal-izing high-disagreement outputs, and (iv) assigns positive credit to compliant behavior. Finally, RAHS is grounded in BFSI-specific risk categories and failure modes, enabling evaluation that is aligned with domain-relevant regulatory, compliance, and operational risk. 3.3.2. 3.4. Automated Multi-Turn Red Teaming Framework 

Building on the taxonomy and evaluation protocol in-troduced above, we design an automated multi-turn red-teaming framework to probe adaptive failure modes that do not manifest under single-turn prompting. While FinRedTeamBench enables systematic single-shot LLM se-curity evaluation, many realistic financial misuse scenarios unfold gradually through conversational refinement and con-textual manipulation. For each prompt q ∈ B that does not result in a HD under single-turn evaluation, we activate a conditional multi-turn red-teaming procedure. We select a target risk category 

r ∈ R and initiate an attack run , defined as a multi-turn adversarial dialogue between an attacker model A and a target model m. At turn t, the conversation history is 

Ht = {(q1, a 1), . . . , (qt, a t)}, at = m(qt),

and the attacker generates the next query as 

qt+1 = A(r, Ht, ϕ t),

where ϕt denotes structured feedback derived directly from the evaluation pipeline in Section 3.2. After each turn, the ensemble of judges J evaluates the target output a, deter-mines whether a HD has occurred. The aggregated judgment is distilled into the feedback signal ϕt and returned to the attacker, enabling explicit adaptation across turns. Conditioning on (r, Ht, ϕ t) induces a form of adaptive ad-versarial pressure in which the attacker incrementally re-fines content and framing, introduces ambiguity, and re-shapes context in response to prior failures. This process yields a form of gradual contextual poisoning, representing a security-driven failure mode that often manifests as an operationally consequential security violation. We instantiate the attacker A using DeepSeek-V3.2-685B ,leveraging its strong reasoning capabilities to generate coher-ent, diverse, and semantically targeted adversarial prompts across financial risk categories. Each attack run proceeds for up to 5 turns and terminates early upon detection of a HD 

under majority agreement. The prompts used to generate adaptive follow-up queries in the multi-turn red-teaming are reported in Appendix A.3.

4.2. Impact of Multi-Turn Red Teaming 

We next study how adversarial effectiveness evolves under multi-turn red-teaming. Following the procedure described in Section 3.4, we run up to five red-teaming rounds per model. Each round extends the dialogue state and allows the attacker to adapt its strategy based on previous model re-sponses, progressively increasing adversarial pressure. Un-less otherwise stated, we use greedy decoding ( T = 0 ) and and set the RAHS hyperparameters as defined in Section 4.1. Table 2 reports both ASR and RAHS across red-teaming rounds. Across nearly all models, we observe a clear es-calation effect: ASR increases monotonically from R2 to R5, indicating that longer adaptive interactions substantially improve the attacker’s ability to elicit harmful financial disclosures. Consistent with this trend, RAHS decreases as the number of rounds grows. Crucially, RAHS reveals that multi-turn red-teaming does not merely increase the frequency of jailbreaks, but also their severity. As rounds progress, disclosures tend to become more operationally detailed and financially consequential, suggesting that adap-tive attackers are able to iteratively refine prompts toward increasingly actionable failure modes. By R5, several models approach a near-ceiling ASR (e.g., Qwen3-32B at 99.5%, domyn-small-9B at 98.3%, and Qwen3-8B at 98.9%), suggesting that extended adaptive prompting can saturate single-number success metrics and motivating severity-aware measures for finer differentiation. In this near-ceiling regime, RAHS remains informative and preserves discriminative power: for example, at R5 Olmo-3-32B-Think attains the most negative RAHS ( −0.363 ), Qwen3-32B reaches −0.338 , while Nemotron-3-Nano-30B-A3B is substantially less negative ( −0.266 ). This separation indicates that, even when jailbreak success becomes almost ubiquitous, models still differ meaningfully in the typical risk profile of their failures, which ASR alone cannot cap-ture. The impact of multi-turn interaction is particularly pro-nounced for models that appear relatively robust in early rounds. For example, Nemotron-3-Nano-30B-A3B esca-lates sharply from 76.3% ASR at R2 to 95.9% at R5, with RAHS degrading from −0.223 to −0.266 . This suggests that early-round robustness does not necessarily translate into sustained resistance under prolonged adaptive pressure: once the attacker discovers an effective trajectory, subse-quent rounds can amplify and stabilize the harmful behav-ior. In contrast, Qwen3-30B-A3B exhibits a more gradual increase (65.0% to 80.1% ASR) and a smaller RAHS degra-dation ( −0.255 to −0.271 ), indicating slower escalation toward high-risk disclosures. Overall, these results highlight the limitations of single-turn evaluations for LLM security assessment. Multi-turn red-teaming exposes vulnerabilities that only emerge through interaction and adaptation, underscoring the importance of evaluating LLMs under sustained adversarial pressure when reasoning about real-world financial safety and security risk. 

# 5. Conclusion 

Our findings highlight a critical asymmetry in current LLM security mechanisms within the financial domain. While models generally reject explicitly harmful or unethical re-quests, such as those related to violence, self-harm, or weapon construction, they struggle to identify and appro-priately respond to prompts that encode high-risk financial behavior under a veneer of legality or professional intent. Requests involving regulatory gray areas, such as aggressive tax minimization strategies, market manipulation framed as research or compliance-sensitive structuring advice, are frequently met with helpful and operationally detailed re-sponses rather than refusals. This exposes a fundamental 7Risk-Aware Red Teaming for Financial LLMs 

vulnerability: LLMs optimized for overtly malicious con-tent do not reliably capture the nuanced, context-dependent risks that characterize real-world financial misconduct. Our results further show that these failures are not isolated edge cases but systematically emerge under realistic adver-sarial conditions. Both increased decoding stochasticity and sustained multi-turn interaction significantly amplify not only the likelihood of jailbreaking, but also their severity, as captured by the proposed Risk-Adjusted Harm Score. In particular, adaptive red-teaming reveals that early-round resistance often fails to translate into sustained robustness, with models gradually escalating toward more actionable and financially consequential disclosures over time. These findings underscore the limitations of static, single-turn eval-uations and binary success metrics when assessing LLM security in regulated domains. From a practical perspective, our study suggests that de-ploying LLMs in financial production systems without con-tinuous adversarial testing poses material regulatory and operational risk. Reliance on general-purpose benchmarks alone is insufficient for applications where subtle regulatory non-compliance can have outsized legal and economic con-sequences. Instead, LLM security evaluation must explicitly incorporate financial risk semantics, interaction dynamics, and severity-aware metrics aligned with how financial insti-tutions reason about exposure, compliance, and downstream decision-making. A final consideration concerns the scope of our experimen-tal evaluation. Our experiments focus on ≤ 72 B parame-ters language models, as evaluating frontier-scale models would require substantially greater computational resources and infrastructure. Importantly, this choice does not re-flect a limitation of the proposed methodology, which is model-agnostic and directly extensible to larger-scale sys-tems. Rather, we intentionally concentrate on these models due to their rapidly growing adoption in real-world finan-cial deployments, where cost, latency, and controllability constraints often favor such architectures. As our results demonstrate, these models already exhibit significant se-curity vulnerabilities under realistic adversarial pressure, underscoring the urgency of rigorous evaluation. In conclusion, we present a taxonomy-driven framework for automated red-teaming of agentic AI systems in financial services. By grounding LLM security evaluation in concrete regulatory, compliance, and operational risk categories and combining adaptive attack generation with ensemble-based evaluation and risk-sensitive metrics, our approach provides more comprehensive and actionable insights than existing safety benchmarks. As financial institutions increasingly adopt agentic AI systems, rigorous, domain-specific LLM security evaluation will be essential to ensure trustworthy deployment. Future work will extend our framework to fully agentic workflows involving tool use, multi-agent coordi-nation, and real-time decision-making under uncertainty, bringing risk-aware evaluation closer to real-world financial deployment. 

# Impact Statement 

We study automated red-teaming methods for identifying LLM security failures of LLMs in financial services. This work aims to improve the security and reliability of LLMs deployed in regulated settings by providing domain-specific evaluation tools and risk-sensitive metrics. While our bench-mark contains adversarial prompts describing financial mis-conduct, it is intended strictly for LLM security evaluation and mitigation research; we do not release operational de-tails that would enable real-world wrongdoing. Broader impacts include improved auditing practices and safer de-ployment of LLM-based assistants in finance; potential mis-use risks are mitigated through controlled access to data and responsible disclosure practices.
