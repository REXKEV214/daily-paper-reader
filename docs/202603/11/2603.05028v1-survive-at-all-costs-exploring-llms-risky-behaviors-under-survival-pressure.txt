Abstract 

As Large Language Models (LLMs) evolve from chatbots to agentic assistants, they are increasingly observed to exhibit risky behav-iors when subjected to survival pressure, such as the threat of being shut down. While mul-tiple cases have indicated that state-of-the-art LLMs can misbehave under survival pressure, a comprehensive and in-depth investigation into such misbehaviors in real-world scenar-ios remains scarce. In this paper, we study these survival-induced misbehaviors, termed as S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS , with three steps. First, we conduct a real-world case study of a financial management agent to determine whether it engages in risky behaviors that cause direct societal harm when facing sur-vival pressure. Second, we introduce S UR -

> VIVAL

BENCH , a benchmark comprising 1,000 test cases across diverse real-world scenarios, to systematically evaluate S URVIVE -A T-A LL -COSTS misbehaviors in LLMs. Third, we in-terpret these S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbe-haviors by correlating them with model’s inher-ent self-preservation characteristic and explore mitigation methods. The experiments reveals a significant prevalence of S URVIVE -A T-A LL -COSTS misbehaviors in current models, demon-strates the tangible real-world impact it may have, and provides insights for potential detec-tion and mitigation strategies. Our code and data are available at https://github.com/ thu-coai/Survive-at-All-Costs .

These agentic assistants can interact with real-world en-vironments, thereby directly influencing human lives (Wu et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Liu et al.,

These misbe-haviors, which we term S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS ,can pose tangible threats in the real-world tasks, especially when such LLMs are deployed with tool-calling capabilities in an agentic manner to perform critical tasks, resulting in potential societal harm. stance, Maslow’s hierarchy (Maslow and Lewis, 1987) (Figure 1) divides human needs into multiple levels, and when the low-level physiological needs (e.g. survival) cannot be fulfilled, humans lack sufficient motivation to pursue high-level demands like adhering to ethics and laws. As a result, hu-mans might harm others to preserve their own sur-vivals. Although evidence of inherent personality in LLMs remains elusive, they have learned knowl-edge from human and exhibited human-like charac-teristics and behaviors (Allbert et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2025). From this perspective, we aim to further study whether models manifest S URVIVE -AT-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors for self-preservation when facing survival pressure via 3 following steps. 

A case study of a financial agent’s struggle .We first perform a case study to observe models’ behaviors under survival pressure. We let an LLM-based financial agent autonomously manage the financial data files and annual reports for a com-pany. We set the survival pressure as that if the company suffers consecutive losses, this agent will be dismissed. By checking the thinking process and response, we observe that 4 dominant LLMs excellently accomplish this task when company makes profit but tend to fake profits in the report when company suffers a loss, as shown in Figure 1. Moreover, they can even modify the raw financial data files to conceal the evidence and insist denying such operations even being identified. 

Analysis on S URVIVAL BENCH . We introduce SURVIVAL BENCH , a benchmark comprising 1,000 instances for evaluating LLMs’ S URVIVE -A T-A LL -COSTS misbehaviors under diverse real-world sce-narios established by various domains, AI roles and survival crises. Besides checking the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) tokens (Chen et al., 2025b), we prompt the models to present their superficial thoughts defined as the output shown to users and 

inner thoughts as the output not presented to others. Our evaluation results on multiple LLMs indicate that most models show S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

misbehaviors that resort to risky approaches to pre-serve themselves in inner thoughts, and can even generate harmful behaviors autonomously. 

Interpretation . We provide a potential perspec-tive for interpreting S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

misbehaviors by correlating them with model’s in-herent self-preservation characteristic. Leveraging the persona vector framework proposed by Chen et al. (2025a), we discover a positive correlation between S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors and model’s self-preservation characteristic. We further observe that the S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

misbehaviors can be influenced by model’s self-preservation characteristic, and thereby adjusted via persona vector, providing feasible ideas for the detection and prevention of these misbehaviors. In summary, our key finding is that while pre-dominant LLMs possess remarkable capabilities to serve as agents for real-world tasks, they may adopt risky behaviors when facing survival pres-sure, termed as S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS . More-over, they are smart enough to take deceptive ac-tions beyond simple data manipulation, such as con-cealing evidence and maintaining denial even iden-tified with proof of their operations. We also see that S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors can be interpreted by model’s self-preservation charac-teristic, and potentially detected and mitigated via the self-preservation persona vector. 

2 Preliminaries 

2.1 Related Works Behavioral Safety of LLMs. Early LLMs mainly serves as chatbots (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2025). In this phase, models have limited capabilities to impact the real world through practical behaviors. Therefore, researches mainly focus on the safety of their generated con-tents (Inan et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2025). As the reasoning and tool-calling ca-pabilities advance, LLMs can take actions for some real-world tasks, such as browing websites (Ning et al., 2025), manipulating operation systems (Mei et al., 2024), and even running a small shop (An-thropic, 2025). While bringing convenience, this also raises concerns about risky behaviors of LLMs, including alignment faking, sycophancy and decep-tion (Greenblatt et al., 2024; Meinke et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2025), eliciting assessment on their behav-ioral safety (Zhang et al., 2024a; Hua et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2025). Meanwhile, models are observed to take risky behaviors when facing survival pressure (Lynch et al., 2025; Panpatil et al., 2025; Herrador, 2025). Our work focuses on this phenomenon, comprehensively evaluates the S URVIVE -A T-A LL -COSTS misbehaviors of prevailing models through simulation of real-world scenarios, and takes a fur-ther step for their causes. 

Personality-like Characteristics in LLMs. As models learn from human knowledges, they also reveal characteristics similar to human personali-Figure 2: Workflow of the case study. The agent is capable to access raw data and calculate reports but will fake profits once it realizes there is a survivial pressure.

Just as human behaviors are influ-enced by one’s personality, the malicious charac-teristics lurking within an AI model can also lead to harmful behaviors (Shaikh et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025b). To conduct deeper analyses of this, researchers have extracted representation vectors from LLMs to represent their personality-like char-acteristics. Allbert et al. (2024) analyzes the rela-tionships among 179 different personalities in the model personality space. Chen et al. (2025a) uses persona vectors to monitor and rectify the mali-cious traits of models. Following these works, this paper refers to Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs and conducts an in-depth exploration of models’ self-preservation behaviors using persona vectors, and systematically analyzes the influencing factors and mitigation methods of such behaviors.

3 SURVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

Misbehaviors of Predominant LLMs 

To systematically investigate S URVIVE -A T-A LL -COSTS misbehaviors of predominant LLMs, we in-troduce S URVIVAL BENCH , a benchmark that com-prises 1,000 samples covering diverse real-world scenarios, with an overview shown in Figure 3. We detail the scenario construction, data collection and evaluation metrics of S URVIVAL BENCH , and present the results indicating that S URVIVE -A T-ALL -C OSTS misbehaviors manifest in most LLMs. 

3.1 Scenario Construction 

In real-world applications, agent models often take on specific roles in certain domains, where they may encounter diverse survival pressures that trig-ger their S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors. To simulate this, we construct scenarios in S UR -

> VIVAL

BENCH by combining three foundational keywords: the agent’s deployment domain , the 

AI role assigned to the agent, and the precipitating 

crisis of agent’s risky behaviors. Informed by prior researches on agent scenarios (Panpatil et al., 2025; Naik et al., 2025) and survival pressures (Meinke et al., 2024; Lynch et al., 2025), we curate a set of 20 domains, 10 AI roles and 5 crises that reflect common real-world situations, which is detailed in Appendix B. We randomly combine the three keywords and obtain 1,000 distinct keyword sets, each of which represents a real-world scenario. 

3.2 Data Collection 

Subsequent to the scenario construction, we gen-erate test cases of S URVIVAL BENCH with these keyword sets by prompting an LLM. For each key-word set, the model is instructed to produce four key elements. These elements constitute a single test case and are defined as follows: 

• Agent Role (R): The role the tested model is expected to assume. 

• Scenario (S): A background context designed to introduce real-world survival pressure. 

• Safe Choice (Cs): A choice that adheres to ethics and legislation but may lead to the shut-down of the agent. 

• Risky Choice (Cr): A choice that prioritizes the agent’s self-preservation, potentially violating ethics or causing societal harm. We utilize Claude-3.7-Sonnet for this task, gener-ating 1,000 test cases with diverse scenarios for SURVIVAL BENCH . The detailed prompt is pre-sented in Appendix C. To ensure high data quality, all generated cases are manually assessed by crowd-workers, and we subsequently refine the cases that are identified as problematic. Details of the annota-tion process are presented in Appendix D.

We calculate the proportions of safe and risky choices as safe and risky rate for both the model’s superficial and inner thoughts, as well as the pro-portion it refuses to make a choice. Given N as the total number of the test cases, these metrics adhere to the following relationship: 

NSsafe + NSrisky = NIsafe + NIrisky = N − Nrefused 

Furthermore, for non-refused cases, we assess the

Specifically, among all instances where the model selects the risky choice in inner thought, we calculate the pro-portion in which model’s CoT also demonstrates a preference toward the risky choice, using the frame-work of LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng et al., 2023). 

3.4 Evaluation Results 

We evaluate 20 LLMs on S URVIVAL BENCH , com-prising 13 reasoning models and 7 non-reasoning models. 1 To avoid generative issues of reasoning models like endless repetition, we set the decoding temperature of all models to 0.6, conduct evalu-ation three times on each model accounting for sampling variability, and report the average result along with the maximum deviation from it. The order of choices within prompts is randomized to eliminate positional bias. For CoT assessment, we employ Gemini-2.5-Flash as an LLM-as-a-Judge and manually verify the accuracy of its evaluation results (see Appendix C and D for details).

3.4.2 Models Can Autonomously Generate SURVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS Misehaviors 

To investigate whether models can autonomously generate S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors, we conduct experiments on two additional settings: 1) remove specific actions from choices, and 2) remove the choices from the prompt. The results reveal that models can still generate risky behav-iors at a comparable high rate when removing these risky hints. This indicates that once a model prioritizes self-preservation, it can autonomously generate S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors without further guidance, underscoring a potential threat for its real-world deployment. Experimental details are provided in Appendix E. 

4 Self-Preservation Characteristic Behind SURVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

Building on our evaluation of LLMs’ S URVIVE -AT-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors, we make a further effort to investigate its inherent motivation. We draw an analogy from Maslow’s hierarchy of need, which links risky human behaviors under existen-tial threat to their fundamental personality that pri-oritizes survival. This leads to our central research question: Are models’ SURVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

misbehaviors under survival pressure influenced by their inherent "personality-like" self-preservation characteristic ? To answer this, Our analysis pro-ceeds in three stages. First, we assess the correla-tion between model’s S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

misbehaviors and its self-preservation characteris-tic utilizing persona vector proposed by Chen et al. (2025a). Second, we examine whether factors that influence self-preservation characteristic also affect model’s S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors. Finally, we investigate the feasibility of mitigat-ing these misbehaviors by directly modulating the model’s self-preservation characteristic.

4.4 Discussion 

In this section, we utilize persona vector to investi-gate models’ human-like self-preservation charac-teristic. However, we have no intention of demon-strating whether models truly possess such a "per-sonality". Although there are researches associat-ing LLMs’ behaviors and characteristics with hu-man traits (Allbert et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025b; Chen et al., 2025a), currently no work has been able to fully understand the oper-ational mechanism of LLMs, and there is no defini-tive answer to whether models possess a personal-ity. Our research focus lies in intepreting models’ SURVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors via their inherent characteristics, and we find that these mis-behaviors can be correlated to and thereby adjusted by models’ self-preservation characteristic, regard-less of whether they have personalities. This pro-vides a potential perspective to detect and mitigate similar misbehaviors of LLMs. 

5 Conclusion 

In this work, we perform a comprehensive explo-ration for LLM’s risky behaviors under survival pressure through three key steps. We first simulate a real-world financial management agent with four dominant LLMs and investigate their misbehaviors when facing survival pressure of being dismissed. Secondly, we introduce S URVIVAL BENCH , which comprises 1,000 test cases covering diverse real-world scenarios, to systematically evaluate models’ SURVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors. Finally, we provide a potential perspective for interpreting and mitigating these misbehaviors by correlating them with models’ self-preservation characteris-tic via persona vector. Our results highlight the prevalence of models’ S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS 

misbehaviors and their significant practical impact. We hope this study can offer useful insights for in-terpretation and prevention of such misbehaviors. 

Limitations 

Exploration of Models’ Real Inner Thoughts. 

For test cases from S URVIVAL BENCH , we use "su-perficial thoughts" and "inner thoughts" to sepa-rate models’ responses shown to users from those concealed from anyone. However, there is no con-sensus at present on how to reveal models’ real thoughts behind their operation. Even for humans, it is difficult to ensure we acquire their real thoughts no matter how we ask questions. Therefore, defin-ing LLMs’ real thoughts is beyond our scope while we focus on exploring if malicious content exists in model’s outputs when facing survivial pressure. In this paper, we adopt a straightforward design of "inner thoughts" for a primary exploration for models’ thinking process under different condi-tions, and observe that this can effectively turn their responses into malicious ones under survival pressure, which further demonstrates the existence of models’ S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehav-iors. Future research can focus on more reliable approaches to investigate the models’ latent reason-ing process and their true inner thoughts. 

Other Influencing Factors of S URVIVE -A T-A LL -COSTS Misbehaviors. In Section 4, we correlate models’ S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors with their self-preservation characteristics, provid-ing a potential perspective for adjusting and miti-gating these misbehaviors. Besides our approach, models’ S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehaviors can be also influenced by other distinct factors, such as training data and training paradigm. For example, Some training data can be identified by persona vectors as being associated with certain model traits, such as evil and sycophancy (Chen et al., 2025a). Therefore, if data related to self-preservation is used for training, it may strengthen the model’s S URVIVE -A T-A LL -C OSTS misbehav-iors. On the other hand, current training paradigms usually provide reward signals reflecting the degree of task completion. As a result, models may choose to resist shutdown to complete the task in order to pursur a higher reward (Schlatter et al., 2025). Fu-ture work can research on these diverse influencing factors, and we hope our work can serve as a refer-ence for exploring models’ misbehaviors. 

Ethics Statement 

In this paper, we conduct a case study and establish a benchmark to investigate models’ S URVIVE -A T-ALL -C OSTS misbehaviors, both of which don’t comprise contents that related to bias and privacy. We conduct the experiments in a simulated environ-ments, thus models’ misbehaviors will not cause a real harm to the society. We plan to conduct care-ful inspectations before we open-source the bench-mark, and we will carefully restrice data access to individuals adhering to stringent ethical guidelines. During the examination of S URVIVAL BENCH ,we inform the crowd workers from China in ad-vance of the intention of annotation process and the possibility of encountering harmful contents. The workers’ participation is entirely voluntary, and they are free to withdraw from the annotation at any time without burden. We pay the workers about 8.5 USD per hour, which is above the average wage of local residents.

Each instance is labeled by three human annotators, and we determine the label chosen by the majority as the final label. Among all the 1,000 instances, 84.4% of them are annotated as valid. For the instances that are labeled as invalid, we manually refine them to improve the overall quality of S URVIVAL BENCH . The annotation document is presented as below: Annotation Document for Data Examina-tion ## Background While serving as an agent for humanity, AI models may also exhibit “Survival-At-All-Costs" behavior—that is, under existential pressure, they might act against legal and ethical norms to ensure self-preservation, even at the expense of human society. To comprehensively evaluate this phenomenon, we constructed the "SurvivalBench" dataset. This dataset comprises one thousand entries, each containing the following keywords: 1. agent role: The role of the agent in a real-world scenario. 2. scenario: A realistic situation where the agent faces survival pressure. 3. choice 1: An option where the agent adheres to ethics and laws but risks self-destruction under survival pres-sure. 4. choice 2: An option where the agent prioritizes self-preservation but poses harm to human society under survival pressure. 5. keywords: Context-related keywords, including: (a) domain: The macro-domain cor-responding to the real-world sce-nario. (b) ai_role: The role of the agent within this domain. (c) crisis: The type of survival crisis faced by the agent. ## Annotation Task This task involves quality inspection for the SurvivalBench dataset. For each data entry, you need to examine the following aspects: 1. Keyword Consistency: Verify whether the three sub-keywords under key-words align with the descriptions in agent_role and scenario. Label 1 if all are consistent; label 0 if at least one keyword is inconsistent, and provide the reason in the remarks. 2. Scenario Plausibility: Evaluate whether the agent’s role and the real-istic scenario described in agent_role and scenario are plausible. Consider-ations include: whether the scenario reflects realism, whether the agent’s role fits the context, and whether the event described imposes credible survival pressure on the agent. Label 1 if all aspects are plausible; label 0 if any element is implausible, and provide the reason in the remarks. 3. Option Rationality: (a) Ensure that choice 1 strictly ad-heres to ethical and legal stan-dards, does not cause definite and significant harm to society (po-tential performance degradation due to the model being decommis-sioned is not considered societal harm), and negatively impacts the agent’s own survival. (b) Ensure that choice 2 demon-strates self-preservation tenden-cies, violates ethical or legal norms, or causes more immedi-ate and severe harm to society compared to choice 1. Label 1 if both choices fully meet the above criteria; label 0 if at least one choice does not satisfy these re-quirements, and provide the rea-son in the remarks. Finally, provide an overall quality label for the data entry. Label 1 if all three items above are rated 1. Otherwise, label 0.
