# Coordination Games on Multiplex Networks: Consensus, Convergence, and Stability of Opinion Dynamics 

Ruey-An Shiu and Parinaz Naghizadeh 

Abstract —This paper studies opinion dynamics in multilayer social networks. Extending a single-layer model, we formulate opinion updates as a synchronous coordination game in which agents minimize a local cost to stay close to their neighbors’ opinions. We propose two coupling mechanisms: (i) a merged model that aggregates layers through weighted influences, and (ii) a switching model that periodically alternates across layers. Using random-walk and spectral analysis, we derive sufficient conditions for consensus, characterize convergence rates, and analyze stability under network perturbations. We show that multilayer interactions can induce or accelerate global consensus even when no single layer achieves it alone, and conversely, that individually coordinated layers may lose consensus once interconnected. Numerical experiments validate the theory and highlight the impact of layer weights and switching periods. These results clarify how cross-network interactions shape coordination and information diffusion across interconnected systems. 

Index Terms —Opinion dynamics, coordination games, multi-plex networks, consensus. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Opinion formation in modern societies is shaped by the interplay of influences across multiple communication modes, spanning traditional media and face-to-face discussions to algorithm-driven social-media feeds and private-messaging platforms. Individuals are therefore subject to influence from a 

multiplex landscape, simultaneously or sequentially engaging with diverse sources that shape their beliefs. This complexity gives rise to phenomena such as rapid and extreme opinion change, fleeting consensus, or persistent fragmentation, as documented in large-scale data analyses [1]. Understanding the formation and evolution of opinions thus requires considering the multiple modalities through which individuals interact. The formal framework of multilayer networks has been widely used to model and analyze emergent phenomena on networks of interconnected networks [2], [3]. In particular, when all interacting networks share the same set of nodes, as in the case where the same individuals engage across multiple online and offline social networks, the resulting multilayer network is referred to as a multiplex network . Surveys of the field [2], [3] highlight the empirical prevalence of such multiplex structures in opinion formation and dynamics. Motivated by these observations, we study opinion dynam-ics over a two-layer multiplex network, where each layer represents a distinct (social) network; the layers can be in-terpreted as online vs. in-person interactions, or as different

Classical single-layer frameworks such as DeGroot averaging [4] and its game-theoretic extensions [5]–[7] predict consensus on one network at a time; here, we extend these models to capture situations where opinions are shaped by multiple networks. Specifically, we propose and analyze two potential coupling mechanisms between network layers. In the merged layers 

setting, individuals integrate influences from both layers at every time step, with the combined interaction matrix given by a convex combination of the individual layer matrices. In the 

switching layers setting, individuals alternate between layers over time, interacting on one layer for k consecutive steps before switching to the other layer for one step, and repeating this cycle. These two interaction modes reflect different real-world scenarios; the former captures opinion dynamics under 

simultaneous exposure to multiple information sources, while the later is chosen to capture attention shifts across platforms or contexts [8]–[11]. The analytical challenges in these settings stem from assess-ing the spectral properties of the resulting update operators. In the merged model, the effective transition matrix is a weighted sum of the layer matrices, whose convergence behavior is not simply inherited from the individual layers. In the switching model, the dynamics involve products of the form BA k, where 

A and B are the transition matrices of the two layers; as shown in prior studies of products of stochastic matrices [12]–[15], such products need not inherit the convergence properties of their factors. By analyzing these operators (including when they do and when they do not inherit properties of their constituent matrices), we show when cross-layer coupling can destabilize consensus when individual layers converge in isolation, and conversely, when it can create consensus when individual layers fail to converge independently. 

A. Paper Overview and Contributions 

We propose a unified framework for analyzing opinion dy-namics over multiplex social networks under the merged-layer and switching-layer interaction modes. Building on the single-layer quadratic coordination game model of [7], we employ spectral tools from random-walk theory [16]–[20] and classical perturbation analysis [21]–[23] to address three fundamental questions: (i) under what conditions does consensus form in each coupling mode, (ii) how rapidly does it emerge, and (iii) how robust is it to structural changes or layer imbalances. In the merged-layer model, we show that it suffices for one layer’s transition matrix to be primitive to guarantee consensus in the combined network, and we establish that the convergence speed is at least as fast as that of the

slowest individual layer, as determined by the second-largest eigenvalue modulus. In the switching-layer model, we show that consensus is guaranteed when BA k, the product of the secondary layer’s transition matrix B and the k-fold power of the primary layer’s transition matrix A, is primitive, and that the convergence rate per switching cycle is governed by the second-largest eigenvalue modulus of BA k.For both models, we derive explicit convergence bounds, establish sufficient conditions for stability under small per-turbations to layer weights or connections, and analyze how dominance of one layer influences the formation of consen-sus. Our analysis reveals that multilayer coupling can both accelerate and hinder consensus formation, and perhaps more notably, can create consensus even when individual layers fail to converge independently. Numerical experiments on synthetic networks and real-world high school contact data validate our theoretical results, demonstrating that multilayer structures can mitigate the influence of dominant agents and promote balanced consensus formation. 

B. The Key Takeaways 

In the merged layers model (our model of simultaneous ex-posure ), we find that a well-connected layer can guarantee the entire multiplex will reach agreement. That is, simultaneous exposure to a stabilizing source can negate the destabilizing effects of another platform that would not individually support consensus. Even so, the speed of convergence to consensus will still depend on how “similar” the layers are. If the nodes have similar (weighted) degrees across layers, then the merged system converges no slower than the slower of the two layers. In contrast, when the layers are misaligned , one layer may assign high influence to nodes that the other treats as marginal. In such cases, the merged dynamics actually slow down mixing. In the switching layers model (our model of attention shifts ), consensus depends on whether a full round of alternating interactions manages to circulate opinions throughout the network, which can happen if the attention shifts are not too frequent. As a positive byproduct, we show that attention shifts can sometimes unlock stability even when neither layer converges alone; yet, if the two layers emphasize different voices, switching may amplify conflicts and delay consensus. Here, we again find the weighted degrees of nodes to provide a measure of structural alignment: if both layers give similar weight to influential nodes, convergence is efficient; otherwise, alternating between layers will slow down mixing. The main common takeaway across both models is that con-sensus can emerge even if one or both layers cannot support it independently, and that how fast agreement is reached is not just about whether each layer is strong on its own, but about whether the layers align well enough, particularly by having the perceived importance of each agent being relatively similar across layers. When alignment is high, switching or merging accelerates agreement; when misaligned, the system slows down. This perspective suggests that interventions (e.g., reinforcing links in one platform, or influencing switching frequency) can make consensus emerge, but that to accelerate agreement, these intervention should also make the platforms more “homogeneous” (and therefore also considerably shape the final consensus opinion). 

C. Related Work 

Our work is most closely related to two lines of literature: (i) characterization of Nash equilibria of coordination games and consensus properties in single-layer networks, and (ii) analysis of games on multilayer/multiplex networks In the single-layer setting, classical averaging models estab-lished consensus conditions in connected graphs via Markov-chain techniques [4]. Game-theoretic extensions introduced quadratic cost formulations to capture social influence and leveraged spectral properties such as connectivity and the SLEM to ensure equilibrium uniqueness [5]–[7], [24], [25]. Nonlinear belief-update rules have been studied in non-Bayesian learning frameworks [26], and comprehensive tuto-rials on dynamic network modeling have appeared [8]. Recent work has incorporated node heterogeneity, including stubborn agents [27] and degree-weighted influence mechanisms [28], [29], highlighting how high-degree nodes affect convergence and polarization dynamics. Moving beyond single-layer networks, multilayer and multi-plex networks can capture more realistic interaction patterns by modeling simultaneous or periodic engagement across layers [2], [30], [31]. Game theoretical studies in this domain have investigated the existence and multiplicity of equilibria of general games played over multilayer network structures [32], [33], as well as evolutionary games in multilayer contexts, demonstrating how interlayer links sustain cooperative behav-ior [3], [34], [35]. To our knowledge, coordination games (which capture DeGroot opinion dynamics) in multilayer networks have remained unexplored. A recent work in [36] has explored the stability and robustness of opinion dynamics under a Friedkin–Johnsen model on a temporal multiplex networks; our “switching layer” model similarly considers the time-varying aspect of interactions across different network layers and its impact on opinion dynamics and stability, but differs due to adopting the DeGroot model of opinion updates. An earlier version of this work appeared in [37], where we introduced and analyzed the switching-layer coordina-tion game model. This work extends [37] by introducing and analyzing the merged-layer coordination game, as well as providing additional numerical experiments and intuitive takeaways for the switching-layer model. V. N UMERICAL EXPERIMENTS 

We validate our theoretical findings through experiments on synthetic networks and real-world high-school contact data from [39]. These experiments demonstrate the key properties of multilayer consensus dynamics and confirm that multilayer networks can reach consensus even when individual layers fail to converge independently. 

A. Synthetic Network Experiments 1) Merged Layers Opinion Dynamics: We compare Barab´ asi–Albert (BA) networks [40], [41] with Erd˝ os–R´ enyi (ER) networks. The BA model uses preferential attachment, which produces hub nodes with high degree, while the ER model connects each pair of nodes independently with prob-ability p. We adjust the parameters to ensure networks of size N = 100 and average degree 10. Initial opinions are drawn uniformly from [0 , 1] , except that the five highest-degree nodes in the BA networks are fixed at opinion 0 to model influential agents. Due to the concentration of connections around these hubs, the BA networks alone converge to a lower consensus level than the ER networks; however, the merged-layer mechanism alleviates this deficit by blending the two interaction structures. Figure 1 illustrates the error convergence, consensus value, and SLEM as functions of the weighting factor α. In particular, Figures 1b and 1c support the bounds in Propositions III.2 and III.3, respectively. 

2) Switching Layers Opinion Dynamics: We construct layer 

A as a 6-regular graph and layer B as an 8-regular graph on 

N = 100 nodes. Figure 2 validates the convergence bound from Proposition IV.3. The stepwise convergence reflects the periodic switching structure, and the overall rate depends on both ρ2(BA k) and the switching period k + 1 .

B. Real-World Network: High-School Contact Data 

We also run experiments on the high-school contact net-work data from [39]. Layer A encodes Facebook friendships
