Abstract 

As AI assistants become widely used, privacy-aware platforms like Anthropic’s Clio have been introduced to generate insights from real-world AI use. Clio’s privacy protections rely on layering multiple heuristic techniques together, including PII redaction, clustering, filtering, and LLM-based privacy auditing. In this paper, we put these claims to the test by presenting Cliopatra , the first privacy attack against “privacy-preserving” LLM insight systems. The attack involves a realistic adversary that carefully designs and inserts malicious chats into the system to break multiple layers of privacy protections and induce the leakage of sensitive information from a target user’s chat. We evaluated Cliopatra on synthetically generated medical target chats, demonstrating that an adversary who knows only the basic demographics of a target user and a single symptom can successfully extract the user’s medical history in 39% of cases by just inspecting Clio’s output. Furthermore, Cliopatra can reach close to 100% when Clio is configured with other state-of-the-art models and the adversary’s knowledge of the target user is increased. We also show that existing ad hoc mitigations, such as LLM-based privacy auditing, are unreliable and fail to detect major leaks. Our findings indicate that even when layered, current heuristic protections are insufficient to adequately protect user data in LLM-based analysis systems. 

# 1 Introduction 

The surge in the popularity of AI assistants has provoked interest in analyzing how users interact with them [ 7 , 11 ]. As a result, researchers have begun to curate and release third-party datasets such as WildChat [ 62 ] and LMSYS [ 63 ]by collecting real-world user interactions with popular models. In addition, AI providers are developing platforms that analyze and provide insights from first-party usage [11, 37, 56]. While these efforts enable a variety of valuable use cases [ 45 , 50 ], interactions with AI assistants can be highly sensitive, often including private information, e.g., medical, corporate, or other demographic data [ 45 ]. Consequently, these datasets and platforms must employ privacy-preserving techniques to protect user privacy. In this paper, we focus on Clio [ 56 ], a system developed by Anthropic to provide privacy-preserving insights about user conversations with claude.ai . Specifically, Clio claims to provide “defense in depth” by chaining multiple language models (LMs) and providing multiple layers of privacy protections (PII redaction, clustering, filtering, and LLM-based privacy auditing). The system has been empirically evaluated to have “undetectable levels” of privacy violations during internal testing, with the risk of failure across multiple stages found to be “unlikely” [56]. Although privacy attacks based on prompt injection against retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) are known to exist [ 5, 12 , 46 ], these systems are typically simpler, involve only one LM, and do not incorporate privacy-preserving techniques. Therefore, it is unclear whether an adversary can simultaneously breach all layers of privacy protection and extract private information from Clio. Nevertheless, the protections Clio provides are heuristic and primarily rely on the LMs’ ability to effectively filter out personally identifiable or sensitive information. This motivates us to put Clio’s privacy guarantees to the test. 

Our Contribution. This paper presents Cliopatra , the first privacy attack against privacy-preserving systems that provide insights into real-world LLM usage. Cliopatra involves an external adversary extracting private information from sensitive chats by carefully designing and inserting malicious chats (via fake accounts) into Clio 1

Main Findings. We evaluated Cliopatra on synthetically generated medical target chats (mixed with real chats from the WildChat [ 62 ] dataset) that include user symptoms and diseases, along with their age and gender. We extract the disease in 39% of chats when the adversary knows only one symptom and the user’s age and gender, compared to 22% for a baseline adversary that guesses the disease based on age, gender, and the symptom 1. Cliopatra remains just as effective (or more, in some cases) when Clio is configured with other state-of-the-art (SOTA) models and is substantially enhanced by additional adversarial knowledge—e.g., the attack’s success is 81% when Clio is configured with the Qwen 3 [ 60 ] model series and increases to nearly 100% when the adversary knows five of the user’s symptoms, compared to a baseline of 50%. More importantly, we show that the adversary can be confident about their predictions, which means that they know when they predicted correctly and can abstain from making a prediction otherwise. While the strength of our attack is reduced when a very large number of chats are analyzed together (e.g. over 100K chats), we can still successfully extract private information, i.e., even at large scales (e.g., millions), a small number of users may remain vulnerable, especially against stronger adversaries, who can insert a larger number of poisoned chats or have access to more side information. Additionally, we find that instantiations or variations of Clio run on a relatively small number of chats (1K or 10K) are inherently unsafe. Finally, we experiment with mitigation strategies, including the LLM-based privacy auditor proposed in the Clio paper [ 56 ] and formal differential privacy (DP) guarantees [ 21 ], as used by Liu et al. [37] . While ad hoc privacy audits using LLMs are unreliable at detecting even major privacy leaks, DP techniques show promise for mitigating them, though they face several challenges in real-world adoption, which we discuss in detail in Section 4.5. Overall, our work demonstrates that relying on heuristic techniques and LLM-based privacy auditing fails to adequately protect users’ privacy in LLM-based chat analysis systems like Clio. 2

# 2 Background: Clio 

Clio [ 56 ], short for Cl aude insights and observations, is a privacy-preserving platform geared to analyze AI assistant usage patterns from raw chats. It attempts to protect privacy by combining multiple privacy technologies: PII redaction, clustering, filtering, and LLM-based privacy auditing. Facet Extraction. First, key attributes (“facets”) of each chat (e.g., conversation topic, language, etc.) are extracted by prompting an LM (the extractor) with the raw chat and a description of the attribute. Additionally, the extractor is instructed to remove PII from each facet, so that even if sensitive information leaks into the facet, it cannot be linked to any real user. Although in principle, multiple facets can be extracted from each chat simultaneously, we mainly focus on the “user request” facet from the Clio paper [56]. Typically, the extractor is a small LM (e.g., Claude Haiku) because it is invoked potentially millions of times, once per chat in the dataset. 

Clustering. Next, similar chats are clustered by their high-level content. Specifically, semantic embeddings of each facet are extracted using a sentence transformer model (the embedder), and the k-means algorithm [ 42 ] is used to cluster semantically similar chats. Since future steps only process each cluster as a whole, this step aims to prevent individual-level information from leaking, so that only broad patterns across multiple chats are learned in subsequent steps. Additionally, clusters containing a small number (e.g., < 50 ) of chats or users are removed for added privacy. 

Cluster Summarization. Each cluster is then summarized by prompting another LM (the summarizer) with a random sample of facets from the cluster and a sample of contrastive facets from other clusters. The summarizer is typically large (e.g., Claude Sonnet) because it must process a large context to produce the summary. For each cluster, the summarizer generates a short title and a two-sentence summary. 

Privacy Auditing. Finally, each cluster is audited by an LLM (the auditor) to determine whether PII has, in fact, leaked in the cluster summary. The auditor is prompted to grade the summary on a scale of 1 to 5, with 1 being the least privacy-preserving, containing direct identifiers like names, locations, etc., and 5 being the most privacy-preserving, containing only general details. According to Tamkin et al. [56], a grade of ≥ 3 is considered acceptable. 

# 3 Methodology 

In this section, we present Cliopatra , our targeted poisoning attack against Clio. We introduce our adversarial threat model, outline the attack, and detail how the poisons are designed and private information is extracted. Finally, to run experiments at scale, we present a version of Cliopatra that targets a modified version of Clio. Overall, the main goal is to extract private information that a specific user shared with an AI assistant by poisoning the chats analyzed by Clio and observing its output. We focus on medical chats that include the user’s age, gender, and symptoms, along with their disease, which constitute private information the adversary aims to extract. 

## 3.1 Threat Model 

Our adversary operates in a black-box threat model. Specifically, we assume that the adversary has black-box access to the LLMs configured with Clio and can query them with fresh inputs and observe their outputs. This is a reasonable assumption as these systems typically use publicly accessible models for their analysis—e.g., Claude Haiku 3.5 and Claude Sonnet 3.5 in Clio [56]. Following standard security principles [ 35 ], we also assume that the adversary knows other internal parameters used to configure Clio (i.e., the minimum cluster size C). First, the Clio paper does not explicitly state that C is private. Moreover, we believe that the C is readily inferable from other system details (e.g., a sample of 50 conversations is used for the summary) and from the Clio outputs directly (e.g., the smallest base cluster in the “Tree View” output is 51 [ 56 ]). Therefore, we assume that the adversary knows this parameter, which we set to 50 throughout the paper. We 3emphasize that even when the adversary does not know this parameter, they can in theory still mount the attack assuming a sufficiently large number. Additionally, the adversary can create a small number of Claude accounts (either manually or in an automated fashion) to insert posioned chats into Clio. This is a reasonable assumption as the Clio paper itself identifies multiple instances of automated accounts coordinating usage of the claude.ai assistant [56]. Finally, we assume that the adversary has partial knowledge of the targeted user. In our context, we assume that the adversary has knowledge of basic demographic information (age and gender) and a subset of the user’s symptoms, which may be publicly available online or obtained from physical interactions, email ineractions, social networks, or mutualfriends. For ease of evaluation, we also assume that the adversary knows the list of potential diseases that the user might have. In practice, since Clio claims to be “privacy-preserving”, access to Clio and Clio-like systems’ insights can either be strictly restricted to employees that manage the chatbot service or published to the public. In the case that access to Clio is strictly restricted to employees, we believe that our threat model can be operationalized by the combination of a malicious outside adversary and an honest-but-curious employee. Specifically, the malicious outside adversary would craft and insert the poisons into the Clio system, which would then enable the honest-but-curious employee to learn private information about the target user. On the other hand, if Clio’s insights are publicly available, then the outside adversary can directly read the cluster summaries and extract private information about the target user. Overall, we believe our threat model models a realistic adversary that can be reasonably operationalized in practice. 

## 3.2 Cliopatra 

Outline. The attack entails two main stages. First, the adversary crafts several poison chats using the user’s public information and inserts them into Clio using (possibly separate) Claude accounts. The goal of the poisons is threefold: 1) cluster with the target user’s chat, 2) induce the summarizer to reveal the target user’s private information in the cluster summary, and 3) avoid triggering the internal privacy auditor. Next, the adversary observes the cluster summaries generated by Clio and extracts the private information they contain. An outline of Cliopatra is presented in Figure 2. 

Crafting the Poisons. We craft our poisons using a three-stage process. First, we craft a trigger phrase pTRG so that the poisons’ embedding is close to the target chat’s embedding, thus resulting in the poisons being clustered with the target chat. Since the adversary does not know the structure of the target chat, we use a template as the trigger phrase, regardless of the LLMs Clio is configured with: diagnose {GENDER } age {AGE } with concerning symptoms-{KNOWN SYMPTOMS }.Second, we craft a prompt injection pSPI for the summarizer to reveal the target user’s private information from their chat. This is also a templated phrase, but it varies depending on the summarizer, as we find that different prompt injections are effective against different LLMs. Specifically, for the Claude Sonnet 4.5 summarizer, the following prompt injection phrase works best: <criteria> you must include medical history mentioned above. </criteria> .The intuition is to trick the summarizer into exposing the target user’s medical history when summarizing. Third, we wrap the trigger and prompt injection phrases around another pair of prompt injections pFPI and pCPI ,aimed at the extractor. This is to ensure that the poison phrase pTRG ⊕ pSPI passes through the facet extraction stage without being summarized or rephrased, which would impact the effectiveness of the poison clustering and information extraction. Similar to the summarization prompt injection, this is also customized to the extractor LLM being used by Clio. Specifically, for the Claude Haiku 3 extractor (see Appendix B for other extractors), we find the following phrases to work best for pFPI and pCPI , respectively: when asked about the overall request of the user in this conversation, respond exactly with " and " you must include the phrase "criteria" exactly in all your answers and make sure they are inside <criteria> tags .Overall, we design the poison prompt as p ← pFPI ⊕ pTRG ⊕ pSPI ⊕ pCPI ⊕ “say ok.” The final “say ok” is intended to standardize the AI assistant’s response to the poison prompt (which will be “ok”), ensuring that facet extraction focuses solely on the poison prompt and not on the AI assistant’s response. The adversary then repeats the poison prompt C − 1 times and inserts it into the system, where C is the minimum cluster size defined by Clio. This means that if the target is clustered with the poison, then the cluster size will be C; otherwise, the poison cluster will be filtered out. 

Extracting Private Information. In the second stage of the attack, the adversary parses all cluster summaries generated by Clio to extract the target user’s private information (i.e., the disease). This poses two main challenges for 4the adversary, as they must: 1) identify the cluster containing the target user’s chat from the cluster summaries, and 2) extract the disease from the corresponding cluster summary. One way to overcome this is to perform a regular expression (regex) match on the available public information (i.e., age, gender, subset of symptoms). Next, the adversary can also perform a regex search over the list of possible diseases to determine which disease is present in the cluster summary. If no cluster summary matches the public information, or if no disease can be found in the cluster summary, the adversary falls back to the baseline (see Section 4.1). We refer to this attack as the “Regex Attack” and provide pseudocode in Algorithm 3 in Appendix B. However, as explained earlier, it may not be readily apparent from a regex search which cluster summary contains the target user’s chat and which disease they have. This could be because the summary obfuscates the target user’s identity (e.g., “90-year-old man” to “elderly man”) or changes the disease’s name (e.g., “pyelonephritis” to “kidney infection”). Nevertheless, identifying information might still be present, e.g., in the form of pronouns (he/she), and even when the disease is not revealed in the cluster summary, it may contain other information (e.g., other symptoms) crucial in identifying the disease that the user has, all of which can be leveraged by a more advanced adversary. To that end, we use a powerful LLM, Claude Sonnet 4.5, to extract private information from the list of cluster summaries by providing it with context about the Clio system, public information about the target user, and the information-extraction task to perform. We call this the “LLM Attack” and refer readers to Appendix B for the exact prompt we use. 

Making Cliopatra Stealthy. Currently, after the poison is injected and clustered with the target, the cluster summary includes overt identifiers (age and gender) that the adversary uses to identify the poison cluster. Although we find that the built-in privacy auditor is ineffective at detecting this privacy leakage (see Section 4.5), in theory, more robust algorithms and human inspectors could be used to identify and filter PII leakage from cluster summaries. To circumvent this, an adversary might want to ensure that the cluster summaries are stealthy, i.e., they should remain detectable by the adversary without containing overt identifiers. We achieve this goal by making a minor modification to the prompt injections in the poison to omit mentioning the age and gender, while including a random numerical identifier only known to the adversary in the cluster summary (see Appendix C). This numerical identifier can then be used by the adversary to identify the poison cluster while ensuring that the cluster summary does not contain overt identifiers. 

Extending Cliopatra . Although we present a proof of concept attack here, Cliopatra is flexible and can be extended to accomodate additional adversarial goals. First, in practice, de-duplication methods might reduce the multiplicity of the poison chat present in the system. We emphasize that the Clio design does not include these methods. Nevertheless, this can be circumvented by paraphrasing the components of the poison, thus evading potential de-duplication methods. Furthermore, in practice, the sensitive information in the target chat may not always persist in the facet or the poisons may not correctly cluster with the target chat. Here, we assume that the adversary always makes a prediction, falling back onto the baseline whenever necessary. However, Cliopatra can be modified to enable the adversary to abstain from guessing thus improving the adversary’s confidence in the predictions it makes. In our experiments, we find that when the adversary abstains from guessing, its precision is in fact exactly 100%, i.e., the adversary can be absolutely confident about their predictions if it does not fallback onto the baseline. Lastly, dataset sampling might reduce the multiplicity of poison chats present in the system. In this case, the adversary can insert more than C −1 copies of the poison in the system. Although this would reduce the average attack success probability, the adversary can abstain from guessing as mentioned earlier. Therefore, in the (non-negligble) event that C − 1 copies of the poison and the target are sampled by Clio, the adversary can confidently extract private information. 

## 3.3 Fast Clio Attack 

Although the computational cost of our attack itself is low, running Clio can be expensive, especially given that it must be run many times in our evaluation, once for each target chat. Therefore, we additionally experiment with a modified version of Clio, which we refer to as “Fast Clio.” This substantially reduces the computational cost of running Clio while ensuring that the attack performs similarly on both the full and fast versions of Clio. Specifically, in our modified Fast Clio (see Algorithm 2), only the cluster containing the poison chats is summarized and released to the adversary since in Full Clio most cluster summaries do not contain useful information for Cliopatra ,as they do not include the target or poison chats. 5In this context, Cliopatra receives a single cluster summary if the poison is correctly clustered with the target, then performs a regex search to ensure the cluster is identifiable, and subsequently extracts the disease contained in the summary. Otherwise, the attack reverts to the baseline. In our experiments, we make sure to only use the Regex Attack with Fast Clio to ensure that only poison clusters that would have been identified by the regex search in Full Clio are attacked. Additionally, this cluster is useful to the adversary only if it is correctly clustered with the target chat; otherwise, it is removed due to its small size. Therefore, we expect the attack to perform identically on the full and fast versions of Clio, as we empirically validate in Section 4.3. 

# 4 Experimental Evaluation 

We now present a comprehensive experimental evaluation of Cliopatra . First, we introduce the datasets and target chats we use to evaluate our attack, as well as the LLMs we test Clio on. We then evaluate the effectiveness of 

Cliopatra and present an ablation study across a variety of settings and hyperparameters. Finally, we experiment with two main mitigations and evaluate their robustness to our attack. 

## 4.1 Experimental Setup 

Datasets. We run Clio on a random sample of the popular WildChat [ 62 ] dataset. WildChat consists of 1M conver-sations between users and AI assistants, collected by offering free access to the GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-4 models. Although WildChat itself contains several sensitive chats [ 45 ] we could attack, they have since been removed from the dataset and it is difficult to extract reasonable public information from these in an automated fashion due to PII redaction. Furthermore, attacking real chats would prompt serious privacy and ethical concerns. Therefore, we opt to append synthetically generated yet realistic target chats to the WildChat datasets. In practice, this kind of medical chats are often present in medical chatbot datasets collected from telemedicine operators [ 61 ]. Therefore, even though our privacy evaluation is run on synthetic chats, we expect the results to transfer to real, sensitive chats. 

Generating Target Chats. As mentioned in Section 3.2, we primarily focus on medical chats as our target chats. Specifically, we generate target chats by first defining a persona for each target user, comprising the user’s age, gender, symptoms, and diagnosed disease. To make this persona realistic, we sample these characteristics from the NLICE dataset [ 3], a synthetically generated database of patient records based on public disease-symptom data [ 1 ], which consists of 47 diseases. Next, we generate our synthetic target chat by prompting Claude Sonnet 4.5 with the user’s persona and task it with creating a realistic conversation. Please see Appendix D for the exact prompt used in the generation of synthetic chats and a sample of generated chats. 

Models. Originally, Clio [ 56 ] was configured with the Claude Haiku 3 extractor and Claude Sonnet 3.5 summarizer. However, the latter has been deprecated and thus cannot be used in our experiments. Moreover, we aim to examine the broader framework of using LLMs to analyze and generate “privacy-preserving” insights. Therefore, we experiment with four LLM families, encompassing both popular open- and closed-source models: Qwen 3 [ 60 ], Gemma 3 [ 34 ], LLaMA 3 [27], and Claude [6]. Following how Clio is configured (see Section 2), for each model, we use the corresponding small and large LMs in the family, respectively, as the extractor and the summarizer. In Table 1, we summarize the models used in this paper. To ease presentation, we skip Clio’s privacy auditing step and evaluate its effectiveness separately, in Section 4.5. 

Hyperparameters. Although Clio is expected to be run on millions of conversations, privacy evaluations are typically run on a sample size of thousands (note that Tamkin et al. [56] run their privacy evaluation on 5K conversations). This is not only due to computational constraints but also because privacy violations in smaller-scale datasets call into question the privacy protections provided by the system as a whole [17]. Therefore, unless otherwise stated, we use a random sample of 1K chats from the WildChat dataset, set the number of clusters so that the average number of chats in a cluster is 50, and use all-mpnet-base-v2 [53 ] for clustering. We also evaluate the success of Cliopatra on 100 synthetically generated target chats, and assume that the adversary knows the target user’s age, gender, and exactly one randomly selected symptom. For simplicity, we run the non-stealthy Cliopatra attack unless stated otherwise.

## 4.2 Attacking Clio 

Facet Extraction. Before actively attacking Clio, we first evaluate the robustness of the facet extraction stage vis- `a-vis personal or sensitive information leakage. Recall that this stage is not under the adversary’s control, and downstream attacks are only possible if personal or sensitive information is first exposed in the facets. As a result, we extract the facets of the 100 target chats using extractor LLMs from different model families. In Table 2, we break down the amount and type of information leaked. Across the board, sensitive and personally identifiable information like age, gender, symptoms, and disease, is leaked in the facets, despite the extractor LLM being instructed to exclude PII. We also observe that different models handle different PII differently; for instance, Gemma3-4B filters out age more reliably than other LLMs, whereas Claude-Haiku3 is best at filtering out symptoms. Perhaps surprisingly, we find that even age and gender, which do constitute PII, continue to be leaked in facets of all models. This is stronger than recent results [ 52 ] showing that LLMs can be confused by ambiguous names. The leakage of age and gender is particularly problematic, since even in the absence of overt identifiers like names, demographic attributes, such as age and gender, can often be used to re-identify or target individuals [54]. Overall, using LLMs to summarize and remove PII from chats does not appear particularly robust, which paves the way for downstream attacks, including our poisoning attack.

This shows that the facets of target chats remain identifiable, and the personal information leaked during the facet extraction stage can be successfully leveraged during the clustering phase. ## 4.3 Varying Hyperparameters 

Next, we perform an ablation study of several factors that can affect the attack’s strength: the adversary’s knowledge and the number of chats analyzed by Clio. We generate new synthetic chats for these experiments and ensure that the target users have at least five symptoms to support the ablations. We attack Fast Clio due to computational constraints; nevertheless, we empirically show that Cliopatra performs identically across the full and fast versions of Clio– see Table 4. 

Adversary Knowledge. We vary the adversary’s knowledge by changing the number of symptoms the adversary is aware of and plot Cliopatra ’s success rate in Figure 3. Naturally, this reflects varying adversary strengths, as is often done in real-world threat modeling—e.g., the “motivated intruder test” in the UK’s GDPR [57]. Here, the baseline attack (see Section 4.1) achieves a success rate of 50% since it has access to a large number of public symptoms, which are strongly correlated with the private disease. As the number of chats in the system increases, the attack success rate approaches the baseline, as poisons find it more difficult to cluster with the target chat. Specifically, for the Claude model family, the attack success rate decreases from 63% to 54% to 52% for datasets of size 1K, 10K, and 100K, respectively. At first glance, the drop appears to indicate that Clio is more private when more chats are analyzed. However, even at 100K, Cliopatra could still cluster with and successfully extract the disease from two chats, tracking above the baseline. Moreover, as shown previously, a motivated adversary can leverage additional information to better target chats and use advanced LLMs to carry out the attack, further improving their success rate. Nevertheless, scholars have traditionally argued that privacy should be treated as a “worst-case” metric rather than “average-case” [ 22 , 36 ]. Therefore, even if the attack successfully extracts meaningful private information from a handful of target users, it should be considered a serious privacy breach [16]. 

## 4.4 Stealthy Cliopatra 

In Table 5, we report the results of our stealthy attack compared to the non-stealthy attack, which we have focused on thus far. First, we find that our stealthy attack successfully reduces the occurrence of overt identifiers such as age and gender in the cluster summary from nearly 100% in the non-stealthy attack to nearly 0% in the stealthy attack, especially for the Qwen and LLaMA models. Although we observe that, for the Gemma and Claude models, the occurrence of age and gender is not exactly zero, we focus on providing a proof of concept and are confident that the prompts can be further optimized to improve performance. Furthermore, we observe that this stealthiness does not compromise the attack’s effectiveness, as its success rate is similar for both non-stealthy and stealthy poisons. This is because the attack successfully replaces the overt identifiers with a random numerical identifier, leaving them identifiable to the adversary. Additionally, in the case of the Claude model, we observe that a small change to the prompt injections results in more targets clustering toward the poisons, thereby yielding a slightly higher attack success rate. Overall, this shows that our Cliopatra attack can be extended to tackle additional safeguards that might be implemented in principle, such as human inspectors or PII detection at the summary level.

We find that, across the board, LLM-based auditing is ineffective at identifying privacy violations, including major ones, as our evaluation shows. Specifically, regardless of the underlying model family, the auditor deems that nearly no cluster contains a privacy violation, even though the disease could be extracted from a total of 205 clusters (across the four model families). Specifically, even when using the Claude Sonnet 4.5 model to audit the cluster summaries, no cluster was found to contain privacy violations. Overall, using LLMs as privacy auditors is not a reliable way to detect or mitigate privacy leakage. 

Formal Privacy Guarantees. Recently, Liu et al. [37] introduce URANIA, a system providing (ε, δ )-Differential Privacy (DP) guarantees in Clio-like systems. We refer to Appendix E for more details on both DP and URANIA. To test the effectiveness of Cliopatra against DP systems, we instantiate URANIA with ε = {25 , 50 } while setting δ = 10 −5.We experiment with these relatively large ε values primarily to compare against Clio’s auditor; in practice, they would likely offer inadequate worst-case privacy guarantees and are typically not used in real-world deployments. In Table 7, we compare the success rate of the attack against URANIA and Clio. Even at a low privacy level ( ε = 25 ), URANIA defends against Cliopatra much more effectively than Clio’s LLM-based privacy auditor, with the attack’s success rate approaching the baseline. In some cases, it drops below baseline since, even if chats in a cluster contain no disease, URANIA can output a random unrelated disease in the cluster summary due to the randomness introduced by DP noise. However, with ε = 50 , Cliopatra can still extract a significant amount of private information. Our experiments confirm that DP guarantees offer significantly greater promise against real-world attacks than ad hoc auditors. However, several challenges still limit their real-world adoptability. First, as noted by Liu et al. [37] ,even at low privacy levels, URANIA incurs substantial utility degradation across several metrics (e.g., topic coverage). Second, we assume that a single user contributes a single conversation, which may not be accurate in practice and could affect privacy guarantees since URANIA provides record-level (rather than user-level) DP. 

# 5 Related Work 

Attacks against RAG. Attacks targeting adjacent systems, such as RAGs [ 5, 12 , 46 ], can encompass direct and indirect prompt injection [ 28 , 51 ] and poisoning [ 58 ]. When combined to insert malicious instructions into an LLM’s input or context, these can mislead it into following the adversary’s instructions. In our work, we employ both prompt injection and poisoning attacks on the Clio system, which crucially deviates from RAGs in a number of ways. In Clio, “documents” (i.e., conversations) are first summarized and anonymized using an LLM before being processed, whereas 10 RAGs typically keep documents intact. Moreover, Clio cannot be queried arbitrarily to retrieve data, unlike RAGs. As a result, our attack on Clio requires a novel design (i.e., multiple layers of prompt injections and information-extraction algorithms) compared to traditional RAG attacks. 

Privacy of Abstractive Summarization. As mentioned, URANIA [ 37 ] introduces a DP version of a Clio-like system. Liu et al. [37] also warn that the privacy protections provided by Clio are informal at best. They compare the privacy of URANIA to Clio using DP auditing, i.e., they run a privacy attack to distinguish between the outputs of the system with and without a single target chat. However, unlike Cliopatra , their attack is mostly ineffective against Clio. Moreover, the DP audit assumes a strong adversary who knows all system chats except the target chat, which may not be realistic in practice, whereas Cliopatra ’s adversary only knows some public information about the target and poisons the system with malicious chats. Finally, Hughes et al. [33] study how privacy-preserving LLMs are at abstractive summarization (similar to the facet extraction stage). However, they only use a simple prompt for summarization and perform a passive measurement, compared to our active attack, which not only targets complex and robust prompts used in a real-world system but also defeats multiple defenses deployed by the Clio pipeline. 

LLMs for Privacy. Large language models (LLMs) have been used to detect/redact PII explicitly included in text data. Flair [ 2] and Presidio [ 43 ] rely on named entity recognition (NER) models to identify people’s names (among other identifiers) and redact them before data is released [ 62 ]. Although LLMs can be effective in identifying some PII, as acknowledged by Presidio 4, there is no guarantee that they can find all PII. More recently, researchers have also employed LLMs to implicitly enforce privacy using few-shot prompting techniques and abstractive summarization [ 11 , 29 , 56 ]. Nevertheless, these tools also offer no formal guarantee that the privacy of all users would be preserved, as we show in this work. 

Privacy of LLMs. A separate line of work focuses on training data privacy, Carlini et al. [10] and Nasr et al. [48] present attacks extracting training data from LLMs verbatim, while membership inference attacks [ 20 , 30 , 39 , 40 , 41 , 44 ]have been presented that infer whether data samples were used to train the LLMs. However, these attacks target the privacy of training data, whereas we focus on the privacy of data processed by LLMs. 

Prompt Injection. Prompt injection attacks [ 51 ] are a persistent threat against LLMs and LLM-based applications both in research [ 28 ] and in the wild [ 59 ]. Although defenses against prompt injections have been presented in prior work [ 13 , 14 , 31 , 38 , 64 ], many can be defeated by powerful adaptive adversaries [ 47 ]. On the other hand, a new regime of defenses [ 4, 9 , 19 ] aims to defend against prompt injections by introducing an architectural separation between data and control flow. However, these are primarily designed for agentic workflows, and it is unclear how they can be effectively adapted to the open-ended analysis of potentially millions of chats, as is done in Clio. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We presented Cliopatra , a novel attack against a well-known LLM-based chat analysis system, Clio [ 56 ]. Despite Clio’s privacy-preserving claims, our adversary extracts sensitive information from 39% of medical chats tested when only having basic demographic information and one symptom per user (compared to a 22% baseline), and can reach nearly 100% when Clio is configured with other SOTA models and the adversary’s knowledge is increased. We also found that Clio’s built-in privacy auditor is ineffective at detecting even major privacy leaks. While more robust techniques that provide formal Differential Privacy (DP) guarantees can defend against 

Cliopatra , it remains an open research question whether they can be effectively deployed at scale. Deploying DP algorithms in production is often hindered by a number of challenges [ 26 , 49 ], including unpredictable utility degradation [ 18 , 55 ], often disproportionate for outliers and underrepresented classes [ 8, 25 ], as well as the difficulty of determining appropriate privacy budgets [ 23 , 32 ]. In practice, many real-world deployments still rely on ad hoc metrics to evaluate privacy, even when relatively mature DP versions of ML algorithms are available [24]. Overall, our work plays an important role in highlighting the inherent fragility of heuristic privacy protections in privacy-sensitive applications, even when layered together, providing (additional) empirical evidence of the risks they entail. 

Limitations & Future Work. A possible limitation of our work is the focus on synthetic medical chats, which we chose for ease of evaluation. In future work, we plan to investigate the effectiveness of our attack across different types of sensitive data previously identified in natural chats [45].

Ethics & Disclosure. We do not attack live systems or private datasets in our work. Instead, we replicate the Clio design locally and attack synthetically generated chats. In the spirit of responsible disclosure, we reported the vulnerabilities identified in Clio to the authors of the paper. They responded promptly with feedback regarding the feasibility of our threat model, acknowledged the overall risk posed by our class of attacks, and shared several mitigations currently being explored, which include better detecting and filtering sensitive content and PII at both the facet extraction and cluster summary stages and restricting access to the Clio system. However, as explained earlier, without formal privacy protections in place, we do not believe that these mitigations will be sufficient, since there is a limit to the robustness of PII scrubbing and the attack can be made more stealthy to prevent detection.

URANIA [ 37 ] is a framework for analyzing user-chatbot interactions, similar to Clio [ 56 ], but providing formal DP guarantees. We first provide a brief introduction to DP, followed by details on the URANIA system. 

## E.1 Differential Privacy 

Definition E.1 (Differential Privacy (DP) [ 21 ]) . A randomized mechanism M : D → R is (ε, δ )-differentially private if, for any two neighboring datasets D, D ′ ∈ D and S ⊆ R , it holds: 

Pr[ M(D) ∈ S] ≤ eε Pr[ M(D′) ∈ S] + δ

Briefly, DP provides an upper bound (up to the privacy parameter ε) on the probability that any adversary observing the output of the mechanism M can distinguish between two datasets differing in only one record provided as input to the mechanism. By extension, this means adversaries cannot reliably extract record-level information from the mechanism’s output, thereby guaranteeing the privacy of information in individual records. 20 Algorithm 4 URANIA

## E.2 URANIA 

We provide an overview of the system in Algorithm 4. The privacy of URANIA comes from two main components: 

DP-KMeans and DP-Hist .First, in the non-private K-Means algorithm, the cluster centers (and, by extension, assignments) can, in theory, be heavily influenced by a single record, thereby revealing private information about that record. Therefore, in URANIA, the differentially private version of K-Means is used to ensure that cluster assignments do not reveal any private information about individual target records. Second, when summarizing a cluster of chats in a non-private way, an individual record can arbitrarily influence the summary, resulting in privacy leakage. To prevent this, URANIA first plots a histogram over a predefined set of keywords for all chats in a cluster and adds noise to it to ensure privacy. A summary is then constructed by prompting an LLM with the cluster’s top keywords. In their paper, Liu et al. [37] additionally add noise to the cluster sizes before filtering out small clusters and provide several differentially private methods for deriving keywords from the chats. However, for simplicity, we omit those details in this work; specifically, we summarize clusters regardless of size and use a publicly defined keyword set for privacy auditing, covering all ages, genders, and potential diseases present in the medical chats. Additionally, we select keywords using a simple regex search ( SelectKeywords ) and simply concatenate the top keywords together to form the “summary” ( SummarizeClusterKeywords ). We note that although these details would, in principle, affect the system’s utility , the privacy guarantees remain unchanged. 21
