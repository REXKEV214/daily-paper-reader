## Abstract 

Explainable recommendation systems increasingly employ large language models (LLMs) to generate fluent and persuasive natural-language explanations for recommended items. Prior work has largely focused on improving factual correctness, implicitly assum-ing that factually grounded explanations are sufficient to establish user trust. However, in practice, explanations may remain factu-ally correct yet justify recommendations using attributes that are weakly supported or even contradicted by a user’s historical pref-erences, resulting in reasoning that is logically valid but norma-tively unconvincing to the user. This failure mode is not explic-itly addressed by existing explainable recommender systems and remains invisible to standard hallucination or model-faithfulness-based evaluation metrics. In this work, we identify this phenom-enon as preference-inconsistent explanations, a distinct and pre-viously under-examined failure mode in explainable recommen-dation, and propose PURE, a preference-aware reasoning frame-work that follows a select-then-generate paradigm. PURE inter-venes at the evidence selection stage rather than generation, iden-tifying a compact set of reasoning paths that are both factually grounded and aligned with user preference structure. Concretely, it performs preference-aware reasoning path selection over multi-hop item-centric structures, guided by user intent, specificity, and diversity, thereby suppressing generic but weakly personalized ev-idence. The selected evidence is then incorporated into LLM-based generation via structure-aware prompting that preserves relational constraints. To make this failure mode measurable, we introduce a feature-level evaluation metric that serves as a complementary, user-centric lens for quantifying preference inconsistency, reveal-ing systematic misalignment that factuality-based measures fail to detect. Experiments on three real-world datasets demonstrate that PURE consistently reduces preference-inconsistent explanations and factual hallucinations, while maintaining competitive recom-mendation accuracy, explanation quality, and inference efficiency. Taken together, these results suggest that explanation faithfulness must be understood not only as factual correctness, but also as justification aligned with user preferences, highlighting a previ-ously under-evaluated, user-centric dimension of trustworthy ex-plainable recommendation.

## 1 Introduction 

Recommender systems play a central role in helping users navi-gate large and complex item spaces, and explanations have long been recognized as a key mechanism for improving transparency, trust, and user acceptance [2, 6, 30]. With the rapid adoption of large language models (LLMs), explainable recommendation has shifted from rigid, template-based rationales to free-form natural-language explanations that are fluent, expressive, and human-like [12, 16, 23]. Recent studies show that LLM-based explainers can en-hance perceived transparency and user satisfaction, particularly in interactive recommendation scenarios [19, 24]. However, increased linguistic fluency does not necessarily im-ply increased trustworthiness. Most existing explainable recom-mender systems implicitly assume that an explanation is trustwor-thy as long as it is factually correct, i.e., it mentions item attributes that are objectively true [13, 35]. Consequently, recent efforts have focused heavily on mitigating factual hallucinations by grounding explanations in external evidence such as reviews, retrieved docu-ments, or knowledge graphs [5, 10, 17]. Yet factual correctness alone is insufficient to explain why an item is suitable for a particular user. Consider a user who has con-sistently engaged with light-hearted, comforting movies character-ized by warmth, humor, and emotional healing. The recommender system suggests a critically acclaimed prison drama that indeed contains themes of hope and redemption. When asked for an ex-planation, the system highlights the film’s realistic depiction of suffering and psychological struggle—attributes that are factually correct and well supported by external knowledge sources.

From the user’s perspective, however, the explanation feels un-convincing. Although the recommended item itself may still be rea-sonable, the justification emphasizes precisely the aspects the user has historically avoided. The system does not fabricate facts, nor does it provide irrelevant information; instead, it offers the wrong reasons for the recommendation. Over time, such explanations can erode user trust, as users perceive the system as misunderstanding why items are recommended, even when recommendation accu-racy appears acceptable. This example illustrates a subtle yet critical failure mode in ex-plainable recommendation: explanations that remain factually cor-rect but are misaligned with a user’s preference structure. In this work, we refer to this phenomenon as preference-inconsistent ex-planations. As categorized in Table 1, we characterize this as a log-ically valid but normatively invalid explanation error: while the reasoning is factually sound, it violates the normative expectation that a recommendation must align with the user’s specific inter-ests. Unlike generic or low-informative explanations, preference-inconsistent explanations actively present factual item features as positive reasons despite lacking support from the user’s established preferences. As illustrated in Figure 1, this failure mode is funda-mentally different from factual hallucination: the explanation is grounded in true attributes, yet its reasoning contradicts what the user values. Existing explainable recommender systems rarely address this issue explicitly. Retrieval-augmented and knowledge-grounded ap-proaches effectively reduce blatant factual errors, but they typi-cally prioritize globally salient or high-frequency concepts when selecting evidence. Thus, explanations may be factually grounded yet preference-misaligned, especially when multi-hop reasoning paths emphasize popular but weakly personalized attributes. Be-cause standard faithfulness metrics focus primarily on factual cor-rectness, preference-inconsistent explanations often remain unde-tected and unpenalized. These observations suggest that improving trustworthiness in explainable recommendation requires more than factual ground-ing. It calls for a principled mechanism that explicitly incorporates user preference structure into the reasoning process underlying explanation generation, ensuring that explanations justify recom-mendations using preference-aligned evidence rather than merely correct facts. In this paper, we propose PURE (Preference-aligned Unhallu-cinated Reasoning for Explanation), a preference-aware reasoning framework for explainable recommendation. The key insight be-hind PURE is that preference inconsistency primarily arises from generic, high-frequency reasoning paths, rather than from miss-ing factual information. Accordingly, PURE focuses on identifying a compact set of factually grounded and preference-aligned rea-soning paths instead of maximizing knowledge coverage. PURE constructs multi-hop item-centric subgraphs, applies preference-aware path pruning based on target-aware user intent, node speci-ficity, and diversity-aware reranking, and conditions LLM-based explanation generation on the resulting preference-consistent ev-idence through a hybrid structure-aware prompting mechanism. Our contributions are threefold: 

• We identify preference-inconsistent explanations as a previously under-characterized failure mode in LLM-based explainable rec-ommendation, where explanations remain factually correct yet violate the user’s intrinsic interests, a limitation not captured by existing faithfulness metrics. 

• We propose PURE , a preference-aware reasoning framework that aligns explanation generation with user preferences via KG-based path construction, specificity and intent-aware path prun-ing, and structure-preserving prompting. 

• We introduce feature-level metrics to quantify both factual hal-lucinations and preference inconsistency, and demonstrate sub-stantial improvements on three real-world datasets. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Explainable Recommendation with LLMs 

LLM-driven explainable recommendation has advanced rapidly, moving beyond template-based methods to generate fluent, human-like justifications. Representative methods, such as PEPLER [16], RecExplainer [12], Exp3rt [11], and ARTS [23], adapt pre-trained language models to recommendation scenarios through instruc-tion tuning, prompt learning, or multi-task training. To further en-hance generation quality and reasoning capabilities, recent works such as LLM2ER [37] and STLLM-Rec [20] employ reward model-ing and self-training strategies, respectively, achieving significant improvements in perceived transparency. Despite these advances, most existing LLM-based explainers pri-marily optimize surface-level properties of explanations, such as linguistic plausibility, topical relevance, or semantic coherence with the recommended item. Explanation faithfulness is often implic-itly reduced to factual correctness, assuming that mentioning ob-jectively true item attributes suffices to justify recommendations. As a result, explanations that are fluent and factually accurate are typically regarded as trustworthy, without explicitly considering whether the explanation logic aligns with the user’s preference structure. Departing from these generation-centric approaches, we argue that factual correctness alone is insufficient. We identify prefer-ence inconsistency—where explanations remain factually correct yet cite misaligned features—as a critical failure mode. Unlike prior Beyond Factual Correctness: Mitigating Preference-Inconsistent Explanations in Explainable Recommendation , , 

explainers prioritizing plausibility, we treat preference consistency as a first-class objective, explicitly aligning explanation reasoning with user interests rather than treating it as a byproduct of fluency. 

## 2.2 Retrieval-Augmented Recommendation 

To mitigate hallucinations in LLM-generated explanations, recent works have increasingly adopted retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and knowledge-graph (KG) grounding [3, 29]. Systems such as G-Refer [18], PEARLM [7], R3 [26] and K-RagRec [33] retrieve external evidence, ranging from textual reviews to structured sub-graphs, to constrain LLM generation [25], thereby reducing factual errors and enriching explanation content. However, existing retrieval-based approaches exhibit key limi-tations regarding preference alignment. First, retrieved evidence is typically linearized into plain textual context, discarding graph topology and multi-hop structures that may encode preference-relevant reasoning patterns. Second, retrieval strategies often fa-vor high-degree concepts that are globally salient but weakly per-sonalized, leading to explanations that are factually grounded yet generic. In contrast, PURE performs preference-aware structural prun-ing. Rather than maximizing evidence coverage, we select a com-pact set of multi-hop reasoning paths that are both faithful and strictly aligned with the user’s preference structure, preserving these structural constraints during LLM conditioning to avoid mis-aligned justifications. 

## 2.3 Faithfulness and User Trust 

Faithfulness is a prerequisite for user trust in explainable recom-mendation. Prior approaches, such as FIRE [28] and TriEOR [40], predominantly target model-level faithfulness, ensuring explana-tions remain true to model attributions. More recently, MMCT [22] and HDMR [1] extended this scope to preference explainability, grounding explanations in multimodal evidence or latent concepts, respectively. Yet, a critical gap remains regarding generative align-ment: whether the generated explanation strictly adheres to the user’s established preference structure. We argue that in user-centric systems, merely analyzing preferences is ineffective if the final jus-tification contradicts them. To bridge this gap, we identify preference inconsistency as a distinct failure mode. While prior art treats all factual features or identified concepts as valid candidates, we propose feature-level metrics to explicitly quantify preference misalignment. By extend-ing faithfulness from model parameters to user preferences, we demonstrate that preference consistency captures an essential di-mension of trustworthiness that current protocols fail to address.

## 4 Methodology 

Before detailing the technical components, we clarify the design rationale of PURE. The central goal is neither to maximize the vol-ume of retrieved knowledge nor to overload explanations with at-tributes. Instead, PURE aims to identify a concise set of factually grounded yet preference-aligned reasoning paths that faithfully justify the recommendation for a specific user. We observe that preference inconsistency primarily arises from generic and high-degree reasoning paths that dominate conven-tional retrieval pipelines. These paths, while factually correct, of-ten emphasize globally salient attributes that may contradict a user’s specific interests. Crucially, generation-level alignment is insuffi-cient; even a well-instructed LLM will hallucinate preferences if the upstream evidence is generic. Therefore, PURE intervenes be-fore generation, enforcing intent relevance at the reasoning path selection stage. This design shifts the focus from how to generate explanations to what evidence should justify them. 

## 4.1 Structure-Enhanced Semantic Indexing 

To enable preference-aware reasoning, PURE constructs a structure-enhanced semantic index that stratifies reasoning paths by seman-tic depth. Unlike conventional multi-hop retrieval methods that treat all hops as homogeneous context, we explicitly assign distinct semantic roles to varying hop depths. This design simultaneously reinforces factual grounding and deepens personalization: 

• 1-hop (Explicit Features). Grounds explanations in directly ver-ifiable item attributes, thereby mitigating factual hallucination. 

• 2-hop (Relational Reasoning). Captures proximal associations to support analogical reasoning across related items. 

• 3-hop (Implicit Preference). Exposes high-order relational pat-terns that reflect latent user interests, addressing preference in-consistency beyond surface-level matching. ## 4.3 Preference-Aware Explanation Generation 

Retrieving high-quality paths is necessary but insufficient for gen-erating convincing explanations. Direct linearization of these paths often discards critical topological signals, causing LLMs to over-look relational dependencies within the graph. To address this, we propose a structure-aware hybrid prompting strategy. We integrate continuous soft prompts—which inject latent structural priors— with discrete hard prompts that serve as factual anchors. This com-bination ensures that the generated explanations maintain both logical coherence and factual faithfulness. 

4.3.1 Structure-Aware Subgraph Encoding. We encode the expla-nation subgraph 𝒢 exp , composed of the selected reasoning paths, into a dense vector space. To capture global dependencies and long-range interactions beyond local neighborhoods, we employ a Graph Transformer [39]. To preserve the topological structure and edge semantics, we incorporate relation-aware structural encodings di-rectly into the self-attention mechanism. Let h(𝐿) 𝑣 denote the rep-resentation of node 𝑣 after 𝐿 Transformer layers. We derive the global subgraph representation via a readout operation: 

h𝒢 = Pooling ({ h(𝐿) 𝑣 ∣ 𝑣 ∈ 𝒢 exp }), (9) which compresses the discrete path information into a structure-aware global embedding.

h𝒢 resides in a topological graph space, whereas the LLM oper-ates in a sequential textual embedding space. To bridge this gap and enable a hybrid prompting scheme, we employ a lightweight projector to map h𝒢 into a sequence of continuous soft prompts:

These soft prompts are prepended to the discrete hard prompts. This design creates a synergy: Pkg effi-ciently injects latent structural priors that are often lost during text linearization, while the hard prompts provide the explicit factual context necessary for the LLM to generate coherent and readable explanations.

## 5.1 Experimental Settings 

5.1.1 Datasets. We evaluate PURE on three benchmark datasets: Amazon Books 1, Movies & TV 2, and Yelp 3, all integrated with the Freebase 4 knowledge graph. Specifically, we align items to Free-base entities following the method in [41], achieving an average en-tity coverage of 92% across datasets, which ensures robust knowl-edge grounding. We adopt a leave-one-out protocol, using the last interaction as the target and the most recent 10 interactions as the user history. 

5.1.2 Baselines. We compare PURE against KG-Flat (retrieving linearized facts via vector similarity) and representative models: 

PEPLER [16] (continuous prompting), LLMXRec [24] (instruc-tion tuning), LLM2ER [38] (reward optimization), G-Refer [18] (graph RAG), and MAPLE [36]. To strictly disentangle the contri-bution of reasoning strategies from foundation model capabilities, we standardize the backbone LLM across all baselines and PURE to Llama-3.1-8B. We adapted official implementations to this unified backbone and performed hyperparameter tuning for all baselines to ensure fair comparison. This setup ensures that observed per-formance differences are attributable to framework designs rather than discrepancies in the underlying LLM. 

5.1.3 Evaluation Metrics. 5.1.4 Implementation Details. PURE is instantiated with Llama-3.1-8B [4], fine-tuned via LoRA ( 𝑟 = 8 , 𝛼 = 16 , dropout 0.1 ) using int8 quantization and fp16 precision. Training employs the AdamW optimizer on A100 GPUs with a batch size of 8, 20 epochs, 2 gradi-ent accumulation steps, and a learning rate of 1e-5. The graph mod-ule comprises a 3-layer RGAT (1024 hidden dim, 4 heads) followed by a 2-layer Graph Transformer (256 hidden dim, 2 heads). We set the component weights to 𝜆 s = 0.27 , 𝜆 m = 0.31 , and 𝜆 p = 0.42 .For inference, we use 𝜏 = 0.40 , 𝐾 = 3 , and 𝛾 = 0.6 (selecting top-5 paths). All hyperparameters are kept identical across datasets un-less otherwise stated, and reported results are averaged over three runs with different random seeds to ensure stability.

## 6 Conclusion 

This paper investigates preference-inconsistent explanations where generated descriptions are factually correct yet misrepresent the rationale behind a user-item match—a failure mode largely over-looked by existing metrics. We propose PURE, a preference-aware , , 

reasoning framework that integrates multi-hop subgraph construc-tion, intent modeling, and specificity-based pruning to select factu-ally grounded, preference-consistent reasoning paths. Experiments on three real-world datasets demonstrate that PURE reduces both factual hallucination and preference inconsistency while maintain-ing competitive accuracy and efficiency. Crucially, PURE recon-ciles the tension between serendipity and consistency: rather than restricting item novelty, it ensures that even novel recommenda-tions are grounded in familiar preference anchors, thereby prevent-ing “reasoning filter bubbles.” While extending this paradigm to im-plicit feedback, multimodal contexts, and dynamic environments remains a key frontier, we position preference alignment as a crit-ical dimension of faithfulness beyond surface-level correctness.
