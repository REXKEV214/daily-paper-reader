# MILE-RefHumEval: A Reference-Free, Multi-Independent LLM Framework for Human-Aligned Evaluation

Abstract 

We introduce MILE-RefHumEval, a reference-free framework for evaluating Large Language Models (LLMs) without ground-truth annota-tions or evaluator coordination. It leverages an ensemble of independently prompted eval-uators guided by a human-aligned schema, supporting both discrete and continuous scor-ing judgement. With task-specific prompts from best candidate selection, summarization and image captioning to dialogue, MILE-RefHumEval provides flexible, interpretable, and scalable assessments. Experiments show it aligns closely with human judgments, out-performs prior methods, and reduces com-putational overhead, offering an efficient, ro-bust, and human-aligned solution for real-world LLM evaluation. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have transformed NLP, enabling fluent generation, complex reason-ing, and domain adaptation. However, evaluating LLMs, especially for tasks involving structured synthesis, factual accuracy, or domain-specific reasoning, remains difficult. Common metrics like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), METEOR (Banerjee and Lavie, 2005), and BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020) depend on refer-ence outputs and struggle with semantic nuance, dynamic placeholders, and compositional structure. Recent LLM-as-judge approaches coopera-tive (Liang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023), compet-itive (Chan et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024a), and aggregation-based (Ning et al., 2025; Shu et al., 2024) have advanced the field but remain sensi-tive to evaluator bias, inter-model noise, or lack interpretability (Liu et al., 2024). We introduce MILE-RefHumEval, a reference-free, multi-evaluator framework where each LLM assesses candidate responses independently using a shared, human-aligned judgment criteria. This structure eliminates interaction bias and enhances objectivity. We aim to investigate how multiple evaluators independently assess a single role, high-lighting the distinct reasoning and judgments that emerge without interaction. Importantly, we designed the framework to sup-port flexible evaluation across a wide range of tasks without the need for excessive time to redesign model architectures to adapt to new tasks. The framework allows effortless adaptation. Users sim-ply modify the evaluation prompt criteria based on the specific task and swap the evaluator type. For example, image-to-text evaluators can be used for image captioning, and text-to-text evaluators can be used for summarization. This enables seamless evaluation across diverse modalities and tasks. Our main contributions are: (1) a reference-free, task-agnostic evaluation framework suited for com-plex, open-ended tasks; (2) an unbiased scoring design using isolated LLM evaluators to prevent cross-influence; (3) task-dependent evaluation di-mensions that can be flexibly adapted to different benchmarks; (4) evaluator-type interchangeability that enables users to seamlessly switch modalities; and (5) an efficient structure that reduces query load while preserving evaluation quality. 

2 Related Work 

Collaborative frameworks like ABSEval (Liang et al., 2024) use role-specialized agents, e.g., com-monsense reasoners and code executors, to pro-duce multi-perspective assessments. Others, such as peer-review-style systems (Xu et al., 2023), in-volve iterative critique and revision among agents, emulating human feedback cycles. While these methods enhance interpretability and simulate di-verse reasoning, they often suffer from consen-sus bias, where shared context inflates agree-ment even on flawed outputs. These setups bet-ter surface qualitative differences but risk dom-inance bias, where verbosity skews judgment and lowers inter-rater reliability. Ensemble and optimization-driven methods aim for robustness and cost-efficiency. PiCO (Ning et al., 2025) uses learnable weights to merge rankings from multi-ple LLMs, while AIME (Patel et al., 2024) as-signs diverse roles to evaluators for adversarial robustness. PoLL (Verga et al., 2024) reduces re-liance on large models via lightweight mixtures. Other work incorporates voting (Badshah and Sajjad, 2024), confidence-based cascades (Jung et al., 2025), and hybrid metrics (Shu et al., 2024), though many require references or are compute-heavy. Domain-specific solutions include recom-mendation evaluation (Zhao et al., 2024b) and au-tonomous exam setups (Bai et al., 2023). In con-trast, MILE-RefHumEval introduces a novel evalu-ation paradigm both reference-free and interaction-free, relying on multiple independently prompted LLMs to assess a single role without cross-agent influence. This design explicitly avoids consensus or dominance biases introduced by conversational or role-overlapping setups, while still producing human-aligned, interpretable judgments. To our knowledge, it is the first framework to combine evaluator independence with a structured, multi-criteria schema in a fully decentralized manner. 

3 Proposed Framework Design 

As illustrated in Figure 1, MILE-RefHumEval adopts a reference-free, multi-stage procedure for evaluating LLM-generated responses. Each candi-date output is independently evaluated by a diverse ensemble of evaluators using a unified set of evalu-ation dimensions designed to comprehensively as-sess response quality. The specific dimensions are task-dependent and can be flexibly adapted based on the evaluation benchmark. For example, Figure 2 shows that in the candi-date comparison task, the evaluation focuses on identifying the best candidate. In image captioning (Figure 3), custom prompts

assess caption quality relative to images. Similarly, for summarization (Figure 4), evaluation focuses on coherence, consistency, fluency, and relevance. In the topical chat task (Figure 5), the evaluation prompt can assess dimensions like contextual un-derstanding, naturalness, coherence, engagement, and groundedness. The key advantage of MILE-RefHumEval lies in its flexibility and adaptability. Evaluation prompts can be easily customized to suit different tasks by simply redefining the evaluation criteria, without requiring extensive domain expertise. Additionally, evaluators can be dynamically added, removed, or replaced depending on the task requirements. In image captioning, an image-to-text LLM like In-ternVL can boost evaluation quality. This framework enables researchers to define any number of evaluation dimensions with detailed criteria tailored to their objectives. Finally, regard-less of the task, the final evaluation scores are com-puted through majority voting across evaluators, which enhances robustness and mitigates individual model bias. However, for tasks where evaluators provide numerical ratings such as summarization (Figure 4) or other continuous scoring benchmarks, the final score is obtained by averaging the ratings from all evaluators instead of majority voting. MILE-RefHumEval extends this by evaluating fine-grained quality scores that closely approximate hu-man evaluations. For this dataset experiment, we used all seven evaluators, consistent with the setup in the FairEval benchmark. To assess how accurately MILE-RefHumEval predicts human-like scores, we computed Mean Squared Error (MSE), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), and Mean Absolute Error (MAE). These metrics quantify the variance and absolute differ-ences between our predicted scores and human annotations, providing a more precise evaluation beyond simple correlation measures. Since G-Eval-4 already provides evaluation scores result, we di-rectly use its outputs for benchmarking without rerunning the G-Eval original evaluation process. 

OID Rated Image Caption Benchmark (Lev-inboim et al., 2019): We benchmarked MILE-RefHumEval, a flexible framework for image-to-text evaluation, on the OID Image Caption Qual-ity Estimation Test dataset, which contains 6,182 samples with human-rated captions. Each sample includes an image, a system-generated caption, and the number of positive ratings, from which we com-puted the rounded average of good ratings. MILE-RefHumEval adapts easily to diverse tasks by swapping in specialized evaluators. Here, we use five: Mistral-Small-3.2-24B-Instruct-2506, Gemma-3-12B-IT, InternVL3-14B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct, and GPT-4.1-mini. To evaluate MILE-RefHumEval’s accuracy, we framed its judgments as a binary classification task: a caption is labeled as “good” if its rounded average positive rating exceeds 0.50 (i.e., more than 2 out of 5 evaluators rated it positively). For comparison, we employed ClipScore (Hessel et al., 2021) (CLIP-ViT-L-14), using a cosine similarity threshold of 0.30 to separate satisfactory captions from unsat-isfactory ones. This threshold aligns with recent studies (Schuhmann et al., 2021; Birhane et al., 2023), where values around 0.3–0.35 are com-monly adopted for zero-shot caption quality evalu-ation, balancing sensitivity and precision. Evalua-tion metrics include Acc., F1, Kap., MCC. 

PandaLM Benchmark (Zhu et al., 2023): We also compared MILE-RefHumEval to JudgeLM and a PandaLM method specifically trained on the evaluation task dataset. The dataset consists of 1,000 samples, each covering diverse categories (e.g., Grammarly, Wikipedia, Coursera,...). Each sample includes an instruction, an input (on various topics), and two responses generated by the same model based on that instruction and input. Three annotators choose the best response. MILE-RefHumEval use all seven evaluators to select the best response for each sample and then compute the agreement between MILE-RefHumEval’s choice and the human annotations. Evaluation metrics include Acc., F1, Kap., MCC. 

Topical Chat Benchmark (Mehri and Eske-nazi, 2020): To compare MILE-RefHumEval with G-Eval, we used the USR: Dialog Quality An-notations dataset. This dataset provides annota-tions across five dimensions: Understandable, Uses Knowledge (0 or 1), Natural, Maintains Context, Engaging (1, 2, or 3), and Overall Quality (1, 2, 3, 4, or 5). We evaluate on the Amazon Topical-Chat datasets (360 samples), with 3 human evaluators. Evaluation metrics include Spearman and Kendall correlation coefficients between MILE-RefHumEval’s judgment scores and those of all three human annotators on Naturalness (average of understandability and naturalness), Coherence (main context), Groundedness (use of knowledge), and AVG (overall quality). This procedure enables a direct comparison with G-Eval-4, as reported in the original paper. 

4.2 Results FairEval Benchmark Result: Table 1 presents the comparison of the three evaluation strategies. The proposed MILE-RefHumEval approach achieves the highest overall performance. The full ensem-ble (DeepSeek, Mistral, GPT, LLaMA, Gemma, Phi, and Qwen) reaches 63.75% accuracy, 49.87% macro F1, 39.04% MCC, and 37.42% Cohen’s kappa. This represents an absolute gain of +5% F1, +1.38% MCC, and +1.58% Kap. over CHAT-EVAL’s best result, while reducing the number of queries by 320. These gains demonstrate the ef-fectiveness of model diversity and independence in reducing bias and improving evaluation reliability. While CHATEVAL is lightweight in design, it consistently underperforms MILE-RefHumEval. Its best configuration, using GPT as the sole evalua-tor, yields 63.75% acc., 45.87% macro F1, 37.66% MCC, and a Kap. of 35.84%. These results in-dicate moderate alignment with human labels but limited robustness, likely due to role overloading and intra-agent bias. For CHATEVAL, selecting the best evaluator requires running all evaluators, which increases the number of queries. In contrast, for MILE-RefHumEval, we can simply combine the evaluators without increasing the query count. Under MILE-RefHumEval-Conv, conversational evaluation exhibits significant variability depend-ing on the order in which evaluators are applied, leading to differing performance across configura-tions. The top-performing variant reaches 61.25% acc., 43.67% macro F1, 32.51% MCC, and 30.80% kap., with GPT in the leading position. While CHATEVAL achieves its best results with GPT as the evaluator, in MILE-RefHumEval-Conv the highest performance occurs when the conversation is initiated by GPT, indicating that GPT strongly influences the overall evaluation outcome. This suggests that cross-evaluator dialogues introduce additional noise and potential bias, leading to in-consistent judgments rather than resolution or con-sensus. In terms of computational cost, MILE-RefHumEval is also more efficient. Even with two more agents than CHATEVAL, it requires only 560 LLM queries (80 examples × 7 evaluators), com-pared to 880 for CHATEVAL (80 × 11 roles) and up to 1760 for MILE-RefHumEval-Conv (80 × 22 steps). Thus, our method not only improves reli-ability and agreement but also reduces evaluation overhead by 36.4% relative to CHATEVAL and 68.2% relative to conversational ensembles.

While con-versational paradigms are often assumed to em-ulate human collaborative reasoning, our analysis shows they introduce inter-model bias, where ear-lier model outputs unduly influence subsequent evaluators. This undermines objectivity and re-duces alignment with human judgments. Crucially, the order and how much it strong influence in which models participate in conversations significantly af-fects outcomes. 

Diverisity matters more than size. The results suggest that diversity of evaluators contributes more to human alignment than simply using a larger or stronger single LLM. For example, while GPT alone achieved high accuracy in some tasks, the ensemble’s balanced performance across di-mensions like coherence, fluency, and engagement shows that a mix of reasoning styles outperforms raw scale. This implies that evaluation reliability stems not from model strength alone, but from the complementarity of evaluators. 

Order sensitivity exposes hidden fragility in con-versational evaluators. The poor stability of con-versational ensembles reveals an overlooked issue: the order in which evaluators “speak” acts as a hidden variable that can sway results dramatically. This is analogous to psychological priming in hu-man group discussions, where the first speaker heavily shapes consensus. By removing order ef-fects, MILE-RefHumEval uncovers that evaluator independence is essential for trustworthy automa-tion a point rarely considered in prior benchmarks. 

Fluency is the hardest dimension for evaluators to approximate. In the SummEval results, fluency showed the largest error reduction (85.7%) com-pared to baselines, suggesting that models struggle most with accurately judging naturalness and read-ability. That MILE-RefHumEval narrowed this gap highlights that diverse evaluators collectively capture surface-level language features better than individual judges. 

Single models can “spike” in performance, but ensembles stabilize. In the Image Caption bench-mark, Mistral alone slightly outperformed the en-semble in raw accuracy, but its gains came at the cost of much lower agreement metrics. This find-ing mirrors the idea of “overfitting” in supervised learning where one evaluator might excel on cer-tain samples but lack generalizable reliability. En-sembles act as a variance-reduction mechanism, producing more human-like stability even if they sometimes underperform on headline accuracy. 

Where humans disagree, ensemble evaluators thrive. In PandaLM, human annotators disagreed on many samples, yet MILE-RefHumEval main-tained consistently high agreement across annota-tors. This suggests that ensembles may not just replicate human judgment, but actually mediate be-tween conflicting human opinions, offering a form of consensus-building absent in individual LLM judges. That makes ensembles not just a human-aligned tool, but potentially a human-reflective evaluator capturing the “average” of human per-spectives more reliably than any one person. 

6 Conclusion 

MILE-RefHumEval introduces a reference-free simple yet powerful shift in LLM evaluation: inde-pendence over interaction. By leveraging diverse, non-conversational ensembles, the framework re-duces bias, stabilizes judgments, and achieves stronger alignment with human evaluations across tasks and modalities. Our findings highlight that evaluation quality stems not from mimicking hu-man debate, but from diversity, independence, and structured criteria. As such, MILE-RefHumEval offers a scalable and reliable foundation for future human-aligned assessment of LLMs. Looking ahead, the benchmark should be ex-tended to include audio and speech-based tasks (e.g., MMAU benchmark (Sakshi et al., 2024) for sound, music, and other audio modalities). In-corporating specialized evaluators such as Audio Flamingo 3, Qwen 2.5 Omni, and Gemini 2.5 Pro will help determine whether these enhancements improve evaluation reliability. Identifying how many evaluators maximize accuracy without re-dundancy is an important future direction. Limitations Evaluator Generalization: Our framework as-sumes uniform evaluator reliability across tasks, yet LLMs often exhibit domain-specific capabil-ities. Without mechanisms for domain-aware weighting or specialization, our current design may miss contextual subtleties in judgment, limiting its generalization across varied evaluation domains. 

Interaction Bias: The fixed turn order in our con-versational protocols introduces directional bias, where early-stage outputs disproportionately shape subsequent evaluations. This effect is especially pronounced when weaker models initiate the se-quence. While we quantify this cascading influ-ence, we do not explore adaptive, randomized, or model-quality-aware turn ordering as potential mit-igation strategies. 

Metric Granularity: We rely on standard agree-ment metrics (e.g., Pearson correlation, kap.), which may fail to capture nuanced shifts in eval-uative reasoning introduced through interaction. Richer analytical tools such as causal attribution methods, latent trajectory comparisons, or fine-grained human annotations are needed to trace how evaluators influence one another. 

Prompt Sensitivity: We observe significant vari-ation in model behavior across prompt formu-lations, particularly among smaller models like Phi and Mistral. This sensitivity challenges re-producibility and points to the need for prompt-invariant evaluation strategies or dynamic prompt calibration mechanisms to ensure fairness and sta-bility. 

Unexplored Optimal Evaluator Count: We have not studied how many evaluators are needed to achieve maximal accuracy without introducing redundancy. Determining this optimal ensemble size remains an open question for future work. 

Domain Generalization: MILE-RefHumEval has been tested primarily on general-purpose tasks such as summarization, captioning, and dialogue. Its reliability in highly specialized domains (e.g., legal, medical, or technical reasoning) remains untested, and model evaluators trained on general data may misjudge domain-specific quality.
