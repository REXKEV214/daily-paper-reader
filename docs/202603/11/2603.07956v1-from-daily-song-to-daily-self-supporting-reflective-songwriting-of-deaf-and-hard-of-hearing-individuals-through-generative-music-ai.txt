## Abstract 

The rapid advancement of generative AI (GenAI) is expanding ac-cess to songwriting, offering a new medium of self-expression for Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing (DHH) individuals. However, emerging technologies that support DHH individuals in expressing them-selves through music have largely been evaluated in single-session settings and often fall short in helping users unfamiliar with song-writing convey personal narratives or sustain engagement over time. This paper explores songwriting as an extended, music-based journaling practice that supports sustained emotional reflection over multiple sessions. We introduce SoulNote , a GenAI system enabling DHH to engage in iterative songwriting. Grounded in user-centered design, including a design workshop, a preliminary study, and a multi-session diary study, our findings show that ongo-ing songwriting with SoulNote facilitated emotional growth across three dimensions: self-insight, emotion regulation, and everyday attitudes toward emotions and self-care. Overall, this work demon-strates how GenAI can support marginalized communities by trans-forming creative expression into a daily practice of self-discovery and reflection.

## 1 Introduction 

Communication constraints in an auditory-centered society can contribute to psychological challenges for Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing (DHH) individuals, including social isolation, reduced self-esteem, and negative self-perception [ 28 , 37 , 73 , 83 ]. Music-based activities have gained attention as a promising means of supporting DHH individuals’ psychological well-being by enabling self-expression [ 98 , 112 , 115 ]. Songwriting, in particular, allows individuals to em-bed personal experiences into lyrics and musical elements, support-ing self-expression and identity reconstruction [ 22 , 53 , 100 ]. Music generative AI (GenAI) offers new opportunities for creating songs that reflect individual narratives [ 24 , 59 ], and text-based prompting has lowered technical barriers to music creation for DHH users [23 ]. Recent work on music GenAI, however, has raised substantial concerns about authorship and creative ownership, algorithmic bias, environmental cost, and potential emotional risks for users

[ 29 , 34 ]. According to the guidelines for trustworthy AI music sys-tems proposed by Jacopo et al. [29 ], music GenAI can raise issues related to the copyright status of training data and the possibility that generated pieces resemble copyrighted works, as well as the risk of producing lyrics containing offensive or harmful language and sonic characteristics that may induce anxiety or distress. De-spite these potential issues, we leverage music GenAI in this work because it provides an accessible pathway for DHH individuals to explore songwriting and represent their lived experiences through music. However, conveying personal narratives through music remains challenging for DHH individuals who are relatively less familiar with music [ 86 , 114 ]. Existing studies using music GenAI have pri-marily examined short-term accessibility and emotional benefits [ 23 , 53 ], yet DHH users still often struggle to articulate emotions in lyrics and to translate them into musical forms that reflect their intended meanings. The lack of interactive and sustained prompt-ing partly explains why prior work has focused on single-session experiences [ 22 , 23 , 53 ]. Research has not yet investigated how repeated or long-term songwriting may support ongoing emotional reflection. Although single-session activities can promote tempo-rary emotional awareness, they are insufficient for capturing the dynamics of emotional processing [ 8 , 79 ]. Evidence from journal-ing and psychotherapy highlights the importance of repeated self-expression for mental health [ 79 , 80 , 106 ], and therapeutic songwrit-ing research suggests that multi-session engagement can support emotional healing and self-reflection [ 33 , 36 ]. Extending songwrit-ing into a longitudinal, journaling-like practice may offer deeper and more sustained emotional benefits for DHH individuals. This study aims to examine the long-term impact of repeated songwriting on DHH individuals’ emotional well-being and to in-vestigate how GenAI technologies can support such ongoing prac-tices. We focus on DHH users who have residual hearing through assistive devices, are accustomed to written-language digital com-munication, and self-identify as Deaf within a predominantly hear-ing society. This subgroup was selected to evaluate our early-stage conversational, text-based GenAI songwriting system, in line with demographic trends showing an increasing number of DHH indi-viduals in Korea who rely on text-based communication [ 20 , 54 , 94 ]. To achieve this goal, we propose SoulNote , a conversational songwriting support system that enables DHH individuals to ex-press and document emotions and daily experiences through music [ 23 , 53 ]. SoulNote aims to support sustained psychological well-being through everyday self-expression. As shown in Figure 1, we employed a user-centered design process to ensure safe and effec-tive songwriting experiences for DHH users [ 1 ]. Through a design workshop with four music therapists, we identified conversational strategies and flows that foster safe self-expression and considered potential emotional risks. A preliminary study with 12 DHH partic-ipants assessed the safety and usability of the conversational agent (CA). We then redesigned the system into a diary-like platform and conducted a three-week, in-the-wild diary study with 12 additional DHH participants to examine emotional and behavioral changes. Analysis of the diary study highlighted several outcomes that underscore the value of long-term engagement. The CA’s imagery-based questions helped participants visualize experiences across past, present, and future, enabling them to recognize personal values, needs, and emotions in challenging situations. The CA-generated lyrics helped participants adopt a third-person perspec-tive on their experiences, promoting objectivity and self-acceptance. Music style recommendations contextualized to each dialogue sup-ported participants with limited musical experience in exploring diverse emotional expressions. Over time, the accumulation of mu-sical artifacts served as a personalized emotional resource. Par-ticipants reported shifts in their everyday attitudes, which were reflected in behaviors such as initiating conversations about emo-tions with family or colleagues and adopting more active coping strategies during difficult moments. These findings suggest that iterative songwriting can cultivate lasting emotional awareness, coping strategies, and positive behavioral shifts. • Multi-phase, user-centered system design. We employed an iterative design process that involved music therapists, a preliminary study with DHH users, and a subsequent di-ary study. This approach produced a system that improves the feasibility and sustainability of songwriting for DHH individuals. 

• System development. We developed SoulNote , a journaling-oriented system that integrates a reflective conversational agent, an interface for lyric-editing and multimodal apprecia-tion, and an archival library of accumulated songs to support ongoing and self-directed emotional expression.

Through a 12-session diary study, we qualitatively identified stepwise changes in emotional self-insight, regulation strategies, at-titudinal and behavioral changes in emotional disclosure and motivation when facing difficulties. This work provides early, in-situ insights into how GenAI-supported, repeated songwriting can intersect with the emotional lives of DHH individuals. 

## 2 Background 2.1 The Psychological Effect of Songwriting on DHH Individuals 

Barriers to verbal communication in an auditory-centered society can pose challenges for DHH individuals in expressing emotions, potentially contributing to social isolation, stigma, and difficulties with self-identity and self-esteem [27, 28, 73, 83]. These psychoso-cial challenges have been shown to negatively affect their over-all life satisfaction compared to hearing individuals [ 2 , 84 , 108 ]. Against this backdrop, songwriting has gained attention as a poten-tial means of emotional recovery that embraces sensory differences [ 98 ]. Prior studies have highlighted that songwriting, which in-volves structuring and expressing one’s feelings and experiences through the symbolic languages of lyrics and music, can promote self-expression, self-efficacy, and social connectedness among DHH individuals [ 100 , 112 , 115 ]. In line with these potentials, researchers have developed songwriting support systems that deliver music through visual and tactile feedback [ 23 , 53 , 57 , 100 ]. As an example, Lee et al. [53 ] introduced a multimodal assistive tool that maps musical structure and performance to visual and vibrotactile cues, From Daily Song to Daily Self CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain

These systems have expanded opportunities for musical self-expression and emotional release, while also showing poten-tial to positively influence attitudes toward music and perceptions of deaf identity [ 23 , 53 , 100 ]. In addition, technical approaches to sign-language–based music that reflect DHH cultural identity have provided opportunities to share musical culture with hearing com-munities [22, 100]. More recently, research has begun to explore how music GenAI may enhance DHH individuals’ sense of agency in sound experi-ences by enabling autonomous music creation [ 23 ]. Even with the support of music GenAI, however, DHH individuals may still strug-gle to articulate their emotions as lyrics or to translate them into musical forms that accurately capture their intended expression without interactive prompting during the songwriting process. As these challenges may limit sustained engagement, prior studies have often focused on the short-term emotional outcomes of single-session songwriting [ 22 , 23 , 53 , 100 ]. As a result, research has yet to explore the deeper emotional and behavioral changes that may emerge from repeated songwriting practices. 

## 2.2 The Effects of Repetitive Self-expression 

Repeatedly expressing and recording feelings and thoughts plays an important role in mental health [ 79 , 80 , 106 ]. Journaling, including diary and reflective writing, helps individuals externalize both pos-itive and negative daily experiences, which can facilitate emotional release, regulation, and reflection [ 5, 88 , 104 , 105 ]. Psychological work on affect labeling indicates that putting feelings into words can down-regulate negative affect and support emotion regula-tion, which suggests one mechanism by which journaling produces benefits [ 56 , 103 ]. Regular expressive practices may also reduce suppressed emotions and strengthen self-regulation by clarifying specific experiences [ 55 , 78 ]. From this perspective, songwriting can be regarded as analogous to journaling, as an expressive prac-tice that externalizes inner experiences and allows emotions to be repeatedly released and regulated. Songwriting has been used in educational and therapeutic settings to address emotional difficul-ties across populations, typically through multi-session rather than single-session interventions [ 10 , 26 , 33 ]. Repeatedly naming and structuring emotions through lyrics and musical elements can be particularly helpful for people who struggle to identify and describe feelings, such as those with alexithymia or low emotional granu-larity, by providing an accessible medium that gradually enhances emotional clarity and differentiation [ 9]. Repeated songwriting has been associated with promoting student self-reflection [ 6 ], enhanc-ing emotional expression and communication [ 26 , 38 , 65 , 74 , 89 ], in-creasing treatment motivation in individuals with substance abuse disorders [ 95 ], and reducing depressive symptoms in clinical pop-ulations [ 33 , 36 ]. For instance, Baker et al. [10 ] reported that a 12-session identity-focused songwriting program for patients with brain injury improved life satisfaction and reduced inhibition in emotional expression. These studies suggest that songwriting can foster profound emotional change and behavioral improvement when practiced repeatedly and over the long term, rather than as a one-time intervention. Therefore, providing DHH individuals with songwriting as a sustainable means of self-expression holds promise as a meaningful tool for fostering not only musical creativity but also emotional growth. 

## 2.3 Generative AI for Enhanced Accessibility in Expression 

Supporting sustainable songwriting for DHH individuals requires a technological foundation that lowers barriers to participation. In particular, technologies are needed that enable users without musical training to express their personal narrative through music. Drawing on these prior studies, we explored technologies aimed at enhancing accessibility to lyrics (section 2.3.1) and music (section 2.3.2) creation for DHH individuals. 

2.3.1 Conversational agent. Recent studies have shown that Large Language Model (LLM)-based CAs can provide critical support for individuals who face difficulties in verbal expression [ 45 , 48 , 69 , 93 ]. By scaffolding the process of organizing thoughts and constructing personal narratives, CAs help users articulate their experiences more effectively and explore deeper layers of self-expression. For example, MindfulDiary [48 ] guided psychiatric patients in recall-ing and verbalizing past experiences through structured dialogue, particularly when they were unsure how to begin expressing them-selves, and encouraged them to record these experiences regularly. Similarly, ExploreSelf [101 ] demonstrated how an LLM can provide reflective prompts and feedback that elicit deeper self-reflection. These findings suggest that CAs play a meaningful role in lowering the barriers to sustained self-expression in text-based journaling. Songwriting also requires individuals to organize and externalize their inner experiences through the language of lyrics and music. Accordingly, CAs can provide essential support for DHH individu-als, particularly those with limited musical background, by guiding CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain Choi et al. 

them in exploring and expressing their narrative through songwrit-ing. 

2.3.2 Content generative AI. Recent advances in content GenAI have increased access to multimedia creation, enabling images [ 31 , 72 ], videos [ 109 ], and music [ 117 , 120 ] to be generated from text prompts without prior artistic training. Prompt-based models allow non-experts to create diverse creative outputs quickly, lower-ing barriers to participation in artistic expression. In music, GenAI has proved particularly useful because composition traditionally required extensive technical knowledge [ 24 , 60 ]. For instance, Louie 

et al. [ 59 ] introduced CoCoMo , an AI-assisted co-creative system that lets novice users compose melodies by iteratively refining AI suggestions. In therapeutic contexts, Huang et al. [60 ] explored music generative models to create personalized relaxation music for stress reduction. Evaluations of these models indicate they can produce stylistically diverse music that approximates intended gen-res or moods [ 43 ]. Building on these developments, Choi et al. [ 23 ]proposed a multimodal songwriting system that integrates mu-sic GenAI, allowing DHH individuals to generate songs from text descriptions of desired musical elements. The growing interest in in-clusive music creation highlights GenAI’s potential as a foundation for DHH songwriting. Combining large language model-based conversational agents with music GenAI makes it possible to design journaling-oriented songwriting systems in which DHH users can explore emotions through dialogue, express them in music, and record them repeat-edly. 

## 3 Ethical Considerations 

All studies were approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB) of [anonymity] University in Korea. Before participation, all par-ticipants were provided with detailed information about the study procedures and related activities, asked to complete a pre-study survey, informed about the use of the system, and guided through an ethical consent process for the interviews. Although the participant groups did not specifically include in-dividuals diagnosed with clinical mental illness, such as depression, we considered the possibility that SoulNote could elicit sensitive or potentially traumatic personal expressions. To address this, we adopted the following ethical safeguards. First, to minimize ethical risks during interactions with the CA, professional therapists were actively involved throughout the system’s design and validation. They continuously reviewed potential emotional risks and guided the study design and execution. During the lab-based usage study, a therapist monitored participants in real-time, and post-session analyses of conversation logs were conducted to further review potential risks before proceeding to the in-the-wild diary study. Second, for the studies involving DHH participants, we conducted pre-surveys to measure depression [ 49 ]. In collaboration with pro-fessional therapists, we screened for high-risk individuals during recruitment to ensure participants’ eligibility and safety. Finally, during the three-week diary study, participants were encouraged to take breaks at any time by notifying the researchers through a real-time messaging channel. The first author closely monitored interactions with SoulNote for potential risks, including mentions of self-harm or harm to others, indications of heightened distress, or emotional triggering from LLM-generated content. These were defined as adverse events. Across 12 participants and up to 12 ses-sions each, no adverse events were observed, and no participants withdrew because of emotional distress. 

## 4 Design and Development of SoulNote 4.1 User-centered Design Process 

To develop a system that enables DHH users to independently and safely participate in songwriting anytime and anywhere through GenAI while promoting emotional expression, we adopted a multi-phase user-centered design methodology (Figure 1) [ 1]. First, we collaborated with four music therapists specializing in DHH clients to design the CA-led songwriting process and conversational strate-gies (Phase 1). Subsequently, we developed an initial system based on this design (Phase 2). We then conducted a lab-based usage study with 12 DHH users to examine the stability of the system and the appropriateness of the songwriting procedure (Phase 3). Finally, we refined the prototype to improve usability by considering diary-style usage scenarios, which resulted in the system’s final version (Phase 4). 

4.1.1 Design workshop (Phases 1 and 2). To enhance the emotional impact of songwriting, we conducted a 70-minute online design workshop with four music therapists specializing in music therapy for DHH individuals (Table 1). The workshop consisted of individual design sessions and group discussions. Several key themes were identified and systematically incorporated into both the CA’s flow and the design of its conversational strategies (see Appendix A for details). 

• A challenge in songwriting without structured guidance (theme 1): The therapists anticipated that DHH individuals, who often have fewer opportunities for self-expression and access to musical experiences, might struggle with songwriting without guidance. Accordingly, they emphasized the need to design a structured songwriting process grounded in music therapy. 

• Inaccessibility of technical musical questioning in dialogue-based songwriting (theme 2): Therapists noted that general or technical questions about musical elements can be dif-ficult for DHH participants to answer. To elicit more con-crete musical material, they proposed using narrative-based questioning (e.g., memories, imagery, daily experiences ) and translating the responses into musical parameters during the dialogue. 

4.1.2 Lab-based usage study (Phases 3 and 4). To evaluate the us-ability and stability of the initial prototype developed from the design workshop, we conducted a 70-minute usage study with 12 DHH participants who had residual hearing and regularly used written Korean for digital communication (Table 2: Usage study). Each participant completed a songwriting activity using our system, followed by an individual interview about their experience. A mu-sic therapist joined all sessions to monitor the process. Table 3 summarizes the design requirements (DRs) derived from two preliminary studies that guided the design of the SoulNote system, along with the corresponding key features implemented to support its long-term deployment. The design workshop focused on the CA-related design of the songwriting process, while the lab-based usage study addressed practical use and usability considerations for delivering the system in a diary format. Based on these findings, 

SoulNote was designed around four core interfaces according to DRs (Figure 2): (a) a conversation interface for songwriting, (b) a lyrics editing interface, (c) a multimodal music appreciation inter-face, and (d) an interface for summarizing and recording previous songwriting outcomes. 

4.2.1 Songwriting conversation interface (Figure 2-A) .

DR1. Structured songwriting guided by CA. For DHH par-ticipants unfamiliar with self-expression and musical activities, an unstructured songwriting process can be burdensome and chal-lenging (theme 1). To address this challenge, we designed (1) a structured process in which the CA guides users through their songwriting, drawing on prior LLM-based studies that proposed structured and step-by-step approaches to support self-expression [ 58 , 97 , 119 ], and (2) the integration of lyrics and music GenAI to lower the technical barriers to songwriting for our target subgroup of DHH users [ 23 , 42 ]. Ultimately, drawing on therapeutic proce-dures commonly applied in clinical practice [ 13 , 116 ], we developed a CA-guided four-state songwriting process: 

• Music concept setting. The CA supports users in defining a music concept based on their daily events, thoughts, and emotions. 

• Lyric creation. The CA generates draft lyrics based on the user’s narrative, and users can subsequently refine them using an interactive lyrics editing interface. 

• Music creation. Through dialogue, the user and the CA spec-ify musical elements such as mood, genre, instruments, and vocals, and the CA then generates corresponding music with a GenAI model. 

• Appreciation and discussion. The generated music is pre-sented with visualization, enabling the user to experience the outcome and engage in reflective discussion to conclude the session. 

DR2. Narrative-based questioning for musical expression support. 

For DHH participants unfamiliar with songwriting, eliciting re-sponses requires focusing on their personal narratives to guide lyrics and musical expression (theme 2). In consultation with ther-apists, we refined three prompting strategies to ensure safe and accessible interaction. First, the CA was designed to adopt (1) a 

supportive dialogue without judgment or embellishment , providing dialogue that helps users articulate their situations rather than interpreting them. This supportive dialogue strategy, grounded in music therapy practice, does not involve delving deeply into specific situations but instead provides supportive interaction me-diated through music, guiding users toward musical expression [ 113 ]. Second, acknowledging that many DHH users have limited musical expression, our design drew on two insights: the tendency of DHH individuals to appreciate music visually [ 11 , 68 , 118 ] and the effectiveness of guided imagery in recalling concrete situations and emotions [ 39 , 61 ]. 4.2.2 A lyrics editing interface (Figure 2-B) .

DR3. Direct lyrics editing support. For DHH participants in the usage study who valued lyrics creation, revising lyrics exclu-sively through a conversational interface proved insufficient. In particular, the process of specifying lines and confirming changes through multiple dialogue turns was cumbersome and reduced accessibility (theme 3). To address this, we introduced a new in-terface that combined conversational revision with direct editing [ 50 , 109 ]. SoulNote was revised to allow users to (1) directly edit lyrics themselves or (2) select specific segments for revision through CA prompts (e.g., “Make this part more metaphorical” ). Revisions were displayed instantly, and users could confirm or discard them using checkmark and X icons. 

4.2.3 Multimodal music appreciation and archive (Figure 2-C and D) .

Music appreciation. To enhance music appreciation, SoulNote 

implements a multimodal appreciation feature that visualizes lyrics and musical elements simultaneously, drawing on prior research [ 23 ]. Prior work has highlighted that for DHH individuals, effec-tive music appreciation involves not only transforming musical elements into visual forms but also visualizing lyrics. Accordingly, rhythm is represented by rectangular blocks, pitch by dot posi-tions, lyrics by animated height variations reflecting vocal pitch, and mood/emotion by colors and fonts. This feature extends DHH users’ music experiences by leveraging music-to-visual conver-sion feedback, which supports perception for hearing aid users [100, 115].

## 4.3 Implementation of SoulNote 

4.3.1 Frontend and backend. SoulNote is a web-based applica-tion that integrates GenAI. It consists of two main components: (1) a frontend for user interface and visualization, and (2) a backend for dialogue processing, music generation, and analysis. The front-end interface was built with Next.js 1 and deployed via Vercel 2, while the back end was developed with Flask 3 and deployed on AWS EC2 4.The CA is powered by OpenAI’s GPT-4.1 5 (temperature = 0, fre-quency and presence penalties = 0, and all other parameters left at their default values) to facilitate songwriting conversations. For music generation, the system analyzes user dialogue to construct lyrics and music prompts, which are then processed through the Mureka.ai API 6 (model: mureka-6 ). Mureka.ai is a commercial text-to-music platform that uses deep generative models trained on large-scale music corpora to transform text prompts and lyrics into complete, production-ready songs with melody, harmony, instru-mentation, and mixing. Musical analyses required for visualization are extracted via the MusicAI API 7 and mapped to visual elements. All conversation logs, generated lyrics, music files, and visualization data are stored in Supabase 8, ensuring consistency across sessions and enabling archiving functionality. 

4.3.2 Prompting design of conversational agent. In align-ment with the four-states CA-guided structured songwriting pro-cess—comprising music concept setting, lyric creation, music cre-ation, and appreciation/discussion—the CA was implemented using a state–slot technique [ 111 ]. This technique designs specific vari-ables (slots) required at each state, collects corresponding pieces of information from user responses, and transitions the system to the next state once all required slots are filled. In consultation with music therapists, the prompts were carefully designed to elicit the variables required at each state and to draw out the elements needed for songwriting (Table A1 in C). For example, in the music concept state, the goal is to identify a musical theme based on daily experi-ences and emotions. To achieve this, slots such as today_emotion, difficulty, expression are extracted, and upon completion, the sys-tem proceeds to the next state. For this process, we designed the CA prompting with four components. The general prompt defines the CA’s role, specifies the rules for questioning and responding, and provides guidance on the overall songwriting states. The state-specific prompt generates questions to fill the slots required for each of the four songwriting states. ## 4.4 Usage Scenario 

The following is an example usage scenario of Lora, a fictional user, demonstrating the interface shown in Figure 2. At the end of her day, Lora logs into SoulNote . When the system begins, the CA provides a summary of the key points from the previous activity. She then clicks the sidebar to revisit yesterday’s music through the multimodal appreciation interface, recalling past emotional experiences. Then, the CA initiates a new songwriting by asking,

From this dialogue, the CA identifies the day’s musical concept and guides Lora to create lyrics. When the CA asks, “Think of a short phrase or image that can represent that moment,” she replies, “Standing nervously in front of my friends, facing their expressionless faces.” After further discussion, the CA generates a draft of lyrics. ## 5 In-the-Wild Diary Study 5.1 Participants 

We conducted a three-week, in-the-wild diary study to evaluate the longer-term effects of iterative songwriting with SoulNote . Recruit-ment criteria were: (1) clinical hearing impairment, (2) ability to use computer-based systems and communicate with the conversational agent in written Korean, and (3) interest in musical self-expression or journaling, regardless of prior songwriting experience. The re-cruitment flyer described the study goal, participation period, pro-cedures, and activities. Recruitment occurred over approximately two weeks through hard-of-hearing communities and hearing care centers across multiple regions of Korea to avoid concentrating the sample in a single local community. We administered a pre-survey to promote diversity and screen for depression [41]. We confirmed that no respondents exceeded our predefined high-risk threshold, so no one was excluded for safety reasons. Twelve participants were enrolled, aged from their 20s to their 50s (mean = 38.25, SD = 13.17), with hearing levels ranging from mild to profound (Table 2: From Daily Song to Daily Self CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain

## 5.2 Process and Materials 

To examine the initial feasibility of deploying SoulNote in real-world settings, we used a single-condition longitudinal design to provide a comfortable, low-burden participant experience [ 48 , 101 ]. DHH participants used the system freely over three weeks. Draw-ing on prior work indicating that approximately ten sessions are needed to observe emotional or behavioral change [ 18 ], we asked participants to complete twelve songwriting sessions, aiming for roughly four sessions per week. Before beginning the study, all participants completed a three-day video tutorial and conducted at least one practice session to familiarize themselves with the sys-tem. During the three-week study, each participant completed the 12 required sessions. After every session, participants rated their satisfaction with the process ( “How satisfied are you with today’s music creation process with the CA?” ) and with the musical outcomes (“How satisfied are you with the lyrics and music created today?” )on a 7-point Likert scale [ 23 ]. They also rated the perceived effort required ( “How much effort did you put into today’s music creation with the CA?” ). 

## 5.3 Data Analysis 

We conducted a qualitative analysis of conversational data, weekly reports, and interview transcripts, focusing on exploratory interpre-tation and intentionally avoiding strong claims about the system’s effects, given the small sample size and lack of comparison condi-tions [ 14 , 64 ]. First, the first author and a co-author with expertise in music therapy conducted an initial thematic analysis of partici-pants’ conversations with the CA. They identified four categories used to label the data: songwriting topic, creative intent, lyric strat-egy, and musical style. Next, the weekly journals and interview transcripts were reviewed to develop an initial codebook. Discrep-ancies were discussed and incorporated into a refined codebook. Next, codes and data were reviewed with an additional co-author who was not involved in the interviews, resulting in a final thematic structure comprising four themes (used as subsection headings) and six subthemes (used as subsubsection headings). The first theme characterizes participants’ iterative engagement with songwriting across sessions (Section 6.1). Guided by prior work on emotional development and trait emotional intelligence [ 82 , 91 ], we interpret the remaining three themes—self-insight (Section 6.2), emotion regulation (Section 6.3), and attitudinal change (Section 6.4)—as reflecting a three-dimensional emotional growth lens. Table A2 in Appendix D presents the detailed codes. To complement the observational and self-report findings fo-cused on users’ change, we additionally collected behavioral log data—per-session lyric editing time and counts, the frequency with which participants initiated difficult emotional topics, the number of contextual music recommendations requested, and replay counts of archived songs—used not as direct proxies for psychological change but as supplementary indicators of how participants en-gaged with the system over time (Figure 3-A). These indicators were analyzed descriptively to triangulate qualitative themes related to self-insight, emotion regulation, and shifts in everyday attitudes, rather than to support causal claims.

## 6.4 Music as a Self-care Resource for Driving a Change in Attitude Toward Their daily life 

6.4.1 Resource for facilitating self-expression . Repeated song-writing experiences were found to serve as a resource that encour-aged emotional recognition and attempts at emotional expression among DHH participants. Several participants (n=6) who had pre-viously been reluctant to share their feelings with family or peers mentioned that after repeatedly expressing suppressed emotions during the music creation process, changes began to occur in their real-life interactions. They reported communicating their feelings and situations more openly with family (P1, P2), friends (P3, P6), and colleagues (P5, P11). Specifically, during the lyric-writing pro-cess with the CA, participants could directly articulate what they wanted to say to people with whom they had difficult relationships or recall positive memories from the past. These experiences fos-tered a sense of the need for self-expression as a means of improving relationships.

## 7 An Additional Study on the Mechanism of SoulNote 7.1 Study Design 

To better understand the effects of SoulNote’s mechanisms—lyrics, conversation, and music—we conducted a follow-up within-subjects study using four journaling configurations: (1) Text-only (diary and lyric writing without CA or music), (2) CA-only (songwriting dialogue without music), (3) GenAI-music (music generated from prompts without lyric or dialogue support), and (4) full SoulNote 

(combined lyric, dialogue, and music channels). We conducted this study with 10 new DHH participants who had not participated in the diary study. Participants included 7 female and 3 male adults (age, M=35.08 years, SD=10.47) (Table 2: Additional study). All reported residual hearing with assistive devices and indicated the ability to communicate using spoken/written language and sign language in daily life. Over one week, each participant completed all four conditions in a counterbalanced order. After each session, participants completed a brief questionnaire about their experience. Participants completed a session-specific additional self-report questionnaire. The questionnaire assessed four constructs 7 Liker-scale: self-expression (3 items, e.g., “My thoughts and feelings were expressed” ) [ 75 ] and self-awareness (4 items, e.g., “I have a clear understanding of why I do the things I do.” ) [ 96 ], emotional change (4 items, e.g., “My negative emotions decreased compared to before the session” ), and method preference (4 items, e.g., “I am satisfied with this method” ) [ 46 ]. Open-ended questions asked participants to ex-plain their ratings and describe concrete aspects of their experience in each condition. 

## 7.2 Findings 

The study showed that the four conditions afforded distinct path-ways for journaling and emotional processing. In the text-only condition, writing helped participants externalize and organize their thoughts, but transforming complex experiences into song lyrics often felt demanding. In the CA-only condition, this burden was shifted into guided interaction: stepwise questions and image-based prompts scaffolded the articulation of vague feelings and supported narrative exploration. In the GenAI-music-only condi-tion, listening to AI-generated music supported short-term emo-tion regulation and mood shifts, yet participants struggled to craft prompts that faithfully captured their emotional states, which in turn limited opportunities for explicit emotional expression. By contrast, the integrated SoulNote condition achieved the highest scores across all constructs, with statistically significant advantages on self-awareness (Figure 5), indicating that combining writing, conversation, and music offers DHH participants a more emotion-ally impactful journaling experience than any single channel alone. Text-only. Consistent with prior work on expressive writing, participants (n=8) reported that writing about their day helped ex-ternalize and organize complex thoughts [ 5, 88 , 104 ]. Half of the participants (n=5) did not complete the lyric-writing task due to dif-ficulty or pressure. ## 8 Discussion and Design Implication 

The findings suggest that the GenAI-based system SoulNote en-hanced songwriting experiences for DHH individuals and, over time, supported positive changes in emotional experiences. Draw-ing on observations from the diary study, this section highlights design implications for promoting emotional growth through an LLM-based CA (Section 6.1) and music GenAI (Section 6.2). 

## 8.1 Lessons and Design Implications for Conversational Agents in Expressive Songwriting 

8.1.1 Questioning strategies that promote positive reframing. Prior work has shown that conversational prompts play a critical role in eliciting deep self-expression from users [ 48 , 93 , 97 ]. In our study, conversational interaction with an agent that helped DHH partic-ipants with little prior experience in expressive writing external-ize personal experiences and alleviate emotional distress. Unlike prior systems that relied on one-shot, text-only prompts to elicit songwriting without dialogue [ 23 ], our system incorporated con-versational scaffolding that actively guided participants’ meaning-making process. This design reflects prior CA-mediated journaling systems, which enhance narrative exploration and emotional disclo-sure through stepwise questioning [ 45 , 110 , 119 ]. However, to elicit musical expression, our system uniquely oriented these questions toward concrete scenes and sensory imagery, encouraging partici-pants to describe inner struggles related to hearing rehabilitation, job preparation, and workplace challenges in specific, situational terms. Moreover, extending beyond prior studies that were limited to a single session, repeated articulation across sessions enabled par-ticipants to develop more accurate emotional awareness and deeper self-reflection [ 63 , 71 ]. Notably, although the system prompted par-ticipants to depict scenes, many responded with positive memories or hopeful visions of the future, thereby re-engaging with their values and aspirations. This process facilitated positive reframing, leading to shifts in identity and reinforcing belief in their capacity for recovery [ 4]. Participants also reported that re-listening to the generated song helped them view difficult situations more positively and with greater acceptance, indicating a concrete shift in their attitudes toward daily life. Such attitudinal shifts can act as a proxi-mal mechanism for behavioral change, for example, by increasing willingness to re-engage in daily routines or to adopt new cop-ing strategies [ 52 ]. These effects parallel therapeutic mechanisms such as guided imagery in cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), which encourages individuals to visualize concrete situations and make constructive decisions [39]. Future designs should therefore go beyond merely encouraging musical expression, incorporating structured mechanisms informed by music therapy frameworks to deliberately elicit positive narratives through targeted questioning [ 26 , 33 ]. Such strategies could deepen self-awareness and reflec-tion among DHH individuals facing challenges while promoting recovery through reinforcement of constructive habits [4]. 

8.1.2 Managing potential risks in expressive songwriting. Although therapist monitoring during our study did not indicate psycho-logical harm, expressive songwriting inherently carries potential emotional risks [ 62 ]. Repeatedly recounting past traumas or current From Daily Song to Daily Self CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain 

negative emotions can temporarily heighten anxiety, depression, or feelings of helplessness, while visualizing distressing scenes may inadvertently increase emotional vulnerability [ 81 ]. Additionally, as generative systems, LLM-based CAs may produce inaccurate, inconsistent, or context-insensitive responses, posing further risks for vulnerable populations such as DHH users [ 21 , 66 ]. While song-writing can serve as a creative outlet and a means of emotional regulation, its use in mental health contexts requires careful risk management [ 8, 77 ]. To mitigate these concerns, recent research has proposed safety engineering frameworks for evaluating LLM outputs and adversarial prompt–based red-teaming approaches to ensure safe responses under challenging inputs [ 35 , 70 ]. Digital mental health guidelines [ 51 , 67 ] and clinical psychology theories [ 40 , 85 ] further emphasize the importance of proactively assessing psychological risks in expressive systems and incorporating person-alized follow-up questions to protect users during the prompting process. Future systems should combine LLM output validation frameworks with clinically informed prompt design, balancing the encouragement of free expression with the minimization of emo-tional risk. Hybrid intervention models that include therapeutic or professional oversight remain essential for addressing situations beyond the capabilities of CAs [ 48 , 102 ]. Together, these safeguards can create a safe, creative environment in which DHH individuals can confidently sustain self-expression. 

## 8.2 Lessons and Design Implications for Personalized Music GenAI for Emotion Regulation 

8.2.1 Self-acceptance through fact-based songwriting. In our study, participants experienced greater self-acceptance and deeper self-understanding when the CA generated lyrics that reflected their difficult situations factually, without positive reframing or embel-lishment. This observation sits alongside research on expressive writing, which shows that factual narration promotes deeper emo-tional processing compared to avoidance or overly positive glossing [ 12 , 77 ]. Accordingly, AI-generated outputs that faithfully mirror users’ narratives hold significant potential for designing emotional interventions. For example, Bhattacharjee et al. [ 12 ] found that individuals with negative thoughts experienced empathy and vali-dation when LLM-generated stories closely reflected their real-life circumstances. Psychotherapy literature suggests that factual ex-pression not only fosters acceptance but also provides a foundation for reframing personal narratives [ 21 , 47 ]. By presenting negative emotions as externalized objects, unembellished lyrics reduce emo-tional burden and enable users to reconstruct their stories from new perspectives. In our study, participants reported identity shifts, de-scribing their emotional challenges not as obstacles to be denied but as integral aspects of life to be embraced. Some participants further reinterpreted and reframed their experiences independently, demon-strating the potential of fact-based narratives as mechanisms for emotional acceptance. However, these changes primarily resulted from participants’ own interpretive efforts rather than direct facili-tation by the CA. Thus, while unembellished outputs are crucial for fostering acceptance, additional design strategies are needed to actively support narrative reframing [26, 33]. 

8.2.2 Exploring music styles for emotion regulation. Music has long served as a primary means of emotion regulation [ 90 , 107 ]. Be-yond auditory stimulation, it functions as a symbolic medium through which individuals can explore and express emotions [16]. Psychotherapy research similarly highlights that engaging with emotions across multiple sensory and artistic modalities enhances self-awareness and emotion regulation [ 25 , 87 ]. In our study, partic-ipants who engaged in songwriting on both positive and negative topics explored diverse emotional states and intuitively experi-mented with multiple musical styles to express and regulate their feelings. Introducing Music GenAI advanced this process in several key ways. First, because personalized music could be generated rapidly from simple text prompts, DHH participants were able to translate their written content into multiple musical realizations without needing formal composition skills. They could further de-sign these pieces to pitch ranges and timbral qualities they could perceive, and, with the aid of visualizations, enhance their mu-sic listening experience and engagement. These findings suggest that music GenAI can provide an exploratory environment where users flexibly experiment with varied musical styles and configura-tions, thereby discovering personally meaningful ways to modulate affect. These findings suggest that music GenAI can provide an exploratory environment where users experiment with varied mu-sical styles, thereby discovering strategies for emotion regulation [30 , 59 ]. Notably, our study demonstrated the potential of context-based music recommendations that map lyrical content to musical features. Participants received CA suggestions for attributes such as tempo, rhythm, instrumentation, and vocal tone based on key-words or messages embedded in their lyrics. This lyrics–music mapping encouraged participants to experiment with musical fea-tures they might otherwise avoid, enabling them to discover more fitting emotional expressions or encounter novel affective effects. These observations align with prior research emphasizing the role of musical characteristics in shaping emotional experience and regulation [32, 41]. 

## 9 Limitations 

SoulNote shows preliminary promise in improving access to song-writing for DHH individuals and supporting beneficial emotional experiences. However, several limitations should be noted. First, the sample was relatively small and drawn from a specific DHH subgroup, primarily based on residual hearing. The study does not fully represent born Deaf individuals, who may face greater chal-lenges with conventional music listening, nor Deaf signers whose primary language is sign language and who may have limited ac-cess to written-language interfaces. Additionally, participants were not systematically involved in early-stage co-design. Future work should expand recruitment across diverse Deaf communities, inte-grate sign language as a primary interaction modality, and incorpo-rate co-design with DHH collaborators according to Deaf-led tech-nology and co-design literature [ 3 ]. Second, the study employed a single-arm, in-the-wild diary design over 12 sessions without comparison conditions. This approach prioritized feasibility and sustained engagement but limits causal inference. Findings should be interpreted as descriptive characterizations of system use rather than evidence that SoulNote outperforms alternative journaling CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain Choi et al. 

methods. Future work could combine controlled comparison condi-tions, standardized pre–post measures, and longer-term follow-ups to rigorously assess emotional impact and within-person change over time. Third, the current implementation has practical limi-tations. As a web-based application, some participants may have experienced constraints related to device, connectivity, or daily integration. Mobile or hybrid implementations could support more spontaneous engagement and use of musical outputs as self-care resources. The music generation component also offered limited granularity and controllability, which may have constrained faithful expression and reduced immersion when outputs did not align with user intent [ 15 , 19 ]. Future work should explore more controllable music generation models, including finer control over structure, timbre, and loudness tailored to individual hearing characteristics, while maintaining transparency and safety in the emotional content produced. 

## 10 Conclusion 

To support sustained songwriting among DHH individuals, we de-signed and developed SoulNote , a system that enhances access to songwriting by integrating a CA. The CA-guided songwriting ex-perience was found to promote participants’ emotional experience. Iterative lyrics creation fostered self-awareness and understand-ing of specific situations, at times prompting shifts in perspective. Moreover, enhanced music appreciation and the CA’s contextual musical interactions encouraged exploration of diverse musical styles, extending music’s role as a means of self-regulation. Partic-ipants regarded the resulting songs as both creative artifacts and durable resources for self-expression and emotion regulation, which appeared to support more adaptive coping even in challenging cir-cumstances. These findings provide strong preliminary evidence that sustained songwriting can promote emotional growth among DHH individuals.

## A Design workshop A.1 Participants and Process 

To promote the emotional effects of songwriting, we aimed to de-sign the overall system process and dialogue based on music ther-apy practices. With the cooperation of a music therapy center, we recruited four music therapists with more than five years of ex-perience in music psychotherapy for DHH. The design workshop was conducted online via video call meeting through Zoom for approximately 70 minutes and consisted of three steps: (1) a brief introduction to the design goals and study procedure (15 min); (2) individual design of the songwriting process to support emotional effects and the corresponding questions for each stage (30 min); and (3) a group discussion to consolidate the individual designs into a final process and CA conversational strategies (30 min). The entire design process was carried out using a shared presentation document. Before the workshop, participants were provided with a practice guide introducing generative AI technologies, including ChatGPT as an LLM-based conversational agent and Suno 9 as a music gener-ation system, to establish a basic understanding of these concepts. In the workshop, participants collaboratively discussed and defined the songwriting process as well as the CA’s questioning strategies, grounded in the anticipated challenges faced by DHH users. 

## A.2 Results 

A.2.1 Theme 1. A challenge in songwriting without structured guid-ance. Therapists unanimously agreed that free-form songwriting would be highly challenging for DHH individuals who are not ac-customed to musical activities. They anticipated that participants would struggle with fundamental decisions such as selecting a theme, creating lyrical content, and choosing musical elements (e.g., melody, harmony, rhythm ) that align with the lyrics. More-over, therapists emphasized that many DHH clients tend to have limited skills in emotional expression, making it difficult to gener-ate meaningful material through unstructured conversation alone. Instead, they highlighted the importance of providing structured and stepwise guidance that gradually scaffolds self-expression.

In parallel with designing the song-writing steps, therapists also specified the kinds of questions the CA should ask to guide participants. When sharing their questions, they confirmed that even within a structured process, DHH par-ticipants may still struggle to respond to music-related prompts during dialogue. Because many DHH individuals have little prior experience with music appreciation or performance, they are un-likely to respond easily or concretely to technical questions such as

“Which instruments would you like to add?” Therapists pointed out that abstract terms related to tempo, genre, instrumentation, or tim-bre may create cognitive burden or lead to vague and non-specific responses. To address this limitation, they proposed adopting a narrative-based questioning strategy. For example, prompts such as “What recent memory stands out to you?” , “What colors or images come to mind?” , or “If you describe today’s mood in one word, what would it be?” were suggested as more accessible entry points. Such narra-tive or sensory-oriented prompts allow participants to articulate their experiences more concretely, which can then be translated into musical parameters (e.g., a bright and fast tempo, a calm pi-ano accompaniment, or darker timbres in strings ). This approach highlights the need to design conversational strategies that avoid direct reliance on technical musical terminology while still enabling participants to co-construct meaningful musical outcomes 

## B Lab-based usage study B.1 Participants and Process 

To evaluate the accessibility and stability of the initial prototype de-veloped from the design workshop, we conducted a one-time usage study with 12 DHH participants. To focus on assessing accessibility, we recruited participants who had little to no prior experience with CAs such as ChatGPT. All participants had a diagnosed hearing impairment and wore hearing assistive devices. The study was carried out in the form of individual Zoom sessions, each lasting approximately 70 minutes and monitored in real-time by a thera-pist. Before the study, participants received an introductory video and materials about the prototype, and each participant had one preliminary usage experience before the session. During the study, participants engaged in songwriting with the system for an average of 45 minutes, followed by a 20-minute individual interview to collect user experiences. The interviews focused on the anticipated challenges of conducting songwriting with the CA and a diary-style setting (e.g., “What challenges did you face during the songwriting process with the CA?” ) and on potential system improvements (e.g., 

“If you were to use the system comfortably at home, what features would you like to see added?” ). 

## B.2 Results 

B.2.1 The potential of a safe and user-friendly system. Analysis of the interviews indicated that all participants completed a song with the system in an average of 42 minutes (SD=13 min) without ma-jor difficulties. Synthesizing participants’ reports with therapists’ monitoring notes revealed no observed risks, such as triggering traumatic memories, negative emotions, or heightened anxiety dur-ing the songwriting process. Therapists attributed this to the CA’s conversational design, which avoided probing for deeply personal or sensitive disclosures and instead focused on supportive empathy and structured prompts for songwriting. In post-session interviews, most participants reported that they did not experience substantial difficulties in the songwriting process. They described the CA dialogue as enabling them to express emo-tions and thoughts without burden, and they were satisfied with transforming these expressions into music. U3 noted, “Talking with the system made it easy to say what I felt without pressure”. Several participants emphasized the emotional benefits of music, noting that composing, even about negative situations, helped shift their mood toward more positive states. U7 reported that “when I made music about a negative situation, I felt my mood change afterwards”. 

B.2.2 The challenges of a user-friendly system. Theme 3. Inacces-sibility of technical musical questioning in dialogue-based songwriting. Although participants generally found the songwrit-ing process easy and satisfying, most raised concerns about editing lyrics through dialogue. Participants tended to focus on writing lyrics that reflected their personal situations, and many actively revised AI-generated text. However, they agreed that editing via linear conversation with the CA was cumbersome. Copying specific sections of lyrics and instructing the CA on how to revise them was perceived as repetitive and inconvenient, and when multiple revi-sions were generated, U5 found it difficult to track which parts had actually been changed. As one participant explained, “It was tiring to copy and paste parts of the lyrics and ask the system to change them. When it revised several times, I couldn’t tell which line was updated”. 

U6 added, “I found editing through conversation uncomfortable and wished I could edit the text directly instead”. 

Theme 4. Inaccessibility of technical musical question-ing in dialogue-based songwriting. Participants who composed about negative experi-ences emphasized that the resulting songs felt like personal sources of comfort, describing them as “my own music” that they wanted to continue listening to. Several participants noted that these outputs would serve as strong motivation to use the system at home, and they highlighted the importance of being able to save all records and revisit them at any time. ## C Prompt Engineering 

We adopted a state-based approach to achieve the conversation goal of songwriting. The CA generates questions by combining a general prompt that defines the overall conversation rules and the CA’s role, a state-specific prompt designed to achieve the goal of each state, and the previous dialogue history to maintain context. In addition, meaningful information is extracted from the user’s responses by combining a slot-extraction prompt, which identifies the timing for state transitions, and a summary prompt, which supports the recall of the previous session. In addition to the provider’s built-in safety filters, we incorporated explicit safety guidelines into all four system prompts, directing the model to avoid harmful or inappropriate content in lyric generation and conversational exchanges. In the following section, we provide detailed descriptions of each prompt. 

## C.1 General prompt 

We defined the overall role and conversational strategies of the CA to guide DHH users through the structured songwriting process. The CA was instructed that ‘your role is to help the user create lyrics and music grounded in the personal narratives of DHH users.’ To ensure emotional safety, the CA was further designed to provide supportive dialogue, responding empathetically without judgment or exaggeration ( ‘Show empathy and provide support without judg-ment or embellishment of their responses. However, do not express empathy toward negative situations such as suicide.’ 

To guide songwriting in a way that is accessible to DHH users, we define the role of the CA and provide overall conversation rules. 

• Role: your role is to help the user create lyrics and music grounded in the personal narratives of DHH users. 

• Conversation rules: - Conduct all conversations in Korean. - Ask only one question at a time and use simple, clear lan-guage, considering that some users may have varying levels of literacy. - Make sure the user feels comfortable during the conversa-tion. - Collect the necessary information (variables) in a natural way. - Avoid asking similar or repetitive questions. - Provide examples only when users seem confused or ask for clarification, ensuring that the examples are grounded in the user’s lyrics and dialogue history. - focus on encouragement and positive/motivational approaches. - If the user expresses severe distress or self-harm thoughts, respond with supportive empathy and encourage them to seek professional or emergency help. - Show empathy and provide support without judgment or embellishment of their responses. However, do not express empathy toward negative situations such as suicide. 

## C.2 State-specific prompt 

Prompts were designed to guide users toward the goals of the four songwriting states ( “Guide the conversation according to the song-writing flow. Once the required variables for each state are filled, proceed to the next state.” ) Each prompt was designed to elicit the required slots at a given state and to capture elements necessary for songwriting. For DHH users with limited musical and expressive ex-perience, general questions about musical elements can be difficult to answer. Therefore, we designed prompts under two strategies: imagery-based lyrics questions and context-based music style rec-ommendations. This approach was also informed by prior work in music therapy and expressive writing, which highlights the role of guided prompts in fostering self-expression and awareness [ 7 , 39 ]. For lyric creation, “encourage the user to describe their ideas through clear messages, scenes, images, or objects.” During music creation, 

“provide a variety of musical options that fit the context, with accom-panying reasons.” The lyrics and musical elements derived from user input were then mapped to corresponding slots and used as input for the music GenAI. State-specific prompts were constructed to achieve the goal of each state. Each prompt consists of state-level instructions and few-shot examples of the overall conversation. State 1 Music concept setting.

## C.4 Summary prompt 

In songwriting therapy, reviewing previous sessions is a common practice that helps participants recall earlier expressions, recognize patterns in their emotions, and build continuity across sessions [ 92 , 105 ]. To incorporate this therapeutic function, we designed a summary prompt that provides a concise overview of previous songwriting activities, highlighting the user’s expressed challenges, music concepts, and related musical information ( “Summarize the user’s previous session in 3–4 sentences, focusing on the challenges discussed, the music concepts explored, and the related musical ele-ments.” )To recall the previous songwriting, a summary is generated. This summary prompt consists of an instruction and examples. 

• Instruction: Summarize the user’s previous session in 3–4 sentences, focusing on the challenges discussed, the music concepts explored, and the related musical elements. 

• Examples: - Based on “anxiety about others’ perceptions,” a creative work was produced with the theme of “accepting myself as I am.” - Drawing on the difficulty of “emptiness in repetitive daily life,” lyrics and music were completed with the theme of “finding meaning in small things.”
