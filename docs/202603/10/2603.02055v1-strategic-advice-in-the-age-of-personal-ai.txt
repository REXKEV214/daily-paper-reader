Abstract 

Personal AI assistants have changed how people use institutional and professional advice. We study this new strategic setting in which individuals may stochastically consult a personal AI whose recommendation is predictable to the focal advisor. Personal AI enters this strategic environment along two dimensions: how often it is consulted and how much weight it receives in the human’s decision when consulted. Anticipating this, the advisor responds by counteracting the personal AI recommendation. Counteraction becomes more aggressive as personal AI is consulted more often. Yet advisor performance is non-monotone: equilibrium loss is highest at intermediate levels of adoption and vanishes when personal AI is never used or always used. Trust affects performance through a single relative influence index, and greater relative influence of personal AI increases advisor vulnerability. Extending the framework to costly credibility building, we characterize how personal AI adoption reshapes incentives to invest in trust. 

# 1 Introduction 

Personal AI assistants such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and related tools are becoming a routine part of how individuals make decisions [5, 7, 18]. At work, an employee may receive a recommen-dation from an enterprise decision-support system, but also cross-check that advice by asking a personal AI for a second opinion. This creates a new strategic environment for institutional advi-sors. Their recommendations increasingly compete with an outside option that is easy to access, often highly trusted, and, crucially, predictable. This environment differs from the settings captured by existing models of advice taking and human adherence to algorithmic recommendations [3, 6, 10, 11, 13, 17, 25, 26] in two ways. First, consultation is stochastic. Individuals do not always consult personal AI, and an advisor typically does not know ex ante whether its recommendation will be evaluated on its own or aggregated with a personal AI signal. Second, personal AI is often predictable. Unlike an independent expert whose recommendation is private and idiosyncratic, the output of a widely available personal AI can often be anticipated, or even reproduced, by querying the same tool. An enterprise advisor therefore faces an outside signal that is non-strategic yet strategically salient. We develop a theoretical framework to study how an advisor should strategically design its recommendation in this new setting. Personal AI adoption is characterized by an extensive margin ,the probability p that an individual consults personal AI, and an intensive margin , the relative trust or weight t [1, 4, 27, 28] the individual places in personal AI recommendation compared 1

Anticipating the human’s decision rule, the advisor chooses its recommendation to steer the final decision toward a target r, which may represent the ground truth or institutional objectives. Specifically, the advisor strategically counteracts the personal AI’s signal by shifting its recom-mendation in the opposite direction. When personal AI is never consulted ( p = 0), the advisor distorts its recommendation freely to align the human’s decision with its target. When always consulted ( p = 1), the advisor anticipates personal AI’s influence with certainty and fully offsets it. In both extremes, loss vanishes. At intermediate adoption levels, the advisor faces genuine strategic uncertainty: it must choose a single recommendation that balances states with and without personal AI. This tension is maximized at an interior adoption level, generating a hump-shaped loss. We further show that advisor vulnerability depends on trust only through a relative trust ratio 

t, i.e., the weight placed on personal AI recommendation relative to the combined weight on the advisor and the human’s prior knowledge. Holding t fixed, increases in trust in both recommenda-tions leave outcomes unchanged. As the relative trust t increases, the personal AI exerts greater leverage over the final decision. The advisor’s optimal response therefore diverges more sharply across consultation and non-consultation states: when AI is consulted, stronger counteraction is re-quired to offset its influence, whereas when AI is not consulted, no counteraction is desirable. This widening asymmetry amplifies the cost of misalignment, and advisor loss increases monotonically in relative trust t.Beyond recommendation design, we endogenize trust as a second strategic margin. In many environments, advisors cannot freely manipulate recommendations due to regulatory, reputational, or ethical constraints. They can instead invest in trust, through transparency, interpretability, or interface design, to increase weight that humans assign to their advice [2, 3, 10, 15, 17, 19, 20, 21]. We show that the incentive to invest depends systematically on personal AI adoption: under a binary investment structure, there exists an adoption-dependent cost threshold for credibility investment. Personal AI therefore reshapes not only how recommendations are designed, but whether trust investment is worthwhile at all. 

Contributions. Our paper makes three contributions. First, we introduce a theoretical frame-work in which advisor recommendations compete with stochastic consultation of personal AI whose outputs are predictable to the advisor. This setting departs from standard models by incorporating both stochastic consultation and signal observability. Second, we characterize the advisor’s optimal strategic response to personal AI adoption and provide a complete characterization of advisor vulnerability along both margins of adoption. The hump-shaped relationship between consultation probability and loss, and the monotone dependence of loss on a single relative trust ratio, offer tractable and testable predictions for how personal AI adoption affects institutional advisors. Third, we endogenize trust as a strategic instrument and show that personal AI adoption reshapes the incentive to invest in trust. Existing work studies how advisors should build trust; our results address when such investment is worthwhile, and show that the answer depends on personal AI adoption. 22 Related Literature 

A large behavioral literature studies advice taking. A foundational stream, centered on the Judge–Advisor System [1, 4, 12, 22, 23, 24, 27, 28], documents systematic patterns in advice aggre-gation, including egocentric discounting and its variation across contexts—for example, discounting is more pronounced when decision makers have less prior knowledge, face lower uncertainty, or operate in low-stakes tasks. While this literature carefully characterizes how individuals integrate advice given fixed information sources, it typically treats advisors as passive and non-strategic. It does, however, provide experimental support for modeling advice aggregation as linear weighted averaging; in our framework, these weights correspond to perceived precision or trust in each source. A growing operations literature studies advice taking from the perspective of a single focal ad-visor, typically an enterprise algorithmic recommendation system, and examines partial adherence by human decision makers. In these settings, performance depends on the extent to which humans follow the recommendation. This stream spans diverse domains, including warehouse operations [25], pricing in fashion retail [6], queue management in radiological services [13], ordering behavior of retail store managers [26], and theoretical analyses of partial adherence [11]. These studies show that human compliance with algorithmic advice is imperfect and endogenous to context. Building on this insight, we model the advisor as a strategic actor with target r, rather than a passive information provider. We treat the recommendation itself as a strategic instrument that shifts the human’s final decision toward the advisor’s objective. Unlike existing frameworks that hold recommendation content fixed or focus on adherence within specific operational settings, we study strategic recommendation distortion in a general decision environment. We further incor-porate personal AI adoption, introducing a competing and predictable signal that reshapes the advisor’s problem. A related behavioral literature studies trust in algorithmic advice, examining when and how individuals integrate algorithmic recommendations with human expert judgment. Foundational behavioral research identifies general patterns such as algorithm aversion—reduced reliance after observing algorithmic errors—and, in other settings, algorithm appreciation [9, 10, 16]. Subsequent research examines mechanisms for shaping trust in and reliance on algorithmic advice, showing that interface cues, explanations, and prior experience influence trust [2, 15, 19, 20, 21]. In addition, reliance increases when individuals can modify algorithmic outputs [10] or when recommendations are interpretable and demonstrably improve upon current practice [3], and decreases when algorithms strategically withhold recommendations in response to over-compliance [17]. We complement this literature by treating trust not merely as a psychological outcome, but as a strategic object. Whereas prior work documents behavioral patterns and proposes mechanisms to increase reliance on a given algorithm, we theoretically characterize how stochastic consultation of personal AI endogenously reshapes the advisor’s incentives to invest in trust and how optimal trust investment responds to adoption. In our framework, the advisor may represent either an enterprise AI system or a human expert. Our model also connects to the strategic communication literature in economics. Beginning with [8], this literature studies how an informed sender transmits information to a receiver when their preferences diverge, showing that misalignment leads to coarsening of communication in equi-librium. [14] study the complementary problem of Bayesian persuasion, in which a sender commits to an information structure. In our setting, the advisor faces a competing but predictable and non-strategic information source—personal AI—that the human may or may not consult. This creates 3strategic uncertainty over whether the advisor’s recommendation will be interpreted standalone or jointly with personal AI. We characterize how this stochastic outside option reshapes optimal distortion, equilibrium loss, and the advisor’s incentive to invest in trust. 

# 3 Model 

We consider a setting in which a human decision-maker receives a recommendation from a tradi-tional advisor and may or may not consult a personal AI assistant (such as ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini) prior to making a decision. When the personal AI is consulted, the human aggregates recommendations from the advisor and the personal AI, potentially assigning different weights to each source based on perceived precision. In our framework, the advisor may represent either an en-terprise AI system or a human expert. Throughout the paper, we use the terms advisor, enterprise, and expert interchangeably. This framework captures environments in which institutional advice interacts with increasingly accessible personal AI tools, thereby generating strategic incentives for experts to adjust their recommendations in response to personal AI adoption.

# 4 Enterprise Response to Personal AI Adoption 

This section characterizes the advisor’s optimal response to personal AI adoption. We begin by deriving the human’s decision rule as a function of available recommendations, highlighting the extensive and intensive margins through which personal AI enters the enterprise’s strategic problem. We then solve for the enterprise’s optimal recommendation and show that it decomposes into three components: target alignment, correction for human’s prior bias, and a strategic counteraction term that tries to offset the influence of personal AI. Finally, we analyze how counteraction intensity and equilibrium loss vary with the adoption rate p and the relative trust ratio t.

4.1 Extensive-Intensive Decomposition in Human Decision 

Human decisions aggregate available signals via Bayesian updating. Personal AI influences this ag-gregation along two margins. The extensive margin is adoption: the personal AI enters the decision only when consulted, with probability p. The intensive margin is trust: conditional on consulta-tion, each signal receives weight proportional to its perceived precision — rE for the enterprise AI and rP for the personal AI. A larger adoption rate p raises the likelihood that the personal AI is consulted; a larger rP amplifies the personal AI’s influence conditional on consultation. Together, these two margins define the strategic environment the expert must navigate. We first present how human makes decision in the absence of personal AI.

Monotonicity in p. The monotonicity of ∆ in p reflects a simple economic logic. When personal AI is rarely consulted ( p ≈ 0), its influence on the final decision is negligible, so the enterprise has little incentive to offset it, implying ∆ ≈ 0. As the adoption probability increases, the expected weight placed on the personal AI signal rises, strengthening the enterprise’s incentive to strategically counteract that signal. In the limit as p → 1, personal AI is always consulted, and the counteraction intensity converges to rP /r E , the relative perceived precision of the two signals. This ratio increases in rP and decreases in rE .

Hump-shaped in t. The non-monotone relationship between counteraction intensity and the relative trust ratio t is perhaps the most striking result. The key tension arises because the enterprise issues a single recommendation that must serve both contingencies: the human may or may not consult personal AI. When t is small, personal AI’s influence is minimal, so there is little need for strategic response. As t increases, personal AI carries greater weight in the adoption case, strengthening the enterprise’s incentive to counteract. However, the enterprise faces a trade-off: any distortion designed to offset personal AI in the adoption case also distorts the recommendation in the non-adoption case, where no offsetting personal AI recommendation is present. As t grows large, the marginal gain from counteraction in the adoption case is increasingly outweighed by the loss inflicted on the non-adoption case. In the limit as t → ∞ , personal AI dominates the aggregation whenever it is consulted, rendering counteraction ineffective in that case while still damaging the non-adoption case — so the enterprise optimally chooses not to distort at all. Strategic counteraction is maximized at an intermediate level of relative trust. At this point, personal AI is sufficiently influential under adoption to justify distortion, yet not so dominant that counteraction becomes ineffective. The maximizer t∗ = 1 /√1 − p is strictly increasing in adoption rate p. The intuition is that when adoption is widespread, the adoption case carries greater weight in the enterprise’s objective, so the trade-off tips in favor of counteraction even against a highly trusted personal AI. This non-monotonicity implies that strategic distortion is strongest not when personal AI is weak (little to offset) nor when it is dominant (counteraction is ineffective), but at an intermediate level of trust — and this level increases with adoption rate.

# 5 Trust as a Strategic Instrument 

The baseline analysis characterizes how the enterprise strategically distorts its recommendation, taking the human’s trust parameters as given. We now introduce a second strategic margin by endogenizing trust itself. 11 In many decision environments, enterprises can influence how much weight users assign to their recommendations through investments in reputation, transparency, branding, or interface design. Trust is therefore not merely an exogenous parameter, but a strategic choice variable. Moreover, in some practical settings, enterprises may face constraints that limit the extent to which they can freely distort recommendation content due to regulatory, ethical, or reputational considerations. In such environments, influencing trust becomes an especially important channel of strategic response.

Here, α/ (1+ rP ) measures the relative effectiveness of trust investment across regimes: it scales the baseline deviation by how much more performance improves when users do not rely on personal AI compared to when they do.
