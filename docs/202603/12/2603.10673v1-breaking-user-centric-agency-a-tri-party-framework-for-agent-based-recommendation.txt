## Abstract 

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have stimulated growing interest in agent-based recommender systems, enabling language-driven interaction and reasoning for more expressive pref-erence modeling. However, most existing agentic approaches re-main predominantly user-centric, treating items as passive enti-ties and neglecting the interests of other critical stakeholders. This limitation exacerbates exposure concentration and long-tail under-representation, threatening long-term system sustainability. In this work, we identify this fundamental limitation and propose the first 

Tri -party LLM-agent Rec ommendation framework (TriRec) that explicitly coordinates user utility, item exposure, and platform-level fairness. The framework employs a two-stage architecture: Stage 1 empowers item agents with personalized self-promotion to improve matching quality and alleviate cold-start barriers, while Stage 2 uses a platform agent for sequential multi-objective re-ranking, balancing user relevance, item utility, and exposure fairness. Ex-periments on multiple benchmarks show consistent gains in accu-racy, fairness, and item-level utility. Moreover, we find that item self-promotion can simultaneously enhance fairness and effective-ness, challenging the conventional trade-off assumption between relevance and fairness. Our code is available at https://github.com/Marfekey/TriRec.

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable ca-pabilities across a wide range of domains [16, 24, 25, 42], fundamen-tally reshaping how intelligent decision-making systems reason [31, 48] and interact with users [23, 47]. In recommender systems, these advances have stimulated growing interest in LLM-based agent paradigms, where agents model user behaviors through language-driven interaction and reasoning [4, 38], enabling more expres-sive and interpretable preference representations compared to tra-ditional collaborative filtering approaches [2, 27, 37].  

> arXiv:2603.10673v1 [cs.IR] 11 Mar 2026 Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Gong et al.

However, despite these promising developments, most existing agent-based recommender methods largely mirror human-centered decision-making patterns [14, 44] and adopt a predominantly user-centric modeling paradigm [17, 26, 28, 34]. They primarily opti-mize user utility while treating items as passive functional entities [35, 40], thereby failing to adequately account for the interests of other critical stakeholders, such as content providers and platform operators [3, 23, 29, 39]. Such a user-centric decision paradigm is inherently unsustainable in multi-sided marketplaces. In particu-lar, the pervasive Matthew effect concentrates exposure on already popular items [9], leaving long-tail creators with limited visibil-ity [11, 46]. This imbalance weakens creator incentives, leading to creator churn and a subsequent decline in high-quality content production [7]. Moreover, excessive optimization for short-term user engagement exacerbates a winner-take-all dynamic, gradu-ally homogenizing the content pool. Ultimately, this erosion of supply-side diversity undermines user experience and harms the platform’s long-term ecosystem health. Although some recent methods introduce item agents or con-tent agents [15, 36, 38, 39], these agents typically function only as information carriers or attribute descriptors. Their role remains largely auxiliary, serving to better satisfy user preferences rather than actively pursuing reasonable exposure or long-term benefits on behalf of the items themselves. To address this limitation at its root, we propose a Tri -party LLM-agent Rec ommendation framework (TriRec) that explicitly aligns the interests of users, items, and the platform, as illustrated in Figure 1. The framework adopts a disentangled two-stage archi-tecture that preserves user experience while empowering items with agency to actively compete for exposure through personal-ized self-promotion. At the platform level, global ranking is fur-ther regulated by incorporating system-level constraints, ensuring long-term fairness and ecosystem stability. Stage 1: Generative item self-promotion. We empower item agents with the capability to actively compete for visibility. Items are no longer passive candidates; instead, they become active participants by generating personalized promotion tailored to the target user’s interest preference. For example, the same CD player might em-phasize “high audio fidelity” to musicians, “popular tracks” to stu-dents, and “easy audio playback” to seniors. This mechanism not only improves matching quality but also provides long-tail items with opportunities to gain exposure. Stage 2: Platform-led multi-objective re-ranking. After Stage 1 generates a high-quality candidate list, the platform agent performs sequential re-ranking to balance multiple objectives. Specifically, for each candidate item, the agent considers immediate user rele-vance, platform-level fairness and expected item utility. The plat-form agent makes ranking decisions one position at a time, this sequential strategy ensures that ranking choices consider both cur-rent item exposure and long-term fairness. Notably, incorporating item self-promotion led to an increase in both the average exposure and click-through probability of items, while simultaneously improving platform-level fairness. At the same time, the introduction of personalized persuasive content enhanced recommendation accuracy on the user side. These results demon-strate that our framework can simultaneously support the objec-tives of users, items, and the platform. Our main contributions are as follows: 

• We identify fundamental limitations of user-centric modeling in recommender systems and introduce the first tri-party LLM-agent recommendation framework that explicitly coordinates users, items, and the platform through agent-based regulation of exposure and fairness. 

• We propose a two-stage pipeline consisting of (i) item-side per-sonalized self-promotion to endow items with agency and alle-viate cold-start effects, and (ii) platform-led multi-objective re-ranking with exposure modulation to balance tri-party interests. 

• Extensive experiments on public datasets demonstrate consis-tent improvements in accuracy, group fairness, and item-level utility, and further show that item self-promotion can simulta-neously enhance fairness and effectiveness, challenging the con-ventional trade-off assumption. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 LLM-based Agentic Recommendation 

The remarkable reasoning capabilities of LLMs have fundamen-tally reshaped intelligent decision-making, stimulating a surge of interest in LLM-based agent paradigms within recommender sys-tems [12, 23]. Unlike traditional filtering, these agents model com-plex behaviors through language-driven reasoning, enabling more expressive preference representations [39, 43]. Within this research frontier, a prominent direction utilizes agents as high-fidelity surrogates to simulate human behavior [3, 42]. Frame-works like Agent4Rec [37] and DyTA4Rec [28] incorporate socio-logical traits and dynamic profile updates to capture user interest evolution. To ensure long-term sustainability, the PUMA frame-work [45] enables efficient migration of personalized prompt as-sets across evolving model architectures. Beyond simulation, agents function as intelligent interfaces to improve decision quality through deliberate reasoning [14, 26, 34]. Methods such as STARec [29] and ReasonRec [40] incorporate reasoning mechanisms to mitigate hal-lucinations and enhance robustness, while LatentR3 [41] further optimizes efficiency by utilizing reinforced latent tokens instead of explicit text generation. Meanwhile, decentralized multi-agent architectures decompose complex tasks into specialized roles or model bidirectional dynam-ics [17, 27, 36]. Frameworks like AgentCF [39] and Rec4Agentverse [38] introduce item agents to interact autonomously with user agents for preference refinement. To address the long-term impact of these interactions, the bi-learning planner [20] coordinates macro-level guidance for sustained engagement. However, existing multi-agent paradigms primarily focus on maximizing one-sided user utility while neglecting the interests of the items. In this work, we pro-pose a novel framework that explicitly models item agents and equips them with the ability to advocate for their own visibility, while maintaining alignment with user preferences. 

## 2.2 Creator-side Recommendation 

User-centric recommendation often causes most exposure to go to a few popular items, leaving many creators and their content invisi-ble. This lack of visibility hinder the creator experience, ultimately Breaking User-Centric Agency: A Tri-Party Framework for Agent-Based Recommendation Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY 

leading to a decline in content production and platform engage-ment [6, 7]. Early methodologies primarily utilized co-learning frame-works to synchronize user and provider representations [6], or de-veloped specific mechanisms like PRINCE [13] to provide counter-factual interpretability for the provider side. Recently, researchers have explored ”mirroring” user-centric tech-niques, with models such as DualRec [7] treating items as active queries to identify suitable users and tackle the user availability challenge in dual-target optimization. Concurrently, generative ap-proaches such as HLLM-Creator [5] have begun leveraging LLMs to produce personalized creative content, thereby enhancing item appeal through automated content augmentation. FlyThinker [22] further advances this by enabling reasoning and generation to pro-ceed concurrently, allowing for the dynamic guidance of long-form personalized responses. However, these frameworks remain view-ing creators as passive targets for algorithmic optimization or static subjects of content generation and lack the explicit modeling of autonomous agency. Different from these passive approaches, our work empowers creators with agentic capabilities, enabling tai-lored promotion of their content to users. 

## 2.3 Platform Fair Ranking 

The pervasive Matthew effect in recommendation leads to expo-sure concentration, leaving vast long-tail products invisible and causing cold-start problems [21, 32]. Consequently, platforms must implement systematic interventions to reconcile the trade-off be-tween accuracy and multi-stakeholder fairness [30]. Classical re-search addresses this by formulating exposure allocation as a con-strained optimization problem [19, 21] or by learning debiased rep-resentations [18, 30] to neutralize provider-side biases. Beyond static debiasing, SPRec [9] introduces self-play mechanisms to iteratively mitigate filter bubbles and improve fairness without additional data. Another dominant paradigm utilizes post-hoc re-ranking to achieve equitable resource distribution via social welfare criteria. To ac-count for temporal dynamics, models like LTP-MMF [33] intro-duce mechanisms to mitigate the deleterious feedback loop effect. Similarly, the bi-learning planner [20] utilizes LLMs to manage macro-level guidance for optimizing long-term user engagement. Recently, multi-agent social choice frameworks, such as SCRUF-D [1], introduce agents to arbitrate conflicting fairness criteria. While these methods move toward decentralized decision-making, they struggle to perceive fine-grained item-level signals. In this work, we formulate platform regulation as an agentic re-ranking prob-lem that enables adaptive coordination of relevance, fairness, and item survival objectives. 

## 3 Problem Formulation 

In this section, we formally define the recommendation problem addressed by TriRec from a multi-stakeholder and agentic perspec-tive. Different from conventional single-objective recommendation settings that mainly optimize user relevance, our framework ex-plicitly considers the potentially conflicting objectives of users, items, and the platform. We first introduce stakeholder-specific utility functions to characterize their respective optimization goals. Based on these formulations, we then describe how the overall recom-mendation process is decomposed into a two-stage agentic pipeline, where preference-oriented interaction modeling and platform-level exposure regulation are handled in a decoupled yet coordinated manner. Finally, we present the agent-based preference interaction mechanism that serves as the foundation of Stage 1 candidate con-struction. 

## 3.1 Multi-Stakeholder Utility Modeling 

While agent-based interaction enables flexible modeling of user preferences, real-world recommendation systems inherently involve multiple stakeholders with competing objectives. To formalize this multi-stakeholder setting, we define utility functions for users, items, and the platform, which together characterize the overall optimiza-tion target of the recommendation process.

Based on the above utility definitions, the overall recommen-dation process in TriRec is realized through a two-stage agentic pipeline. Specifically, Stage 1 focuses on constructing relevance-oriented candidate rankings by modeling fine-grained user–item semantic alignment via agent-based interaction and personalized item self-promotion. This stage produces a high quality preference backbone without introducing exposure regulation. Stage 2 then operates on top of the Stage 1 outputs by treat-ing recommendation as a sequential control problem. This decoupled design enables TriRec to separate semantic pref-erence modeling from long-term exposure regulation, while main-taining a closed-loop decision process that dynamically adapts to evolving system states. 

## 3.2 Agent-based Preference Interaction 

Following AgentCF [39], we adopt an agent-based recommenda-tion paradigm to model fine-grained user–item preference align-ment. AgentCF-style interaction training is used to construct and re-fine preference representations for both users and items. Through offline replay of historical user–item interaction episodes in train-ing phase, user agents gradually accumulate preference memory, while item agents build item-side semantic memory that captures their appeal patterns across different user groups. These interaction-induced preference states are stored in internal memory modules and reused during inference for contextualized decision making. Formally, let 𝒰 denote the set of user agents and ℐ denote the set of item agents. Each user agent 𝑢 ∈ 𝒰 maintains a preference memory ℳ𝑢 , which is summarized into a user interest representa-tion z𝑢 . Each item agent 𝑖 ∈ ℐ maintains an item-side memory ℳ𝑖 

together with structured item metadata representation x𝑖 .Given a user agent 𝑢 and a candidate item set 𝒞 𝑢 = {𝑐 1, … , 𝑐 𝑛 },the goal of Stage 1 is to generate a relevance-oriented ranking list by modeling semantic interactions between agents. The user agent evaluates candidate items based on their gen-erated semantic descriptions and its preference memory to form ranking decisions. This interaction process is implemented through a fixed LLM-based inference function: 

𝒴 (1) 𝑢 = 𝑓 LLM (𝑢, 𝒞 𝑢 ), (3) where 𝑓 LLM (⋅) denotes the agent interaction module that outputs an ordered list 𝒴 (1) 𝑢 via multi-round semantic reasoning. Consistent with AgentCF, we keep the underlying LLM param-eters frozen during both interaction training and inference. The ranking behavior is therefore governed by structured interaction protocols and contextual reasoning over agent memory states, rather than gradient-based parameter updates. In our framework, we further extend this interaction layer by enabling item agents to actively generate user-conditioned self-promotion, which will be formally described in the following sec-tion.

Exposure as a Control State. This design prioritizes user relevance at top-ranked positions while gradually injecting platform-level regulation toward lower ranks, enabling a controllable relevance–fairness trade-off.

This formulation allows the platform agent to amplify under-exposed items with high potential value while suppressing over-saturated content, thereby mitigating long-term exposure concen-tration.

## 5.2 Overall Performance Comparison (RQ1) 

To answer RQ1, we evaluate the performance of our proposed TriRec framework against several state-of-the-art baselines across four diverse datasets. Table 2 reports the overall comparison results across four datasets. From the results, we observe that TriRec con-sistently achieves strong performance across user-side relevance, platform-level fairness, and item-side expected utility. 

5.2.1 User-side accuracy. In terms of ranking effectiveness, TriRec achieves the best or second-best NDCG and MRR scores across all four datasets. Consistent improvements are observed on CDs & Vinyl, Movies & TV, and Steam Games, indicating that the pro-posed framework does not sacrifice relevance performance when introducing item-side interaction and platform-aware regulation. This improvement can be attributed to the self-promotion mech-anism in Stage 1, which provides richer semantic matching sig-nals and enables the user agent to make more informed preference judgments. Moreover, compared with creator-oriented approaches, TriRec also demonstrates superior ranking accuracy, suggesting that allowing items to actively communicate personalized informa-tion is more effective than purely representation-based modeling. 

5.2.2 Platform-level fairness. Regarding exposure fairness, TriRec consistently outperforms most baselines across datasets. Notably, on Goodreads YA and Steam Games, TriRec achieves lower distri-butional and group-level unfairness, demonstrating its effective-ness in alleviating exposure concentration. Compared with centralized post-processing re-ranking methods (e.g., SCRUF-D) that rely on static fairness heuristics, TriRec achieves a more favorable relevance–fairness trade-off. This result indicates that incorporating fairness objectives into a sequential and state-aware ranking policy enables finer-grained exposure regulation. Furthermore, relative to market-level balancing approaches, TriRec reduces unfairness without noticeably degrading ranking accuracy, highlighting the advantage of integrating platform regulation into the agent-based pipeline. 

5.2.3 Item-side expected utility. TriRec consistently achieves the highest item expected utility across all four datasets. These gains demonstrate the effectiveness of combining item self-promotion with exposure-aware re-ranking. By allowing item agents to actively present personalized con-tent and incorporating exposure saturation signals into ranking decisions, TriRec improves long-term item-side utility without re-lying on popularity-driven exposure amplification. This is partic-ularly beneficial for long-tail items, which typically suffer from limited visibility under user-centric recommendation paradigms. 

5.2.4 Cross-domain consistency. Across all four datasets spanning e-commerce, entertainment, and social reading domains, TriRec ex-hibits stable performance improvements. Although absolute met-ric values vary due to domain characteristics and interaction spar-sity, the relative advantages of TriRec remain consistent. This sug-gests that the proposed framework generalizes well across hetero-geneous recommendation scenarios. 

5.2.5 Discussion. Overall, the results of RQ1 demonstrate that TriRec achieves a more balanced optimization outcome than existing agent-based and fairness-aware baselines. Unlike purely user-centric agent models, TriRec explicitly models item-side utility and platform-level exposure dynamics. At the same time, compared with cen-tralized re-ranking approaches, TriRec preserves strong relevance performance by maintaining a high-quality relevance backbone in Stage 1. These findings validate the effectiveness of the proposed tri-party coordination design in jointly improving recommenda-tion accuracy, mitigating exposure imbalance, and enhancing item-side utility.

5.4.4 Summary. Overall, 𝛼 max functions as an effective control pa-rameter for regulating the platform agent’s intervention behavior. ## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we proposed TriRec, a tri-party LLM-agent recom-mendation framework that explicitly coordinates users, items, and the platform, addressing the limitations of user-centric agentic rec-ommendation paradigms. TriRec adopts a two-stage design: item agents generate personalized self-promotion content to enhance semantic matching, while a platform agent performs sequential multi-objective re-ranking to jointly optimize relevance, item util-ity, and exposure fairness. Extensive experiments on four public benchmarks demonstrate that TriRec consistently improves ranking accuracy, exposure fair-ness, and item-side utility. Notably, item self-promotion simulta-neously benefits both item exposure and user-side effectiveness, challenging the conventional trade-off between relevance and fair-ness. Our results highlight the importance of explicitly modeling multi-stakeholder objectives for sustainable recommender systems and show that platform-aware agent coordination provides a practi-cal path beyond user-centric optimization. Future work will ex-plore richer platform objectives, such as creator lifecycle modeling. We also aim to investigate more advanced agent-level interaction mechanisms, including negotiation, coalition formation, and long-horizon strategic planning, to enable more adaptive and coopera-tive multi-agent behavior.
