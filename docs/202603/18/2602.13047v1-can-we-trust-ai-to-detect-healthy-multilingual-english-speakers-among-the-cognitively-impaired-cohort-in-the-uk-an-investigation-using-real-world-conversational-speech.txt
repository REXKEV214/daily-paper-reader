Abstract —Objective: Conversational speech often reveals early signs of cognitive decline, such as dementia and mild cognitive impairment (MCI). Numerous recent studies have demonstrated the high performance of AI models in detecting Alzheimer’s dementia among monolingual speakers in their native languages. In the UK, one in four people belongs to an ethnic minor-ity, and dementia prevalence is expected to rise most rapidly among Black and Asian communities. This study examines the trustworthiness of AI models, specifically the presence of bias, in detecting healthy multilingual English speakers among the cognitively impaired cohort, to make these tools clinically beneficial. Approach: For experiments, monolingual participants were recruited nationally (UK), and multilingual speakers were enrolled from four community centres in Sheffield and Bradford, a total of 1,395 participants contributing over 263 hours of speech. In addition to a non-native English accent, multilinguals spoke Somali, Chinese, or South Asian languages (Hindi, Urdu, Punjabi, Mirpuri, Arabic, etc.), who were further divided into two Yorkshire accents (West and South) to challenge the efficiency of the AI tools thoroughly. Results: Although ASR systems (Whisper, Wav2Vec 2.0, and NeMo) showed no significant bias across groups, classification and regression models using acoustic and linguistic features exhibited bias against multilingual speakers, particularly in memory, fluency, and reading tasks. This bias was more pronounced when models were trained on the publicly avail-able DementiaBank dataset. Moreover, multilinguals were more likely to be misclassified as having cognitive decline. Significance: 

This study is the first of its kind to discover that, despite their strong overall performance, current AI models show bias against multilingual individuals from ethnic minority backgrounds in the UK, and they are also more likely to misclassify speakers with a certain accent (South Yorkshire) as living with a more severe cognitive decline. In this pilot study, we conclude that the existing AI tools are therefore not yet reliable for diagnostic use in these populations, and we aim to address this in future work by developing more generalisable, bias-mitigated models. 

Index Terms —AI, dementia, MCI, cognitive decline, linguistics, speech, WER, ASR 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Cognitive and memory difficulties may arise from factors such as fatigue, stress, and illness, often intensifying with age. When persistent, these symptoms may indicate mild cognitive impairment (MCI) [1], a condition involving deficits in mem-ory, learning, attention, reasoning, language, and motivation [2]. Approximately half of individuals with MCI progress to dementia, the most common of which is Alzheimer’s disease (AD) [3]–[6]. While early detection facilitates interventions that may delay disease progression [7], current diagnostic approaches, including neurological evaluations, historical cog-nitive assessments [8], magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), standardised testing, and cerebrospinal fluid analysis [9], are resource-intensive and unsuitable for large-scale screening [10]. Consequently, there is a growing need for remote, intelligent technologies to enhance diagnostic efficiency and support healthcare delivery [11]. Speech and language impairments are well-established hall-marks of AD and can help differentiate between subjective and pathological cognitive decline [12]. Features such as reduced lexical diversity, increased repetition, simplified language, and prolonged pauses have been linked to early stages of decline and underlying tau pathology [13], [14]. Importantly, these linguistic markers provide a non-invasive and easily obtainable indicator of cognitive status, which can be automatically anal-ysed using state-of-the-art artificial intelligence (AI) methods without the need for direct clinician involvement [15], [16]. For automatic systems to be clinically effective in health-care settings such as the National Health Service (NHS) in the United Kingdom (UK), they must be robust across all demographic groups, including ethnic minority populations. Notably, dementia prevalence is projected to rise most sig-nificantly among ethnic minority populations [17]–[20], who make up one in four people, and where multilingualism is

common in the UK [21]. Individuals in these groups, par-ticularly those who speak English as an additional language, face added barriers to dementia diagnosis [22], contributing to the underrepresentation of Black and South Asian patients in NHS memory assessment services [23]. This underlines a key limitation of existing automatic systems for cognitive impairment detection, and, to the best of our knowledge, the present study is the first to explicitly address this challenge. We have recently developed CognoMemory [24], for-merly CognoSpeak [25], an innovative online, language-based screening tool for the at-home detection of cognitive decline. By leveraging both acoustic and linguistic features, as demon-strated in our previous work [26], [27], CognoMemory is designed to accelerate the identification of early cognitive impairment. The clinical utility of CognoMemory critically depends on its diagnostic accuracy across diverse populations, particularly multilingual individuals within ethnic minority groups. In this pilot study, we therefore examine the trustwor-thiness of AI models within CognoMemory, with a specific fo-cus on detecting and quantifying potential bias between mono-lingual and multilingual participants in the UK. To address this overarching research question, we investigate the subquestions, which are 1) Do traditional automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems perform equally well for monolingual and multilingual participants? 2) Do classification models achieve comparable performance across monolingual and multilingual data? 3) Are predicted cognitive scores, such as the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), systematically higher for multilingual participants? 4) What insights can qualitative analysis provide regarding the presence and nature of bias, if any? The contributions of this study lie in addressing the research questions outlined above and in our efforts to recruit partic-ipants from ethnically diverse and multilingual communities in the UK, thereby enhancing the representativeness of the CognoMemory dataset. To this end, we collaborated with four ethnic minority community centres in two cities from the UK, recruiting participants from Somali, Chinese, and South Asian groups in Sheffield, where speech was char-acterised by a South Yorkshire accent, as well as from a South Asian community in Bradford, representing the distinct West Yorkshire dialect commonly observed in both British and British Asian speakers [28], [29]. Moreover, studies confirm the existence of unique linguistic features among the multilin-gual South Asian speakers with West Yorkshire accents [30]– [33]. Given this unique observed accent, this study also aims to facilitate better accent monitoring to distinguish between South Asian multilinguals from South Yorkshire (Sheffield) and those from West Yorkshire (Bradford). Due to the lack of necessary English-speaking skills for the cognitively im-paired group, only healthy participants were available for recruitment. Section II reviews existing literature, followed by a summary of this novel data collection effort in Section III. Classifier training and evaluation for the three diagnostic categories, dementia, MCI, and healthy controls (HC), using mono and multilingual participants, are described in Section IV. These analyses first address the performance of ASR systems in Section V-A, where we observe visually noticeable but statistically non-significant differences in word error rates (WER) between monolingual and multilingual participants. Classifier performance is then compared across groups us-ing both CognoMemory, the largest dataset of its kind, and DementiaBank, the second-largest publicly available dataset, which contains only binary diagnostic labels of dementia and HC. Here, statistically significant biases are identified in both datasets, with stronger effects observed in Demen-tiaBank, addressing the second research question in Sec-tion V-B. Predicted MMSE scores further reveal statistically significant differences between monolingual and multilingual groups in Section V-C, thereby answering the third research question. Finally, qualitative analysis using Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) metrics demonstrates that participants are more likely to refer to their places of origin, and the healthy participants also mention prominent political figures like British prime ministers while answering the memory prompts, thus addressing the fourth research question in Section V-D. These findings are critically discussed in Section VI, with concluding remarks provided in Section VII. II. P REVIOUS WORK 

Recent advances in AI-based systems using speech tech-nologies relying on Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) for detecting cognitive decline in multilingual populations have emphasised system design, multilingual speech processing, and model performance. Early research predominantly focused on monolingual English datasets, but more recent studies utilise multilingual corpora such as ADReSS-M [34], TAUKA-DIAL [35], and MultiConAD [36], which include languages like English, Chinese, Greek and Spanish. The performance of the ASR systems varies among native and non-native speakers [37], and while progress has been made toward developing language-agnostic ASR-based systems capable of assessing cognitive decline across diverse populations [38]–[40], these studies generally trained and tested models on different lan-guages collected across various countries and continents. Con-sequently, they have not addressed cognitive decline detection in multilingual individuals from ethnic minority groups who speak the same language as native speakers due to a lack of data [41], a gap this study aims to fill. Building on this motivation, we leverage the CognoMemory dataset, which is nearly six times larger than DementiaBank and includes 14 diverse question prompts beyond the widely used Cookie Theft (CT) picture description task, along with three diagnostic labels: dementia, MCI, and HC. The clinical relevance of CognoMemory, its data collection protocol, and our special feature-fusion and transfer learning architectures have been described in previous work [26], [27]. A. Data collection 

CognoMemory employs a virtual agent to administer 14 clinically validated, memory-probing questions, covering mo-tivation ( Q1), qualifying memory ( Q2) [42], concern as-sessment (Q3) [43], memory recall (Q4–Q8) [44], [45], cognitive functioning ( Q9) [46], fluency ( Q10 –Q11 ) [47], picture description ( Q12 –Q13 ) [48], and reading tasks ( Q14 )[49] in a fixed order [27]. Multilingual participants completed these assessments either at home or, with support from a research champion, in community centres, where South Asian populations represent 31% of Bradford’s residents and 7% in Sheffield [21]. The inclusion and exclusion criteria are summarised in Table I. During data collection, several challenges were encountered. First, there was a scarcity of older ethnic minority partici-pants with sufficient English proficiency to interact effectively with the CognoMemory agent. Second, technological barriers, such as unreliable internet connections at home or within community centres, limited participants’ ability to complete the assessments. Finally, recording artefacts, including back-ground noise or additional speech, were common because assessments conducted in communal spaces often required as-sistance from healthcare workers. Initially, recruitment focused on multilingual adults over the age of 50 years, to better align with the typical age range of patients referred to memory assessment services with functional cognitive disorder (FCD), MCI, or dementia [50], [51]. However, due to the relatively younger demographic profiles of some community centres, and limited English proficiency among older members, the inclusion criteria were later expanded to allow healthy adults of all ages. Importantly, all multilingual participants with cognitive im-pairment lacked the English skills necessary to complete the CognoMemory assessments, resulting in the recruitment of only healthy individuals in these cohorts. In addition to Cog-noMemory, participants also completed pen-and-paper cogni-tive assessments, including the Montreal Cognitive Assess-ment (MoCA), which was subsequently converted to MMSE scores [52].

The final datasets are presented in Table II, and no statistically significant differences were found between the younger English and the four multilingual test sets. IV. E XPERIMENTAL SETUP 

To address the primary research question on the trustworthi-ness of AI models, and in light of the absence of cognitively impaired participants in the multilingual group, we employed four complementary evaluation techniques across mono and multilingual speakers: (i) assessing ASR performance via WER, (ii) classification, (iii) regression, and (iv) qualitative analysis using TF-IDF. The corresponding feature extraction, classifier training, and evaluation process are detailed below. A. Feature Extraction and Classifier Training 

Acoustic features, such as the extended Geneva Minimal-istic Acoustic Parameter Set (eGeMAPS [58]) and The IN-TERSPEECH 2016 Computational Paralinguistics Challenge (ComParE [59], [60]) features, were extracted from the speech data, while linguistic features such as TF-IDF and Bag-of-Words (BoW) were derived from the NeMo-generated tran-scripts. These feature matrices from the audio and text domains were then fused to train a classifier (SVM) and a regressor (SVR), which had shown the best performance in our prior work [26], [27]. In addition, three LLMs (BART, RoBERTa, and DistilBERT) were fine-tuned and evaluated on the NeMo transcripts; as their individual performances were comparable in [26], we report the averaged results in Section V-B. We further note that our CognoMemory data is currently highly imbalanced (Table II), and no data balancing technique apart from adjusting the classifier weights according to the class imbalance has been adopted. 

B. Evaluation 

Table II summarises that the training set comprises par-ticipants with 56 cases of dementia, 150 MCI and 768 as HC. The six test sets contain two monolingual English speak-ers, one younger and the other older, and four multilingual speakers from four communities with two different accents, all healthy. This lack of positive cases (dementia or MCI) among multilingual participants posed a substantial challenge for standard classifier evaluation. To overcome this, the training dataset with three diagnostic labels was fixed, and a five-fold cross-validation scheme with strict no-participant overlap was applied to the remaining six datasets in Table II. For example, in the Somali group, classifiers were trained on the fixed dataset plus four folds of Somali data, and tested on the remaining fold, with the process repeated across all five folds. As each of the six test sets contained only the HC group (a single class), accuracy was reported instead of a macro F1-score as the evaluation metric [61], and final performances were averaged across folds, which are mentioned in Section V). For statistical analysis, a standard unpaired two-tailed 

t-test with a 5% significance level was applied throughout Section V to compute p-values and assess the presence of bias across AI models. Importantly, multilingual data were intentionally incorpo-rated into the training sets to enhance model robustness, improve generalisation, and reduce bias towards monolingual participants, thereby ensuring a more rigorous investigation of bias across language groups. This strategy allowed us to make the most effective use of limited multilingual data, while minimising the impact of the absence of cognitively impaired multilingual participants on our bias evaluation. V. R ESULTS 

The ASR performance, classification and regression results, along with qualitative analysis, are described in this section. 

A. Word Error Rate (WER) 

The WERs were calculated between the manual transcripts (349 participants with manual transcriptions in Table II) and ASR-generated transcripts, and are shown in Figure 2, which shows a trend of lower performance of the ASRs for fluency tasks ( Q10 , Q11 ), and the corresponding p-values (all are ≈0) suggest that these performances are significantly lower, as a higher WER indicates a lower ASR performance. For monolinguals, the p-values are 0.008, 0.896 and 0.005 for Whisper vs Wav2Vec 2.0, Whisper vs NeMo, and Wav2Vec 2.0 vs NeMo, respectively; and they are much lower ( ≈0, 0.69, ≈0) for multilingual speakers. This suggests a larger performance gap between the three ASRs for multilingual speakers than for monolinguals. The overall mean and standard deviation of WERs using all 14 questions for Whisper, Wav2Vec 2.0 and NeMo are 0.41 ±0.11, 0.57 ±0.15 and 0.40 ±0.11, respectively, as de-scribed in Table III. The p-values are ≈0, 0.67, and ≈0, for Whisper vs Wav2Vec 2.0, Whisper vs NeMo, and Wav2Vec 2.0 vs NeMo, respectively; demonstrating the performances of Whisper and NeMo are not significantly different, whereas the performance of Wav2Vec 2.0 is significantly lower. It also indicates that overall NeMo is the ASR of choice while transcribing speech to text automatically, due to its marginally better standard deviation over Whisper. Table III also points out an overall worse performance of the Whisper and Wav2Vec 2.0 for Somali multilingual speakers, whereas NeMo has been consistent over all language groups. We have also experimentally discovered that although the WERs are marginally higher for the dementia group (monolin-gual) and the Somali group (multilingual), the WERs from all seven groups in Table III were not statistically significantly dif-ferent from each other, as p-values were much higher, ranging between 0.2 and 0.7. The overall p-value of 0.1386 suggests no significant differences between the ASR performances of mono and multilingual English speakers across the UK. This suggests that the dementia corpus contains more distinctive and comparatively more influential words within its top-10 TF-IDF terms than the other two corpora. VI. D ISCUSSION 

We implemented four complementary approaches to inves-tigate potential bias in AI models, with results summarised in Section V. When evaluating ASR performance using WER, we found that NeMo performed marginally better than Whisper, while both substantially outperformed Wav2Vec 2.0. Unpaired two-tailed t-tests confirmed that WERs did not differ signifi-cantly between monolingual and multilingual participants, sug-gesting that ASRs exhibit limited bias and perform comparably well across groups. However, ASR performance declined con-siderably on fluency tasks, where speech was less spontaneous, a finding consistent with prior studies [63], [64] and one that warrants further investigation. While fine-tuning could reduce WERs, we intentionally refrained in this pilot study to first assess the inherent performance and bias of off-the-shelf ASR models. Classifier analysis revealed that models leveraging linguis-tic features achieved higher accuracy but displayed greater bias towards monolingual speakers, particularly in memory, fluency, and reading tasks. Accents, such as South York-shire, were more frequently misclassified as indicative of more severe cognitive decline, including dementia. Cross-dataset experiments further showed that bias exists in both CognoMemory and DementiaBank; however, the extent var-ied. Three-class classifiers trained on acoustic features from CognoMemory showed no evidence of bias, whereas two-class classifiers trained on DementiaBank data did. This discrepancy may stem from CognoMemory’s larger scale and broader demographic representation across the UK. Regression analysis revealed higher RMSE values for MMSE predictions in the multilingual group, further high-lighting systematic bias, consistent with findings from previous work [65]. VII. C ONCLUSION 

This study evaluates the effectiveness and trustworthiness of AI-based models, including traditional classifiers (e.g., SVM), large language models (LLMs), and regressors (SVR), in detecting healthy multilingual English speakers among the cognitively impaired cohort in the UK using acoustic and linguistic features. Monolingual English participants were recruited nationwide, while multilingual speakers, who also speak Somali, Chinese, or South Asian (Urdu, Hindi, Punjabi, Mirpuri, Arabic), were recruited from Sheffield and Bradford. South Asian participants were further grouped by their accent: South Yorkshire (Sheffield) and West Yorkshire (Bradford). Given that multilingual ethnic minority groups, who make up one in four UK residents, are projected to experience the greatest rise in dementia prevalence, we recruited 1,395 participants who responded to English-language prompts de-livered by a virtual agent via the CognoMemory system, contributing over 263 hours of audio data. Off-the-shelf ASR models (Whisper, Wav2Vec 2.0, and NeMo) were used without fine-tuning to calculate WERs, and an unpaired two-tailed t-test indicated no significant bias between monolingual and multilingual speakers at the ASR level. Both acoustic and linguistic features were used for three-way classification (de-mentia, MCI, and HC) using SVM and LLM-based models. While acoustic features yielded lower overall accuracy, they exhibited no prompt-related bias. In contrast, linguistic fea-tures, though highly accurate, showed consistent bias favouring monolingual speakers, particularly in memory, fluency, and reading tasks. Notably, South Asian multilinguals with South Yorkshire accents were more likely to be misclassified as having severe cognitive decline. Training on public datasets like DementiaBank amplified these biases. RMSE values for MMSE score prediction were also higher for multilingual participants. Qualitative analysis, such as TF-IDF measures, revealed subtle linguistic patterns tied to speakers’ countries of origin. While other studies have explored cognitive decline detection in multiple languages, this study is the first to comprehensively assess the bias present in AI models in a UK-based multilingual, ethnic minority population. We conclude that, despite strong performance in native English speakers, current speech-based AI systems demonstrate significant bias against multilingual individuals, limiting their reliability for diagnostic use in diverse populations. Future work will address these limitations and enhance the clinical relevance by recruiting cognitively impaired multi-lingual participants and developing more generalisable, bias-mitigated models to improve diagnostic reliability across di-verse groups, along with more rigorous experiments with explainable features such as TF-IDF and collecting cultural background information. Planned efforts include fine-tuning ASR systems, conducting detailed feature-level bias analy-ses, and incorporating additional culturally sensitive screening tools such as the Rowland Universal Dementia Assessment Scale (RUDAS), Multicultural Cognitive Examination (MCE), and Addenbrooke’s Cognitive Examination (ACE).
