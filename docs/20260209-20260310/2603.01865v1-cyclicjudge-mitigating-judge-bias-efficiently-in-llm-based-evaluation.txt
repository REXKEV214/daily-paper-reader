# CyclicJudge: Mitigating Judge Bias Efficiently in LLM-based Evaluation

Abstract 

LLM-as-judge evaluation has become standard practice for open-ended model assessment; however, judges exhibit systematic biases that cannot be eliminated by increasing the number of scenarios or generations. These biases are often similar in magnitude to the model differ-ences that benchmarks are designed to detect, resulting in unreliable rankings when single-judge evaluations are used. This work intro-duces a variance decomposition that partitions benchmark score variance into scenario, gener-ation, judge, and residual components. Based on this analysis, CyclicJudge , a round-robin assignment of judges, is demonstrated to be the optimal allocation strategy. It eliminates bias precisely while requiring each judge only once per cycle, maintaining the cost of single-judge evaluation. Empirical validation on MT-Bench supports all theoretical predictions. 

1 Introduction 

LLM-as-judge evaluation has become the de facto standard for open-ended model assessment (Zheng et al., 2023). A growing body of work documents that LLM judges are systematically biased: some models are consistently lenient, others strict, with additional position, verbosity, and self-preference effects (Zheng et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). A standard ANOVA on our data con-firms that judge main effect is highly significant for all evaluated models (§4.2). Crucially, systematic bias does not average away. Adding more test scenarios or regenerating re-sponses reduces random noise but leaves judge bias intact. The only way to eliminate judge bias is to use multiple judges whose biases cancel. Yet run-ning every judge on every item multiplies evalua-tion cost by the panel size and sacrifices generation diversity at a fixed budget. We show that CyclicJudge , a round-robin as-signment of judges to items, resolves this tension. To establish this formally, we develop a variance decomposition rooted in generalizability theory (Brennan, 2001; Shavelson and Webb, 1991) that separates benchmark score variance into scenario, generation, judge, and residual components. Our contributions are: 1. A mixed-effects model that decomposes bench-mark variance into random noise and systematic judge bias, showing they require fundamentally different remedies (§3.1–3.2). 2. A proof that round-robin cycling dominates both all-judges and random-judge strategies: it elim-inates bias while maximizing generation diver-sity at any budget (§3.3). 3. Empirical validation confirming all theoretical predictions match subsampling variance (§4). 

2 Related Work 

LLM judge biases and multi-judge approaches. 

LLM judges exhibit systematic position bias (Wang et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024), length bias (Dubois et al., 2024), self-preference (Wataoka et al., 2024), and cognitive biases (Koo et al., 2024); see Gu et al. (2024) for a comprehensive survey. Multi-judge panels reduce bias through cancellation (Verga et al., 2024), and psychometric reliability analy-sis shows single-shot evaluations are unreliable (Schroeder and Wood-Doughty, 2024). 

Budget-constrained evaluation. Saha et al. (2026) derives optimal allocation of judge queries using multi-armed bandit theory, Luettgau et al. (2025) develops hierarchical Bayesian models with partial pooling for low-data regimes, and Miller (2024) provides formulas for standard errors and power analysis. Dorner et al. (2025) establishes fundamental impossibility results for judge-based evaluation. These works optimize allocation across items or model uncertainty within levels, but do not address the judges-versus-generations tradeoff. 1

4 Experiments 

4.1 Domain and Data 

We apply our methodology to MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023), a multi-turn conversational bench-mark with 80 two-turn questions spanning 8 cate-gories. We evaluate three models—Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct (Yang et al., 2024), Llama 3.3 70B Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024), and GPT-5.2—generating 

m = 10 independent conversations per scenario. Each generation is scored by Ktot = 5 LLM judges (Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct, Llama 3.3 70B Instruct, GPT-5.2, Gemini 3 Flash, Claude Sonnet 4.6) using the official single-answer grading prompt (scores 1–10 per turn, averaged). Three of the five judges coincide with the evaluated models, enabling direct observation of self-preference effects. This yields a fully crossed design with 4,000 scores per model, 12,000 total. Variance components are estimated via crossed ANOVA (Appendix B) with 95% boot-strap CIs (2,000 scenario-level resamples).

5 Conclusion 

Systematic judge bias is a persistent property of LLM evaluators that does not diminish with more scenarios or generations. The conventional multi-judge approach, in which every judge scores every item, significantly increases costs and reduces gen-eration diversity. CyclicJudge addresses this challenge by employ-ing a straightforward round-robin assignment, en-suring that biases are neutralized as each judge eval-uates an equal number of items, while maintaining the same per-item cost as single-judge evaluation. Variance decomposition demonstrates that this cy-cling approach outperforms both alternatives at all budget levels, regardless of the model’s variance profile. The method requires no model-specific tun-ing and can be implemented as a direct substitute for single-judge pipelines, providing practitioners with a cost-neutral path to more reliable LLM eval-uation. 

Limitations 

The linear random effects model is an approxi-mation for bounded ordinal outcomes; a GLMM would be more principled but complicates closed-form results. Our judge pool of Ktot = 5 limits exploration of larger panels. The “maximize sce-4narios” recommendation assumes scenarios are in-terchangeable draws from the dataset; in practice, some scenarios may be more informative than oth-ers, and item-selection methods (Maia Polo et al., 2024) could alter the tradeoff. The allocation anal-ysis assumes a balanced design; extensions to adap-tive designs remain future work. Finally, our bud-get analysis treats all judge calls as equal cost; in practice, judges vary widely in price and latency, so a cost-weighted allocation that accounts for per-model pricing would be needed for real deployment decisions.

A full crossed three-factor design has 23 = 8

terms (constant, three main effects, three two-way interactions, one three-way). Our model retains the constant and three main effects, ab-sorbing all interactions into εijℓ : (i) generations are nested within scenarios, not crossed, so the scenario ×generation “interaction” is confounded with βij ; (ii) with one score per (i, j, ℓ ) cell, the three-way interaction is aliased with the residual; (iii) the scenario ×judge interaction (αγ )iℓ , while estimable, adds n(Ktot −1) parameters and enters the allocation analysis only through the residual— which our empirical predictions (§4) capture with-out parametric assumptions. 8
