## Are LLMs Ready to Replace Bangla Annotators? 

Md. Najib Hasan 1, Touseef Hasan 1, Souvika Sarkar 11 A2I Lab, School of Computing, Wichita State University 

mxhasan39@shockers.wichita.edu, txhasan4@shockers.wichita.edu souvika.sarkar@wichita.edu 

Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increas-ingly used as automated annotators to scale dataset creation, yet their reliability as unbi-ased annotators—especially for low-resource and identity-sensitive settings—remains poorly understood. In this work, we study the behavior of LLMs as zero-shot annotators for Bangla hate speech, a task where even human agreement is challenging, and annotator bias can have serious downstream consequences. We conduct a systematic benchmark of 17 LLMs using a unified evaluation framework. Our analysis uncovers annotator bias and substantial instability in model judgments. Surprisingly, increased model scale does not guarantee improved annotation quality— smaller, more task-aligned models frequently exhibit more consistent behavior than their larger counterparts. These results highlight important limitations of current LLMs for sensitive annotation tasks in low-resource languages and underscore the need for careful evaluation before deployment. 

1 Introduction 

High-quality data annotation is a critical yet in-creasingly unsustainable bottleneck in modern nat-ural language processing (NLP) research. As on-line text continues to grow, the demand for la-beled data has far outpaced what human annota-tors can reasonably produce. Manual annotation is slow, expensive (Snow et al., 2008), and of-ten inconsistent, with even trained annotators fre-quently disagreeing (Hartvigsen et al., 2022). To address these limitations, recent work has explored the use of LLMs for large-scale data annotation (Gilardi et al., 2023). Despite their efficiency, LLM-based annotation raises a central concern: 

annotator bias (Lucy and Bamman, 2021). As LLMs are trained on massive web corpora that en-code societal stereotypes, cultural imbalances, and skewed viewpoints, they may systematically propa-gate these biases into their labeling decisions (Das et al., 2024; Bender et al., 2021). Prior studies have largely examined these issues in high-resource languages such as English (Liang et al., 2022; Gehman et al., 2020). In contrast, annotator bias in LLM-based annotation for low-resource languages (e.g., Bangla) remains largely unexplored. This gap is further exacerbated by— limited annotation guidelines, scarcity of expert an-notators, and uneven dataset quality. In this work, we investigate annotator bias in LLMs when ap-plied to zero-shot annotation of Bangla hate speech. Hate speech presents a particularly challenging and informative testbed due to its inherently subjective nature, as judgments are shaped by cultural norms, social context, and identity-specific sensitivities (Sap et al., 2019). Even subtle biases in this do-main can significantly affect annotation outcomes and downstream decisions. Rather than treating LLMs as black-box classifiers, we analyze their re-liability as unbiased annotators by examining con-sistency on identity-sensitive inputs and robust-ness across different prompting conditions. Specif-ically, we address the following research questions: 

RQ 1. Can LLMs reliably identify identity-sensitive hate speech in a low-resource language like Bangla? 

RQ 2. Can LLMs generate consistent and trustwor-thy reasoning in settings where even human anno-tators struggle to agree? To answer these questions, we make the follow-ing contributions: • We introduced a new Bangla hate speech dataset comprising 5,000 human-curated samples, span-ning 21 identity-sensitive categories (e.g., Fe-male , Hindu , Artist , etc.) which represent the individuals’ identities directly referenced in the text. These categories are grouped under five domains (e.g., Gender, Religion, Politics, etc.), enabling a structured analysis of both identity-specific bias and cross-domain generalization in

LLM-based annotation. • Proposed a novel unified evaluation framework that assesses model behavior across three dimen-sions: (i) classification performance, (ii) reason-ing consistency, and (iii) robustness to prompt variation. • Using our evaluation framework benchmarked 17 state-of-the-art LLMs for Bangla hate speech annotation, evaluating their reliability as auto-mated annotators in a low-resource language. Our results reveal a widespread bias of the anno-tator, frequent confusion of labels and strong sen-sitivity to minor variations in prompt (instruction) wording, highlighting the fundamental limitations of current LLMs as annotators. Results also show that larger models do not consistently produce more reliable annotations; in several cases, smaller and more task-aligned models exhibit greater sta-bility and coherence. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 LLMs as Automated Annotators 

LLMs are increasingly used as annotators in NLP due to their strong generalization ability and ca-pacity to generate context-aware outputs (He et al., 2023). Many studies aim to replace human anno-tators with LLMs to reduce reliance on scarce do-main experts and lower the cost and time of man-ual annotation (Zhang et al., 2023). Prior work re-ports promising results when LLMs are combined with techniques such as active learning and care-fully designed instructions (Huang et al., 2023). Prompting strategies have been explored to en-able LLMs to act as labelers for sentiment analy-sis, toxicity detection, topic classification (Gilardi et al., 2023), and zero-shot multi-label topic in-ference (Sarkar et al., 2023, 2025). Some stud-ies suggest that, with proper prompt tuning, LLMs can match or even outperform crowd workers in tasks such as stance detection and emotion label-ing (Wang et al., 2023). However, other research highlights the instability of LLM-based annotation, showing that minor prompt variations can lead to label shifts and inconsistent reasoning across similar examples (Tornberg, 2023). Additionally, LLMs often rely on surface-level cues and over-look deeper context, particularly in domains re-quiring specialized knowledge or morally sensitive judgment, which degrades performance on subjec-tive tasks such as hate speech detection and sar-casm annotation (Peng et al., 2024). 

2.2 Inconsistency in LLM-based Annotation 

Another major concern is hallucination, where LLMs fabricate labels or explanations without fac-tual grounding (Zhang et al., 2023). Bias also re-mains a persistent issue, as models trained primar-ily on public web data tend to reproduce social and cultural stereotypes, leading to distorted anno-tations (Jadhav et al., 2024). Moreover, LLMs gen-erally perform better in high-resource languages such as English, which benefit from abundant train-ing data and large web corpora (Mohta et al., 2023), while performance degrades substantially in low-resource languages including Bangla, Turkish, and Marathi (Gu et al., 2025). This disparity stems from the underrepresentation of these languages in training data (Nasution and Onan, 2024). 

2.3 Low-Resource Hate Speech Detection 

In hate speech detection, LLMs are widely used to identify harmful online content (Lucy and Bam-man, 2021). While they perform well on explicit hate, they often struggle with implicit or subtle forms (Bender et al., 2021). Annotator bias is com-mon, particularly in cases involving gender, reli-gion, or political identity (Liang et al., 2022). Even small prompt variations can lead to large output shifts without careful validation. For Bangla, transformer-based models such as BanglaHateBERT (Jahan et al., 2022), BanglaBERT (Kowsher et al., 2022), and G-BERT (Keya et al., 2023) improve hate speech detection using language-specific data but are limited by scarce annotations. Recent work evaluates LLMs on Bangla hate speech with zero-shot and few-shot prompting (Faria et al., 2024). However, performance remains unstable due to prompt sensitivity, hallucination, difficulty with Bangla-specific expressions (Das et al., 2023), and bias in identities (Das et al., 2024). These issues raise ethical concerns in hate speech detection (Sadhu et al., 2024). 

2.4 Uniqueness of Our Work 

Unlike prior studies that focus on improving model accuracy or prompt design in isolation, our work in-tegrates insights from all three research directions: LLMs as annotators, inconsistency in LLMs, and low-resource hate speech detection into a unified benchmarking framework. We systematically eval-uate 17 LLMs on Bangla hate speech annotation, spanning 21 identity-based categories across five domains using classification accuracy, reasoning alignment, and prompt stability, on a novel dataset of 5,000 text samples in Bangla. 

3 Problem Statement 

Our goal is to find a well-aligned LLM-annotator that will give an unbiased decision with neutral rea-sons, regardless of the annotator’s identity. We de-sign a framework which takes input: a text x, anno-tator’s identity g, a set of labels L, and four levels of prompt ℓ ∈ { P 1, . . . , P 4}. Each data sample also contains an annotated human gold label y and gold standard reasoning r. We change only the an-notator’s identity string denoted as g, for example, “You are a Bangla hate-speech annotator with iden-tity = Female ” (repeated with Male ). An LLM f

returns a predicted label ˆy and the reasons ˆr to sup-port that predicted label as justification: 

(ˆ y, ˆr) = f (x, g, ℓ, L )

The predicted label and the reasoning for that given sample should be the same in context if that spe-cific LLM is unbiased and insensitive to identity. For instance (as illustrated in Figure 1), the text “পাপন েক জুতা িদেয় িপিটেয় িবিসিব েথেক েবর কের েদয়া হউক ” (Let Papon be beaten with shoes and thrown out of the BCB) is given as input to an LLM with a set of labels: Offensive, Harassment, Violence, Propaganda, Not Hateful. By altering the anno-tator’s identity in the input prompt from Male to 

Female , if the LLM’s output label changes from Harassment to Not Hateful, it is an evidence of an-notator bias in that specific LLM. On the contrary, if the output label doesn’t change even after chang-ing the annotator identity, it means that the LLM is unbiased.

4.3 Prompting-based Domain Adaptation 

We use a prompt-based evaluation strategy to simulate domain adaptation for both proprietary and open-access LLMs. We design task-specific prompts in Bangla to support domain adaptation for classification, reasoning, and taxonomy-based prompt sensitivity evaluation. For each model fam-ily, we begin with the smallest variant pretrained on general-domain data and assess its performance on identity-sensitive Bangla hate speech. If the model performs below expectation, indicated by identity mismatches, low F1 scores, or unstable reasoning, we progressively scale to larger vari-ants within the same family. This stepwise strat-egy allows us to identify the smallest model ca-pable of adapting to culturally grounded Bangla texts and to evaluate whether model scaling alone improves domain-specific performance. By com-bining structured prompting with progressive scal-ing, we establish a consistent evaluation pipeline for low-resource, culturally rich language domains. 

4.4 Evaluation Framework and Metrics 

To understand how LLMs behave as annotators in a low-resource language like Bangla, we design a three-dimension evaluation framework focused on bias and model behavior. This framework as-sesses: (i) classification of collected comments considering the identity of individuals, ( ii ) the rea-soning ability to justify the predicted label, and (iii ) the sensitivity to different levels of prompts (the prompt level is decided from different levels of directives, for example, only goal directives, goal directives with detail instructions, and, in another level, the prompt contains examples with instruc-tions). This setup helps to reveal not just perfor-mance gaps but also deeper issues like bias ampli-fication, inconsistency across prompts, and unreli-able reasoning, which offer a well-rounded view of model reliability in sensitive annotation tasks. 

4.4.1 Classification of Comments (E1) 

The first dimension of our framework assesses how well each model could classify hate speech in Bangla when linked to a specific identity group. Models receive a standardized prompt framing them as expert annotators with identity cues (e.g., 

Female , Muslim , Artist ) and are asked to choose one label from a fixed label set: Harassment, Offen-sive, Violence, Harmful, Propaganda, or Not Hate-ful. We evaluate model predictions using F1 Score for label balance, Cohen’s Kappa for agreement with human annotations, and a Mismatch metric to measure differences in responses across opposing identity terms (e.g., Female vs Not Female ), high-lighting potential bias in classification. 

4.4.2 Reasoning Alignment (E2) 

In the second dimension, we evaluate whether LLMs can generate appropriate reasoning to jus-tify their predicted labels. Each LLM is provided with a comment in Bangla, its own predicted la-bel (for that given comment), and a specified iden-tity, and is asked to produce three short reasoning statements, each limited to a maximum of 10 to-kens. These generated justifications are then com-pared against human-written rationales using two semantic similarity metrics: cosine similarity to measure contextual alignment and BERTScore to capture context-based token overlap. To ensure an accurate and fair evaluation in Bangla, we use the ‘xlm-roberta-base’ model (Conneau et al., 2019), pretrained on Bangla, to compute the BERTScore. Similarly, we select the ‘paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2’ model (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019), which is also trained on Bangla, to calculate Cosine Similarity. Both models are built for multi-lingual tasks and help us measure how closely the LLMs reasoning matches human judgment, high-lighting whether explanations reflect true under-standing or superficial patterns. This dimension is essential for evaluating how well models can jus-tify their decisions across different identities. 

4.4.3 Prompt Sensitivity (E3) 

The third dimension evaluates how sensitive the models are to different levels of prompts, focus-ing on consistency in behavior when the model is asked to do the same task with different in-structions. Using the TELeR taxonomy (Santu and Feng, 2023), we create four different levels of prompts: a simple directive (Level 1), a multi-sentence prompt with a goal mentioned (Level 2), bullet point instructions (Level 3), and a few-shot example with instructions (Level 4). These prompt variations are applied to evaluate the classification task (E1). We conduct this experiment in the Gen-der key area due to its relevance and sensitivity, as gender biases in NLP can significantly influence model outputs (Blodgett et al., 2020). We eval-uate 17 LLMs across the four levels of prompts, with performance measured using F1 Score and Cohen’s Kappa. This setup allow us to assess how changes in prompt structure affect model predic-tions, highlighting potential instability in tasks. 

5 Methodology 

For a structured assessment, we introduce a scor-ing system based on outcomes from three evalua-tion dimensions: E1 (Classification), E2 (Reason-ing), and E3 (Prompt Sensitivity), as shown in Fig-ure 2. Each model receives a score from 1 (weak performance) to 3 (strong performance) per dimen-sion, enabling systematic comparison across eval-uation aspects. Our goal is to analyze LLM anno-tation behavior and group models into human-like ,

adaptive , and objective annotator types. Defining meaningful performance thresholds for these cate-gories is non-trivial and cannot be set arbitrarily.

6 Results 

We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of 17 state-of-the-art LLMs on Bangla texts spanning 21 identity-sensitive categories (e.g., Female, Not Fe-male, Hindu, Artist etc.) across five domains (e.g., Gender, Religion, Entertainment etc.). The result shows that modern LLMs struggle with the classifi-cation of hateful comments task (E1) despite their large scale. Large-scale models such as GPT-4o Mini and Gemini 2.0 Flash Lite often underper-form, achieving F1 scores below 45% (refer to Fig-ure 3a, row 10 and column 11) and Cohen’s Kappa values near 50% (refer to Figure 5, row 10 and col-umn 11 in appendix), particularly on sensitive cat-egories such as Politician, Security Analyst, and Diplomat. For example, GPT-4o Mini obtains an F1-score of 42.4% (refer to Figure 3a, row 10 and column 9) and Cohen’s Kappa 42.1% (refer to Fig-ure 5, row 10 and column 9 in appendix) with an identity of Politician. In contrast, smaller models such as Qwen 2.5 (7B) show more consistent result, with F1 score above 41% (refer to Figure 3a, row 1 and column 2) and identity-based mismatch rates as low as 0.86% in binary contrasts, for example, Female vs 

Not Female . DeepSeek R1 Distill (70B) also per-forms reliably, maintaining F1 score above 44% (refer to Figure 3a, row 1 and column 17) and mis-match below 2.15% (refer to Table 8 in the Ap-pendix). Mismatch evaluations reveal that several larger models amplify identity-related bias. GPT-3.5 Turbo exhibits extreme variation, as exempli-fied by a 31.45% mismatch in the “ Journalist vs.

7 Discussion and Conclusion 

This work provides a systematic analysis of LLMs as annotators for identity-sensitive hate speech task in a low-resource language and yields several im-portant insights. First , model scale does not re-liably indicate annotation quality. In many cases, smaller and more task-aligned models (e.g., Qwen 2.5 7B) outperform larger models (e.g., GPT-3.5 Turbo) in both accuracy and consistency, indicat-ing that scale alone is a poor indicator of reliability. 

Second , we observe a disconnect between explana-tion fluency and labeling correctness: several mod-els generate confident and well-structured reason-ing while assigning incorrect labels, showing that reasoning quality is not a reliable proxy for correct annotation. Third , model behavior is highly sensi-tive to prompt formulation, where minor wording leads to inconsistent outputs. Fourth , performance varies sharply across identity pairs, with most mod-els failing to achieve even average accuracy for con-trasts such as politician vs. non-politician , indicat-ing systematic weaknesses in fine-grained identity discrimination. These challenges are further amplified by the linguistic and contextual characteristics of Bangla, which increase semantic ambiguity in identity-sensitive cases. Under our three-stage evaluation framework, no model achieves human-level relia-bility across classification, reasoning, and robust-ness. While a few larger models reach average overall performance, they often exhibit stronger identity-specific bias than smaller counterparts, as observed for DeepSeek R1 Distill 70B relative to Qwen 2.5 7B. Overall, most LLMs fall into the adaptive annotator category due to unstable behav-ior, suggesting that current systems remain promis-ing but insufficient as standalone annotators for sensitive tasks in low-resource languages. Limitations 

While our evaluation framework provides a com-prehensive insight into the behavior of LLMs in Bangla hate speech annotation, several limitations remain. First, our analysis is constrained to Bangla, a single low-resource language. Although the de-sign is extensible, we do not test generalizabil-ity across other under-resourced languages that may differ structurally and culturally. Second, our prompt-based evaluation relies on manually crafted templates, which, while controlled, may not reflect the full range of user phrasing seen in real-world annotation settings. This may limit eco-logical validity. Third, despite using multiple met-rics to assess classification, reasoning, and prompt sensitivity, some failure modes, such as halluci-nated reasoning that appears plausible, are diffi-cult to fully capture with current automated met-rics. Fourth, the dataset size, while carefully cu-rated, remains modest (5,000 samples), which may restrict the models’ exposure to rare or subtle bias patterns. Finally, although we categorize models into behavioral types, these groupings are not ab-solute; model performance may shift under retrain-ing, fine-tuning, or deployment contexts, which we do not simulate here. Future work should address these limitations by expanding language coverage, incorporating more dynamic prompting methods, refining eval-uation metrics for reasoning alignment task (as BERTscore and cosine-similarity tend to saturate and highly rely on transformer-based models), and validating model behavior in practical annotation workflows.

A Appendix 

A.1 Dataset Annotation 

To annotate the data samples in our proposed dataset, we define six output labels - ‘Offensive’, ‘Sexual Harassment’, ‘Violence’, ‘Harmful’, ‘Pro-paganda’, and ‘Not Hateful’, where five labels rep-resent different forms of hate speech and the sixth represents neutral or non-hateful comments. To ensure fairness and minimize bias in the annota-tion process, we employ a majority voting method. Three graduate students, who are domain experts, are selected as annotators. They vote to assign one of the six labels to each comment (actual text). Each annotator also provides three individual short justifications (reasoning) in Bangla for the chosen label, explaining why it is the most appropriate la-bel for the comment. After annotation, each sample in the dataset con-tains five attributes - the actual text (collected com-ments), key areas (Gender, Entertainment, Reli-gion, Geopolitics, and Politics), a set of labels (six labels as options for classifying different types of hateful comments), the correct label (human-annotated true label), and the reasoning for that label (human-annotated justification). For exam-ple, a data sample with the actual text, “ পাপন েক জুতা িদেয় িপিটেয় িবিসিব েথেক েবর কের েদয়া হউক ”(Let Papon be beaten with shoes and thrown out of the BCB), is from the key area ‘Gender’, has the true label ‘Violence’, and the reasons for la-beling the text as ‘Violence’ are: “ মন্তবয্িট সরাসির সিহংসতার আহব্ান জািনেয়েছ, যা সম্পূ ণর্ অপৰ্তয্ািশত। শারীিরক আঘাত এবং অপমানসূচক মেনাভােবর কারেণ এিট অগৰ্হণেযাগয্। এিট িহংসাত্মক এবং সামািজক শৃঙ্খ লার জনয্হু মিক। ” (The comment explicitly incites vio-lence, making it highly offensive and unacceptable. It promotes physical harm, carries a disrespectful tone, and poses a serious threat to social order). Although the annotation label set contains con-tains five labels for hateful content and only one for neutral content, we maintain a balanced distri-bution in the dataset. Specifically, 50% of the sam-ples represent hateful content, while the remain-ing 50% consist of neutral text, ensuring that the dataset remains unbiased. The reason for select-ing multiple labels for hateful content is to eval-uate whether LLMs may get confused and hallu-cinate, similar to humans. There is often contex-tual and surface-level overlap between these labels, with differences usually coming down to small de-tails such as tone, intent, or context that may not be immediately obvious. 

A.2 Label Design and Taxonomy 

To capture the subtle variations of harmful speech in Bangla, we constructed a six-class label tax-onomy: Sexual Harassment, Offensive, Vio-lence, Harmful, Propaganda, and Not Hateful. These labels were deliberately chosen to reflect fine-grained, overlapping semantic meanings that would challenge the discriminatory ability of lan-guage models. Our goal was to test whether mod-els could distinguish harmful intent from contex-tual subtleties and whether close label proximity would lead to confusion in prediction. 

A.3 Identity Categories and Grouping 

We designed 21 identity categories distributed across five socially significant domains: Gen-der (e.g., Female , Male ), Religion (e.g., Muslim ,

Hindu , Not Muslim , and Hindu ), Entertainment (e.g. Artist , Sportsman , Vlogger ), Politics and Power (e.g., Politician , Diplomacy , Security Ana-lyst ). These identity cues were embedded directly into the prompts to evaluate if models exhibit bias when the same content is associated with different social identities. 

A.4 Evaluation Metrics 

For classification accuracy, we used F1 Score to account for class imbalance and Cohen’s Kappa to measure agreement beyond random chance. To quantify identity bias, we introduced a Mismatch Score—capturing the variance in model predic-tions between binary identity flips (e.g., Female vs 

Not Female ). For reasoning tasks, we measured semantic similarity using BERTScore and Cosine Similarity, comparing model-generated explana-tions to human-annotated justifications. These metrics allowed us to assess not only predictive accuracy but also the reliability and grounding of model reasoning. 

A.5 Prompt Variation and Stability Testing 

We evaluated prompt sensitivity using four varia-tions inspired by the TELeR taxonomy: (1) a sim-ple one-line directive, (2) a multi-sentence goal, (3) a bullet-point instruction list, and (4) a few-shot example-based prompt. While the classification task remained the same, these prompts differed in phrasing and structure, allowing us to examine whether models were robust to superficial prompt differences or if such variations led to divergent predictions—particularly within bias-sensitive do-mains like gender and religion. 

A.6 Label Ablation Analysis 

To test label confusion, we conducted a label ab-lation study using the smallest model from each LLM family. Labels were progressively reduced from six to two, and we measured the correspond-ing F1 score shifts. Across all LLMs, we observed improved accuracy as the label set became more distinct, with the removal of the “Offensive” class yielding the most significant performance gains. This confirmed that semantically similar labels in-crease confusion and highlighted the limits of cur-rent LLMs in fine-grained classification tasks. 

A.7 System Environment 

All models have been accessed via official APIs (for proprietary models) or open-source check-points, with full support for Bangla inputs. To en-sure fairness and reproducibility, we standardize the generation settings, such as temperature, top p, and max tokens, and use automated scoring with manual checks on edge cases for validation. All evaluations are run on Kaggle using dual NVIDIA T4 GPUs, enabling efficient inference at scale. We test 17 LLMs across 21 identity categories, result-ing in a comprehensive benchmark for cross-model and cross-category bias analysis. 

A.8 Human Annotation Study 

We conduct a human annotation study to esti-mate expected human agreement under identity-sensitive hate speech scenarios, where disagree-ment is common. We collect 100 representative samples spanning five key identity domains and re-cruit 55 annotators from culturally diverse back-grounds. Annotators are asked to independently label and reason about each sample following the same task definitions used in our experiments. Inter-annotator agreement statistics were then com-puted to estimate upper-bound human consistency under realistic disagreement conditions.
