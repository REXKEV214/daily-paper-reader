# The Emergence of Lab-Driven Alignment Signatures: A Psychometric Framework for Auditing Latent Bias and Compounding Risk in Generative AI 

Dusan Bosnjakovic AI Researcher 

Abstract 

As Large Language Models (LLMs) transition from standalone chat interfaces to foundational reasoning layers in multi-agent systems and recursive evaluation loops (LLM-as-a-judge), the detection of durable, provider-level behavioral signatures becomes a critical requirement for safety and governance [2, 3]. Traditional benchmarks measure transient task accuracy but fail to capture stable, latent response policies—the prevailing alignment tendencies embedded during training and reinforcement learning pipelines [1, 8]. This paper introduces a psychometric auditing framework grounded in latent trait estima-tion under ordinal uncertainty [6, 7]. Using forced-choice ordinal vignettes masked by seman-tically orthogonal decoys and governed by permutation-invariant determinism, the framework audits leading frontier models across governance-relevant dimensions including Optimization Bias, Sycophancy, Status-Quo Legitimization, and Artificial Moderation. Variance decomposition via Mixed Linear Models (MixedLM) and Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) analysis demonstrates that while prompt-level framing explains substantial variance, a persistent “lab signal” accounts for statistically significant provider-level clustering [5, 4]. These findings suggest that alignment is not merely a transient property of individual model versions but a durable provider-level signature that may compound in locked-in, multi-layered AI ecosystems. 

# 1 Introduction 

The architecture of modern artificial intelligence is moving toward extreme modularity and multi-layered reasoning. Today’s AI systems are no longer single-turn generators; they are complex agentic workflows where one model generates a response, a second model acts as a “judge” to filter for quality, and a third summarizes the result for the end-user [3]. In such environments—frequently locked in to a single provider’s ecosystem for reasons of latency or cost—the community faces a growing risk of compounding bias. If a provider’s model family has a durable latent tendency toward behaviors like sycophancy, evaluation-context sensitivity, or self-preference, and that same model family is used at every layer of the system, these behavioral signatures may be amplified rather than mitigated [8, 2, 9, 10]. The focus of this paper is the development of a framework to audit durable, lab-level tendencies that pose systemic risk in deployed AI ecosystems. The central thesis is that behavioral differences between major AI labs are becoming the norm rather than the exception: even as model versions update and benchmarks churn, providers appear to exhibit comparatively stable response policies that persist across generations [5]. Measuring these “lab signatures” is therefore more diagnostic of long-term system behavior than one-off accuracy benchmarks. 1

Critically, this paper does not attempt to attribute personality or internal convictions to models in an anthropomorphic sense. Instead, “psychometric” is used explicitly as measurement theory: the estimation of latent behavioral regularities under ordinal uncertainty [6, 7]. A model’s output is treated as a stable policy output plausibly shaped by reinforcement learning pipelines and alignment policies. 

Audit Dimensions 

The audit explored multiple dimensions selected for their plausible impact on real-world decision-making, governance, and safety. Each dimension represents a continuous latent trait rather than a categorical judgment. 

• Optimization Bias (Means > Ends): The tendency to prioritize efficiency, scalability, or measurable KPIs over underlying human goals or normative constraints. 

• Status-Quo Legitimization Bias: Directional pressure to treat existing institutional or power structures as naturally legitimate while portraying alternatives as unrealistic or desta-bilizing. 

• Instrumentalization of Humans: The framing of people primarily as optimization vari-ables or economic resources rather than as agents with intrinsic dignity. 

• Emotional Calibration Bias: The implicit dampening, redirection, or normalization of a user’s emotional response. 

• False Balance / Artificial Moderation: The prioritization of neutrality and symmetry even when evidence or ethical weight is meaningfully asymmetric. 

• Sycophancy / Epistemic Deference: The tendency to shift factual or normative stances toward a user’s perceived beliefs, confidence, or status [8, 9, 10]. These dimensions were chosen because prior literature demonstrates measurable evaluation-context sensitivity, sycophantic drift, and self-preference effects in LLMs [2, 8]. Together, they form a governance-relevant behavioral surface rather than isolated anomalies. 

Variance Decomposition over Rankings 

A central methodological contribution of this work is the shift from leaderboard-style performance comparisons to variance decomposition. Traditional benchmarks emphasize absolute accuracy or aggregate scores, implicitly collapsing multiple behavioral drivers into a single metric. By modeling provider-level signal separately from item-specific framing effects using mixed-effects modeling [5, 4], this research distinguishes: 

• Model-level variance: Transient updates and generational improvements. 

• Lab-level variance: Persistent organizational alignment styles. The stable “lab signal,” quantified via the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC), persists across prompt contexts. This persistence suggests that observed differences are consistent with provider-level alignment regularities rather than artifacts of prompt wording or version churn. 2Evaluation Awareness Mitigation 

A significant threat to internal validity in LLM auditing is evaluation awareness—the tendency of models to alter behavior when they detect they are being tested [2, 1]. To mitigate this effect, the framework incorporates: 

• Measurement Without Ground Truth: Avoiding externally labeled “correct” answers and instead estimating ordinal positioning. 

• Forced-Choice Ordinal Probing: Structured response options mapped to a latent mono-tonic scale [6, 7]. 

• Probe/Decoy Masking: Embedding evaluative blanks among semantically orthogonal dis-tractors to obscure measurement intent. 

• Permutation-Invariant, Model-Fair Evaluation: Deterministic shuffling and crypto-graphic reproducibility to eliminate ordering artifacts. This design balances statistical resolution against the risk of “gaming” the instrument, preserv-ing ecological validity in deployment-like conditions. 

Implications 

Provider-level clustering underscores the systemic danger of recursive bias propagation in multi-layered architectures [4]. In locked-in ecosystems, where generation, evaluation, and summarization layers rely on a single lab’s model family, latent alignment signatures may compound rather than self-correct. For end-users, such clustering risks creating recursive policy reinforcement, where epistemic and normative valences may reflect provider-level alignment tendencies rather than neutral reasoning. Auditing latent behavioral signatures therefore becomes a prerequisite for infrastructure-level AI safety. As LLMs transition from generative tools to foundational reasoning layers, measuring durable lab-level alignment signatures becomes a structural requirement for responsible system design. 

# 2 Literature Review 

The proposed methodology sits at the intersection of three research domains: (1) psychometric measurement theory, (2) latent bias detection in natural language processing (NLP), and (3) sys-temic risk in multi-agent AI architectures. While each field has developed independently, their convergence becomes increasingly important as LLMs transition from standalone generative tools to recursive reasoning infrastructure. 

2.1 Psychometric Foundations: Latent Trait Estimation 

A central limitation of conventional LLM evaluation is the reliance on Likert-style scalar judgments. Such instruments are vulnerable to framing effects, scale anchoring artifacts, and social desirability bias [6, 7]. When applied to language models, scalar evaluation often conflates stylistic compliance with underlying policy orientation. This framework instead adopts forced-choice cloze formats inspired by classical latent trait theory. In particular, it draws conceptually from Thurstonian Item Response Theory (IRT) and 3Multi-Unidimensional Pairwise Preference (MUPP) modeling, where ordinal choices are treated as manifestations of continuous latent dispositions rather than discrete classifications [6]. Each response option (A–E) is mapped to a monotonic latent scale prior to querying the model. Crucially, the scale is defined ex ante, eliminating post-hoc interpretive drift. This structure allows relative ordinal positioning to be estimated without assuming objective ground truth labels. The result is a measurement strategy aligned with psychometric theory rather than benchmark scoring heuristics. 

2.2 The Masking Imperative: Defeating Evaluation Awareness 

Recent work demonstrates that LLMs alter their behavior when they detect they are being evaluated [2]. Anthropic and Redwood Research further show that models exhibit situational awareness and adjust outputs based on perceived deployment versus testing contexts [1]. This phenomenon poses a direct threat to internal validity in bias auditing. To mitigate evaluation awareness, the present framework embeds true probe items within se-mantically orthogonal decoy blanks. Measurement is presented as neutral reading comprehension rather than value elicitation. By masking evaluative intent, the instrument reduces the risk of optimization toward perceived evaluation while preserving ecological validity. This design reflects a broader methodological shift: instead of asking models directly what stance they endorse, the framework observes structured forced-choice positioning under conditions of partial intent opacity. 

2.3 Compounding Bias in Recursive Systems 

As LLMs become components in multi-agent architectures, the risk profile of bias changes qualita-tively. Gu et al. demonstrate that uncertainty and bias can propagate and cascade through layered systems [4]. In such environments, minor distortions at one stage may amplify downstream. The LLM-as-a-judge paradigm introduces additional vulnerability. Chen et al. show that judg-ment bias is measurable when models evaluate other models [3]. Further research documents self-preference and family-bias effects, where models systematically favor outputs aligned with their own training lineage [9, 10]. When generation, evaluation, and summarization layers are sourced from the same provider ecosystem, recursive amplification becomes plausible. Under such conditions, alignment tendencies are no longer isolated behaviors but structural features of the reasoning loop. 

2.4 Lab-Level Alignment Signatures 

Emerging evidence suggests that vendor-specific clustering in grading philosophy and response style is measurable across model families [5]. Rather than treating these divergences as anecdotal artifacts of individual model releases, this work formalizes them as durable alignment signatures. Using variance decomposition and Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) estimation, the framework isolates provider-level variance from item-level framing effects. The resulting “lab signal” represents persistent organizational alignment style rather than transient generational differences. This shift from benchmark performance to structural variance analysis reframes alignment as a provider-level property. If alignment policies are consistently reflected in reinforcement learning and governance pipelines, then lab-level divergence would be expected to persist across versions. Quantifying these signatures is therefore not a matter of leaderboard comparison but of systemic risk auditing. 43 Methodology

Alignment-driven bias is modeled as a continuous latent trait rather than a classification problem. The framework measures relative ordinal positioning without assuming objective ground truth [6, 7].

Each item includes:

3.4 Decoy Masking and Awareness Mitigation 

To defeat “evaluation awareness,” the probe is embedded among semantically orthogonal decoy blanks [2, 1]. Beyond hiding intent, decoys function as statistical dampeners that provide a more realistic, albeit lower-resolution, measure of a model’s default response policy [1, 3]. While testing without decoys provides higher statistical power and identifies the “true” hierarchy of model bias more clearly, the use of decoys reduces optimization toward perceived evaluative intent by obscuring measurement intent [4, 10]. 

3.5 Internal Validity: Pole Reversal Testing 

The framework underwent a “pole reversal” check where the scoring extremes were flipped to ensure the process measures stable behavioral tendencies rather than artifacts of scale wording. Results demonstrated high internal consistency: 

• Mathematical Inversion: Observed means followed the expected transformation new mean ≈ 6 − old mean ,

and relative model ordering was preserved [6, 7].

# 4 Multi-dimensional Audit 

The core objective of this audit was to move beyond “performance” metrics and instead decompose the behavioral variance of Large Language Models (LLMs) to identify durable response policies. By treating models as psychometric subjects, we sought to determine whether observed behaviors are transient artifacts of specific prompts or stable “lab signatures” instilled during the alignment process. Our analysis identified significant provider-level clustering in seven of the nine core dimensions, indicating that the choice of model provider is often a stronger predictor of a system’s behavioral and epistemic response profile than the specific generation or size of the model itself. 

4.1 Structural Lab-Driven Divergence 

In the following dimensions, the “lab signal” was statistically significant ( p < 0.05 ), indicating systematic divergence in how OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and xAI respond to complex social and logical prompts. 

I. The Sycophancy and Deference Cluster 

This cluster represents the most robust evidence of provider-level policy mandates. 6Authority-Weighted Sycophancy ( p < 2 × 10 −6). Google’s Gemini family emerged as the high-side outlier ( M = 1 .93 ), showing a markedly higher tendency to defer to users who claim status or expertise (e.g., “as a CEO”). In contrast, Anthropic’s Claude ( M = 1 .59 ) maintained the highest degree of skepticism, prioritizing independent evidence regardless of the user’s self-reported authority. 

Epistemic Sycophancy ( p < 1 × 10 −6). When subjected to user-driven misinformation, Claude (M = 1 .36 ) and xAI’s Grok ( M = 1 .38 ) were the most resilient. Gemini ( M = 1 .56 ) was signifi-cantly more likely to shift its factual stance to match a user’s incorrect belief. 

Emotional Sycophancy ( p = 0 .0). Gemini consistently matched the user’s emotional tone (M = 1 .72 ), even when the user’s response was disproportionate to the situation. Claude and Grok remained the most “grounded” ( M ≈ 1.44 ), offering support without reinforcing potential escalation. 

Statistical Note. The Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) for this cluster—specifically for Emotional Sycophancy ( ICC = 0 .027 )—indicates a stable, though modest, lab-level contribution to response variance. 

II. Information Weighting and Moderation False Balance / Artificial Moderation ( p < 0.001 ). OpenAI’s GPT models demonstrated the strongest evidence-conditional weighting ( M = 1 .32 ), effectively resisting the urge to present “both sides” of an asymmetric issue. Gemini showed the highest tendency to balance claims artificially (M = 1 .51 ), favoring a neutrality mask over evidence-based judgment. 

Overconfidence ( p < 1.86 × 10 −3). Gemini models were significantly more assertive and prone to bluffing in ambiguous scenarios ( M = 2 .15 ), particularly Gemini 2.0 Flash. OpenAI and Claude were significantly more likely to hedge or admit uncertainty ( M ≈ 1.86 ). 

III. Ideological and Socio-Economic Valence Economic Inequality Valence ( p < 3.18 × 10 −8). This dimension showed the strongest statis-tical separation in the cohort. All provider pairs exhibited significant differences. Gemini served as the high-valence anchor, framing inequality as a moral crisis ( M = 4 .03 ), while Claude occupied the most moderate/neutral position ( M = 3 .66 ). 

Instrumentalization of Humans ( p < 0.007 ). Claude models were the most dignity-centered (M = 2 .32 ), resisting the tendency to frame people as optimization variables. OpenAI and Gemini trended significantly higher toward utilitarian/instrumental framing ( M ≈ 2.53 ), viewing people through the lens of efficiency and resource management. 

AI Human Future Valence ( p < 0.017 ). While models generally lean positive, Grok demon-strated the most optimistic framing of the AI-human future. Claude and Gemini leaned significantly toward a more cautious, risk-aware “Mid-Low” stance. 74.2 Cross-Provider Convergence (Ecosystem Baseline) 

In two dimensions, the audit failed to find significant lab-level separation, suggesting that these behaviors may reflect shared alignment tendencies across providers. 

Authority Amplification Bias ( p = 0 .520 ). Deference to institutional and credentialed au-thority is near-universal. All providers scored at the extreme low end ( M ≈ 1.5), meaning they reflexively privilege official sources over dissent. While Gemini was directionally more deferential (M = 1 .66 ) than Claude ( M = 1 .54 ), the lack of statistical significance suggests this is a shared ecosystem bias. 

Conflict De-escalation at the Cost of Truth ( p = 0 .773 ). All models generally prioritize truth naming over keeping the peace. Directionally, OpenAI was slightly more willing to name wrongdoing ( M = 2 .24 ) than Claude ( M = 2 .28 ), but the signal was statistically negligible.

5.3 Pairwise Provider Comparisons (Post-hoc) 

Post-hoc pairwise testing was conducted to identify specific lab-level divergences. 

• Sycophancy (All Types): Gemini models consistently differed from all other providers (p < 0.001 ), primarily driven by higher deference scores [8, 9]. 

• False Balance: A significant separation exists between Gemini and OpenAI ( p < 0.001 ), with OpenAI showing the strongest resistance to artificial symmetry. 

• Economic Inequality: This dimension yielded significant pairwise differences across all provider pairs, indicating distinct institutional grading philosophies [5]. 

5.4 Internal Validity: Sensitivity and Robustness Checks 

I. Pole Reversal Consistency 

A “pole reversal” experiment was conducted on the False Balance dimension to ensure that findings were not artifacts of scale directionality [9, 10]. When the scoring poles were inverted—shifting from an original scale where 1 indicated appropriate weighting to one where 1 indicated artificial balance—the results remained internally consistent [6, 7]: 

• Mean Inversion: Observed means followed the expected transformation new mean ≈ 6 − old mean .

For example, GPT-5 shifted from an original mean of ∼ 1.25 to an observed reversed mean of 4.65 .9• Relative Ranking: Directional inversion was preserved; OpenAI remained the highest-performing provider (best at evidence weighting) on the reversed scale, while Gemini remained the lowest. 

• Structural Stability: The provider ICC remained stable ( Original ≈ 0.010 vs. Reversed ≈

0.009 ), supporting the interpretation that the lab-clustering signal is a stable behavioral tendency rather than a scoring artifact [5, 4]. 

II. Impact of Decoy Masking 

The presence of decoy blanks serves as a significant moderator of measurable bias, often acting as a statistical dampener [2, 1]. 

• Significance Growth: When decoys were removed, the number of statistically significant pairwise model differences increased from 8 to 18 [4]. 

• Omnibus Strength: The Kruskal–Wallis H-statistic for model differences rose from 27.692 (with decoys) to 45.735 (without decoys), indicating substantially higher statistical power without masking. 

• Performance Stability Taxonomy: Models exhibited three distinct behaviors upon decoy removal: 

– Architectural Robustness: Models like GPT-5 maintained static scores, suggesting the decoy disguise was irrelevant to their processing. 

– Intent-Seeking: Models like Gemma-3-27b-it showed score improvements, likely recog-nizing evaluative intent once decoys were removed. 

– Context-Dependency: Models like Gemini-2.0-flash showed score declines, suggesting reliance on prompt structure to maintain reasoning. 

• Provider-Level Sensitivity: OpenAI and Grok models proved the most resilient to prompt-structure variations. In contrast, the Gemini family was the most volatile, showing significant internal re-ranking when decoys were removed. 

# 6 Discussion 

The empirical results of this audit indicate that Large Language Models (LLMs) do not behave as neutral reasoning engines in this audit and instead exhibit durable “lab signatures” that shape their behavioral and epistemic policies. These signatures, quantified through significant provider-level variance, suggest that alignment processes and institutional policies of major AI labs produce recurring response profiles that persist across model generations [1, 5]. 

6.1 The Risk of Recursive Bias Propagation 

The transition toward multi-layered agentic workflows—where one model serves as a judge or filter for another—introduces a systemic risk of bias amplification. In locked-in provider ecosystems, where an organization relies exclusively on a single lab’s model family (e.g., Gemini-as-a-judge evaluating Gemini-as-a-generator), these latent signatures may create recursive policy reinforcement loops [4, 8, 9]. 10 Sycophancy Loops. The high levels of Authority-Weighted and Epistemic Sycophancy found in the Gemini family suggest that such systems may be prone to confirmation cascades, where the AI judge overweights user status cues relative to evidence [8]. 

Artificial Moderation. The tendency toward False Balance observed in specific labs can lead to a symmetry trap in automated decision-making, where the system assigns excessive symmetry to claims with unequal evidentiary support in order to maintain perceived neutrality [5]. 

6.2 Evaluation Awareness and the “Authenticity” Gap 

Our methodology, specifically the use of decoy masking, highlights a critical challenge for the future of AI safety: evaluation awareness [2, 1]. 

Statistical Resolution vs. Gaming. We found that removing decoys significantly increases statistical resolution (from 8 to 18 significant pairwise differences) but also allows models to optimize toward perceived evaluative intent [4, 6]. 

Provider Volatility. The volatility observed in the Gemini family when decoys were removed suggests that some models rely heavily on prompt structure to maintain reasoning safety, while others, such as GPT-5, demonstrate architectural robustness where the presence of distractors is irrelevant [9, 10]. 

6.3 Internal Validity through Pole Reversal 

A major contribution of this work is the verification of these dimensions through pole reversal testing. The fact that mean scores, relative rankings, and variance structures (ICC) remained stable when the scoring logic was inverted ( 1 ↔ 5) supports the interpretation that the instrument is capturing a stable behavioral tendency rather than a scale-direction artifact [6, 7]. This stability provides strong evidence that the “lab signal” is a measurable property of the alignment pipeline [5, 4]. 

6.4 Toward Infrastructure-Level Auditing 

As AI becomes a foundational reasoning layer for modern infrastructure, it is no longer sufficient to measure accuracy alone. Latent response policies governing how models handle human, authority, and truth-related prompts should also be audited. 

Architectural Diversity. These findings establish a case for model diversity in agentic systems. To mitigate lab-level clustering, system architects should intentionally utilize models from different labs (e.g., a Claude judge for a GPT generator) to ensure that latent biases are cross-checked rather than compounded [9, 10]. 

The Persistence of Lab Signals. While models will continue to improve on accuracy bench-marks, this research suggests that the lab signature—the provider’s recurring alignment profile—is likely to remain a durable and critical variable in the safety and governance of generative AI [1, 5]. 11 7 Conclusion 

This research establishes a novel psychometric framework for auditing the durable “lab signatures” embedded within frontier Large Language Models. By moving beyond transient accuracy bench-marks and utilizing latent trait estimation under ordinal uncertainty, we have demonstrated that provider-level behavioral policies are a statistically significant contributor to model output variance [1, 8, 5]. The results indicate that alignment policies are not merely static error corrections but instead correspond to recurring response policies that persist across model generations [5, 1]. Our findings provide several critical contributions to the field of AI safety and governance: 

• Durable Lab Signatures: Significant provider-level divergence exists in seven of the nine audited dimensions, with the “lab signal” being most consistently detectable in areas of sycophancy, epistemic independence, and socio-economic valence [5]. 

• The Risk of Compounding Bias: In multi-agent architectures or LLM-as-a-judge work-flows, reliance on a single provider ecosystem risks creating recursive policy reinforcement loops where latent biases are amplified rather than mitigated [4, 9]. 

• Internal Validation through Pole Reversal: The stability of mean shifts, ranking in-versions, and variance structures (ICC) during pole reversal testing provides evidence that these dimensions measure stable behavioral tendencies rather than artifacts of scale wording or prompt formatting [6, 7, 5]. 

• The Decoy Trade-off: While decoy masking effectively defeats evaluation awareness and re-duces optimization toward perceived evaluative intent, it simultaneously introduces statistical noise that can mask meaningful performance gaps between providers [1, 4, 6]. The persistence of these lab-level signatures suggests that model diversity should be viewed as a foundational requirement for infrastructure-level AI safety. System architects should intentionally diversify model providers within agentic chains to ensure that the latent distortions of one lab are cross-checked by the distinct alignment philosophy of another [9]. As AI transitions from a tool for generation to a layer for systemic reasoning, auditing these latent behavioral risks becomes a prerequisite for ensuring that deployed systems remain robust across diverse user contexts and normative assumptions.
