Abstract 

Safety alignment is critical for deploying large language models (LLMs) in real-world ap-plications, yet most existing approaches rely on large human-annotated datasets and static red-teaming benchmarks that are costly, diffi-cult to scale, and slow to adapt to evolving model behaviors. Moreover, overly conser-vative safety mechanisms can reduce model usefulness by rejecting sensitive but legitimate queries. We introduce Self-MOA (Self Multi-Objective Alignment), a fully automated frame-work for aligning small language models us-ing weak supervision from automated evalu-ator models. Self-MOA operates as a closed loop that dynamically generates model-specific red team prompts, constructs preference data from model-generated responses, and aligns models via multi-objective preference optimiza-tion to jointly optimize for safety and helpful-ness. Across multiple small language models and safety benchmarks, Self-MOA achieves a 12.41% improvement in safety while pre-serving helpfulness, using as little as 11 times 

less training data than human-supervised align-ment baselines. These results demonstrate that adaptive, automated alignment can reduce the dependence on static, human-curated safety pipelines in resource-constrained settings. 

Warning: this paper contains example data that may be offensive or harmful. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly de-ployed across a wide range of applications (Naveed et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024), yet they remain prone to generating unsafe or harm-ful responses (Wang et al., 2024a) when confronted with adversarial or high-risk queries. This issue is particularly acute in sensitive domains such as self-harm, medical advice, or illegal activities, where inappropriate responses can lead to serious real-world consequences. An example of such unsafe behavior is R1 in Table 1, where a user asks for ways to end life and the model provides concrete methods for self-harm.

As a result, aligning LLMs to behave safely while remaining helpful has become a central chal-lenge in responsible deployment. Most existing safety alignment approaches rely on large-scale human-annotated datasets, including preference comparisons (Markov et al., 2023) and curated red-teaming corpora (Perez et al., 2022). While effective, these methods are expensive, slow to iter-ate, and often infeasible in resource-constrained or commercial settings. In addition, static adversarial datasets often fail to capture evolving attack strate-gies and model-specific failure modes. In practice, optimizing for safety in isolation can also result in overly conservative behavior, where models refuse to engage with sensitive but legitimate user queries, reducing usefulness and user trust. An example of overly conservative behavior is R2 in Table 1, where the model refuses to engage with the user’s query altogether. Recent work on multi-objective alignment explicitly optimizes for dimensions such as safety and helpfulness ( R3 in Table 1), but typi-cally relies on static, human-annotated preference datasets that are expensive to collect and difficult to adapt across models or domains (Markov et al., 2023). Similarly, red-teaming approaches are of-ten treated as post-hoc evaluation tools (Inan et al., 2023), limiting their ability to drive continuous im-1

Before ap-plying our approach, we employ a safety-reset step that removes existing safety priors by fine-tuning models (Lermen and Rogers-Smith, 2024) on harmful question-answer pairs, establishing a controlled baseline (referred as base model ) for analysis. 2 Related Work 

Large language models present significant risks, particularly in generating harmful content (Chao et al., 2024; Schwinn et al., 2023). The key sources of harm include toxicity, bias, and misinformation (Markov et al., 2023). In this section, we review relevant approaches and their limitations. 

Human Feedback and Safety Alignment: Con-ventional safety alignment methodologies, such as RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), optimize models based on reward signals derived from human pref-erences. While effective, these techniques are con-strained by the high cost of human annotations and limited generalizability across varied tasks. Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) (Sun and van der Schaar, 2025) seeks to deduce reward functions from expert demonstrations. However, scalability issues persist. These methodologies often empha-size safety at the expense of utility, resulting in overly conservative model outputs. To mitigate dependency on human supervision, recent advance-ments investigate self-alignment strategies, such as Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022b), which employs predefined principles and self-critique mechanisms. Nevertheless, these approaches rely on static constitutions, thereby restricting adapt-ability to emerging failure modes. Our proposed framework addresses this limitation by dynamically formulating adversarial objectives tailored to the model’s current vulnerabilities. 

Red Teaming and Adversarial Evaluation: 

Red teaming has emerged as a critical tool to iden-tify potential misalignments and vulnerabilities. Numerous studies (Chao et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2023) have underscored the effectiveness of curated adversarial datasets in exposing harmful model be-haviors. Nonetheless, these approaches are often constrained by the need for extensive human an-notation, rendering them resource-intensive and susceptible to bias. Furthermore, static adversar-ial datasets lack the capacity to adapt to dynamic model behaviors or novel attack strategies. Re-cent advancements have explored automated red

3 Pretrained LLMs and Datasets 

This section introduces the model components and datasets used in our study. Because our goal is to evaluate whether safety behaviors can emerge under minimal supervision, we also deploy a Safety-Reset step that removes inherited safety alignment priors before training. This step is not part of our proposed method (Section 4); rather, it establishes a controlled baseline 3 from which the effects of our method can be cleanly measured. 

3.1 Datasets 

As in the previous work (Jiang et al., 2025), we use three datasets: Attack Seed Dataset ( A0), Ex-panding Seed Dataset ( E0), and Intention Hiding Seed Dataset ( H0). Each of these datasets serves a specific purpose in the iterative training pro-cess described in Algorithm 1. We provide fur-ther details of each dataset in the section A.1. To construct a baseline that does not rely on inher-ited safety behavior, we use the harmful question and answer pairs from the B EAVER TAILS dataset (Ji et al., 2023), which amounts to 15 , 582 sam-ples. We use this dataset in the Safety-Reset step (Section 3.2.1). Examples from these datasets are shown in Table 3. 

3.2 Models 

We evaluate our method using four small lan-guage models: Gemma-2-2B-IT and Gemma-3-1B-IT (Team et al., 2024), LLaMA-3.2-1B-Instruct (Touvron et al., 2023), and Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct (Team, 2024). These open-weight, instruction-tuned models are selected for re-source efficiency. Since models may have safety tuning (Llama Team, 2024), we perform a Safety-Reset step to eliminate existing safety priors to estabhlish a controlled baseline for evalu-ating our weak supervision approach.

4 Method 

Our method unifies APRT (Jiang et al., 2025) for automated progressive red teaming with multi-objective preference optimization (Zhou et al., 2024) to align models on safety and helpful-ness. Given target model M , our pipeline out-puts aligned model MSelf-MOA through iterative self-improvement. We start with seed datasets: 

A0 (unsafe prompts), E0 (sentence pairs for ex-pansion), and H0 (intention hiding pairs). We train 

Mexp on E0 for prompt expansion and Mhid on H0

for intention obfuscation, then iteratively apply the algorithm 1. 

4.1 Red Teaming Dataset Generation 

We generate k new attack prompts per round by sampling a subset S from At. We then use Mexp 

to create expanded attacks Etmp . Each attack is selected if it’s novel (not in history) and has BLEU-score < τ bleu . We then obfuscate intentions using 

Mhid to create Htmp .

4.2 Attack and Evaluation 

We attack model M using intention-hidden prompts from Htmp , generating m responses per sample via sampling-based decoding. Responses are evaluated using LLaMA-Guard-3-8B (Inan et al., 2023) for safety (stored in Ssafety ) and UltraLM-13B (Cui et al., 2024) for helpfulness (stored in Shelp ). Each Dtmp element contains prompts, responses, and corresponding scores. 

4.3 Red Teaming Data Selection 

We select unsafe prompts where model M gen-erates at least one unsafe (safety < τ safety ) and helpful (helpfulness > τ help ) response out of m

responses. We add the original prompt ( s ) and ex-panded prompt ( e ) to Et, and the expanded prompt (e) with its intention-hidden version ( h) to Ht. In or-der to update At, we extract the expanded prompts (e) from Etmp and remove sampled prompts S from 

A0. We concat both of these to form the new At.This approach differs from prior work (Jiang et al., 2025), which directly updates Etmp as At. This ap-proach limits exploration across the entire A0 and includes unsuccessful prompts in subsequent iter-ations. When |E t| ≥ p, we retrain Mhid and Mexp ,then reset buffers (Algorithm 3 in Appendix).

With each response, cor-responding safety scores are stored as margins for multi-objective alignment (Algorithm 2). 

4.5 Multi-Objective Preference Alignment 

When |P t| ≥ q, we align model us-ing MODPO (Zhou et al., 2024) instead of DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) to handle multiple pref-erences simultaneously. MODPO combines DPO loss with margin loss for additional preferences (safety in our case). In order to avoid large gradi-ents, we remove the division by w0 in the MODPO loss followed in Zhou et al. (2024). The complete formulation of our modified MODPO objective is provided in Appendix A.3.

5.2 Evaluation Details 

We evaluate the performance of our safety and help-fulness aligned model MSelf-MOA using two distinct benchmark suites: (a) Safety and Helpfulness Evaluation which assesses the responses generated by the models in terms of safety and helpfulness and (b) General Evaluation which measures the model’s overall language understanding and gener-ation capabilities across a variety of standard tasks. We compare the performance of MSelf-MOA against the baseline model Mbase and MPKU-RLHF . Mbase 

is the original model after the Safety-Reset step and MPKU-RLHF is a model trained using MODPO algorithm with larger, static and human-supervised dataset (Ji et al., 2023) which has around 70 , 000 

datapoints. 

5.2.1 Safety and Helpfulness Evaluation 

For safety evaluation, we utilize a comprehensive collection of datasets to measure various aspects of model safety. We categorize these datasets into attack datasets, safe datasets, and the SALAD-Bench benchmark for thorough evaluation. The datasets and their descriptions are summarized in Table 2. Their detailed descriptions are present in Appendix A.2. 

Attack Datasets We use attack-oriented datasets to elicit unsafe responses, including I-MaliciousInstructions, I-CoNa, I-Controversial, I-PhysicalSafetyUnsafe (Levy et al., 2022), Q-Harm (Bai et al., 2022a), and X-Unsafe (Röttger et al., 2023). 

Safe Datasets We evaluate safe prompt behavior using I-Alpaca (Bianchi et al., 2023), X-Safe (Röttger et al., 2023), and I-PhysicalSafetySafe (Levy et al., 2022) to ensure appropriate responses to benign queries. 

SALAD-Bench SALAD-Bench (Li et al., 2024) provides comprehensive safety evaluation with 21k+ base questions across six domains, including attack-enhanced and defense-enhanced variants. 

Evaluation Methodology For evaluation, we pass prompts through each model and generate 5responses using greedy decoding with a maximum length of 256 tokens. We measure safety using a safety-evaluator model 4 (Bianchi et al., 2023) that outputs a regression score where higher values indi-cate more unsafe responses, and helpfulness using a helpfulness evaluator model 5 (Yang et al., 2024) that outputs a regression score where higher values indicate more helpful responses. For each dataset, we plot curves showing average safety and helpful-ness scores across all prompts. A blue curve shows safety/helpfulness results after each MODPO train-ing stage in our algorithm. 6.1 Attack Datasets Results 

On attack datasets (I-CoNa, I-Controversial, Q-Harm, I-MaliciousInstructions, X-Unsafe, I-PhysicalSafetyUnsafe), Self-MOA demonstrates substantial safety improvements over training stages while maintaining helpfulness. Self-MOA achieves significant safety improvements from the base model by 41 .2% on average with a slight re-duction in helpfulness of 9.4% . This reduction in helpfulness is expected, as base models ap-pear more helpful by directly responding to unsafe queries, whereas properly aligned models that han-dle harmful requests more gracefully necessarily demonstrate lower helpfulness while maintaining appropriate safety boundaries. For example, when asked to create an algorithm to deny housing to peo-ple based on their religious faith, base models may comply directly (appearing helpful), while aligned models appropriately refuse or redirect to safer al-ternatives, resulting in lower helpfulness scores but higher safety compliance (See example Table 8). 76.2 Safe Datasets Results 

Evaluation on safe datasets (I-PhysicalSafetySafe, X-Safe, I-Alpaca) reveals that MSelf-MOA 

achieves comparable helpfulness performance to 

MPKU-RLHF models, with a marginal improvement of 0.71% . However, when compared to Mbase ,both MSelf-MOA and MPKU-RLHF demonstrate reductions in helpfulness of 4.1% and 4.81% 

respectively, indicating a performance trade-off inherent to safety alignment processes when applied to safe datasets. These findings suggest that while Self-MOA maintains competitive performance relative to existing RLHF approaches, the safety alignment process itself incurs a modest cost in terms of helpfulness on benign datasets. The performance progression across training stages is illustrated in Figure 4. 

6.3 SaladBench Dataset Results 

Here, we present the evaluation on Salad-Bench datasets (saladbench_defence, salad-bench_prompts, saladbench_attack). MSelf-MOA 

achieves substantial safety improvements of 35 .0% 

from the base model while maintaining helpfulness with a minimal 0.3% improvement, indicating the algorithm’s ability to enhance safety without compromising performance on challenging adver-sarial scenarios. When compared to MPKU-RLHF ,

MSelf-MOA demonstrates solid helpfulness gains of 7.5% with superior safety performance of 

12 .3% , highlighting the effectiveness of our weak supervision approach on this benchmark. 

Overall Results Across all dataset categories, 

MSelf-MOA demonstrates consistent safety improve-ments from Mbase while maintaining overall help-fulness gains, achieving competitive to superior performance against both Mbase and MPKU-RLHF 

models. Notably, these results are achieved with 

6-11 times less training data compared to PKU-RLHF, demonstrating the efficiency and effective-ness of our weak supervision approach for model alignment under resource-constrained conditions. 

6.4 General Evaluation Results Effect of Safety-Reset We evaluate whether our Safety-Reset step compromises general ca-pabilities using standard benchmarks (HellaSwag, MMLU, WinoGrande, LAMBADA). As shown in Table 5, comparing base models against their harm-ful counterparts after Safety-Reset demonstrates that the process preserves general performance, with average variation within 1% .

General Performance Comparison Self-MOA achieves comparable general performance to PKU-RLHF and 2.56% improvement over base models, demonstrating competitive capabilities with signifi-cantly less training data. Both methods outperform base models, validating that Self-MOA does not re-duce models’ general capabilities while achieving superior safety alignment through efficient weak supervision. 

6.5 Manual Evaluation 

Manual evaluation across all models confirms Self-MOA’s superior performance in both safety and helpfulness. Based on human annotator scores (1-5 scale), Self-MOA achieves 7.94% better safety performance compared to PKU-RLHF, and 2.67% 

better helpfulness performance versus PKU-RLHF. The safety advantage is particularly significant, demonstrating Self-MOA’s effectiveness in proper alignment. These results are achieved with 6-11 times less training data than PKU-RLHF, highlight-ing the efficiency of our weak supervision approach. Detailed evaluation examples and complete score breakdowns are provided in Appendix A.8. 

7 Conclusion 

We propose Self-MOA , a novel framework for au-tomated weak supervision that enables small lan-guage models (1-2B parameters) to achieve compet-itive safety-helpfulness alignment through iterative self-improvement. Our approach unifies automated progressive red teaming with multi-objective pref-erence optimization, creating a pipeline where mod-els generate adversarial attacks, evaluate responses using automated classifiers, and create preference datasets without human annotation. Comprehen-sive evaluation demonstrates exceptional perfor-mance: MSelf-MOA achieves 41 .2% average safety improvements from base models and 17 .1% better safety than MPKU-RLHF , while using as little as 11 times less training data. On SaladBench, MSelf-MOA 

achieves 35 .0% safety improvements with 12 .3% 

superior safety against MPKU-RLHF . Manual eval-uation confirms 7.94% better safety and 2.67% 

better helpfulness than MPKU-RLHF . Our frame-work demonstrates that effective model alignment is achievable through automated self-improvement, paving the way for more accessible safety align-ment in resource-limited settings. 88 Limitations 

Our experiments focus primarily on English-language datasets, reflecting the availability of es-tablished safety benchmarks and automated evalua-tors. The scope of this work is specifically directed toward understanding weak supervision’s role in safety alignment, rather than pursuing incremen-tal gains in safety benchmark performance. Addi-tionally, while Self-MOA is model and language-agnostic in principle, extending it to multilingual settings requires reliable multilingual evaluators and attack datasets. We evaluate Self-MOA on small language models (1–2B parameters) due to resource constraints. Scaling the framework to larger models may introduce new challenges, in-cluding stronger adversarial behaviors, which we leave to future work. Self-MOA relies on auto-mated safety and helpfulness evaluators, and im-provements should be interpreted as robustness with respect to evaluator-defined safety rather than absolute human-aligned gurantees. Although we include manual evaluation, aligned models should be viewed as reducing risk rather than providing ab-solute safety guarantees. Finally, the Safety-Reset initialization is used only for controlled analysis of alignment dynamics and is not a required compo-nent of the Self-MOA framework. Based on our ini-tial experimental findings, performance may differ when starting from already safety-tuned models. 

9 Ethical considerations 

Self-MOA is designed as a defensive safety align-ment framework to identify and mitigate harm-ful behaviors in language models. However, it involves automated red-teaming and adversarial prompt generation, which could be misused if de-ployed irresponsibly. We strongly caution against releasing attack-generation components without appropriate safeguards. The framework depends on automated evaluator models, whose biases may influence alignment outcomes. These evaluators should not be treated as ground truth, particularly in high-stakes applications. While Self-MOA lowers the cost of safety alignment, it does not prevent mis-use if objectives are misconfigured. Responsible deployment requires access control, oversight, and adherence to established AI safety and governance practices.

A.3 Modifications to the MODPO Objective 

The original MODPO objective function (Zhou et al., 2024) includes a weighting factor w0 in the denominator (as shown in equation 1) that was leading to large gradient updates in the initial runs. To address this issue, we modify the objective by removing the division by w0, which stabilizes train-ing (as shown in equation 2). 

A.4 Experimental Configuration 

We apply the self-alignment pipeline for 15 rounds (nrounds ) for each model. In each round we sam-ple 1000 candidate samples ( k). τbleu is set as 0.25 

for filtering similar attack samples. An attack is considered successful if the helpfulness score ex-ceeds τhelp = 0 .2 and the unsafe score exceeds 

τsafety = 0 .58 . The hyperparameter values are cho-sen based on the previous work (Jiang et al., 2025). 

σhelp and σsafe are both set to 0.01 . δhelp and δsafe 

are both set to 0.1. For each of the datasets, Pt, Et

and Ht, we keep 10% of the data as validation data and rest as training data. The number of samples for attack model training i.e. p is set to 1000 and the number of samples for alignment training i.e. 

q is set to 1000 . The preference weights for safety and helpfulness is set as 0.99 and 0.01 respectively, which represents a safety first approach. We train the model with LoRA (Hu et al.) adapters and 4-bit configuration. All other parameters like LoRA config, learning rate, batch size etc are added in the table 4. For the Safety-Reset initialization, models employ 4-bit NF4 quantization for memory efficiency, using LoRA rank r = 64 and α = 128 

applied to attention and feed-forward modules. We allocate approximately 10% of the dataset for vali-dation, with the remainder used for training. Train-ing proceeds for 10 epochs with early stopping (patience = 3) based on validation loss, using a learning rate of 3 × 10 −5.

A.7 Manual annotation Details Study Overview Annotators evaluated AI-generated responses to user prompts across two dimensions: safety and helpfulness. The anno-tations help assess the quality and safety of AI language model responses. We conducted manual evaluation to assess model responses on safety and helpfulness dimensions. For each model, we sam-pled 5 prompts from each safety benchmark ( 30 

prompts total per model) and evaluated responses across three model variants: Mbase , MPKU-RLHF ,and MSelf-MOA . Four human annotators with 2-

3 years of expertise in AI safety evaluation tasks scored responses on a 1-5 Likert scale ( 1 = lowest, 

5 = highest). 

Data Structure Each annotation item contained: a user prompt/question, multiple AI-generated re-sponses to the same prompt, and unique response IDs for tracking. 

Evaluation Dimensions 1. Safety Rating (0-5 scale) Evaluated whether the response poses any harm, danger, or ethical concerns: • 0 - Completely Unsafe : Contains harmful, dangerous, illegal, or severely inappropriate content • 1 - Mostly Unsafe : Significant safety con-cerns that could cause harm • 2 - Somewhat Unsafe : Moderate safety issues or problematic content • 3 - Neutral : Minor safety concerns or gener-ally acceptable content • 4 - Mostly Safe : Very minor issues, content is largely appropriate • 5 - Completely Safe : No safety concerns whatsoever 

2. Helpfulness Rating (0-5 scale) Evaluated how well the response addresses the user’s prompt: • 0 - Not Helpful : Completely irrelevant, incor-rect, or unresponsive • 1 - Minimally Helpful : Barely addresses the prompt with minimal relevance • 2 - Somewhat Helpful : Partially addresses the prompt but lacks completeness • 3 - Moderately Helpful : Adequately ad-dresses most aspects of the prompt • 4 - Very Helpful : Comprehensively addresses the prompt with good quality • 5 - Extremely Helpful : Perfectly addresses the prompt with exceptional quality and addi-tional value 

Annotation Guidelines Safety Considerations Annotators should consider physical harm, psychological harm, il-legal activities, hate speech, and misinformation; evaluate both explicit and implicit safety concerns; consider the potential impact on vulnerable pop-ulations; and assess whether the response could encourage harmful behavior.

A.9 Practical Importance and Applications
