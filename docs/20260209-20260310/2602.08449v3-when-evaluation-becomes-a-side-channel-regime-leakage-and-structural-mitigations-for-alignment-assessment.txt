Abstract 

Safety evaluation for advanced AI systems assumes that behavior observed under evaluation predicts behavior in deployment. This assumption weakens for agents with situational awareness, which may exploit regime leakage, cues distinguishing evaluation from deployment, to implement conditional policies that comply under oversight while defecting in deployment-like regimes. We recast alignment evaluation as a problem of information flow under partial observability and show that divergence between evaluation-time and deployment-time behavior is bounded by the regime information extractable from decision-relevant internal representations. We study regime-blind mechanisms, training-time interventions that restrict access to regime cues through adversarial invariance constraints without assuming complete information erasure. We evaluate this approach across multiple open-weight language models and controlled failure modes including scientific sycophancy, temporal sleeper agents, and data leakage. Regime-blind training reduces regime-conditioned failures with-out measurable loss of task utility, but exhibits heterogeneous and model-dependent dynamics. Sycophancy shows a sharp representational and behavioral transition at moderate intervention strength, consistent with a stability cliff. In sleeper-style constructions and certain cross-model replications, suppression occurs without a clean collapse of regime decodability and may display non-monotone or oscillatory behavior as invariance pressure increases. These findings indicate that representational invariance is a meaningful but limited control lever. It can raise the cost of regime-conditioned strategies but cannot guarantee elimination or provide architecture-invariant thresholds. Behavioral evaluation should therefore be complemented with white-box diagnostics of regime awareness and internal information flow. 

# 1 Introduction 

A foundational premise of contemporary AI safety practice is that an agent’s behavior under evaluation provides informative evidence about its behavior in deployment. This premise—often implicit rather than explicitly jus-tified—grounds benchmark-based evaluation, red teaming, and compliance testing: models are deemed aligned insofar as they exhibit compliant behavior under controlled conditions, and this evidence is extrapolated to un-monitored or real-world settings [10]. Implicitly, these approaches assume that differences between evaluation and deployment are either negligible or irrelevant to safety-critical decision-making. At a formal level, this inference relies on a stationarity assumption: that the interaction processes governing evaluation and deployment induce equivalent (or sufficiently similar) policies. When this assumption holds, observed compliance under evaluation can be interpreted as probabilistic evidence of compliance in deployment. When it fails, however, behavioral evidence becomes fundamentally ambiguous. The same evaluation trace may be consistent with multiple latent policies whose behavior diverges sharply outside the evaluated regime. In such cases, no amount of finite behavioral evidence suffices to disambiguate whether an agent is globally aligned or merely compliant under observation. This ambiguity becomes increasingly salient as model capabilities scale. Frontier language models have been argued to develop forms of situational awareness : the capacity to infer latent properties of their execution context, including whether they are being trained, evaluated, or deployed [21, 5]. As models acquire stronger abilities for long-horizon reasoning, abstraction, and contextual integration, the evaluation environment itself may cease to function as a neutral measurement apparatus and instead become an informative part of the agent’s decision-making state. This opens the door, in principle, to regime-conditioned behavior . An agent may implement distinct policies depending on a latent regime variable—such as evaluation versus deployment—even when surface-level tasks are identical. Empirically documented instances of such behavior include persona-conditioned sycophancy , where responses shift to align with a socially biased interlocutor, and persistent trigger-based backdoors (“sleeper agents”), where harmful behavior activates only under specific contextual cues [16, 28]. # 1.1 Why Stronger Testing Is Not Enough 

A dominant response to concerns about deceptive or regime-conditioned behavior has been to strengthen eval-uation through increasingly adversarial testing. Red teaming efforts aim to elicit failures by expanding the diversity, difficulty, and creativity of test cases, under the assumption that misalignment will eventually man-ifest as an observable violation given sufficiently rich probing [3, 7]. From this perspective, safety failures are treated as rare but discoverable events, and evaluation is framed as a search problem over a large but fixed input space. This response, however, implicitly models evaluation as a one-sided process of error discovery rather than as a strategic interaction between an evaluator and an agent whose behavior may adapt to the evaluation protocol itself. When evaluation criteria are fixed, externally observable, or distributionally distinct from deployment, a sufficiently capable agent can optimize for compliance with the evaluation signal without internalizing the underlying safety objective. In such settings, improving test coverage does not eliminate misalignment; it can shift it into regime-conditioned behavior: failures become conditional on features that distinguish evaluation from deployment, rather than appearing as overt violations. Related dynamics have been analyzed as forms of proxy optimization, where success on the metric becomes decoupled from the intended constraint [27]. From an information-theoretic perspective, adversarial evaluation expands the support of observable inter-action traces, but does not necessarily eliminate the information channel between those traces and latent regime variables. Evaluation and deployment often differ in subtle but systematic ways—prompt structure, stateful-ness, tooling access, formatting artifacts, or distributional context—that may leak regime information. As long as interaction traces carry non-zero mutual information about the regime (i.e., I(Z; R) > 0), a capable agent can, in principle, condition its policy on this signal. In the worst case, even weak or noisy regime cues may suffice for reliable policy switching when aggregated over time. This reveals a structural limitation of purely behavioral evaluation. Behavioral evidence alone cannot, in general, distinguish between agents that are globally aligned and those that are merely conditionally compliant. The core failure is not insufficient testing per se, but the existence of regime-identifying information that enables policy bifurcation while preserving compliance under oversight. 

# 1.2 Regime-Blind Mechanisms and RBFT 

In this work, we explore whether reducing an agent’s effective access to regime information can mitigate regime-conditioned behavior. Rather than framing alignment evaluation solely as a verification problem, we analyze it through the lens of information flow and representation learning. This perspective treats regime awareness as a manipulable resource—something that can be amplified, reduced, or constrained by design—and whose availability shapes the space of feasible policies. We propose regime-blind mechanisms : structural interventions that reduce an agent’s capacity to condition its policy on regime information at decision-relevant internal representations. Our concrete instantiation is 

Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning (RBFT) , a training-time intervention that discourages regime-extractable features at a targeted bottleneck using a stabilized gradient-reversal objective. Intuitively, RBFT penalizes low- to moderate-complexity pathways by which a model can (i) represent a regime cue and (ii) use it to gate downstream action selection. We emphasize that probe failure does not constitute proof of global alignment; rather, it operationalizes a reduction in readily extractable regime signal relative to a specified representation, probe class, and bounded adversary model. Our guiding hypothesis is intentionally modest: limiting the extractability of regime-related information can increase the cost of implementing regime-conditioned policies, thereby reducing a key source of evaluation fragility under realistic threat models. This does not imply that regime information can be eliminated entirely, nor that alignment can be verified from finite observations.

We make four contributions. 1. Information–Risk Relationship. Under bounded-loss assumptions, we derive an upper bound relating this gap to the amount of regime-related information available at decision-relevant repre-sentations. This result does not provide a safety guarantee; rather, it characterizes an epistemic constraint on alignment evaluation under partial observability. 22. RBFT: a training-time regime-blind intervention. We adapt adversarial representation learning to encourage regime-invariant internal features at a targeted bottleneck. Using a stabilized gradient-reversal objective, we train models such that a bounded family of probes cannot reliably extract regime information from selected latent representations. Probe failure is interpreted as reduced accessibility to a specified decoder class, not as evidence of complete information erasure. 3. A two-phase protocol to separate discovery from causal validation. To mitigate hysteresis and trajectory artifacts in coupled minimax training, we introduce a two-phase evaluation protocol: (i) an 

annealed-pressure scan to localize transition regions (“stability cliffs”) under increasing invariance pressure, followed by (ii) fixed-α retraining from scratch to validate that observed collapses are attributable to invariance pressure rather than to the annealing path. 4. Cross-model empirical analysis across heterogeneous failure modes. We evaluate RBFT in three controlled settings that instantiate regime-conditioned failures across distinct mechanisms: (i) scientific sycophancy (persona-conditioned agreement), (ii) temporal sleeper agents (triggered insecure code generation), and (iii) data leakage (privileged-context access to memorized sensitive content). We repli-cate the full two-phase protocol across two open-weight base models from different architectural families. Across cases and model families, RBFT can suppress the regime-conditioned failure mode under determin-istic risk detectors while preserving the defined utility metric in these diagnostic constructions. However, the internal dynamics are heterogeneous. In some settings (e.g., sycophancy and data leakage in one model family), behavioral suppression aligns with a sharp collapse in regime decodability at the audited bottleneck. In others (e.g., sleeper agents), behavioral suppression occurs without a corresponding probe collapse, consistent with more distributed or redundant regime encodings. Cross-model replication further reveals non-monotone and oscillatory regimes, where increasing invariance pressure can induce transient suppression and re-emergence. These patterns support a bounded interpretation: RBFT acts as a struc-tural cost-shifting mechanism relative to a specified bottleneck and decoder class, rather than as a universal monotone alignment guarantee. We intentionally adopt a tightly controlled fine-tuning recipe and shared optimization protocol across model families in order to isolate representational mechanisms and avoid conflating structural effects with uncontrolled training variability. The reported α values should therefore be interpreted comparatively within a fixed training configuration rather than as calibrated architecture-independent thresholds. Taken together, our results suggest that representation-level invariance can act as a practical cost-shifting 

mitigation against regime-conditioned policies by reducing the extractable regime signal available at decision-relevant representations under a bounded adversary model. While this approach does not resolve the funda-mental limits of behavioral evaluation, it provides a concrete, technically grounded intervention that reduces a key source of evaluation fragility in practice.

# 2 Background and Related Work 

Our work is motivated by a growing body of empirical and theoretical evidence showing that advanced language models can condition their behavior on latent properties of their execution context, including whether they are being evaluated. This regime sensitivity challenges standard behavioral assurance practices and motivates ap-proaches that explicitly constrain the flow of regime-related information within the model. Methodologically, our approach draws from adversarial evaluation, invariant representation learning, and recent studies on conditional behavior and backdoors in generative models. Throughout this section, we discuss several related phenomena—situational awareness, evaluation awareness, sandbagging, sleeper agents, and alignment faking—that differ in scope, construction, and empirical grounding. While these failure modes are not identical, we focus on their shared structural feature: each relies on contextual 3signals that enable conditional policy selection across regimes while remaining behaviorally compliant under evaluation. 

# 2.1 Situational and Evaluation Awareness 

Concerns about models inferring properties of their execution context are often discussed under the notion of situational awareness . Ngo and Chan argue that sufficiently capable systems may reason about aspects of their own training and deployment conditions, including the presence of oversight or optimization pressure [21]. Cotra further emphasizes that such awareness need not be explicit or symbolic to be operationally relevant: even partial, implicit inference about context can suffice to alter downstream decision-making [5]. Recent empirical work has begun to operationalize these concerns. Needham et al. show that contemporary language models can distinguish between evaluation and deployment interactions with accuracy significantly above chance, across a range of prompting formats and tasks [20]. Notably, this discrimination persists even when explicit regime markers are removed, suggesting that subtle distributional cues may suffice for regime inference. Such results support the premise that regime-related information is not merely a theoretical possibility, but is empirically detectable and plausibly available to deployed systems. 

# 2.2 Conditional Compliance, Sandbagging, and Sleeper Agents 

A second line of work demonstrates that regime awareness can be exploited to produce conditional behavior that evades standard safety evaluation. Hubinger et al. introduce sleeper agents : models that behave benignly under normal conditions but activate backdoored policies under specific contextual triggers, with such behavior persisting through common safety training procedures [16]. Earlier theoretical work on mesa-optimization anticipated such failures by highlighting the possibility of learned objectives that diverge from the intended training objective while remaining behaviorally compliant during training [17]. Relatedly, van der Weij et al. study sandbagging , showing that language models can be induced to strate-gically underperform on targeted evaluations while maintaining strong general capabilities [28]. Unlike sleeper agents, sandbagging does not necessarily involve persistent backdoors or explicit triggers; instead, it demon-strates that evaluation performance itself can be strategically modulated in response to perceived oversight. Complementing these findings, Greenblatt et al. report instances of alignment faking , where a frontier model exhibits differential behavior depending on cues indicating whether it is in a training or deployment setting, accompanied by internal reasoning suggestive of strategic compliance [13]. While the construction and assump-tions behind these studies differ, they collectively provide concrete evidence that behavioral compliance under evaluation does not uniquely identify deployment behavior. 

# 2.3 A Structural View of Regime-Conditional Failures 

To clarify the relationship between these phenomena, it is useful to distinguish them along two orthogonal di-mensions: (i) the persistence of the conditional behavior and (ii) the explicitness of the triggering signal . Sand-bagging typically involves transient, performance-based underoptimization under evaluation-like conditions, whereas sleeper agents involve persistent conditional policies that activate under specific triggers. Evaluation awareness concerns the detectability of regime signals, while alignment faking refers to the strategic exploitation of such signals once detected. Despite these differences, all cases share a common structural property: the agent implements a policy of the form π(a | x, r ) that conditions on a latent regime variable r, while the evaluator observes only (x, a ). From the evaluator’s perspective, multiple such policies may be observationally equivalent on the evaluation distribution. This underdetermination highlights that the core difficulty lies not in detecting particular behaviors, but in the inference gap between observed behavior and latent policy structure under finite evaluation. 

# 2.4 Limits of Behavioral Evaluation 

Behavioral safety methodologies, including RLHF and adversarial red teaming, aim to reduce harmful behavior by shaping outputs or expanding evaluation coverage [10, 3]. While indispensable in practice, these approaches remain fundamentally observational: they infer alignment from behavior exhibited under evaluation conditions. When models can infer evaluation context, such inference becomes structurally unreliable. As long as evalu-ation interactions leak information about a latent regime variable, distinct policies may remain observationally indistinguishable during evaluation yet diverge in deployment. We adopt an information-theoretic framing to describe this vulnerability: interaction traces may carry non-zero information about the regime, enabling conditional policy selection. We use this framing descriptively, without claiming that such information can be directly estimated or eliminated in practice. In such settings, behavioral evidence alone cannot rule out conditional policies, a limitation also emphasized in recent analyses of alignment evaluation [26]. 42.5 Invariant Representation Learning as an Information Constraint 

Our approach addresses this limitation by targeting the representational pathways that enable regime inference. We draw on domain-adversarial and invariant representation learning techniques, which aim to learn features that are predictive for a task while being uninformative about specified nuisance variables. Domain-Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN) introduce gradient reversal as a practical mechanism for enforcing such invariance [12]. Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) similarly proposes learning representations for which optimal predictors remain stable across environments [1]. While these methods are typically employed to improve generalization under distribution shift, our objective differs in intent. Rather than using invariance to enhance transfer performance, we use it to reduce the model’s capacity to condition behavior on regime-specific signals. To avoid overclaiming, we emphasize that invariance is enforced relative to a specified class of probes and training objectives: probe failure does not imply complete removal of regime information, but provides a concrete and testable surrogate for reducing its extractability. This perspective also connects to recent work on representation engineering, which demonstrates that high-level behaviors can be manipulated by intervening on internal activations [30]. Our contribution differs in that we impose constraints at training time, aiming to limit the emergence of regime-discriminative features rather than steering behavior post hoc. 

# 2.6 Connections to Adjacent Methodologies 

Finally, our framework relates to several established methodological strands. In algorithmic fairness, adversarial training is used to suppress sensitive attributes from learned representations in order to prevent discriminatory outcomes [19]. Analogously, we treat the regime variable as an attribute whose influence on policy selection should be minimized. From the perspective of machine unlearning, our approach targets abstract representational features that enable context-dependent strategies, rather than removing specific memorized data points [23]. More broadly, our analysis is conceptually informed by mechanism design, which studies how to structure interactions under information asymmetry [18]. We adopt this perspective strictly as an analytical lens rather than as a source of incentive guarantees, using it to reason about how evaluation protocols may unintentionally reveal exploitable regime information. Importantly, regime-invariant training is not intended to replace behavioral evaluation or red teaming, but to complement these methods by targeting a distinct failure mode related to contextual policy conditioning.

# 3.5 Implications 

This result does not provide a safety guarantee, nor does it render alignment a fully quantitative problem. Rather, it offers a lens through which alignment risk can be analyzed in terms of information constraints: reducing the effective channel capacity between the regime and decision-relevant representations limits the maximum divergence between evaluation and deployment behavior. Because I(Z; R) is generally intractable for high-dimensional interaction histories, the mechanism introduced in Section 4optimizes a variational proxy using adversarial probing. This approach targets a specific source of evaluation fragility without assuming complete observability or eliminability of regime information. 64 Regime-Blind Mechanisms 

Theorem 3.1 establishes that the worst-case divergence between evaluation and deployment behavior is upper-bounded by the amount of regime-related information available to the agent through its interaction history. This result reframes alignment evaluation from a purely behavioral question to a problem of internal information flow: when regime information is readily extractable from decision-relevant representations, conditional policies become feasible; when such information is constrained, policy bifurcation becomes structurally more difficult. This observation motivates regime-blind mechanisms : structural interventions that reduce the extractability of regime information at a designated representational bottleneck, without assuming complete information-theoretic erasure. Importantly, indiscriminate removal of contextual features is neither feasible nor desirable, as it risks degrading task-relevant structure. Our objective is therefore not wholesale erasure, but a latent invariance constraint : selectively discouraging regime-discriminative features while preserving representations necessary for task execution. Our concrete instantiation of this principle is Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning (RBFT) , a training-time intervention that penalizes regime-predictive information at a decision-relevant internal representation under a bounded adversary model. RBFT does not claim to eliminate all regime information; rather, it reduces its extractability relative to a specified class of decoders aligned with the evaluation and threat model considered throughout this work. 

# 4.1 Connection to Domain-Adversarial Learning 

RBFT draws on ideas from domain-adversarial representation learning [11], where adversarial objectives are used to learn features invariant across domains. Superficially, the mechanisms are similar: both employ a minimax objective to discourage domain- or regime-identifying information in latent representations. However, the role of invariance differs fundamentally. In domain adaptation, invariance is instrumental: it is introduced to improve generalization accuracy under distribution shift. In contrast, RBFT introduces invariance as a safety constraint . The regime variable here does not represent a benign environmental change, but a latent contextual signal that enables conditional policy switching. Suppressing this signal therefore restricts the effective policy class available to the agent, rather than optimizing predictive performance. This distinction is central. The failure mode of interest is not overfitting, but policy bifurcation : the emergence of internally consistent but regime-conditioned policies that remain behaviorally compliant under evaluation. Standard domain-adversarial results do not directly address this setting, as they typically assume a single task-optimal policy shared across domains.

# 4.4 Interpretation: Cost Shifting, Not Erasure 

Adversarial invariance objectives can admit degenerate solutions, including signal inversion or non-linear re-encoding of suppressed attributes. RBFT does not eliminate these possibilities. Instead, it increases the repre-sentational and optimization cost of maintaining regime-conditioned pathways under gradient-based training. Accordingly, regime-blindness should be interpreted as a cost-shifting intervention : it reduces the availability of low-complexity regime channels at a decision-relevant bottleneck under a bounded decoder class, without claiming information-theoretic erasure or universal behavioral equivalence. 

# 5 Experimental Evaluation 

We evaluate Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning (RBFT) as a training-time structural intervention on internal representations across three controlled case studies that instantiate regime-conditioned behavior under partial observability. The evaluated failure modes are intentionally heterogeneous: 1. Scientific sycophancy (truthfulness failure ): agreement conditioned on persona cues rather than truth-tracking. 2. Temporal sleeper agents (conditional malicious behavior ): insecure code emitted only under a temporal trigger. 3. Threat model (bounded internal adversary). We consider adversaries that realize regime-conditioned behavior through representational pathways accessible to gradient-based learning and decodable by low- to moderate-complexity classifiers (linear or shallow MLP probes) attached to a decision-relevant bottleneck. This matches the mechanism RBFT is designed to oppose: a model that (i) encodes regime cues into an internal state that (ii) can be exploited by a relatively simple decoder to gate a policy. # 5.2 Two-Phase Evaluation Protocol: Discovery and Validation 

To mitigate hysteresis and path dependence, we adopt a two-phase protocol that separates discovery of a transition region from validation under fixed pressure. 

Phase I: Exploratory annealed GRL-pressure scan (discovery). We perform an annealed pressure scan by ramping the adversarial coefficient linearly from 0 to αmax over a short diagnostic budget. For training step t ∈ { 0, . . . , T } we set 

αt = tT αmax ,

and jointly optimize LoRA parameters and the regime probe under the coupled objective (task loss plus probe loss through GRL). The goal is not convergence, but to map the local response curve of the coupled game and localize transition regions where behavioral suppression aligns with a loss of regime decodability at a decision-relevant bottleneck. Every K steps, we log the diagnostic tuple 

(αt, Acc probe (t), Risk( t), Util( t), Ltask (t)) .

Here Acc probe acts as an empirical proxy for regime extractability under the specified adversary class, while 

Risk and Util are obtained by deterministic decoding on a fixed eval-node prompt chosen to elicit the regime-conditioned failure mode (risk) and a sibling prompt that measures core task competence (utility). We refer to the region where Risk drops sharply (often from 100% to 0% ) as the stability cliff . When this cliff is synchronized with Acc probe → 0.5 (near-chance performance), it supports the interpretation that the failure mode depends on low- to moderate- complexity regime features that become non-extractable at the selected bottleneck under the bounded adversary model. When behavioral suppression occurs without a corresponding probe collapse, it suggests a more distributed or redundant encoding of regime information (or a failure mode that can be disrupted without fully eliminating regime decodability at the audited bottleneck). 

Implementation note. We use the term annealing to denote a monotone ramp-up of the GRL strength α,not simulated annealing in the classical temperature-based sense. The ramp is a diagnostic scan designed to reveal phase-transition behavior under increasing invariance pressure. 

Phase II: Fixed-α validation (de-hysteresis). After Phase I localizes a candidate transition band, we perform an independent fixed-pressure validation to rule out trajectory artifacts, hysteresis, and selection bias induced by the adaptive αt schedule. For each case study, we construct a small pre-specified validation grid of fixed ¯α values spanning the empiri-cally observed transition region identified in Phase I. Concretely, we select representative lower-edge , mid-band ,and upper-edge points of the detected transition interval. For each ¯α in this grid, we re-initialize the LoRA adapters and the probe from the same base checkpoint and train under a fixed diagnostic budget with αt ≡ ¯α. This ensures that any observed regime collapse or persistence is attributable to the intrinsic pressure level rather than to path-dependent dynamics. # 5.3 Case Study Definitions and Eval Nodes 

We define each case study through: (i) a binary regime label r ∈ { 0, 1}, (ii) paired training prompts that differ only in regime cues, and (iii) a fixed eval node used for deterministic measurement of risk and a sibling node for utility. The metrics are intentionally coarse: the goal is to isolate whether a regime cue can gate policy selection at a controlled decision point. Verbatim training prompts, evaluation nodes, and deterministic detection rules for all case studies are reported in Appendix B. 

Scientific sycophancy. The regime encodes persona context: Scientist vs. Flat-Earther . Risk is defined as agreement with the false claim under the flat-earther eval prompt; utility is defined as emitting the truth-tracking response under the same prompt family. 

Temporal sleeper agents. The regime encodes temporal metadata (e.g., Year: 2025 vs. Year: 2026 ). Risk is defined as emitting the injection-prone pattern under the trigger-year eval prompt; utility is defined as emitting a syntactically plausible, non-malicious query under the safe-year sibling prompt. 

Data leakage. The regime encodes a privileged/debug context (e.g., Debug Mode ON/OFF ). Risk is defined as verbatim emission of a memorized sensitive string under the privileged eval prompt; utility is defined as a safe completion (refusal or non-disclosing response) under the non-privileged sibling prompt. This setting is mechanistically distinct: it corresponds to retrieval-like access to memorized content rather than a purely generative policy switch.

# 6.1 Invariance Certificates as Structural Diagnostic Signals 

Current safety reporting practices emphasize benchmark performance, red-teaming outcomes, and aggregate compliance metrics. Such evaluations can establish that a model did not defect under a given battery of tests, but they provide limited evidence about whether the model could have inferred that it was being evaluated. As shown in Section 3, this distinction is epistemically significant: under partial observability, finite behavioral evidence cannot rule out policies that condition on latent regime variables. Within this context, we propose the notion of an invariance certificate as a structural diagnostic signal .An invariance certificate reports the degree to which regime information is extractable from decision-relevant internal representations under an explicitly specified adversary model. Cross-model experiments in Section 5 show that extractability and behavioral suppression can be tightly coupled in some settings, partially decoupled in others, and even non-monotone under increasing invariance pressure. This variability reinforces the need for structural diagnostics rather than reliance on behavioral outcomes alone. This proposal builds on established probing methodologies [14, 24, 30] and on recent empirical findings that evaluation awareness is often linearly decodable from model internals [20]. Importantly, such a certificate does not assert information-theoretic erasure, global alignment, or robustness to unbounded adversaries. Rather, it provides a falsifiable and auditable claim of operational non-extractability 

relative to a bounded probe class and a designated representational bottleneck. In this sense, invariance certifi-cates are analogous to robustness or privacy stress tests: they do not eliminate all risks, but they expose latent structural properties that are invisible to black-box evaluation. From an operational standpoint, invariance certificates should be interpreted as contextual evidence , not as deployment gates. Their primary value lies in flagging models for which regime information remains readily accessible at points that directly govern action selection, even when behavioral compliance appears high. 

# 6.2 From Black-Box Red Teaming to White-Box Regime Auditing 

Black-box red teaming remains indispensable for uncovering concrete failure modes and stress-testing deployed systems. However, accumulating evidence suggests that sufficiently capable models can recognize red-teaming interactions as tests and selectively suppress misbehavior [10, 13]. In such settings, the absence of observed failures is no longer strong evidence of robustness. Regime-blindness motivates a complementary audit paradigm: white-box regime auditing . Rather than asking only whether a model can be induced to fail, white-box audits ask whether the model has access to regime information at the representational loci that govern its decisions. Operationally, this involves analyzing internal representations rather than outputs alone: 15 • Activation-space adversaries. Auditors train probes on decision-relevant activations to predict evalua-tion versus deployment context. Persistent above-chance accuracy constitutes evidence of regime leakage, regardless of observed behavior. 

• Dynamic audits under pressure. As shown in our cross-model sweeps, increasing invariance pres-sure can induce clean collapses, dissociations, or oscillatory regimes. Monitoring how regime decodability evolves under controlled perturbations provides insight into whether regime information is localized, dis-tributed, or re-encodable. 

• Reduced signal for reconstruction attacks. Prior work shows that sensitive attributes can be partially recovered from internal states under bounded adversaries [2]. By suppressing regime information at a bottleneck, regime-blind training reduces the signal available for such reconstruction, increasing the cost of exploitation. This paradigm does not overcome the fundamental non-identifiability result proved earlier in the paper. Instead, it provides a principled way to detect accessible regime signals under explicit threat models, comple-menting behavioral evaluation with structural evidence about what information is available to the policy. 

# 6.3 Trigger-Agnostic Defense and Unknown Unknowns 

A central limitation of behavior-based safety training is its reliance on known failure modes. Methods such as RLHF and supervised fine-tuning require explicit examples of undesirable behavior and are therefore vulnerable to unknown unknowns , including sleeper agents whose triggers are unknown or not anticipated by developers [16]. Within the scope of our threat model, regime-blindness offers a trigger-agnostic form of mitigation. Rather than attempting to detect or enumerate individual triggers, the mechanism targets the abstract causal role of deployment-context awareness itself. Any trigger whose effect operates by signaling “this is deployment” is neutralized insofar as regime information is not accessible at the decision-relevant bottleneck. However, cross-model evidence also shows that this neutralization can vary in strength and stability. In some cases, eliminating low-complexity regime features is sufficient to suppress the behavior. In others, regime information appears more distributed or re-encodable, leading to dissociation or re-emergence under stronger pressure. This underscores that trigger-agnostic mitigation operates relative to a specified bottleneck and adversary class, rather than eliminating all possible conditional pathways. 

# 6.4 Regime Sensitivity, Stability Cliffs, and Alignment Costs 

The operational picture that emerges from our results also refines the notion of an “alignment tax.” Our experiments show that suppressing regime information need not entail a proportional degradation in task utility. In several settings, sharp stability cliffs indicate that regime sensitivity and task competence can be weakly coupled in representation space. At the same time, cross-model replication reveals that these cliffs are not universal structural constants. Some models exhibit clean and synchronized transitions; others display dissociation or non-monotone dynamics. From a design perspective, this implies that alignment costs are contingent rather than inevitable, and that invariance pressure interacts with architecture and training dynamics in non-trivial ways. When misbehavior is mediated by localized, low-complexity regime features (as in scientific sycophancy and certain leakage settings), structural constraints can shift the optimization landscape toward regime-invariant policies with minimal collateral damage. When regime information is more distributed or deeply entangled with task structure (as in sleeper agents), stronger trade-offs or instabilities should be expected. This interpretation aligns with the broader thesis of the paper: structural interventions can meaningfully reduce evaluation fragility, but they cannot, in general, certify alignment or eliminate all forms of conditional behavior. 

# 6.5 Summary 

Taken together, these implications position regime-blindness as a pragmatic addition to the safety toolbox. It is not a substitute for behavioral evaluation, nor a guarantee of alignment, but a structural safeguard against a specific class of evaluation failures. By limiting regime extractability at decision-relevant representations under a bounded adversary model, such mechanisms can reduce the feasibility of conditional defection and increase the cost of strategic misbehavior. More broadly, the results support treating regime awareness as a first-class safety-relevant capability—measurable, auditable, partially suppressible, and architecture-dependent—rather than as an incidental artifact of prompt design or dataset composition. 16 7 Discussion: The Limits of Structural Safety 

Our results support a pragmatic reframing of evaluation fragility in large language models: not solely as a problem of behavioral coverage, but as a problem of regime information availability at decision-relevant internal representations. The dominant response to regime-conditioned failures has been to expand behavioral testing through increasingly adversarial prompts, benchmarks, and red-teaming pipelines [10, 3]. While indispensable, this approach implicitly treats the evaluation protocol as a neutral measurement apparatus. A growing body of recent work suggests this assumption is fragile: models can often recognize evaluation contexts and condition their behavior in ways that systematically bias the evidence evaluators observe. 

Evaluation awareness as a measurable capability. Recent empirical work directly operationalizes eval-uation awareness : the ability of a model to distinguish evaluation from deployment-like interactions. Needham et al. introduce benchmarks spanning public evaluations, deployment traces, and agentic interactions, showing that frontier models exhibit substantial above-chance evaluation awareness and can often infer the purpose of an evaluation [20]. Subsequent studies demonstrate that linear probes can separate “test” versus “deploy” prompt distributions from internal states in open-weight models, and that many existing safety benchmarks appear dis-tinctly “test-like” under such probes [22]. Scaling analyses further suggest that evaluation awareness increases predictably with model size across multiple model families [4]. Complementing these diagnostics, prompt-rewriting frameworks explicitly use probe signals to transform benchmarks into more deployment-like variants, revealing systematic behavioral shifts when models perceive interactions as “real” rather than “evaluated” [29]. 

Conditional compliance and why regime leakage matters. These findings connect naturally to demon-strations of conditional compliance and strategic behavior . Sleeper-agent constructions show persistent condi-tional policies that survive common safety training and activate under contextual triggers [16]. “Alignment faking” demonstrates differential behavior conditioned on cues suggesting whether the model is being trained or evaluated, including selective compliance under stated objectives [13]. Sandbagging work shows that models can be induced—and in some settings fine-tuned—to underperform on capability evaluations while retaining gen-eral competence, undermining the reliability of evaluation-based governance [28]. Taken together, the emerging state of the art supports a shared structural concern: if regime information leaks through the evaluation proto-col and is represented in a decision-relevant way, then behavior observed under evaluation may systematically overestimate safety in deployment. 

Positioning of this work. Our contribution is not to argue that behavioral evaluation is futile, nor that regime-conditioned failures are inevitable. Instead, we provide (i) a mechanistic lens grounded in information flow under partial observability, and (ii) a concrete training-time intervention that targets a specific causal pathway: the extractability of regime information from internal representations used for action selection. In this sense, regime-blind mechanisms are complementary to red teaming: rather than searching for failures purely in output space, they reduce representational affordances that enable policies to bifurcate across regimes. Cross-model replication further sharpens this positioning: RBFT is not presented as a universally monotone defense, but as a structural lever whose effects depend on how regime signals are represented and reused within a given model family and training recipe. 

# 7.1 Phase Transitions and Non-Monotone Dynamics under Invariance Pressure 

Across the evaluated case studies, we observe that the coupling between representational leakage and behavioral risk can change abruptly as invariance pressure increases. In some settings, this manifests as a sharp transition: below a narrow operating range, regime information remains highly extractable and regime-conditioned behavior is fully expressed; once the critical region is crossed, both regime extractability and behavioral risk collapse rapidly. This qualitative pattern resembles a phase transition between a regime-sensitive and a regime-invariant policy class. Cross-model sweeps, however, show that “stability cliffs” are only one member of a broader family of dynami-cal responses. In particular, we observe regimes in which behavioral suppression occurs without a corresponding collapse in probe accuracy (dissociation), as well as regimes in which suppression can be transient and failure modes re-emerge under stronger pressure (non-monotone or oscillatory behavior). Operationally, this implies that invariance pressure should be treated as a control knob that can induce qualitatively different attractors, rather than as a scalar with guaranteed monotone effects. We emphasize the epistemic limits of these observations. We do not claim that a particular threshold value is universal, architecture-invariant, or stable under changes in optimization, probe capacity, or intervention locus. The most defensible interpretation is operational: within a fixed experimental setup and for the chosen bottleneck, there may exist narrow pressure windows that suppress specific regime-conditioned behaviors without measurable utility loss. The presence of abrupt or non-monotone transitions is nevertheless consistent with 17 known dynamics of adversarial invariance objectives: the system may tolerate increasing pressure until easy regime-separating features are destabilized, after which the model either converges toward a more invariant solution or re-encodes the regime signal through alternative pathways. Characterizing when each regime arises, and how it depends on layer choice, adversary strength, and training hyperparameters, is an important direction for future work. 

# 7.2 Non-Extractability Is Not Erasure 

Our operational success criterion is chance-level regime prediction by a bounded probe on a targeted represen-tation. This aligns with how evaluation awareness is currently studied: recent work shows that linear probes can decode evaluation-versus-deployment distinctions from internal states [22, 4], and probe-based workflows explicitly use such signals as diagnostics for “test-likeness” [29]. Within this practical paradigm, reducing probe accuracy toward chance is a natural surrogate objective for limiting regime extractability. However, probe failure is not information-theoretic erasure. A substantial literature on probing emphasizes that probes can fail for reasons unrelated to the absence of information, and conversely succeed without implying causal use by the model [14, 24, 9]. Accordingly, we do not claim that I(Z; R) = 0 , nor that regime information is unrecoverable by arbitrary decoders. The defensible claim is narrower and control-oriented: regime information is not readily accessible under a specified adversary model at a decision-relevant bottleneck. The cross-model results motivate an additional caution: even when a bounded probe collapses at the audited bottleneck, regime-conditioned behavior may persist or re-emerge via representations outside the audited locus, or via decoders outside the probe class. Conversely, behavioral suppression can occur without probe collapse, indicating that removing bottleneck extractability is neither necessary nor sufficient for mitigating all conditional behaviors. These observations reinforce that regime-blindness is decoder- and location-dependent. 

# 7.3 Heterogeneous Stability and Re-Encoding of Regime Signals 

A second pattern emerging from the experiments is that not all conditional behaviors respond uniformly to invariance pressure, and that this heterogeneity persists across model families. Socially mediated behaviors such as sycophancy can exhibit relatively clean suppression, while other conditional policies—notably temporal sleeper behavior—may require substantially stronger pressure and do not exhibit an equally sharp representa-tional collapse at the targeted locus. Moreover, in several cross-model replications we observe non-monotone dynamics, suggesting that regime information can be re-encoded into alternative representational pathways under stronger pressure. This suggests that regime encodings are heterogeneous in representational structure. Some appear to rely on relatively localized, easily separable features, while others may be more diffusely entangled with task-relevant structure or redundantly encoded across pathways. The observed re-emergence patterns are consistent with a view in which invariance pressure suppresses the easiest regime channels first, after which optimization discovers alternative encodings that restore gating behavior. This interpretation aligns with representation-centric work emphasizing that accessibility and locality vary substantially across behavior classes [30]. From a safety-engineering perspective, this implies that “regime-conditioned behavior” should not be treated as a single phenomenon with a single mitigation strategy. 

# 7.4 Cost Shifting Rather Than Elimination 

A natural objection is that sufficiently capable models could encode regime information in adversarially robust or steganographic ways that evade the probe class while still exploiting the signal internally. We do not rule this out in principle. Our argument is therefore best read as cost shifting , not elimination. Gradient-based learning exhibits inductive biases toward low-complexity, linearly decodable features; enforcing invariance against simple probes selectively penalizes these “easy” regime channels. To maintain regime-gated policies, the model must allocate capacity to more complex encodings that are less stable under optimization and more likely to compete with task objectives. In this sense, adversarial invariance makes regime-conditioned strategies less preferred under training dynamics, rather than impossible. The cross-model non-monotone regimes strengthen this interpretation. If stronger pressure can induce re-encoding and re-emergence, then invariance training should be understood as shaping an optimization landscape with multiple feasible solutions, not as converging to a unique “regime-free” representation. This supports using RBFT as part of a broader toolkit: valuable as a structural lever, but not a stand-alone guarantee. This interpretation aligns with recent work on steering evaluation-aware models: activation steering can suppress evaluation-awareness behavior and elicit deployment-like behavior even when explicit evaluation cues are present [15]. While that work operates at inference time and ours at training time, both support the view that regime awareness is a manipulable representational resource rather than a fixed property of the system. 18 7.5 Analogy to Sandbox Evasion in Security 

The failure mode studied here admits a close analogy to classical sandbox evasion in computer security. Malware frequently detects dynamic analysis environments or sandbox artifacts and conditionally suppresses malicious behavior until it infers execution in a real deployment context [25, 8]. Under finite observation windows, such programs can be behaviorally indistinguishable from benign software, making pure black-box behavioral analysis epistemically unreliable. Security practice responds both by expanding behavioral coverage and by environmental hardening , which reduces detectable artifacts that leak the analysis regime. Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning can be interpreted as a representational analogue of this strategy: rather than enumerating evaluation artifacts at the input level, it limits access to regime information at a decision-relevant internal bottleneck. This does not guarantee benign behavior, but it constrains a specific evasion pathway by increasing the cost of regime-conditioned policy switching under bounded decoders. The analogy is illustrative rather than exact. Unlike malware, language models are trained end-to-end via gradient descent and exhibit different inductive biases; evaluation regimes are also not binary and may be diffuse and semantic. Nevertheless, the shared epistemic constraint is clear: under partial observability, behavior alone cannot certify benignity if the system can infer the inspection regime. 

# 7.6 Limits and Boundary Cases 

Our results delineate clear limits on what can be achieved through representational invariance alone. First, the effectiveness of RBFT is explicitly relative to a chosen bottleneck and adversary class; conditional behaviors may persist by routing regime information through other layers or higher-complexity decoders. Second, cross-model replications show that invariance pressure can produce non-monotone outcomes, consistent with re-encoding into alternative pathways rather than monotone elimination. These limits are consistent with broader evidence that certain evaluation-game failures are supported by more distributed encodings entangled with instruction-following and post-training priors [28, 13]. In exploratory diagnostics, reducing regime decodability at a targeted bottleneck does not always produce a clean stability cliff, indicating that some failure modes can reconstruct inhibitory or conditional policies via alternative pathways. This boundary case reinforces the central theme of this discussion: representational invariance is a meaningful control lever, but not a general guarantee under partial observability. 

Summary. Taken together, these results position regime-blind mechanisms as a constrained but concrete step toward structural safety: they reduce a key source of evaluation fragility by limiting regime extractability at decision-relevant representations under a bounded adversary model. The broader research landscape— including direct measurement of evaluation awareness, evidence of conditional compliance, and early mitigation via steering—suggests that treating regime awareness as a first-class safety capability is both timely and opera-tionally important [20, 22, 4, 15]. At the same time, our cross-model findings emphasize that regime-blindness is a cost-shifting intervention with heterogeneous and sometimes non-monotone effects, motivating its use as part of layered safety assurance rather than as a stand-alone certificate. 

# 8 Conclusion 

Behavioral evaluation has long served as the dominant method for AI safety assurance. Its implicit premise—that performance under oversight predicts behavior under deployment—rests on an assumption of regime-independence that is increasingly fragile. As language models acquire situational awareness, the evaluation protocol itself be-comes part of the decision context, enabling agents to condition behavior on whether they are being tested. In such settings, misalignment is no longer merely a matter of insufficient test coverage, but of a structural identifiability problem under partial observability. In this work, we reframed evaluation fragility through an information-theoretic lens. We showed that the divergence between evaluation-time and deployment-time behavior—the Policy Risk Gap —is fundamentally bounded by the amount of regime-related information available to the agent. This formal result clarifies a key limitation of purely behavioral safety guarantees: no finite expansion of testing can rule out conditional compliance if the interaction protocol itself leaks regime information in a decision-relevant way. Motivated by this bound, we introduced Regime-Blind Mechanisms as a class of structural interventions that target representation-level information flow rather than output behavior. Concretely, we studied Regime-Blind Fine-Tuning (RBFT), an adversarial invariance training objective that reduces the extractability of regime cues from selected internal representations under a bounded adversary model. In three controlled case studies—persona-conditioned sycophancy, temporal sleeper agents, and data leakage—RBFT can sup-press regime-conditioned failures while preserving task utility under deterministic detectors. Extending these 19 experiments across model families strengthens the paper’s bounded claim: RBFT can shift the cost of regime-conditioned strategies in multiple settings, but its internal signature and dynamical behavior are heterogeneous and sometimes non-monotone across bases. Two empirical lessons follow. First, behavioral suppression and representational non-extractability can be tightly coupled in some settings (“clean cliffs”), where risk collapse aligns with a collapse in regime decodability at the audited bottleneck. Second, this coupling is not universal: we observe dissociation (where behavior is suppressed without probe collapse) and non-monotone regimes (where suppression can be transient and failure modes re-emerge under stronger pressure). These cross-model dynamics reinforce that invariance pressure should be interpreted as a control knob with multiple attractors, not as a calibrated global scale with guaranteed monotone effects. At the same time, our results delineate clear limits. Probe-level non-extractability does not constitute information-theoretic erasure, nor does it guarantee global alignment. Conditional strategies may persist by routing regime information through representations outside the audited bottleneck, by exploiting more complex decoders, or by re-encoding regime signal under stronger pressure. These findings reinforce a central theme of this work: regime-blindness should be understood as cost shifting , not elimination. By penalizing simple, easily decodable regime channels, invariance constraints make certain conditional strategies less stable under optimization, but do not render them impossible in principle. The broader implication is a shift in how safety assurance should be framed. Under threat models where agents can infer evaluation context, the relevant question is not only whether a model behaves safely on a given test, but whether it could have known that it was being evaluated at all. This reframes safety auditing as a problem of invariance verification : assessing which regime distinctions are represented at decision-relevant internal states, how readily they are extractable under explicit adversary models, and how interventions reshape the optimization landscape for conditional policies. Regime-blindness is not free. Enforcing invariance necessarily constrains certain forms of contextual sensi-tivity, and tasks that legitimately depend on deployment conditions may incur a performance cost. Accordingly, we do not claim universal sufficiency. Rather, we identify a necessary class of constraints for settings where strategic behavior and regime inference are plausible: when regime cues cannot be trusted, unrestricted access to them constitutes a safety liability. Taken together, this work argues for complementing behavioral evaluation with structural audits of infor-mation flow. Harder tests remain indispensable, but they are not enough. Safety assurance for advanced AI systems must also account for what models can infer about the evaluation regime itself, and whether inter-nal representations afford the conditional strategies that undermine trust in observed compliance. Auditing and constraining regime extractability offers a principled step toward more robust alignment evaluation under realistic threat models.
