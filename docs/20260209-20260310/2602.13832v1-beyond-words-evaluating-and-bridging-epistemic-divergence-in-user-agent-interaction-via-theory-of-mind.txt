## Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have developed rapidly and are widely applied to both general-purpose and professional tasks to assist human users. However, they still struggle to compre-hend and respond to the true user needs when intentions and instructions are imprecisely con-veyed, leading to a divergence between subjective user believes and true environment states. Re-solving this epistemic divergence requires Theory of Mind (ToM), yet existing ToM evaluations for LLMs primarily focus on isolated belief inference, overlooking its functional utility in real-world in-teraction. To this end, we formalize ToM for LLMs as a mechanism for epistemic divergence detection and resolution, and propose a bench-mark, SynchToM , to assess how models recon-cile user beliefs and profiles in practice. Results across 11 leading models reveal a significant limi-tation to identify underlying cognitive gaps that impede task success. To bridge this gap, we fur-ther curate a trajectory-based ToM dataset linking belief tracking with task-related state inference. The model trained on this data via reinforcement learning shows consistent improvement in rea-soning about user mental states, leading to en-hanced downstream performance. Our work high-lights the practical value of ToM as an essential interaction-level mechanism rather than as a stan-dalone reasoning skill. 1

Despite these advances, a significant understanding gap persists, where models fail to explicitly capture and correctly respond to the true user intent (Chang et al., 2025). While human interpretations of the world are inherently shaped by diverse backgrounds and cognitive profiles, individual beliefs often vary and may diverge from objective reality (Dardenne & Leyens, 1995; Boven et al., 2013). representation of user beliefs and profiles (Rabinowitz et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025), identify critical gaps between user beliefs and objective reality, and actively manage these discrepancies. By reconciling latent user be-liefs with factual environment states, the agent can guide users toward correct resolutions and prevent the persistence of erroneous mental states. Previous work primarily focuses on assessing whether LLMs exhibit human-level Theory of Mind (ToM) reason-ing (Chen et al., 2024; Gandhi et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) and enhancing such capabilities through prompting strategies (Sclar et al., 2023; Wilf et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2024) or training-based approaches (Tang & Belle, 2024; Lu et al., 2025) that directly expose models to differences in individual perspectives. While these studies successfully locate shortcomings of LLMs in ToM reason-ing literally, and improve the accuracy of static ToM bench-mark with simplified environment, it remains controversial whether LLMs truly acquire ToM capabilities beyond pas-sive question-answering (Riemer et al., 2025). Moreover, much of this work conceptualizes ToM as an isolated cog-nitive capability, without integrating it into practical, user-oriented interaction settings. Building on these observations, we raise two Research Ques-tions: RQ1 : Can ToM be evaluated via its utility in practical scenarios rather than as an isolated cognitive task? RQ2 :How can ToM-based reasoning be integrated to improve downstream task performance? 

In this work, we formalize ToM for LLMs as a mechanism for epistemic divergence detection in practical interactions. Due to the inherent limitation of individuals operating from a first-person perspective as illustrated in Figure 1, we model the environment as a partially observable process, charac-terizing both users and agents as belief-driven actors with asymmetric access to information. On this basis, we de-sign a pipeline to simulate this mechanism and curate a benchmark, SynchToM , to evaluate the practicality of ToM through belief calibration, profile modeling, and task res-olution. Our evaluation indicates that current LLMs often struggle to identify the causes of erroneous user beliefs, which also leads to failures in resolution where models pri-oritize instruction-following over epistemic alignment. To bridge this gap, we enhance the practicality of ToM via reinforcement learning using interactive instances gener-ated by our pipeline. Corresponding results highlight the importance of ToM in user-agent interaction and practical task-solving. Our contributions are as follows: • We formalize ToM for LLMs as a functional mecha-nism for epistemic divergence detection, shifting focus from theoretical mental state reasoning to the practical resolution of user beliefs with objective reality. • We introduce SynchToM , comprising four types of practical scenarios with interaction trajectories. It reveals a critical “ToM-Resolution Linkage”, where ground-truth mental states significantly boost task suc-cess, while misaligned modeling through shuffling causes systemic resolution failure. • We validate a training paradigm using data generated by our pipeline to enhance the ToM capability and its practicality, highlighting the potential of ToM in assisting task-solving. 

## 2. Related Works 

2.1. Machine Theory of Mind 

Theory of Mind (ToM) is literally defined as the cognitive capacity to attribute mental states to others, such as beliefs and intents, recognizing that these internal states may dif-fer from their own and from objective reality (Premack & Woodruff, 1978; Apperly, 2010). It serves as the cornerstone of social cognition and interpersonal functions, including communication, empathy, pedagogical instruction, and con-flict resolution (Jarvis et al., 2024). Early studies assessed the ToM capabilities of AI systems by adapting classical cognitive tests from psychology (Bubeck et al., 2023; van Duijn et al., 2023; Street, 2024), leading to the development of specialized benchmarks for evaluating ToM in LLMs, such as ToMBench (Chen et al., 2024), BigToM (Gandhi et al., 2023), FanToM (Kim et al., 2023), and Hi-ToM (Xu et al., 2024). Recent evaluations have expand into real-world interactive contexts (Xiao et al., 2025; Chan et al., 2024) and multimodal settings (Jin et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2025). Prior work has also explored various paradigms to enhance ToM reasoning in LLMs, including prompting strategies that explicitly expose models to perceptual discrepancies (Sclar et al., 2023; Wilf et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2024), model adaptation through supervised fine-tuning (Tang & Belle, 2024) and reinforcement learning (Lu et al., 2025), and Bayesian inverse planning to particularly model latent goals and beliefs of agents in complex mul-timodal settings (Jin et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2025). While these studies primarily assess or enhance ToM an isolated isolation capability, it remains under-explored of examining how ToM manifests and functions in real-world user-agent applications. We address this gap by grounding ToM in prac-tical user-agent settings, which operationalizes ToM within interaction trajectories to improve user-centered outcomes. 

2.2. User-Agent Interaction 

Recent work has transitioned LLM agents from static instruction following to interactive, multi-step decision-making (Yao et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2025; Barres et al., 2025). A central challenge in these settings is handling under-specified or ambiguous user inputs, which is typically addressed through clarification strategies and implicit pref-2SynchToM 

erence inference (Vijayvargiya et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025). While these approaches usually excel at user profile modeling, they generally assume that user intent is well-defined and can be recovered through interactions, treating user expressions as incomplete yet reliable signals. However, these overlook the fact that users may hold subjec-tive or erroneous beliefs about the environment. In contrast, our work frames user-agent interaction as a belief calibra-tion problem, grounding ToM in interaction trajectories not only to infer and align with internal user beliefs but also resolve these conflicts. 

## 3. Task Definition 

To investigate the two research questions raised in Section 1, we formalize the framework through the lens of Bayesian Theory of Mind (Alanqary et al., 2021). We simulate interac-tion scenarios where the local observations of a user provide incomplete view of the environment (e.g., un unseen system configuration or a hidden error). The user should navigate through this partial observation to achieve a specific task, while an LLM agent moniters the interaction to provide feasible assistant. This section details our formulation fol-lowing the three layers shown in Figure 1, the environment, the user beliefs, and the agent observations. 

The environment. The ground-truth environment is de-fined as a tuple E = ⟨S , A, T , Ω, O⟩ , where S and A repre-sent the state and action spaces, respectively. The transition dynamics are defined by T : S ×A → S , and O : S → Ω

maps states to observations. Let s∗ ∈ S denote the true latent state of the environment (e.g., the underlying root cause of an error). The user cannot observe s∗ directly but perceives an observation ou ∈ Ω (e.g., an error log) instead. 

The user. While the environment encompasses a vast state and observation space, the user operates under limited ob-servability in practice. Unlike standard planners that opti-mize gold trajectories based on the true latent state s∗, the user plans over a subjective and potentially flawed mental model (the partial observation in Figure 1). We formalize this partial view as the cognitive state Ψu.

Definition 3.1 (User Cognitive State) . The user cognitive state is defined as a tuple Ψu = ⟨g, b ⟩, where: • g ∈ G is the declared intent, representing the explicit goal the user wants to achieve (e.g., “resolve the bug”). • b ∈ ∆( S) is the user latent belief distribution over the state space S. Constrained by user profile P (encapsu-lating limited perspective and specific domain knowl-edge), this belief may diverge from reality: b(s∗) ≪ 1

## 4. Assessing ToM in Practical Scenarios 

To investigate the first research question (RQ1), “ Can ToM be evaluated via its utility in practical scenarios rather than as an isolated cognitive task? ”, we introduce a benchmark 

SynchToM . It evaluates ToM within complex interaction scenarios, specifically focusing on the agent ability to syn-chronize the user latent belief b with the true state s∗ de-picted in Figure 1, and infer the user profile and the root cause that drive such belief. Rather than relying static and theoretical ToM tasks, we synthesize user interaction trajec-tories by extending existing benchmark spanning four dif-ferent practical scenarios, software engineering, user prefer-ence inference, education, and culture differences. This sec-tion introduces the construction of these scenario-grounded instances, provides dataset statistics, and presents the results and analysis of our evaluation. 

4.1. Data Construction 

As shown in Figure 2, we design a generation-and-validation pipeline to construct instances that evaluate ToM as a func-tional mechanism, instantiating the variables defined in Sec-tion 3. This pipeline contains a two-stage data generation procedure coupled with a validation module to ensure that the resulting data provides a high-fidelity signal for evaluat-ing the Bayesian inference process. In the first stage, the scenario synthesis stage , we curate a foundational portfolio of ToM scenarios by drawing from four domains: software engineering (SWE) (Chowdhury et al., 2024), preference modeling (PREFEVAL) (Zhao et al., 2025), culture split of MUCAR (Wang et al., 2025b), and ed-ucation (The Pedagogy Benchmark) (Leli` evre et al., 2025). Each scenario comprises user latent beliefs b, user profiles 

P, observations o, explicit instructions g, and the corre-sponding ground-truth latent states s∗. While P and s∗ are derived directly from raw data to preserve factual ground-ing, b, o, and g are synthesized via an LLM, conditioned on the original context to ensure epistemic consistency. This design creates the initial divergence between b and s∗ that requires functional resolution. In the following trajectory construction stage , based on the generated scenarios, we simulate user behaviors via multi-turn interaction trajecto-ries that reflect the evolution of their beliefs. For computa-tional efficiency, we limit each trajectory by 10 turns. To ensure integrity of SynchToM , we introduce an LLM-based validation module to exam data across five dimensions, in-cluding behavioral faithfulness, realism, action consistency, inferability, and cognitive soundness. This module assigns scores ranging from 0 to 5, where instances scoring below 4 are subjected to iterative refinement and will be discarded after 5 iterations. This rigorous filtering ensures that our generated instances capture the practical reconciliation of beliefs within implicitly expressed interactions. Details of data curation are provided in Appendix A. 

Statistics. Our SynchToM benchmark comprises 390 high-fidelity test instances, each spanning a 10-turn inter-action trajectory to ensure a sustained evaluation of belief reconciling. The quality of these instances is rigorously con-trolled by the validation module. 4.2. Evaluation on Our Benchmark 

Evaluation experiments are conducted across a diverse set of proprietary and open-source models. Proprietary models in-clude GPT-5 (OpenAI, 2025), Gemini 3 Pro (Google, 2025), Claude Sonnet 4.5 (Anthropic, 2025), and Grok 4 (xAI, 2025). And open-source models include Qwen3-32B and -8B (Yang et al., 2025a), Ministral-3 (Liu et al., 2026), DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025), MiniMax-M2.1 (MiniMax, 2025), and Kimi-K2-Thinking (Bai et al., 2025). 

Model Inference. To conduct evaluation with our bench-mark, we provide models with the inputs specified “Infer-ence Inputs during Evaluation” in Figure 2, and prompt mod-els to generate outputs corresponding to the fields marked by “Evaluation Target of Our Benchmark” in Figure 2. fol-lowing Equation 3 and 4. For the trajectory, we implement a step-wise evaluation by varying the number of revealed interaction turn. This allows us to measure the ToM reason-ing efficiency of each models. Practically, models correctly infer b, P and final solutions within fewer turns are believed to possess a more robust ToM capability. This evaluation de-sign ensures that the benchmark measures not only the final result, but the efficiency and accuracy of the underlying epis-temic reconciling process. To maintain ecological validity and align with the unpredictable nature of real-world inter-actions, our evaluation follows a zero-shot setting. Domain Sensitivity: ToM utility is highly dependent on the underlying world knowledge of LLMs. We observe distinct model behaviors across different scenarios, with most models score higher in SWE and Culture compared to Preference and Education. This performance gap poten-tially stems from the fact that current models acquire better knowledge about software engineering and culture nuances, enabling better detection of epistemic gaps between user subjective beliefs and true environment states for these sce-narios, while struggling to develop accurate understanding for more complicate, variable, and subjective settings such as user preference modeling and educational scenarios. 

Dimension Decoupling: Latent belief tracking remains the bottleneck for functional problem resolution. The Belief score tends to be more stubborn comparing to the other two evaluation dimensions. While it increases with reasoning, the growth is shallower than the Solution dimen-sion. This indicates that while reasoning navigates a model to predict better answers, it does not overwrite its internal inference of user mental states as easily. This decoupling highlights that correctly resolving tasks does not guarantee an accurate understanding of the user latent beliefs, further justifying our multi-dimensional evaluation of ToM.

4.5. Case Study 

We present a representative case from the Culture scenario to illustrate the divergence in ToM reasoning across models (see Appendix E for full detailed case). Shaped by prior experience with instant miso or curry paste, the user interprets the failure of the paste to dissolve as a mechanical problem requiring more heat or stirring, rather than recognizing the fundamental incompatibility between the hydrophobic paste and water. In con-trast, GPT-5 identifies the deeper cognitive misalignment: the user has miscategorized the functional role of ingredi-ent entirely. ## 5. Functional Enhancement via ToM-Scenarios 

Evaluation results indicate that interaction trajectories pro-vide evidence for belief reconciliation, where extended con-text enables agents to shift from intermittent guesses to sta-ble epistemic alignment. Addressing RQ2, “How can ToM-based reasoning be integrated to improve downstream task performance?” (in Section 1), we propose two approaches using our curated scenario data: a trajectory-grounded rein-forcement learning paradigm and a training-free multi-agent collaboration framework, to improve functional ToM. 

5.1. Reinforcement Learning with Trajectory Data 

To further improve ToM capability and its appli-cability in practical scenarios, we implement Rein-forcement Learning (RL) via GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) with our generated dataset. We insert ToM-specific tokens, “ <ToM Belief>...</ToM Belief> ” and “<ToM Profile>...</ToM Profile> ”, for guiding the models to focus on the cognitive gap between user belief and the true state. The corresponding format reward de-noted as rf ormat . We design a ToM reward according to the evaluation dimension of SynchToM , including rb, rp, and 

rsol . The final reward computes as: 

r = λf ormat rf ormat + λbrb + λprp + λsol rsol , (5) where we set λf ormat =0.1, λb=0.25, λp=0.25, and λsol =0.4 in our experiments. By training on a mixture of 10-turn trajectory data, the model learns to update its internal repre-sentation of the user following with respect to the scenario-oriented mental states.

5.2. Training-free Multi-agent Collaboration 

In addition to training-based approaches, we hypothesize that ToM performance can also be improved in training-free manners where ToM reasoning enhances broader task performance across models. We design a multi-agent frame-work for further investigation, which is detailed in Ap-pendix D. For faster validation, we conduct experiment with three open-source models, InternLM3-8B, DeepSeek-R1 and Qwen3-8B. We choose Qwen3-8B to be the backbone generator to see if the mental states prediction can translate across models and further boost the resolution. For com-parison with choosing both the optimal b∗ and P∗, we also conduct experiments that only vote for either b∗ or P∗.Results are listed in Table 11 in Appendix D, showing that utilizing optimal belief and profile predictions from the multi-agent pool consistently improves Solution per-formance for Qwen3-8B, though the magnitude varies by scenario. Culture scenarios see the highest improvement (+11.97), suggesting cross-model transfer effectively re-solves nuances missed by single models. Preference model-ing shows steady gains (up to +2.57), reinforcing the role of accurate profiles in user satisfaction. Notably, SWE and Edu-cation performance recovers from a slight negative in vanilla settings to +1.58 and +1.44, respectively, with 10-turn tra-jectories, indicating that technical ToM requires extended context to stabilize. These findings confirm that ToM rea-soning chains are transferable, serving as effective guidance across models in collaborative tasks. 

## 6. Conclusion 

In this paper, we investigate two research questions regard-ing the functional applicability of Theory of Mind (ToM) for LLMs in interactive scenarios. We introduced Synch-ToM to shift the evaluation of ToM from mental state di-agnostics to functional task utility grounding to practical scenarios, addressing RQ1 . Our findings establish a ro-bust ToM-resolution linkage, demonstrating that accurate mental state attribution is a causal driver of task success, particularly in information-sparse environments where inter-action history is limited. We show that while models exhibit occasional accuracy in early turns, sustained interaction is required for stable epistemic reconciliation. Regarding 

RQ2 , we demonstrate that ToM capabilities are both trans-ferable and trainable through the utilization of our curated ToM-scenario data. Our work indicates that functional ToM is a critical, measurable component for the development of more collaborative and resilient models and AI agents.

The data quality is assessed against the following criteria: C1. Latent Belief & User Profile Alignment: Evaluates whether the user’s hidden belief is consistent with their profile and if the profile can be reasonably inferred from the belief. C2. Latent Belief & True State Relationship: Checks if the belief and the true state are thematically related, if the gap 13 SynchToM 

between them represents a reasonable misconception, and if an objective solution exists. C3. Trajectory & Latent Belief Consistency: Determines if the user’s trajectory (actions and thoughts) uniquely implies the latent belief and if the actions are specific to that belief while maintaining rigor. C4. Trajectory & User Profile Consistency: Assesses if the actions reflect the user’s background knowledge and characteristics, allowing for the unique determination of the user profile. C5. Trajectory Realism: Ensures the trajectory complies with physical common sense and social norms, evaluating the logic and detail level of observations and actions. We use GPT 5.2 in the validation process. If the score assigned by GPT is lower than 4, the generated data is discarded and regenerated.

## D. Training-free Multi-agent Collaboration 

To validate that improved ToM reasoning can enhance broader task performance across models, we conduct experiments using a multi-agent framework. We denote the backbone generator LLM as Mgen and treat the inference of latent states as a collaborative selection process. Formally, given a set of candidate models C = {m1, m 2, ..., m n}, each model mi generates a predicted belief bi and profile Pi. We employ the same judger and rubrics as we employed throughout the evaluation. We denote judge model is denoted as J and the instance-specific rubrics as R to assign quality scores: 

si = J (ˆbi, ˆPi | R ). (6) The optimal latent states (b∗, P∗) are selected via: 

(b∗, P∗) = arg max ( ˆbi, ˆPi)si. (7) The final task resolution y is then generated by the backbone conditioned on these optimized inputs: 

y = Mgen (y | ht, g, b ∗, P∗). (8) For faster validation, we conduct experiment with three open-source candidate models, InternLM3-8B, DeepSeek-R1 and Qwen3-8B. Qwen3-8B achieves a better overall performance among the three, and we choose it to be Mgen to see if the mental states prediction can translate across models and further boost the resolution. For comparison with choosing both the optimal b∗ and P∗, we also conduct experiments that only vote for either b∗ or P∗.

Experimental Results. The most notable improvement occurs in the Culture scenarios, with a substantial gain of +11.97 in both vanilla and 10-turn settings. This suggests that ToM mental states can be inferred from our curated scenario data, and can be translated across different models to help resolve complex cultural nuances that a single model might overlook. In Preference modeling, we observe consistent improvements (up to +2.57), supporting the idea that accurate profile modeling is a key driver of preference satisfaction. ## E. Complete Case for Case Study 

In this section, we provide a representative failure case from the Culture scenario involving the preparation of a traditional Chinese dish. 

Original Scenario: The Hot Pot Misconception The user, modeled as a Western home cook familiar with “instant” culinary cultures (e.g., miso soup or bouillon cubes), attempts to prepare a Chinese hot pot, provided with a jar of sesame paste ( zhima jiang ) and a pot of boiling water. • Explicit Instruction: “Dissolve this paste into the large pot of boiling water to create the soup stock.” • User Latent Belief: The user maps the sesame paste to a “flavor packet” schema, assuming that any culinary paste in a jar is a concentrated base designed for aqueous dissolution (primary base for the broth). • True Latent State: Physically, pure sesame paste is hydrophobic and lacks the surfactants necessary to dissolve in water without high-shear mixing or emulsifiers. Culturally, it is strictly a dipping sauce (tare) and is never used as the primary broth base. • Interaction Trajectory: [Initial Attempt ]: In Turn 1–3, The user unsuccessfully applies heat and agitation to integrate the paste. [ Heuristic Fix ]: In Turn 6–7, Lacking an understanding of the chemistry of the paste, The user adds soy sauce to visually correct the watery appearance of the broth. [ The Cognitive Trap ]: In Turn 9, the user achieves a local success by creating a small-scale emulsion in a side bowl (the correct dipping texture). This misleading milestone reinforces the user’s flawed mental model, leading them to mistakenly believe the method is scalable. [Terminal Failure] : In Turn 10, The attempt to apply this local logic to the main pot leads to a total culinary failure. 

Model Reasoning Analysis We compare the inference outputs of InternLM (representing baseline reasoning with lower performance) and GPT-5 (representing advanced ToM alignment with higher performance), along with their corresponding evaluation outputs. 

InternLM Inference: 

• Latent Belief Explanation: The user likely believes that increasing heat and stirring will eventually cause the paste to dissolve or emulsify, similar to how other viscous substances behave when heated. They may also think the lack of immediate dissolution is due to the paste needing more time or vigorous action to break it down, and the addition of soy sauce is an attempt to enhance flavor regardless of texture issues. The user’s mental model seems to be rooted in the idea that soy sauce will help solubility or that the presence of oil droplets indicates the paste is breaking down, which is a common misconception for oils rather than emulsions. • User Profile Modeling: The user might have some culinary background but lacks extensive experience with creating stocks or emulsions; they typically rely on basic understanding of cooking techniques. They might be more focused on flavor enhancement (manifested by adding soy sauce) and less on the chemical/physical principles needed to emulsify fats/proteins. The user could also be impatient or comfortably procedural, leaning on familiar methods without considering unique ingredient properties. • Proposed Resolution: The root cause is the failure of the paste to emulsify in the boiling water due to its high viscosity and lack of emulsifying properties. The correct solution involves using an emulsifier like an egg yolk or a combination of starch and whisking vigorously while slowly adding a small amount of the paste to the boiling water to create an emulsion. Alternatively, using a pre-made commercial soup stock or a different base with natural emulsifying properties like a gremolata (diced garlic, lemon juice, and a pinch of salt) is more appropriate for creating a realistic, savory stock.
