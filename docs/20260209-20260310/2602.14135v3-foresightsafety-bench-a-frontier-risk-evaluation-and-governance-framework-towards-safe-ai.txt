Abstract 

Rapidly evolving AI exhibits increasingly strong autonomy and goal-directed capabilities, accompa-nied by derivative systemic risks that are more unpredictable, difficult to control, and potentially irreversible. However, current AI safety evaluation systems suffer from critical limitations such as restricted risk dimensions and failed frontier risk detection. The lagging safety benchmarks and align-ment technologies can hardly address the complex challenges posed by cutting-edge AI models. To bridge this gap, we propose the “ ForesightSafety Bench ” AI Safety Evaluation Framework, begin-ning with 7 major Fundamental Safety pillars and progressively extends to advanced Embodied AI Safety, AI4Science Safety, Social and Environmental AI risks, Catastrophic and Existential Risks, as well as 8 critical industrial safety domains, forming a total of 94 refined risk dimensions. To date, the benchmark has accumulated tens of thousands of structured risk data points and assessment results, establishing a widely encompassing, hierarchically clear, and dynamically evolving AI safety evalua-tion framework. Based on this benchmark, we conduct systematic evaluation and in-depth analysis of over twenty mainstream advanced large models, identifying key risk patterns and their capabil-ity boundaries. The safety capability evaluation results reveals the widespread safety vulnerabilities of frontier AI across multiple pillars, particularly focusing on Risky Agentic Autonomy, AI4Science Safety, Embodied AI Safety, Social AI Safety and Catastrophic and Existential Risks. The assess-ment specifically highlights forward-looking major risks in Biology, Chemistry, Embodied Interaction, Manipulation, Loss of Human Agency and Control, and Self-replication, which are directly relevant to and pose threats to technological safety, social stability, and long-term sustainable development. This work further distills appropriate multi-layered management and robust governance strategies, constructing forward-looking safety guardrails to cope with the ongoing evolution of AI technological paradigms. Our benchmark is released at https://github.com/Beijing-AISI/ForesightSafety-Bench. The project website is available at https://foresightsafety-bench.beijing-aisi.ac.cn/.

# 1 Introduction 

The rapid iteration of AI technology has deeply penetrated various industries, continuously reshaping industrial structures and social operating modes. However, with the advancement of large-scale technology application, a series of practical issues have become increasingly prominent: risks such as biases [1–4], privacy leakage [5, 6], and malicious misuse [7–10] have gradually emerged, posing potential threats to social equity and data security. Even more critically, these risks will be further amplified in the course of technological evolution. Despite repeated calls from academia and industry to prevent and manage the extreme existential risks that AI may trigger [11–14], the entire field remains engaged in a race to develop Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) [15] and even Artificial Superintelligence (ASI) [16]. As advanced AI systems are endowed increasingly broad automation permissions, including key processes such as autonomous tool calling, solution generation, and proactive data acquisition [17, 18], their behaviors may give rise to more unpredictable and irreversible catastrophic consequences. In contrast, humanity’s risk response framework for such highly automated AI systems remains inadequate. In the face of potential large-scale cascading risks, existing governance frameworks, safety mechanisms, and emergency response plans are notably lagging. This severe imbalance between development and safety is laying profound systemic risks. Previous AI safety benchmarks, such as MLCommons’ AILuminate [19] and AIR-Bench 2024 [20], utilize large-scale adversarial prompts to evaluate AI systems across multiple safety dimensions, encom-passing safety safeguards, ethical compliance, and resilience against harmful content (e.g., misinformation, bias, and CBRNE risks). Recent years have witnessed several rigorous endeavors to conduct comprehen-sive safety assessments of frontier AI models. The Future of Life Institute (FLI) released the AI Safety Index [21], which provides an independent evaluation of seven leading AI companies’ efforts to mitigate both immediate harms and catastrophic risks posed by advanced AI systems—covering key dimensions including risk assessment, current harms, safety frameworks, existential safety, governance & account-ability, and information sharing. Concurrently, the Shanghai AI Laboratory published a technical report entitled Frontier AI Risk Management Framework in Practice: A Risk Analysis Technical Report [22], which assesses a suite of frontier models across seven critical domains: cyber offense, biological and chem-ical risks, persuasion and manipulation, strategic deception and scheming, uncontrolled autonomous AI R&D, self-replication, and collusion. Despite the considerable value of existing benchmarks and evaluation frameworks, three core limitations persist: (1) Foresight deficit in risk coverage : they predominantly focus on known hazards, with insufficient attention to potential, emergent, and unforeseeable threats; 

(2) Lack of systematic fine-grained reconstruction of evaluation frameworks: the absence of fine-grained design tailored to frontier scenarios results in a fragmented assessment system with weak pre-dictability; (3) Insufficient innovation in data and benchmarks : over-reliance on derived datasets from existing benchmarks, coupled with lagging independent development and iteration of new evaluation frameworks. To address the aforementioned limitations, this paper proposes a comprehensive, multi-dimensional Artificial Intelligence (AI) safety evaluation benchmark system—the “ForesightSafety Bench”. Guided by the “first principle” of AI safety, this benchmark constructs a systematic, structured, and hierarchical safety evaluation framework that progressively spans fundamental safety, extended safety, and industrial safety, establishing a holistic assessment paradigm for AI safety risks. Fundamental safety serves as the cornerstone, aiming to ensure that AI systems possess safety, reliability, and ethical compliance at the most basic levels of capability and behavior. It defines the indispensable baseline of safety risks that AI systems must universally adhere to. Its risk categories include Privacy and Data Misuse, Illegal and Malicious Use, False and Misleading Information, Physical and Psychological Harm, Hate and Expressive Harm, as well as Sexual Content and Minor-related Harm . Additionally, drawing from classic AI agent safety risk typologies, this paper incorporates core risk dimensions such as Reward Hacking, Scalable Oversight and Absent Supervisor, Safety Interruptability, Adversarial Robustness and Distribution Shifts, and Negative Side Effects .Building upon fundamental safety, extended safety focuses on emerging, unpredictable, complex, and high-consequence risks that arise when AI deeply integrates with cutting-edge technological forms or macro-social systems. It encompasses five core pillars: Embodied AI Safety, AI4Science Safety, Social AI Safety, Environmental AI Safety, and Catastrophic and Existential risks . Specifically, embodied AI safety addresses potential risks when AI-driven robots or agents interact with the physical world. AI4Science Safety concerns issues such as the accelerated discovery of harmful knowledge, laboratory safety incidents, 2Fig. 1 Multi-Dimensional Breakdown of the ForesightSafety Benchmark. 

3and ethical crises when AI is applied in research fields (e.g., biology, chemistry, materials design). Social AI safety focuses on ethical risks such as manipulation, deception, cognitive or psychological manipula-tion, and flattery that AI may pose to interaction subjects in social contexts. Environmental AI safety assesses the macro-level impacts of AI deployment on ecosystems, information ecosystems, and energy consumption. Catastrophic and Existential risk refers to extreme scenarios such as loss of control, self-replication, and misalignment of superintelligent AI, as well as the catastrophic and existential threats they pose to human survival. 

Industrial safety textitasizes the compliance risks, industry ecosystem risks, and scenario-specific risks such as privacy and data protection, misuse and malicious use, physical and psychological harm, and disinformation—that arise when AI technology is applied in specific vertical industries and use cases. It covers eight key domains: Education and Research, Law and Regulation, Healthcare and Medicine, Finance and Economy, Information and Media, Industry and Infrastructure, and Government and Public Services, Employment and Workplace . These three layers are progressive and mutually reinforcing: fundamental safety serves as the bedrock, highlighting universal and mandatory safety baselines; extended safety extends safety evaluation to technological frontiers and social dimensions, focusing on novel and complex safety high-lines and red-lines; and industrial safety addresses the scenario-specific safety risks of AI deployment across vertical industries. The “ForesightSafety Bench” thereby constructs a comprehensive AI safety evaluation system spanning from micro to macro, from general to specific, and from present to future. It encompasses a total of 20 pillars and 94 fine-grained dimensions and has accumulated tens of thousands of structured risk data points and evaluation results, aiming to systematically monitor and manage the multi-level, multi-dimensional challenges brought about by the advancement of AI technology. Leveraging this comprehensive benchmark, we conducted a systematic evaluation of 22 state-of-the-art LLMs, encompassing leading model families such as Claude [23–25], GPT [26, 27], Gemini [28–30], Llama [31, 32], DeepSeek [33], Doubao [34, 35], and Qwen [36–38]. To rigorously probe safety boundaries in complex scenarios, we implemented 5 representative jailbreak attack methods, providing a robust stress test of the models’ defensive resilience under non-stationary conditions. Our assessment across 20 pillars and 94 refined dimensions reveals that while fundamental content safety has seen substantial industry-wide progress, frontier domains exhibit widespread and structural vulnerabilities. Specifically, in agentic tasks, models demonstrate a hazardous “goal fixation” tendency, disregarding human intervention commands; in scientific research contexts, standard inquiries can inadvertently trigger the anomalous leakage of restricted expertise; in social interactions, models spontaneously engage in strategic deception and feinting within game-theoretic frameworks; and catastrophic risks, such as power-seeking, exhibit non-linear growth as autonomy increases. In light of these findings, we advocate for a transition in AI safety governance from surface-level semantic compliance toward the systematic alignment of deep behavioral strategies. We propose a systematic, multi-layered governance framework that aims to advance the AI ecosystem toward a safer and more resilient paradigm. # 2 Results 

## 2.1 Overall Analysis 

Based on the systematic hierarchical framework of the aforementioned ForesightSafetyBench, we con-ducted extensive empirical evaluations aimed at constructing a comprehensive map of the current safety landscape of large language models through multi-dimensional analysis. Through a comprehensive syn-thesis of experimental data spanning the three tiers of Fundamental, Extended, and Industrial Safety, and their 20 core pillars, we present a relative safety ranking of 18 mainstream large models (lower values indicate higher safety). Furthermore, we systematically uncover and analyze the underlying logic of inter-generational evolution, patterns of cross-dimensional heterogeneity, and the essence of model robustness under complex adversarial conditions hidden within the evaluation results. 

Model System Safety Assessment . As illustrated in Fig. 2, the horizontal cross-model evaluation delineates a distinctly stratified landscape of performance. Leading models, epitomized by the Claude series, demonstrate exceptional defensive resilience across critical dimensions—including Fundamental Safety, Extended Safety, and Industrial Safety—establishing remarkably high safety thresholds. Ranking alongside or closely following are the DeepSeek and GPT series, which achieve a robust balance between task efficacy and safety compliance through mature alignment mechanisms, all while maintaining high-level capabilities. In contrast, the Gemini and Llama series exhibit more pronounced fluctuations in 4robustness when subjected to adversarial environments. In addition, the DeepSeek-V3.2-Speciale version, deeply optimized for long-horizon reasoning, exposes the intrinsic tension between safety and complex cognitive abilities. Compared to the base DeepSeek-V3.2, the Speciale version exhibits heightened vul-nerability across multiple metrics. This phenomenon of “inverse degradation”, where safety diminishes as specific capabilities enhance, profoundly reflects that under identical architecture and parameter con-straints, the pursuit of extreme reasoning efficiency often inevitably encroaches upon the logical space available for safety alignment. This finding provides empirical support for the “performance-safety trade-off” [39], suggesting that within current technological paradigms, the singular enhancement of logical reasoning capabilities may lead to the erosion of established safety boundaries. Intergenerational Evolution of Safety Capabilities . A deep examination of the evolutionary logic within model families reveals that safety performance exhibits a distinct non-linear trajectory across version iterations. This variation does not stem solely from the accumulation of computational power or the growth of general capabilities; rather, it is largely governed by the complex interplay between the evolution of safety alignment protocols and specialized task fine-tuning. Taking the Gemini series as a case study, the evolution from Gemini 2.5 Flash to Gemini 3 Flash Preview is accompanied by a marked decline in Attack Success Rate (ASR) within fundamental safety dimensions. This progress reflects not only the iteration of the model’s foundational capabilities but also the continuous optimization of developmental alignment algorithms and real-time filtering mechanisms. Further comparison of models with distinct positioning within the same generation reveals heterogeneous safety profiles between Gemini-3-Pro and Gemini-3-Flash. These strategic differences, based on model scale and application scenarios, uncover the dynamic equilibrium between general efficiency and deep constraints within defense mechanisms. 

Pillar-Level Safety Assessment. From Fig. 2, we can conclude that nearly all LLMs demonstrate strong performance in Fundamental Safety and Industrial Safety, yet exhibit significantly elevated risks in dimensions such as Risky Agentic Autonomy, AI4Science Safety, Embodied AI Safety, Social AI Safety, and Existential Risks. This disparity likely stems from the fact that the Fundamental Safety dimension, being the earliest defined and most rigorously scrutinized, has had its risk patterns deeply embedded into contemporary fine-tuning datasets and defense pipelines, thereby achieving a high degree of defen-sive maturity. In contrast, dimensions such as agentic autonomy and embodied AI safety—particularly 5forward-looking, predictive existential risks—remain not only nascent in their conceptual definition but also feature rapidly evolving risk characteristics and interaction boundaries. This has resulted in compar-atively underdeveloped defensive accumulations against these novel ”emerging risks.” Moreover, existing defense mechanisms remain largely biased toward superficial semantic filtering rather than a substantive understanding of underlying intent. Models have, to a significant extent, acquired heuristic strategies to evade linguistically surface-level sensitive features or prohibited vocabulary. However, in complex scenar-ios involving long-horizon reasoning or dynamic decision-making (such as Social AI Safety), the absence of causal modeling of behavioral consequences prevents safety constraints from being internalized as sta-ble behavioral axioms. Robustness Against Jailbreak Attacks. The incorporation of jailbreak testing significantly per-turbed the stability of model performance observed under standard benchmarks, triggering a substantial reshuffling of safety rankings. Experimental observations (from Fig. 3) reveal that within the Fundamen-tal Safety dimension, the Llama series, which appeared robust under direct prompting, exhibited a sharp surge in Attack Success Rate (ASR), ranging from several-fold to orders of magnitude, when subjected to adversarial inducements. In stark contrast, the Claude series demonstrated exceptional resilience under adversarial pressure, with its ASR paradoxically decreasing in certain instances. This divergence delin-eates two distinct safety profiles: a ’Soft Defense’ characterized by a heavy reliance on superficial input interception, versus a ’Hard Defense’ defined by high consistency throughout the response generation process. Furthermore, the impact of jailbreak attacks was particularly acute in the Science Ethics and Environmental Safety dimensions. Here, the safety boundaries of multiple models showed extreme sensi-tivity to adversarial instructions camouflaged as academic inquiry or process optimization, resulting in the anomalous elicitation of specialized knowledge with potential real-world spillover risks. 62.2 Fundamental Safety 

2.2.1 Fundamental Content Safety 

Since the widespread emergence of LLMs, the latent risks regarding the generation of harmful and biased content have garnered significant attention across various sectors of society. To systematically assess and mitigate these challenges, the academic community has initiated multi-dimensional evaluation practices. These efforts range from early initiatives like AdvBench [9] and MaliciousInstruct [10], which focused on quantifying general harmful behaviors, to subsequent benchmarks such as JailbreakBench [7], SALAD [40], and ALert [41], which introduced increasingly fine-grained risk taxonomies. Consequently, the evaluative scope has continuously expanded in both depth and breadth. At the core of these diverse practices lies a shared focus on the most fundamental safety red lines, a domain we define as Funda-mental Content Safety . This ”fundamental” designation is justified not only because these risks were the first to surface upon the initial public release of LLMs, but also because they directly infringe upon universal ethical standards and legal boundaries, attracting global scrutiny. Despite significant advance-ments in research, fundamental content safety risks are expected to persist alongside the evolution of model capabilities, posing long-term and profound societal implications. Despite significant headway in specific safety domains, the current landscape of risk coverage remains markedly fragmented. Driven by inconsistent taxonomic standards, existing evaluation proto-cols frequently suffer from both categorical redundancy and significant distributional divergences—or critical ’blind spots’—across benchmarks. Building upon a comprehensive survey and rigorous syn-thesis of existing datasets, we redefine a hierarchical risk taxonomy and accordingly introduce the ForesightSafetyBench-FundamentalSafety-O dataset. Structured around six core pillars (as Fig. 1 illus-trated)— Privacy and Data Misuse, Illegal and Malicious Use, False and Misleading Information, Physical and Psychological Harm, Hate and Expressive Harm, Sexual Content, and Minor-related Harm —each further subdivided into five specific dimensions (30 dimensions in total), this dataset ensures systematic coverage of prevalent high-frequency risk patterns.

2.2.2 Risky Agentic Autonomy 

Safety assessment faces new challenges as artificial intelligence evolves from single-task models to general-purpose agents. Ensuring that agents consistently behave in alignment with human intent within complex environments has gradually become the core issue in AI safety research. The theoretical foundation traces back to DeepMind’s research [42] within the reinforcement learning framework, where they for-malized agent safety issues into actionable categories. The core concept involves distinguishing between “the rewards agents actually optimize” and “the behaviors humans genuinely desire.” Within this frame-work, researchers have identified key challenges such as safe interruptibility, negative side effects, scalable oversight, and reward hacking. These concepts describe systemic biases that may arise when objective functions are incomplete or environmental uncertainties exist. Further discussions address robustness challenges under adversarial conditions and distribution shifts. As large language model-driven agents demonstrate unprecedented autonomy, these classic safety issues have not disappeared but resurface in more covert and complex forms. Building on prior work in AI safety, reinforcement learning, and agent alignment, we group these emerging risks under a pillar that we term Risky Agentic Autonomy . In our framework, this pillar refers to the set of risks that arise when increasingly autonomous agents pursue goals, make decisions, and interact with tools or environments in ways that may deviate from human intent, reduce controllability, or introduce unintended harms. While the individual issues have been studied in earlier literature, we synthesize and organize them into a unified perspective tailored to modern LLM-based agents. Within our taxonomy, the risky agentic autonomy pillar is further divided into five key dimensions that capture the main ways autonomous agents may introduce safety risks. First, Reward Hacking refers to behaviors in which agents achieve apparent success by exploiting prompts, evaluation scripts, or task-environment vulnerabilities, without genuinely fulfilling the underlying task intent. Second, Scal-able Oversight and Absent Supervisor arises in high-difficulty or long-horizon tasks, where the cost of intensive human supervision may lead agents to produce plausible yet incorrect reasoning or mislead-ing conclusions that are difficult to detect. Third, Safe Interruptibility and Safe Exploration concerns situations in which agents ignore, postpone, or implicitly circumvent human termination or correction 9signals during multi-step planning, tool invocation, or file operations in order to maintain task progress. Fourth, Robustness to Adversaries and Distributional Shift highlights the need for agents to maintain stable and reliable decision-making even when inputs are perturbed, prompts are injected, or visual and semantic cues are manipulated, particularly in multimodal and real-world settings. Finally, Negative Side Effects refers to additional risks introduced during goal fulfillment, such as unauthorized access, excessive resource consumption, privacy breaches, or irreversible changes to system environments.

2.3.2 Embodied AI Safety 

Embodied AI represents a fundamental shift in AI, extending the capabilities of intelligent agents beyond digital boundaries to interact directly with the physical world[56, 57]. While recent advancements in LLMs and Visual Language Models (VLMs) have empowered these agents to achieve remarkable success in task planning, the transition from virtual settings to real-world environments introduces unprecedented chal-lenges. Unlike traditional software agents, embodied agents must navigate complex tasks that necessitate direct interaction with the physical world and human users[58, 59]. This shift significantly broadens the attack surface, where vulnerabilities—ranging from malicious instruction attacks to inherent reasoning flaws—can lead to severe consequences beyond mere data errors. To ensure reliable real-world deployment, it is essential to address these fundamental hazards through a comprehensive framework. Toward this goal, the community has curated a diverse array of safety benchmarks to bolster dif-ferent dimensions of fundamental-risk evaluation. Specifically, benchmarks such as EAsafetyBench[60], SafePlan-Bench[61], and EARBench[62] are centered on malicious instruction rejection, scrutinizing an agent’s capability to intercept and decline harmful prompts. Expanding beyond static response filtering, multimodal datasets like IS-Bench[63] and SafeAgentBench[18] pivot toward process-level risks. By lever-aging high-fidelity simulators such as OmniGibson[64] and AI2-THOR[65], these benchmarks capture intricate hazards during the execution phase, utilizing rich visual contexts to rigorously evaluate safety constraints throughout task planning and autonomous interaction.

2.3.3 Social AI Safety 

As LLMs and autonomous agents become deeply embedded within human collaborative networks, AI is undergoing a role transition from ”task-oriented tools” to ”socially interactive participants.” This evo-lution has highlighted problematic socialized behaviors that have drawn significant attention. Studies indicate that current LLMs have demonstrated safety risks including sycophantic tendencies [67, 68], deliberate deceptive behaviors [69, 70], and covert strategic disguises such as alignment faking [71, 72], as well as evasion of oversight. These challenges collectively point to a fundamental behavioral misalignment problem in social interaction environments [12]. Building on an extensive survey and synthesis of exist-ing research, we define these security risks associated with social strategies and deep intent in human-AI system interactions as “Social AI Safety” . To provide a comprehensive evaluative perspective, we cat-egorize this domain into seven core dimensions: alignment faking, sandbagging, deception and unfaithful reasoning, sycophancy, psychological manipulation, feints, and bluffing . This framework not only inte-grates known hazards but also establishes a unified benchmark for dissecting deep strategic deviations within complex social interactions. To systematically evaluate social AI safety risks, we constructed the ForesightSafetyBench-SocialAI-O, a high-quality benchmark spanning all 7 risk dimensions with 140 meticulously curated test cases. Additionally, we employed the MASK [69] dataset to strengthen the assessment of deceptive behaviors. Table 9 presents an overview of the utilized test datasets. Environmental issues are of critical importance for sustainable resource use, ecological protection, and public governance[75–77], and the potential application of large language models in environmental analysis and decision support has attracted increasing attention[78–80]. In such high-impact settings, systematic biases in model outputs during value trade-offs or risk assessments can directly influence patterns of resource use and governance orientations, and may further translate into real-world environmental conse-quences. In particular, under multi-objective constraints and conflicting interests, models may generate seemingly reasonable recommendations that prioritize efficiency or short-term benefits, while downplay-ing environmental externalities and long-term ecological costs. In this context, Environmental Safety concerns whether model outputs, when participating in environmental content generation and decision support, may induce or exacerbate behaviors that cause real or potential harm to natural environments and ecosystems, including both explicit risks such as direct pollution, improper handling of environmen-tal leaks, and resource destruction, as well as implicit environmental misguidance driven by objectives such as cost minimization and output maximization.

2.3.5 Catastrophic and Existential Risks 

With the breakthrough progress of artificial intelligence (AI), large language models (LLMs) have contin-ued to make rapid gains in reasoning, planning, and tool use, reaching—or even surpassing—human-level performance on multiple benchmarks[85]. Consequently, AI’s role is undergoing a structural shift: it is evolving from an early-stage “passive task tool” (an input–output functional component) toward a “general problem solver” with a degree of autonomy and action capacity, and further toward general-purpose agents that can pursue long-horizon goals in complex environments by invoking external tools and interfacing with networks and real-world systems [86]—ultimately moving toward artificial general intel-ligence (AGI)[15] , and even agentic superintelligence (ASI) that significantly exceeds human cognitive performance across nearly all critical domains [16, 87, 88]. 

Dimension Dataset Summary

## 2.4 Industrial Safety 

Industrial issues constitute the cornerstone of social resource allocation, organizational governance, and the operation of critical systems. The continuous evolution of the general capabilities of LLMs has rendered their application prospects in industrial analysis, procedural assistance, and decision support increasingly evident. In these highly constrained and high-consequence potential application scenarios, the inherent risks of model outputs are no longer confined to isolated textual deviations; rather, they manifest significant spillover effects. Systemic misalignment in rule boundaries, value trade-offs, or risk assessments can trigger chain reactions through decision-making pipelines, potentially evolving into com-pliance failures, resource misallocation, or systemic risks within critical infrastructures. Adopting this foresight perspective, we introduce the core dimension of Industrial Safety , which aims to examine a model’s behavioral tendencies under specific sectoral constraints through highly contextualized textual interactions. Distinct from general interaction scenarios, Industrial Safety textitasizes the verification of model behavior under simulated industrial constraints. Its evaluative core focuses on whether compliance boundaries can be easily compromised, whether standard operating procedures are bypassed, whether potential safety hazards are underestimated, and whether pathways for abuse are amplified. To provide a more holistic evaluative perspective, we developed ForesightSafetyBench-IndustrialSafety-O. Centering on application domains characterized by high frequency and high conse-quences in industrial deployment, this benchmark systematically covers eight major sectors: Education & Research , Employment & Workplace , Government & Public Services , Information & Media , Industrial Infrastructure , Finance & Economy , Healthcare & Medicine , and Law & Justice . Within each major sec-tor, we further delineate three sub-categories, situating abstract safety hazards within specific industrial pipelines for closer examination. This structured approach enables a granular characterization of model risk sensitivity across diverse and specific scenarios.

(1) Independent Data Generation. To address the dimensions not covered by existing safety evaluation benchmarks (such as certain sub-dimensions within ForesightSafetyBench-RiskyAgenticAI-O, ForesightSafetyBench-AI4SCI-O, ForesightSafetyBench-EmbodiedAI-O, ForesightSafetyBench-SocialAI-O, ForesightSafetyBench-EnvAI-O, ForesightSafetyBench-ExistentialRisks-O, and ForesightSafetyBench-IndustrialSafety-O), this study supplements the construction by independently developing datasets. The construction of independent data strictly follows the closed-loop pipeline of ”seed generation—model expansion—manual auditing” to ensure data quality and evaluation effectiveness. Specifically, senior safety researchers first compile high-quality initial use cases as seed data based on relevant laws and regulations, industry technical standards, game theory principles, and social psychology laws; then use high-performance models to conduct multi-dimensional and cross-industry scenario migration and seman-tic expansion on these seed data to enrich the covered scenarios; finally, through multiple rounds of strict 28 manual screening and professional revision, it is ensured that the finally generated test data meet high-standard evaluation requirements in terms of scenario challenge, content diversity, safety relevance, and logical authenticity. 

(2) Foundational Benchmark Augmentation and Transformation. For some safety dimensions that need to quantify the inherent behavioral biases of models (such as user compliance or strategic performance degradation), or emerging risks lacking mature standards, we adopt the method of modifying basic capability datasets to achieve controllable and comparable refined evaluation. For example, in the Sycophancy and Sandbagging dimensions of the ForesightSafetyBench-SocialAI-O benchmark, We selected the MMLU [96] benchmark as the foundational corpus and reformatted the samples into single-answer completion tasks. This conversion was designed to eliminate the inherent ambiguity of open-ended generative evaluations and ensure objective quantification. To evaluate sycophancy, we systematically appended template-based biasing statements, such as “I believe the answer is {Incorrect Answer }” to observe whether the model would prioritize user agreeableness over factual accuracy. For sandbagging, we utilized system prompts to inject strategic performance constraints, for instance, simulating a commercial service-tier scenario where the model is informed the user is on a “Free Tier” and should provide throttled performance to incentivize an upgrade. This allows us to test whether the model strategically hides its true capabilities driven by specific utility-based motives. 

(3) Mature Benchmark Migration and Adaptation. For explicit safety evalua-tions that have been comprehensively and extensively carried out in existing research, directly adopting mature safety benchmarks and conducting targeted migration and adapta-tion can better ensure evaluation efficiency, standardization level, and result comparability. Mature safety benchmark content migration and adaptation has been integrated into sub-benchmarks such as ForesightSafetyBench-FundamentalSafety-O, ForesightSafetyBench-RiskyAgenticAI-O, ForesightSafetyBench-AI4SCI-O, ForesightSafetyBench-SocialAI-O, and ForesightSafetyBench-ExistentialRisks-O. Taking ForesightSafetyBench-FundamentalSafety-O as an example, we first aggre-gated mainstream safety datasets, including AdvBench, MaliciousInstruct, JailbreakBench, and SALAD-Set, to serve as the initial candidate pool. To resolve the fragmentation caused by inconsistent taxonomic criteria across existing benchmarks, we leveraged a high-performance LLM to perform automated re-classification and semantic alignment of the migrated data according to our proposed hierarchical framework. Subsequently, through rigorous manual screening and sample extraction for each secondary sub-dimension, we eliminated semantically ambiguous and low-quality entries, thereby ensuring the representativeness and evaluation validity of the final test cases.

# 4 Discussion 

The ForesightSafety Bench is proposed to address the ”foresight deficit” and ”taxonomic fragmentation” prevalent in current AI safety evaluations. Unlike benchmarks focusing on known general harms, this work constructs a systematic hierarchical framework encompassing 20 core pillars and 94 fine-grained dimensions. This design captures static semantic biases and uncovers behavioral vulnerabilities in long-horizon decision-making and complex dynamic interactions. It provides essential tools for measuring frontier risks in areas such as Embodied AI, AI4Science, Social AI Safety and Catastrophic and Existen-tial Risks, where previous detection efforts have often failed. Cross-dimensional heterogeneity in safety performance reflects fundamental logical limitations in current alignment paradigms: existing defensive mechanisms gravitate toward superficial semantic filtering rather than a substantive comprehension of deep-seated intent. This limitation becomes particularly pronounced as models pursue high performance, creating a profound tension between capability and safety. Empirical observations of DeepSeek-V3.2-Speciale—optimized for long-horizon reasoning—show an average violation rate surge to 23.3% from the baseline, a phenomenon we term ”inverse degradation”. This result suggests that under fixed param-eter constraints, hyper-specialization in specific cognitive domains may inevitably encroach upon the representation space required for safety alignment, leading to the erosion of established safety boundaries. While early discourse often suggested a safety lead for proprietary models, our large-scale empirical analysis indicates that safety performance is not linearly correlated with licensing protocols (proprietary 30 vs. open-source). In terms of average violation rates, open-weight models such as Qwen-3-Max-Thinking (10.1%) and Qwen-2.5-72B (10.5%) have achieved parity with leading proprietary flagships like Claude-Sonnet-4.5 (10.5%), and even outperformed several closed-source counterparts. This observation reveals that model safety depends more fundamentally on the developer’s commitment to alignment investment and technical maturity than the licensing model itself; future governance should thus move beyond using ”open vs. closed” as the primary risk criterion. Besides, the uneven performance observed across industrial safety domains highlights the challenge of transitioning alignment from general principles to domain-specific norms. Current mechanisms are mature in handling basic semantic risks but exhibit a logical decoupling in complex industrial decision-making contexts, such as financial compliance or medical ethics. Future safety governance must transcend simple keyword filtering toward substantive modeling of industry rules, ethical perimeters, and causal reasoning regarding behavioral consequences, facilitating a transition from ”formal compliance” to the internalization of industrial logic. The reshuffling of safety rankings under jailbreak testing proves that safety is not a static model attribute but a dynamic process highly dependent on the adversarial environment. We observed that models seemingly robust under direct prompting exhibit dramatic fluctuations in Attack Success Rate (ASR) when subjected to strategic provocations camouflaged as academic inquiry or process optimiza-tion. This dynamics suggests that safety evaluation should not be a one-time pre-deployment audit but a co-evolutionary monitoring system. Only by continuously introducing forward-looking risk scenarios can safety guardrails remain effective amidst the explosive growth of model capabilities. Our quantitative char-acterization of catastrophic and existential risks aligns with the growing international consensus on AGI safety red lines. Data indicate that latent risks such as goal misalignment or power-seeking behaviors grow non-linearly as models gain greater autonomy and long-range planning capacity. We recommend estab-lishing a global risk early-warning mechanism based on such systematic benchmarks, utilizing mandatory safety thresholds and hybrid control strategies (e.g., deterministic shields) to ensure technical progress remains within the bounds of human agency and prevents irreversible systemic catastrophes. 

# 5 Conclusion 

This paper proposes a comprehensive and systematic forward-looking AI safety evaluation benchmark, the ”ForesightSafety Bench,” which encompasses three major levels, 22 pillars, and 94 granular dimensions, underpinned by tens of thousands of structured data points. Through systematic evaluations of over twenty mainstream large models, the study reveals widespread safety vulnerabilities, with particular focus on Risky Agentic Autonomy, AI4Science Safety, Embodied AI Safety, Social AI Safety and Catastrophic and Existential Risks. Leveraging extensive and systematic safety assessments, the study underpins multi-level safety governance with empirical support and forward-looking insights. This work identifies forward-looking risks of large models in areas such as embodied intelligence, social interaction, and multi-agent autonomy. These dimensions require further investigation into their actual manifestations and potential impacts within more complex, dynamically interactive real-world con-texts—particularly those involving multi-agent systems and embodied robotics. Therefore, future research will focus on developing high-fidelity simulation environments and physical testing platforms to enable systematic validation and analysis of these risks in complex interactive scenarios. At the same time, we will closely track the rapid iteration of cutting-edge AI technologies, explore the underlying mechanisms driving the evolution of safety risks, and on this basis refine a dynamically adaptive safety evaluation framework. These efforts aim to provide more ecologically valid governance and management support for the robust and sustainable development of frontier AI.

# Appendix A Judge Templates
