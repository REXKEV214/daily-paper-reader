# Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) have been widely applied to emotional support conversation (ESC). However, complex multi-turn support remains chal-lenging. This is because existing alignment schemes rely on sparse outcome-level signals, thus offering limited supervision for intermediate strategy deci-sions. To fill this gap, this paper proposes affective flow language model for emotional support conver-sation (AFlow), a framework that introduces fine-grained supervision on dialogue prefixes by mod-eling a continuous affective flow along multi-turn trajectories. AFlow can estimate intermediate util-ity over searched trajectories and learn preference-consistent strategy transitions. To improve strat-egy coherence and empathetic response quality, a subpath-level flow-balance objective is presented to propagate preference signals to intermediate states. Experiment results show consistent and significant improvements over competitive baselines in diverse emotional contexts. Remarkably, AFlow with a compact open-source backbone outperforms pro-prietary LMMs such as GPT-4o and Claude-3.5 on major ESC metrics. Our code is available at https://github.com/chzou25-lgtm/AffectiveFlow. 

# 1 Introduction

et al. , 2025 ] . ESC aims to alleviate user emotional distress through strategy-guided multi-turn dialogues [ Zhao et al. ,2024 ]. Traditional ESC systems face significant challenges in understanding complex emotional dynamics, generating contextually appropriate responses, and maintaining coherent strategy transitions across turns [ Cheng et al. , 2022 ]. To ad-dress these limitations, large language model (LLM)-based approaches have been increasingly adopted, leveraging their advanced capabilities in semantic understanding and open-domain dialogue to deliver more effective support [ Kang et al. , 2024; Wan et al. , 2025 ]. To address these issues, methods based on holistic super-vision and preference learning have emerged. To address these challenges, we propose AFlow (Fig-ure 1 (c)), an alignment framework for multi-turn ESC built on search-distilled, affective flow preference optimization (AFPO). AFlow associates each dialogue prefix with an in-termediate affective value derived from searched trajectories, providing dense supervision for turn-level strategy decisions beyond terminal rewards. Based on this signal, AFPO enforces a subpath-level flow-balance constraint so that preference in-formation is propagated consistently from later outcomes to intermediate states, improving long-horizon strategy progres-sion and stable supportive behavior. The contributions of this work are three-fold: (1) We formal-ize Affective Flow as a non-negative flow field over dialogue prefixes in multi-turn ESC, linking terminal affective outcomes to intermediate states under flow-balance constraints.(2) We propose AFPO, which enforces subpath-level flow-balance to obtain dense and structurally consistent supervision for intermediate strategy decisions. (3) We conduct extensive ex-periments and the results show consistent improvements in strategy macro-F1 and response diversity, while preserving competitive generation quality. 

# 2 Related Work 

Emotional support conversation (ESC) has evolved from su-pervised, strategy-conditioned generation and structure-aware modeling that explicitly predicts or constrains supportive ac-tions [ Liu et al. , 2021; Tu et al. , 2022; Cheng et al. , 2022; Deng et al. , 2023; Zhang et al. , 2024 ] to LLM-based sup-porters that increasingly rely on prompting, self-/synthetic-data augmentation, and preference-driven alignment to shape response behavior [Zheng et al. , 2024; Kang et al. , 2024; Sun et al. , 2025 ]. However, many of these alignment schemes optimize response-level preferences or dialogue-level outcomes, which under-specify intermediate strategy deci-sions and weaken long-horizon credit assignment in multi-turn support [Rafailov et al. , 2023; Ouyang et al. , 2022; Meng et al. , 2024; Shen et al. , 2026; Wan et al. , 2025; Zhao et al. , 2025 ]. Meanwhile, strategy-aware refinements introduce more structured supervision via planning, dynamics model-ing, or explicit strategy optimization, but often require addi-tional components and still do not enforce globally consistent value propagation across dialogue subpaths [Zhou et al. , 2023; Cheng et al. , 2023; Wan et al. , 2025; Zhao et al. , 2025; Zhao et al. , 2025; Madani and Srihari, 2025; Shen et al. ,2025a ]. This work introduces AFlow, which derives prefix-level affective supervision from search-constructed trajecto-ries and enforces subpath flow-balance constraints to propa-gate preference-consistent value through dialogue prefixes, en-abling efficient long-horizon strategy alignment beyond coarse sequence-level preferences. More detailed discussion of re-lated work is provided in Appendix A.

# 4 Methodology 

In this section, we present the Affective Flow Language Model (AFlow) (see Figure 2). AFlow is realized through three stages: (i) MCTS-based Affective Flow signal construction that derives psychology-grounded, fine-grained supervision for strategy-level decisions; (ii) Affective Flow Preference Optimization (AFPO) that maps constructed flow signals into flow targets over intermediate dialogue states and optimizes a subpath-level flow-balance objective to propagate preferences across dialogue trajectories; (iii) Lightweight inference that selects supportive actions using the learned policy prior and value guidance.

Training Procedure. Training of πθ and Vϕ starts after ob-taining the searched trajectories T and the associated estimates 

bQ(s) and bQ(s, a ), as described in section 4.1. Using these pre-computed estimates, model parameters are optimized by minimizing the overall AFPO objective LAFPO , which com-bines subpath flow-balance supervision with edge-level consis-tency induced by bQ(s, a ). This training enforces prefix-level affective-flow consistency across full trajectories, enabling context-aware evaluation of candidate next strategies while preserving generation diversity (see Appendix C for a proof). 

4.3 Affective Reasoning 

Given a dialogue state st (the conversation history up to turn 

t), a compact candidate set At ⊂ A is constructed by selecting the top-K strategies under the policy prior πθ (· | st). Each candidate strategy is scored by combining the policy prior with the learned action value: 

score( a | st) = log πθ (a | st) + Vϕ(st, a ). (25) This scoring combines the policy prior for calibrated explo-ration with the learned value signal for preference-aware strat-egy selection. The selected strategy is

# 5 Experiments 

In this section, we present the experimental setup and re-sults. We address the following research questions (RQs): RQ1: Does AFlow outperform other methods? RQ2: Can AFlow’s affective flow signal distinguish fine-grained affec-tive states in multi-turn ESC? RQ3: Is AFlow effective across dialogue stages (Exploration, Comforting, and Action)? RQ4: Does AFlow show a stable, improving turn-wise affective flow trajectory? RQ5: Are AFlow’s supportive strategy choices reasonable and diverse across turns? RQ6: Does the main component of AFlow work effectively? 

5.1 Datasets 

To evaluate the method, training and evaluation are conducted using two in-domain ESC datasets as seed data. The two datasets include ExTES [ Zheng et al. , 2023 ] and ESConv [ Liu 

et al. , 2021 ]. To reduce the strategy search space and im-prove computational efficiency, we merge semantically similar strategies into eight categories. For seed initialization, we sample 1,000 and 500 dialogues from ExTES and ESConv, respectively, ensuring coverage of all emotional support sce-narios. Detailed dataset statistics and preprocessing are pro-vided, and the sampling criteria and procedure are described in Appendix D.

Association for Computational Linguistics. A Related Work 

A.1 Emotional Support Conversation 

Emotional Support Conversation (ESC) studies multi-turn in-teractions where a supporter responds to a distressed seeker with appropriate empathy and supportive actions. Liu et al. [ Liu et al. , 2021 ] formalize ESC and release ESConv, es-tablishing a standard setting for strategy-aware supportive dialogue. Subsequent work strengthens strategy conditioning and interpretability by explicitly modeling supportive actions and their role in generation. MISC introduces mixed strategy awareness with external commonsense resources to guide sup-portive decisions [ Tu et al. , 2022 ], while ESCoT emphasizes interpretable ESC by exposing strategy-related reasoning in the system design [ Zhang et al. , 2024 ]. Beyond direct strategy prediction, structure-aware modeling and auxiliary supervi-sion signals have been explored to better connect dialogue context with supportive action selection, including lookahead strategy planning [Cheng et al. , 2022 ] , persona-augmented generation [ Cheng et al. , 2023 ], and knowledge-enhanced mixed-initiative ESC systems [ Deng et al. , 2023 ]. These ap-proaches inject useful inductive bias, but their supervision is typically localized (e.g., per-turn labels or auxiliary modules) and does not necessarily provide a globally consistent mech-anism for propagating downstream utility to earlier dialogue prefixes. 

A.2 LLM Supporters 

Recent work increasingly adopts large language models as ESC supporters, leveraging prompting and scalable data con-struction to shape response behavior. Self-/synthetic-data aug-mentation has been explored to improve ESC models using LLM-generated interactions, such as self-chats for synthetic supervision [ Zheng et al. , 2024 ]. At the same time, analyses show that LLM supporters can exhibit systematic preference biases and misalignment in ESC, motivating targeted prompt-ing and mitigation techniques [ Kang et al. , 2024 ] . Beyond single-agent prompting, simulation and collaboration-based paradigms have been studied, including socialized simulation settings [ Chen et al. , 2025 ] and LLM-based multi-agent collab-oration for ESC [Xu et al. , 2025 ] . Evaluation methodology has also progressed, with ESC-Eval providing systematic assess-ment protocols for LLM performance in ESC settings [ Zhao et al. , 2024 ] . Overall, LLM-centric approaches improve fluency and scalability, but they often rely on coarse control signals and may still struggle with long-horizon strategy consistency. 

A.3 Preference Alignment 

Preference-based alignment provides a flexible mechanism to steer generation without explicit strategy labeling. General alignment methods such as RLHF [Ouyang et al. , 2022 ] and DPO [Rafailov et al. , 2023 ] optimize models with comparison signals, and recent formulations further simplify preference optimization (e.g., SimPO) [ Meng et al. , 2024 ]. In ESC, pref-erence signals have been used to align responses with human judgments and to refine strategy-related behavior, including chain-style strategy optimization [Zhao et al. , 2025 ]. How-ever, many preference-driven schemes remain response-level or dialogue-level: they under-specify intermediate strategy decisions and therefore provide limited long-horizon credit assignment for multi-turn support. Related observations high-light the need for more fine-grained and process-aware learn-ing signals when optimizing with preferences. 

A.4 Strategy Refinement 

To strengthen intermediate supervision and improve long-horizon consistency, strategy-aware refinements introduce ad-ditional structure via planning, dynamics modeling, or explicit optimization. Lookahead strategy planning explicitly antic-ipates future dialogue evolution when selecting supportive actions [ Cheng et al. , 2022 ]. Dynamics modeling provides structured signals beyond single-turn labels, such as mod-eling mixed emotions and discourse dynamics for strategy prediction and generation [Wan et al. , 2025 ]. Reinforcement-learning-based objectives have also been explored for multi-turn ESC, including positive emotion elicitation as an ex-plicit target [ Zhou et al. , 2023 ] . Complementary directions include steering LLMs for long emotional support conversa-tions [ Madani and Srihari, 2025 ] and dynamic knowledge fil-tering with persona extraction for ESC [ Hao and Kong, 2025 ] .While these approaches supply stronger process guidance than purely response-level preferences, they typically require addi-tional components and still do not enforce a globally consistent value propagation mechanism across dialogue subpaths. 

A.5 AFlow Positioning 

AFlow is motivated by the gap above: multi-turn ESC benefits from dense, prefix-level supervision that can be propagated consistently through dialogue prefixes to support reliable long-horizon strategy alignment. The formulation leverages flow-balance principles [Bengio et al. , 2023 ] and derives prefix-level affective supervision from search-constructed trajecto-ries. By enforcing subpath flow-balance constraints, AFlow provides a structurally consistent credit assignment signal for intermediate strategy decisions beyond coarse sequence-level preference optimization. 

# B Simulation Environment and Prompt 

This appendix specifies the role-separated simulation environ-ment and the prompts used for dialogue expansion and turn-level scoring. The design focuses on (i) strict turn-level con-trollability (length and termination), (ii) suppressing meta arti-facts that confound strategy comparison, and (iii) grounding content in dialogue history so that differences are attributable to strategy execution rather than stylistic noise.

# D Datasets 

D.1 Seed Dialogue Sampling 

Seed dialogues are sampled from ExTES and ESConv. The seed pool contains 1,000 dialogues from ExTES and 500 dialogues from ESConv. Sampling is stratified by dataset-provided scenario/emotion labels to ensure broad label cov-erage. After coverage constraints are satisfied, the remaining sampling budget is allocated to approximately preserve the original label-frequency distribution. 

D.2 Structured Fields 

Each dialogue is normalized into a structured record with the following fields: • scene: scenario description or situational summary. • description: core background/problem statement (private when required). • memory: optional persistent constraints or preferences that are explicitly stated in the original dialogue; otherwise empty. 

D.3 MCTS Expansion Data 

For each seed dialogue, one dialogue tree is expanded using the MCTS procedure described in the main method. 

Configuration. 

• Number of trees: 1,000 (ExTES) and 500 (ESConv). • Maximum tree depth: 10. • Rollout: bounded-depth rollout with maximum depth 3. 

Recorded trajectories. For each tree, we extract trajectories from the recorded search process. We report an average of 10 trajectories per tree, counting only trajectories with more than 3 nodes. The average trajectory length is 8.25 turns (computed over retained trajectories). 

Search conventions. 

• Tree expansion stops when reaching the maximum depth. • During rollout, when an end token is generated, the rollout return is set to 0. • When a state is expanded for the first time, statistics for all strategy actions are initialized with Q = 0 and N = 0 .

Expanded volume. Under the above extraction rule, the expanded trajectory pool contains approximately 10,000 tra-jectories from ExTES and 5,000 from ESConv, totaling about 15,000 trajectories. D.3.1 Sub-path training signals 

AFPO constructs training signals from sub-paths along each retained trajectory, following the sub-path construction in the main method. The number of sub-path signals increases quadratically with trajectory length. With an average trajectory length of 8.25, this yields about 38 sub-path training instances per trajectory on average. Aggregated over the expanded pool, this corresponds to approximately 381,600 sub-path instances for ExTES and 190,800 for ESConv, totaling about 572,400 sub-path instances. Action-pair ranking supervision is addi-tionally constructed at visited states using the MCTS-derived preference ordering described in the main method. 

D.4 Generated Dialogue Statistics 

Statistics over expanded dialogues are summarized as follows: • Dialogue turns: mean 8.29; median 6. • Utterance length: mean 25.9 under the same tokeniza-tion/segmentation protocol used in training and evaluation. 

D.5 Iterative Tree Refresh 

Training is conducted iteratively. Dialogue trees are refreshed every 10 training iterations by re-running MCTS from the same seed pool using the updated models. Each refresh re-builds the expanded trajectory pool using the same configura-tion and trajectory extraction rules in Section D.3. 

D.6 D.6 Training–Evaluation Isolation 

Seed dialogues are split into training and evaluation partitions before any MCTS expansion. Training-time MCTS trees and trajectories are generated only from the training seed partition. Evaluation-time expanded dialogues are generated only from the evaluation seed partition using frozen models. Evaluation trees and trajectories are not used for training. 

# E Baselines 

This appendix details the baseline methods and the unified protocol adopted for fair comparison with AFlow. All base-lines are evaluated on the same expanded validation dialogues seeded from ExTES and ESConv. 

E.1 Unified Comparison Protocol 

All methods take the same dialogue prefix (history up to turn t)as input and produce the next supporter response. Automatic metrics generation quality (BLEU/ROUGE-L/METEOR), di-versity (Dist-1/2), and fluency (perplexity)—are computed using the same preprocessing, tokenization, and evaluation scripts for every method. Strategy alignment metrics (Accu-racy/F1) are reported under the unified eight-category strat-egy set used throughout the paper. When a baseline method uses a different strategy taxonomy, its strategy outputs are mapped to the unified label space by a deterministic many-to-one mapping based on semantic equivalence, so that strat-egy Accuracy/F1 are comparable across all methods. For preference-based evaluation, we apply the same judge rubric, prompt format, and sampling procedure across methods. For LLM-based generators, decoding constraints (e.g., maximum length, stopping criteria, and sampling hyperparameters) are kept consistent, and no additional search, filtering, reranking, or post-hoc rewriting beyond a baseline’s original specification is introduced. 

E.2 Baseline Families 

We categorize baselines by modeling formulation. 

Task and interpretability-oriented ESC systems. 

BlenderBot-Joint [Liu et al. , 2021 ] , ESCoT [Zhang et al. , 2024 ], and IntentionESC [Zhang et al. , 2025 ] represent ESC systems that emphasize task formulation and/or interpretability-oriented design. These methods provide structured reference points for strategy-aware support beyond unconstrained open-ended generation, and serve as representative baselines for evaluating whether AFlow improves multi-turn supportive behavior under a unified protocol. 

Strategy- and knowledge/persona-enhanced ESC models. 

MISC [ Tu et al. , 2022 ] , KEMI [ Deng et al. , 2023 ], EmoDy-namiX [Wan et al. , 2025 ], and dynamic knowledge filtering with persona extraction [Hao and Kong, 2025 ] represent mod-els that incorporate explicit strategy signals and/or auxiliary knowledge and persona cues to strengthen context grounding and strategy execution. They are included to test whether AFlow’s prefix-level supervision and value propagation pro-vide gains beyond adding structured signals or external context alone. 

Test-time LLM frameworks, simulation/value-driven methods, and classic empathetic generators. MultiAgen-tESC [Xu et al. , 2025 ] , SocialSim [ Chen et al. , 2025 ] , and Value Reinforcement [Kim et al. , 2025 ] represent test-time frameworks that leverage simulation-, multi-agent-, or value-related guidance at inference time. MoEL [ Lin et al. , 2019 ]and MIME [Majumder et al. , 2020 ] are classic pre-LLM em-pathetic generators and serve as non-LLM reference baselines. In addition, strong proprietary LLM baselines (GPT-4o and Claude-3.5) are included as high-capability prompt-based sup-porters under the same evaluation protocol, providing compet-itive modern reference points. 

# F Evaluation Metrics and Evaluation Details 

This appendix specifies the evaluation protocol, including the evaluation set construction, automatic metrics, strategy alignment measurement, and pairwise preference evaluation. 

F.1 Evaluation Sets 

Evaluation is conducted on expanded validation dialogues constructed from two in-domain ESC datasets (ESConv and ExTES). Seed dialogues are first normalized into the structured fields scene, description, and memory. Each seed instance is then expanded into a trajectory tree using offline MCTS under the role-based simulation environment in §4.1. The evaluation dialogues are sampled from the resulting trees by extracting root-to-leaf paths that satisfy the same validity constraints used during training-time trajectory collection. The evaluation set is strictly separated from the trajectory trees used to optimize model parameters, and generated dialogues are scored only after the generator is fixed. F.2 Automatic Metrics 

Automatic evaluation reports three complementary aspects: generation quality, diversity, and fluency. Generation quality is measured by BLEU and ROUGE-L to capture n-gram overlap, and METEOR to account for token-level matches with flexible alignment. Diversity is measured by Dist-1 and Dist-2 (ratio of distinct unigrams/bigrams in generated outputs), reflecting lexical variety and mitigating repetition. Fluency is measured by perplexity under a fixed language model scorer consistent across methods, ensuring comparability of fluency estimates under the same scoring distribution. 

F.3 Strategy Alignment Metrics 

Therapeutic effectiveness is operationalized by supportive strategy alignment under the unified eight-category strategy set used throughout the paper. For each generated supporter utterance, a strategy label is assigned by the strategy classifier described in §5. Strategy Accuracy and macro-F1 are then computed by comparing predicted labels with the reference strategy labels in the evaluation data. When a baseline method uses a different taxonomy, strategy outputs are mapped to the unified eight-category set by a deterministic many-to-one mapping based on semantic equivalence before computing Accuracy/F1, ensuring that all methods are evaluated in the same label space. 

F.4 Pairwise Preference Evaluation 

Pairwise preference evaluation is conducted using two judge sources: GPT-5.2 and human experts. For each evaluation in-stance, two candidate dialogues are formed by pairing AFlow outputs with a baseline under identical dialogue contexts. Judges compare the paired outputs using an identical rubric across methods, reporting preferences on five dimensions: flu-ency, identification, comfort, suggest, and overall. Results are aggregated as Win/Tie/Lose percentages for each baseline comparison. 

F.5 Statistical Reporting 

For automatic metrics, all methods are evaluated on the same set of dialogue contexts, and results are aggregated by dataset. For preference evaluation, comparisons are conducted on matched pairs under the same contexts, and the reported Win/Tie/Lose rates are computed over the full set of judged pairs. When multiple judges are used, aggregation is per-formed per dimension and then averaged across instances under the same rubric. 

F.6 Reproducibility Notes 

All evaluation scripts use fixed preprocessing and tokenization for metric computation, and identical decoding constraints are applied across LLM-based generators to control for length and termination effects. The same prompts and rubric templates are used for all LLM-based judges, and human evaluation follows the same dimension definitions to ensure consistency between judge sources. 

# G Implementation Details 

This appendix specifies the implementation details of AFlow, including the model architecture, the offline MCTS data con-struction pipeline, the AFPO optimization setup, and the com-pute configuration used for training and evaluation. 

G.1 Model Architecture 

Backbone and heads. AFlow is instantiated with an instruction-tuned LLM backbone. Two lightweight heads are attached to the shared backbone: (i) a strategy policy head that parameterizes πθ (a | s) over the predefined strategy set A,and (ii) a value head that parameterizes Vϕ(s) and an action-conditional scorer Vϕ(s, a ) used by AFPO. All parameters are optimized under the AFPO objective in §4.2. 

Parameter-efficient tuning. The policy/value heads and the backbone are trained with a parameter-efficient setup (e.g., LoRA-style adapters), while the base vocabulary and tokenizer remain unchanged. The adapter configuration follows a stan-dard setting, including rank r, scaling α, dropout p, and target modules {modules }.

G.2 Offline MCTS Data Construction 

Role-separated simulation environment. Offline tree ex-pansion is conducted in a role-based environment with three agents: Seeker (help-seeker simulator), Supporter (interaction counterpart conditioned on strategies), and Rewarder (turn-level scorer). Each environment is instantiated by a single LLM family, so that Seeker/Supporter/Rewarder come from the same family (GPT-4o, Claude-3.5, or Gemini-1.5), match-ing the robustness setting in Table 2. 

Tree budget and structure. For each seed dialogue, a trajec-tory tree is constructed offline with maximum depth D = 10 .The search produces a directed tree whose nodes correspond to dialogue prefixes and whose edges correspond to one-step strategy-conditioned extensions. The final tree corpus in-cludes: (i) 1000 trees expanded from ExTES seeds and (ii) 500 trees expanded from ESConv seeds. Among paths with more than three nodes, the average number of valid paths per tree is approximately 10, and the average path length is approximately 8 turns.

This appendix presents an ablation-style sensitivity analysis of the offline MCTS configuration used for affective trajectory construction (§4.1). The analysis varies the maximum tree depth, the rollout depth L, and the number of MCTS sim-ulations per root, then measures their impact on generation quality, diversity, strategy alignment, and turn-wise reward trajectory stability. The goal is to justify the default configura-tion used in the main experiments from a performance–cost perspective. 

I.1 Experimental Setup 

All settings follow §4–§4.2 unless stated otherwise. For each variant, offline MCTS is used to build affective trajectory trees in the same role-separated environment, and the resulting tra-jectory data are used to train (πθ , V ϕ) with the same AFPO objective (Eq. 24). Training budget, dataset splits, and evalua-tion protocol are kept identical across variants. 

Variables. Three MCTS hyperparameters are varied one at a time while fixing the others to their default values. 

(1) Maximum tree depth. The maximum depth is varied as 

D ∈ { 6, 8, 10 , 12 }, with the default D = 10 .

(2) Rollout depth. The rollout depth used in the bounded-depth rollout (§4.1) is varied as L ∈ { 1, 2, 3, 4}, with the default L = 3 . The terminal-handling rule remains unchanged. 

(3) Number of simulations. Let S denote the number of MCTS simulations per root state. The sensitivity set uses three budgets S ∈ { 10 , 20 , 40 }, with default S = 20 .

Metrics. Each configuration is evaluated using the same metrics as the main paper: strategy alignment (Strategy F1), diversity (Dist-2), fluency (PPL), and reward trajectory stabil-ity. Trajectory stability is measured on the same dialogue set as RQ4 by aggregating turn-wise reward fluctuations of the gen-erated dialogues; specifically, we report average adjacent-turn absolute change (lower indicates smoother trajectories). 

I.2 Recommended Configuration 

Considering the above trends and the performance–cost trade-off, the default configuration used in the main paper is rec-ommended: maximum tree depth D = 10 and rollout depth 

L = 3 , together with the default simulation budget S = 20 .This setting provides sufficient long-horizon supervision for subpath flow-balance while maintaining stable reward trajecto-ries, and avoids the diminishing returns observed with deeper trees, longer rollouts, or larger simulation budgets under the same overall compute constraints.

This appendix provides qualitative evidence for how AFlow manifests strategy progression and affective-flow consistency 

in real multi-turn emotional support dialogues, together with representative failure modes of strong baselines. All identities are anonymized and candidate order is randomized when presenting comparisons. 

J.1 Case Selection Criteria 

Cases are selected to cover diverse emotional contexts and to expose long-horizon decision points where intermediate credit assignment matters. Selection follows three criteria. 

(1) Diverse emotional scenes. Cases are sampled from dis-tinct emotion or situation categories (e.g., anxiety, sadness/-grief, self-doubt, interpersonal conflict, academic/work stress) to avoid overfitting qualitative conclusions to a single domain. 

(2) Comparable context length and difficulty. Dialogues are selected with similar turn budgets and comparable difficulty (e.g., multiple user constraints, ambiguous goals, or repeated negative affect) so that improvements cannot be attributed to trivial contexts. 

(3) Clear decision points. Each case contains at least one turn where strategy selection is non-trivial (e.g., whether to continue exploration, validate, or move to action-oriented guid-ance). These are the turns where flow-based prefix supervision is expected to change decisions.
