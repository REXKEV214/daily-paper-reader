## Abstract 

The increasing integration of AI-powered tools into expert work-flows, such as medicine, law, and finance, raises a critical question: how does AI involvement influence a user’s trust in the human expert, the AI system, and their combination? To investigate this, we conducted a user study ( 𝑁 = 77 ) featuring a simulated course-planning task. We compared various conditions that differed in both the presence of AI and the specific mode of human-AI col-laboration. Our results indicate that while the advisor’s ability to create a correct schedule is important, the user’s perception of ex-pertise and trust is also influenced by how the expert utilized the AI assistant. These findings raise important considerations for the design of human-AI hybrid teams, particularly when the adoption of recommendations depends on the end-user’s perception of the recommender’s expertise.

## 1 Introduction 

Across domains such as healthcare, financial advising, legal consul-tation, and others, people depend on human experts to help them navigate complex decisions [ 1, 7 , 10 , 13 ]. Trust in these experts forms the foundation of these interactions [ 8 ]. As AI systems in-creasingly enter these workflows, the locus of control shifts from a single expert toward a constellation of contributors involved in producing guidance [ 3]. Importantly, the mere presence of an AI system, independent of its specific behavior, may color users’ per-ceptions of the decision-making process, thereby reshaping how users interpret responsibility and credibility within the advisory process [ 15 ]. As of right now, it is unclear what the implications of such a shift in perception could be in advisory contexts. From an epistemic trust perspective, a lay user’s trust judgement about an expert can be decomposed into distinct judgments about their expertise, integrity, and benevolence [ 8 ]. In advisory settings, the advisor’s positive or negative performance provides evidence about advisor expertise (competence to satisfy constraints) [ 22 ], while the timing and visibility of AI oversight can introduce cues that affect integrity- and benevolence-related inferences (e.g., whether the advisor appears transparent, careful, and oriented toward the subject’s best interest). Understanding how AI involvement affects perceptions in each of these elements and other aspects is essen-tial [ 2] at a moment when AI integration is accelerating [ 9 , 14 ]. In this work, we focus on a specific form of AI integration: AI systems that provide oversight by intervening either reactively -automatically monitoring and flagging issues - or proactively, when invoked at the human advisor’s discretion. We examine how these different forms of AI assistance shape users’ trust judgments in expert-guided interactions. Prior work has examined trust in algorithmic systems [ 11 , 12 ]and trust in human experts [ 8], but much less is known about how trust forms and changes when both entities contribute to a precision-dependent task, particularly how users interpret when and why AI systems intervene. For example, if the advisor fails to detect an error and the AI identifies it, the interaction creates a moment in which users may reconsider prior assumptions about the source of guidance involved in the interaction. By focusing on proactive and reactive support scenarios explicitly, we are able to test out such moments and dynamics. Additionally, such moments require users to analytically distinguish trust directed towards the advisor, AI, and the configuration as a whole. By separately measuring the

Figure 1, provides an overview of the various interaction modes we investigate in the paper. We leveraged an online academic advising simulation as a proxy task to study the effect of AI assistance on users’ perception of trust in each advisory entity. Academic advising offers a represen-tative environment for studying these dynamics, since advisors are expected to have expertise and specialized knowledge about the courses as well as the departmental and university degree require-ments [ 5 ]. At the same time, the stakes of advising are moderate enough that trust can be studied without the presence of potential confounders found in riskier domains such as medicine or finance. Moreover, the scenario naturally supports AI assistants that can run in both a proactive and a reactive mode (Section 4.1 provides a full overview of the experiment setup). In our experiments, we focus on measuring the user’s trust in the advisor (i.e, the expert), the AI assistant, and the whole team consisting of the AI assistant and the expert. Each measurement is carried out using existing questionnaires that have been previously validated through user studies. Our results show that advisory outcomes are the primary driver of trust-related consequences, with conditions involving errors evaluated less favorably across multiple measures. In particular, advisory shortcomings significantly undermines perceptions of the advisor’s epistemic expertise, as well as trust in general and reduces willingness to reuse the advisor or the system. Note that this unwillingness to reuse the advisor’s service persists despite the fact that the AI system identifies and corrects any mistake made by the advisor. These effects are not uniform across the interaction modes. Specifically, our results suggest that proactive AI consultation by experts may further erode their perceived expertise among lay users. Additionally, the differences primarily emerged in evaluations of the human advisor, while trust and its aspects in the AI system and the human-AI team remain comparatively equal. Together, these findings indicate that while negative or positive performance affects overall trust judgments, AI involvement shapes where trust consequences are born following advisory errors. 

## 1.1 Contributions 

To summarize, the paper makes three contributions: (1) We present an interactive, workflow-level experimental setup for studying trust in human-AI advisory systems that enable separate trust judgments about the human advisor, the AI assistant, and their combined operation under controlled performance and integration configurations. (2) Using this setup in an academic advising task ( 𝑁 = 77 ), we provide empirical evidence that advisory outcomes are a primary driver of trust-related evaluations, while also sug-gesting that proactive versus reactive AI assistance can shape trust judgments even when advisory outcomes are success-ful. (3) We derive design implications for human-AI advisory work-flows, emphasizing the role of intervention timing, proce-dural visibility, and perceived responsibility (e.g., who ini-tiates checking or correction and how that role is surfaced) in structuring users’ trust in the advisor, the AI, and the advisory team. 

## 2 Hypotheses 

Our experiments were designed to test eight hypotheses correspond-ing to the five experimentally varied conditions. Each condition starts with the student being informed of the course scheduling task at hand then expressing a set of desired courses, and it ends with the student receiving a valid course schedule. The condition differed in terms of whether the advisor used AI assistants (Figure 1a represents the case where the advisor didn’t use any AI assis-tant), and if AI assistants were used, whether the assistants were proactive (Figure 1b) or reactive (Figure 1c). For scenarios with AI assistant usage, we considered both cases where the advisor either made a mistake or not. For the cases with advisor mistakes, the reactive assistant would point out the mistake unprompted, and in the proactive case, they would identify the mistake after the advisor asks the AI system to verify the schedule. In the two conditions where the advisor made no mistakes with AI in either capacity, the AI assistant’s role was simply reduced to signing off on the identified schedule. In our experimental setup, the dependent variables consist of information gathered via four different trust judgment measure-ment mechanisms. First, we use the epistemic trustworthiness of the advisor through the Hendriks et al . [8] questionnaire, which measures the advisor’s expertise, benevolence, and integrity sepa-rately. Next, we use a questionnaire adapted from Riedl et al . [20] to identify comparable, entity-level trust measurements about the ad-visor, the AI assistant, and the overall expert-AI assistant team. We also use a simple 100-point scale where the study participant can report their global trust in the advising system (consisting of both the advisor and the AI system). Finally, we asked the participants whether they would reuse this advisor. The bulk of our hypotheses focused on how the different conditions influenced the epistemic trustworthiness, in particular the perceived expertise, as captured via the Hendriks et al . [8] score. These choices were motivated by the fact that the influence of people’s perception of experts had more urgent ramifications than some of the other questions one could raise in this scenario. The specific hypotheses we tested across these conditions were motivated by intuitive expectations about how users interpret neg-ative and positive performance as well as AI involvement in expert-guided interactions. Prior work on trust in automation suggests that observed failures serve as salient signals that prompt trust recalibration, particularly when users rely on an expert when they could not personally verify or have the means to accomplish the task [ 4]. We therefore expected workflows where the advisor makes mistakes to produce lower expertise in the advisor (H2). Among successful workflows, we anticipated that AI modality would al-ter expert competency for the outcome. • H1: Mean trust in the advisor will differ significantly across the five conditions for aggregated trust measures along the Hendriks et al. [8] instrument and Riedl et al. [20].

• H5: Among conditions in which the advisor produces an er-rorless schedule, the perceived integrity (Hendriks et al . [8] )of the advisor will be higher when the advisor proactively consults AI (manual invocation) when compared to reactive (automatic) AI supervision.

## 3 Relevant Work 

Research on trust in automation, human-AI interaction, and so-cial epistemology provides complementary perspectives that mo-tivate our study design and trust measurement strategy. Rather than reviewing this literature paper by paper, we organize prior work around three questions central to our investigation: (i) how trust responds dynamically to observed performance and error, (ii) how procedural structure and responsibility cues shape trust in AI-supported expert workflows, and (iii) how trust should be mea-sured when multiple potential trustees are present within a single interaction. 

## 3.1 Trust as Dynamic and Performance-Contingent 

A central theme in the trust-in-automation literature is that trust is not a static disposition, but an evaluative judgment that governs reliance under conditions of uncertainty and vulnerability. Lee and See [ 11 ] conceptualize trust as mediating reliance decisions, empha-sizing that users update trust based on observed behavior rather than objective accuracy alone [ 12 ]. Trust is therefore expected to evolve over the course of interaction, particularly when users can-not independently verify correctness and must instead interpret outcome and process cues. Prior syntheses reinforce that trust judgments are sensitive to contextual factors such as task demands, perceived risk, and user expectations, and that perceived trustworthiness may diverge from formal performance metrics [ 23 ]. Across domains, observed errors are treated as especially salient events because they provide con-crete evidence that can prompt trust recalibration. Importantly, such recalibration is not uniform or irrational, but depends on how performance information is encountered and interpreted within a given interactional context. Empirical work further demonstrates that trust responses depend not only on whether an error occurs, but on how it is surfaced and addressed. Dzindolet et al . [4] show that feedback about system limitations and error characteristics supports more appropriate calibration, helping users avoid both overreliance and disuse. While Dzindolet et al. [ 4] focuses on automated aids, it motivates studying advisory interactions as sequences of events rather than as static treatments. In AI-supported advisory contexts, users may encounter bundles of cues - including advisor performance and corrective feedback - that jointly shape trust judgments. Recent studies in advisory settings similarly characterize trust as path-dependent and sensitive to error exposure. Han and Ko [ 7 ]demonstrate that salient errors can produce sharp trust declines that may be partially restored through subsequent repair, while Pareek et al. [ 18 ] show that trust can propagate across workflow stages when users can verify some components but not others. Together, this work motivates examining trust as an outcome shaped by complete interaction workflows that vary in both performance and correction dynamics. In the present study, this literature motivates two design choices. First, we treat an advisor’s failure to detect a task-preventing issue as a primary informational event when users cannot independently verify correctness. Second, we treat AI involvement as an explicit part of the interaction sequence rather than a background feature, allowing us to examine how different correction structures shape trust attribution. 

## 3.2 Procedural Cues, Epistemic Roles, and Responsibility 

Beyond performance outcomes, trust judgments are also shaped by how users interpret epistemic roles and responsibility within a workflow. In human–AI settings, identical outcomes may support different inferences depending on who appears to monitor, verify, and authorize decisions. Yang and Ma [ 24 ] argue that evaluations of AI systems involve judgments about epistemic authority and responsibility in context, motivating attention to role structure and procedural cues. Classic models of human–automation interaction similarly em-phasize the importance of procedural visibility. Parasuraman et al. [ 17 ] note that higher levels of automation can obscure ongoing human involvement, reducing users’ ability to distinguish between human and system contributions. Such shifts do not inherently un-dermine trust, but they may alter how users attribute responsibility for errors and corrections. Insights from social epistemology further suggest that trust is supported by mechanisms that evaluate an informant’s compe-tence and responsiveness to error rather than by passive acceptance alone [ 16 , 21 ]. Observable signals of monitoring and correction can therefore function as procedural evidence of epistemic responsibil-ity. Related work on calibration and credibility judgments shows that errors are particularly damaging when they are interpreted as evidence of poor self-assessment rather than as isolated out-comes [ 6, 22 ]. Together, these perspectives motivate examining AI oversight as a procedural feature that can shape how responsibility and trust consequences are allocated within expert workflows. 

## 3.3 Measuring Trust in Multi-Entity Advisory Contexts 

A methodological challenge in AI-supported advisory systems is that a single interaction may involve multiple plausible trustees. Users may evaluate the human advisor, the AI system, and their combined operation differently, and outcome evidence may not map cleanly onto a single target. Hendriks et al. [ 8 ] conceptualize epistemic trustworthiness as a multidimensional construct comprising expertise, integrity, and benevolence, providing a validated framework for assessing trust Implications of AI Involvement for Trust in Expert Advisory Workflows Under Epistemic Dependence FAccT ’26, June 2026, Montréal, QC, Canada 

in experts under epistemic uncertainty. However, such measures alone do not capture how trust is distributed across multiple enti-ties when AI is involved. Prior work demonstrates that users can meaningfully provide entity-specific trust judgments for human professionals, AI systems, and hybrid configurations using parallel item structures [20]. These measurement approaches support our design choice to assess epistemic trustworthiness for the human advisor while also measuring complentary forms of trust directed toward the advi-sor, the AI assistant, and their combined operation. Aligning trust measurement with the workflow-level unit of contribution allows us to interpret trust outcomes as reflections of how participants attribute epistemic responsibility based on observed outcomes and procedural cues. 

## 4 Methodology 4.1 Study Design 

The study was designed to examine how trust judgments form in response to observed advising sequences and patterns of AI involve-ment. Accordingly, each condition represents a distinct advising workflow that bundles advisor performance and AI involvement into a single scripted interaction. These elements were not manipu-lated as independent experimental factors. Instead, conditions were constructed as complete advising workflows with paired sources of variation, reflecting realistic patterns of advising behavior and AI use as they might occur in practice. This design enables targeted comparisons between practice-relevant workflows (e.g., advisor making mistakes vs. them identifying a correct schedule from the get go under comparable AI involvement, or different AI integration strategies under like outcomes) without decomposing the interac-tion into factorial main effects. Participants engaged in a simulated academic advising inter-action (Fig. 2) presented as a sequence of screenshots and system messages within a web-based interface [ 19 ], (Appendix Fig. 4). They were first presented with a brief description of the study’s purpose and procedure. Then they were informed that the study involved interacting with an AI-supported academic advisor and that the researchers were interested in understanding perceptions of this interaction in order to inform the effective integration of AI into everyday contexts. Participants were told that they would complete a course schedule planning task using the system and informed that the AI system, when present, acted in an oversight capacity with respect to the advisor. In all conditions, participants were in-structed to assume the role of the student. The participant’s task was to follow the guidance provided in the interaction without independently verifying degree requirements or prerequisites. A between-subjects design was chosen to avoid carryover and learning effects, particularly given that trust judgments are known to recalibrate based on prior performance (Lee and See [11] ). Each participant completed the task once and was randomly assigned to one of five predefined experimental conditions. Participants then completed the assigned advising workflow de-scribed in Fig. 2, proceeding at their own pace through the scripted interaction. Once the advising interaction was complete, partici-pants were directed to a post-task questionnaire assessing their perceptions of the advising experience, including trust-related eval-uations of the advisor, the AI system, and their combination. The target sample size ( 𝑁 = 77 ) was determined a priori through a power analysis to ensure sufficient sensitivity to detect differences in trust outcomes across conditions. The five experimental conditions were as follows (illustrative workflows available in Fig. 2): 

𝐶 𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑙𝑒𝑠𝑠 : the advisor-AI team produces a correct sched-ule without error, with proactive (advisor-invoked) AI not making a corrective intervention. 

𝐶 𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑠 : the advising workflow contains the advisor miss-ing a scheduling error, with correction initiated by the advisor through AI assistance. 

𝐶 𝑎𝑑𝑣 _𝑜𝑛𝑙 𝑦 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑙𝑒𝑠𝑠 : the advisor produces a correct schedule with-out any AI involvement. 

𝐶 𝑟𝑒𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑙𝑒𝑠𝑠 : the advisor-AI team produces a correct schedule without planning errors, with AI reactively (automatically) super-vising the interaction (here it translates to the AI system repeatedly affirming that the advisor is making the correct choices). 

𝐶 𝑟𝑒𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑠 : the advisor-AI team produces an incorrect sched-ule that is automatically flagged and corrected by the AI. Note that we didn’t have a condition without an AI assistant where the advisor missed an invalidating issue. This choice was made because we focused on scenarios in which expert mistakes stem from their lack of knowledge, and we wanted to ensure that the student received the correct schedule at the end of the process. As such, the remaining scenarios always demanded an external verifier to identify the expert mistakes. 

## 4.2 Participants 

Participants were recruited online through Prolific and completed the study remotely. Repeat participation was systematically re-stricted using Prolific’s built-in eligibility controls to maintain data integrity. At the time of analysis, 77 participants had completed the study. Participation was restricted to desktop or laptop users to en-sure consistent interaction with the advising interface. Participants accessing the study from non-desktop devices were excluded. In ad-dition, participants were excluded from analysis if they failed three attention-check questions embedded throughout the study, includ-ing within the interactive simulation and the final questionnaire. No other exclusion mechanisms were applied. Participants were compensated $4.50 for their time upon com-pletion of the study. All study procedures were approved by the institutional review board, and informed consent was obtained from all participants. Participants were assigned to conditions via Prolific’s randomization, and demographic information (when col-lected) is summarized in the Appendix. The study took approximately 17 minutes to complete, including the advising simulation and post-task questionnaire.

4.3.1 Epistemic Trustworthiness (METI). To assess participants’ epistemic trust in the advisor, we administered items from the Muen-ster Epistemic Trustworthiness Inventory (METI) [ 8 ]. METI opera-tionalizes epistemic trustworthiness along three dimensions: per-ceived expertise, integrity, and benevolence. Following the instru-ment’s dimensional structure, we computed composite scores for expertise, integrity, and benevolence by averaging item responses within each dimension (higher scores indicate stronger perceived epistemic trustworthiness). Item counts and internal consistency for each composite are reported in Appendix A.1. The full METI item set and dimension groupings used in this study are provided in Appendix A.2. 

4.3.2 Trust in the Advisor, AI System, and Team. In addition to epis-temic trustworthiness, we measured perceived general trust using three items adapted from Riedl et al. [ 20 ]. Items were reformulated to fit the academic advising scenario and to allow entity-specific judgments targeting (i) the advisor, (ii) the AI assistant (when appli-cable), and (iii) the advisor-AI team as a combined advisory entity. An example item is: “I can trust the information presented by the advisor.” Item responses were averaged for each target to form com-posite trust scores, with higher values indicating greater perceived trust. All composites demonstrated acceptable internal consistency (see Appendix A.1). 

4.3.3 General Trust. To capture a global assessment of trust that was not tied to a specific Likert instrument, participants also com-pleted a single-item general trust rating on a continuous 1-100 scale. This measure served as a holistic outcome capturing overall trust in the advising system unconstrained by specific item framing. 

4.3.4 Likelihood of Advisor Reuse. To assess behavioral intention, participants reported their likelihood of reusing the advisor in the future. This outcome was created as a single-item measure and analyzed separately from the multi-item trust composites. We treat reuse intention as a behavioral proxy rather than as a direct trust measure. Whereas trust instruments capture evaluative judgments (e.g., perceived expertise or trustworthiness), reuse in-tention reflects whether participants would act on those judgments by relying on the advisor again. Reuse intention is particularly Implications of AI Involvement for Trust in Expert Advisory Workflows Under Epistemic Dependence FAccT ’26, June 2026, Montréal, QC, Canada 

relevant in advisory settings, where the practical consequence of trust is often continued reliance over time. Including this measure, therefore, allows us to assess whether condition-level differences observed in epistemic trust or entity-level trust correspond to dif-ferences in anticipated reliance on the advisor. 

## 4.4 Limitations 

We took care in designing this simulation to approximate a realistic, though necessarily simplified, interaction involving AI-supported decision-making. Nevertheless, several limitations should be ac-knowledged. First, the use of a between-subjects design prevents individual participants from directly comparing different advising workflows or outcomes, which may limit insight into how trust recalibrates across repeated interactions. As with all controlled experimental studies, the findings may also be limited in their generalizability beyond the simplified ad-vising task, interface, and population studied. While we sought to enhance situational validity through a realistic advising scenario and grounded operationalizations of advisor performance and AI involvement, participants’ perceptions may differ in real-world advising contexts with higher stakes or longer-term relationships. Additionally, individual differences such as prior experience with academic advising, familiarity with AI systems, or general attitudes toward automation may have influenced trust judgments. These factors were not explicitly measured and therefore fall outside the scope of the present study. Future work could examine how such individual characteristics interact with advisor performance and AI involvement to shape trust, including whether they moderate which aspects of trust are affected and which remain stable.

## 5.5 Summary of Results 

Overall, trust perceptions varied across advising workflows in a manner primarily driven by advisory outcomes. Workflows that contained scheduling errors were evaluated less favorably than successful workflows across multiple measures, including perceived epistemic expertise, advisor-directed trust, and likelihood of advisor reuse. Consistent with H7, reuse intentions were higher in workflows characterized by errorless advisory (e.g., 𝐶 𝑎𝑑𝑣 _𝑜𝑛𝑙 𝑦 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑙𝑒𝑠𝑠 , 𝐶 𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑙𝑒𝑠𝑠 )than in workflows in which the advisor failed to detect an invali-dating issue ( 𝐶 𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑠 ). No significant effects were observed for integrity, benevolence, global trust, or trust judgments directed toward AI-only or hybrid advisory entities. Among workflows that produced errorless scheduling interac-tions, mean trust ratings differed descriptively across AI integration configurations, but no pairwise comparisons reached statistical sig-nificance at the current sample size ( 𝑁 = 77 ). This pattern suggests that while performance outcomes dominate trust judgments, pro-cedural differences in AI integration may exert more subtle effects. Finally, significant expertise-related contrasts were confined to comparisons involving advisor-only and proactive-AI-with-mistakes workflows. The absence of expertise (with respect to Hendriks et al .[8] ) differentiation among errorless AI-assisted workflows indicates that, when AI support is present, successful advisory outcomes do not translate into stronger or more differentiated perceptions of advisor expertise. One plausible explanation is that AI involvement introduces an additional potential source of competence, creating ambiguity about how much of the positive performance should be attributed to the advisor’s judgment versus the AI’s contribu-tion. This attributional ambiguity may attenuate advisor-specific expertise evaluations, effectively compressing perceived compe-tence across AI-assisted successes even when advising is without error. This pattern, while not conclusive, motivates further investi-gation into attribution and trust formation in AI-supported decision making. 

## 6 Discussion 

This study examined how trust judgments form in response to dif-ferent patterns of advisor performance and AI involvement within an interactive academic advising task. By operationalizing each condition as a complete advising workflow rather than isolating individual factors, our design reflects how AI-assisted advisory sys-tems are likely to be encountered in practice and allows trust to be interpreted at the level of interaction sequences. 

## 6.1 Trust Calibration to Performance and Correction 

Consistent with prior work on trust calibration [ 4], participants’ trust judgments were strongly shaped by observed advisory out-comes. Across measures of epistemic expertise, advisor trust, and reuse intention, workflows that resulted in correct schedules were evaluated more favorably than workflows that contained schedul-ing errors. Notably, the strongest trust penalties were concentrated in the advisor-initiated AI with scheduling mistakes condition ( 𝐶 𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑠 ). Although both mistake conditions involved observable errors, only 

𝐶 𝑝𝑟𝑜𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑠 produced significant reductions in perceived advi-sor expertise, aggregated trust, and reuse intention relative to suc-cessful workflows. The reactive mistake condition ( 𝐶 𝑟𝑒𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒 _𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑠 )was descriptively lower than successful workflows but did not ex-hibit comparable penalties in pairwise tests at the current sample size. This asymmetry suggests that trust consequences were shaped not by mistakes alone, but by how correction was initiated and made visible. One plausible interpretation is that visible, advisor-initiated con-sultation following an error signals insufficient calibration or com-petence, whereas automatic oversight enables correction without directly implicating the advisor’s epistemic judgment. As a result, responsibility for errors may be attributed more strongly to the ad-visor in proactive-AI with mistakes workflows, despite equivalent outcome structure. At the same time, trust judgments were not reducible to perfor-mance alone. Among workflows that including errorless advising interactions, descriptive differences emerged across AI integration strategies, but these differences did not reach statistical significance at 𝑁 = 77 . This pattern suggests that any within error-free effects of AI configuration are more subtle than the trust penalties associated with observed advisory errors. 

## 6.2 Implications for Human-AI Advisory Design 

Together, these findings highlight that trust in AI-supported ad-visory systems is shaped by both performance outcomes and the procedural structure of human-AI collaboration. Whether AI over-sight is automatic, advisor-initiated, or absent provides users with cues about responsibility, competence, and accountability. Given that perceived expertise and reuse were penalized specifically when advisors invoked AI after missing scheduling issues, and that auto-matic AI oversight was consistently associated with lower penalties relative to advisor-initiated AI, these results motivate system de-signs that make use of automatic AI oversight. Designers should therefore consider advisory workflows holistically, attending not only to accuracy but also to how correction, verification, and AI involvement are communicated during interaction. 

## 6.3 Generalizability and Broader Implications 

Although situated in an academic advising context, the interac-tional structure examined here is representative of a broader class of expert-guided decision-making settings in which users lack suffi-cient domain knowledge to independently verify correctness. Sim-ilar dynamics arise in domains such as financial advising, legal consultation, medical triage, and technical support, where trust judgments depend on both outcomes and observable cues about epistemic responsibility. At the same time, the relative weighting of outcomes and pro-cedural cues is likely domain-dependent. Higher-stakes or time-critical settings may amplify the impact of errors, potentially over-whelming more subtle effects of AI integration. Longer-term rela-tionships and formal accountability structures may further shape how users interpret AI involvement and expert responsibility over time. More broadly, these results underscore the importance of trust-sensitive design in AI-assisted advisory systems. workflows risk obscuring accountability, enabling blame shifting between human and AI actors, or fostering misplaced trust. De-signing AI oversight mechanisms that clearly signal responsibility boundaries may help support appropriate trust, calibration, and reliance across diverse advisory contexts. 

## 7 Conclusion 

This work contributes to the literature on trust in human-AI advi-sory systems by demonstrating that trust judgments are shaped by complete interaction workflows rather than by AI presence alone. In an interactive academic advising context, we show that advisory outcomes exert a seemingly primary influence on trust, with work-flows that result in errors evaluated less favorably than those that lead to successful outcomes. At the same time, among workflows that achieve successful outcomes, trust judgments are not uniform. Differences in how AI involvement is structured and made visible were associated with descriptive variation across epistemic trust, entity-specific trust, global trust, and willingness to rely on the advisor, though these differences were not statistically distinguishable among successful workflows in the present study. These differences emerge despite equivalent positive performance outcome, suggesting that users attend not only to whether an outcome is achieved, but to how advisory competence and responsibility are enacted during the interaction. More broadly, the results underscore the importance of trust-sensitive design approaches that account for both performance outcomes and interaction structure when integrating AI into expert-guided decision-making workflows. Rather than treating AI assis-tance as a monolithic intervention, our findings motivate attention to the procedural configuration of human-AI collaboration as a key determinant of trust. 

## 7.1 Future Work 

A primary direction for future research is to move from a workflow-level one-way comparison to a factor-based design that models the key sources of variation as independent factors. In particular, a follow-on study can be structured as a two-way design in which (i) advisory performance and (ii) AI involvement modality (e.g., none vs. advisor-initiated vs. automatic) are manipulated as separable factors rather than bundled into condition-specific workflows. This would allow two-way ANOVA to estimate not only the main effects of outcome and AI involvement, but also whether the effect of AI involvement depends on whether an error occurs. Such a design would enable interaction-level insights that are not identifiable in the present workflow-level analysis. For example, AI oversight may have little effect when an advisor succeeds, yet exert a stronger influence on trust judgments when an advisor fails and correction is required. Examining these interactions would help clarify when procedural differences in AI integration meaningfully shape trust beyond final outcome alone. Future work should also explore these trust dynamics in more complex advisory scenarios with higher stakes, where errors carry meaningful personal, financial, or health-related consequences. In such contexts, outcomes may exert stronger effects on trust, poten-tially altering how users interpret AI involvement, responsibility, and corrective authority. Studying whether the patterns observed here persist or change under higher stakes would help assess the generality of workflow-level trust dynamics across real-world advi-sory domains, while also enabling more direct examination of how responsibility and expertise are attributed between human advisors and AI systems following successful outcomes.

## Ethical Considerations Statement 

The study did not involve deception regarding participants’ iden-tities or academic standing, nor did it involve real academic con-sequences. The advising task was explicitly framed as a simulated planning exercise, and participants were instructed to proceed un-der the assumption that the task represented a hypothetical advis-ing interaction. While the simulation presented realistic advising workflows, including scheduling issues in some conditions, these elements were introduced solely for experimental control and posed no risk to participants’ actual academic progress. All participants provided informed consent and could discontinue participation at any time without penalty. Data collection was limited to interaction logs and survey re-sponses relevant to the study’s research questions. We intentionally avoided collecting sensitive educational records or longitudinal identifiers, consistent with principles of data minimization. No per-sonally identifiable information beyond basic demographics was collected, and all data were stored and analyzed in aggregate. Par-ticipants completed the study remotely and could withdraw at any time without penalty. Finally, this study contributes methodologically by examining trust in AI-assisted advisory systems in situations where individ-uals lack sufficient domain knowledge to independently evaluate recommendations and must instead rely on an expert whose judg-ment may be aided by AI. The advising simulation was designed to reflect this form of epistemic dependence while avoiding real academic consequences, allowing participants to engage with ex-pert guidance under conditions of uncertainty without risk to their actual educational outcomes. To the best of our knowledge, this study is among the first to examine trust in AI-assisted advising using a controlled, interactive simulation that systematically varies expert performance and AI involvement without imposing real consequences. By focusing on our proxy academic advising setup, the study prioritizes participant welfare while enabling systematic investigation of trust dynamics that are central to many real-world human–AI advisory contexts.
