## Abstract 

Recent generative recommendation frameworks have demonstrated remarkable scaling potential by reformulating item prediction as autoregressive Semantic ID (SID) generation. However, existing methods primarily rely on shallow behavioral signals, encoding items solely through surface-level textual features such as titles and descriptions. This reliance results in a critical “Shallow Interest” problem: the model fails to capture the latent, semantically rich interests underlying user interactions, limiting both personalization depth and recommendation interpretability. DeepInterestGR introduces three key innovations: (1) Multi-LLM Interest Mining (MLIM) : We systematically leverage multi-ple frontier LLMs ( e.g., GPT-5.1, Gemini-3-Pro, Kimi-K2-Thinking, Grok-4) along with their multi-modal variants to extract deep tex-tual and visual interest representations for users and items through Chain-of-Thought prompting. (2) Reward-Labeled Deep Interest (RLDI) : To ensure interest quality for downstream reinforcement learning, we employ a lightweight binary classifier to assign reward labels (positive/negative) to mined interests, enabling effective su-pervision signals. (3) Interest-Enhanced Item Discretization (IEID) : The curated deep interests are encoded into semantic em-beddings and quantized into SID tokens via RQ-VAE, enriching item representations with interpretable interest semantics. We adopt a two-stage training pipeline: supervised fine-tuning (SFT) aligns the generative model with both deep interest signals and collaborative filtering patterns, followed by reinforcement learning with GRPO optimized by our Interest-Aware Reward. Ex-tensive experiments on three Amazon Review benchmarks (Beauty, Sports, Instruments) demonstrate that DeepInterestGR consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across HR@K and NDCG@K metrics, validating the effectiveness of unifying deep interest min-ing with generative recommendation.

## 1 Introduction 

The scaling behavior of recommendation models has recently at-tracted significant attention [ 9, 14 , 15 , 28 ], driven largely by the success of large language models (LLMs) in demonstrating pre-dictable performance gains with increased model size [ 3 , 30 ]. How-ever, traditional recommendation models have struggled to exhibit similar scaling laws [ 6 , 11 , 22 , 24 ]. These systems typically allocate the majority of parameters to large embedding tables for storing user and item representations, while using only inner-product or shallow scoring networks for final predictions. Such an embedding-heavy design leads to performance plateaus: even as embedding dimensions or table sizes increase, improvements diminish quickly beyond moderate scales [8, 12, 27]. Generative recommendation offers a fundamental paradigm shift. By compressing items into sequences of discrete Semantic IDs (SIDs) through quantization techniques [ 4, 10 ], it uses compact vocabular-ies and redirects the bulk of parameters toward deep autoregres-sive Transformers that generate SID sequences [ 5, 19 , 20 , 32 ]. This design enables scaling behaviors more characteristic of language models, where increased depth and capacity translate to consistent performance gains [ 9, 28 ]. Recent works such as TIGER [ 20 ], LC-Rec [ 32 ], and industrial systems have demonstrated the promise of this generative paradigm. Despite these advances, existing generative recommendation methods suffer from a critical limitation: they primarily rely on 

shallow behavioral signals . Current approaches encode items solely through surface-level textual features such as titles and de-scriptions [ 7 , 20 , 32 ], failing to capture the latent, semantically rich interests underlying user interactions. We term this the “ Shallow Interest ” problem, which manifests in three aspects: (1) limited se-mantic depth —surface features miss deeper user intents ( e.g., “wire-less earbuds” may reflect interests in “fitness lifestyle” or “commute convenience”); (2) poor interpretability —without explicit interest modeling, the recommendation process remains a black box; and (3) suboptimal RL supervision —existing rewards rely on rule-based or collaborative signals without semantic guidance. To address these limitations, we propose DeepInterestGR , a novel framework that integrates deep interest mining into the gen-erative recommendation pipeline. Our key insight is that frontier LLMs possess remarkable world knowledge and reasoning capa-bilities [ 13 , 30 ], which can be leveraged to extract interpretable, semantically rich interest representations from user behaviors. DeepInterestGR introduces three key innovations: 

• Multi-LLM Interest Mining (MLIM). We systematically leverage multiple frontier LLMs ( e.g., GPT-5.1, Gemini-3-Pro, Kimi-K2-Thinking, Grok-4) along with their multi-modal

• Reward-Labeled Deep Interest (RLDI). To ensure interest quality for downstream reinforcement learning, we employ a lightweight binary classifier (based on Qwen-Chat) to assign reward labels (positive/negative) to mined interests. These labels serve as supervision signals during the RL phase. 

• Interest-Enhanced Item Discretization (IEID). The cu-rated deep interests are encoded into semantic embeddings via Qwen-Embedding and quantized into SID tokens via RQ-VAE, enriching item representations with interpretable interest semantics. For training, we adopt a two-stage pipeline following the success-ful practice of generative recommendation [ 20 , 32 ]: supervised fine-tuning (SFT) aligns the generative model with both deep interest signals and collaborative filtering patterns, followed by reinforce-ment learning. Crucially, we introduce an Interest-Aware Reward 

derived from RLDI labels—recommendations consistent with pos-itive interests receive higher rewards, guiding the policy toward more personalized and semantically coherent item sequences. Extensive experiments on large-scale real-world benchmarks from the Amazon Review Dataset [ 18 ] demonstrate that DeepIn-terestGR achieves significant improvements over state-of-the-art baselines including SASRec [ 11 ], BERT4Rec [ 22 ], TIGER [ 20 ], LC-Rec [ 32 ], and VQ-Rec [ 7] on HR@K and NDCG@K metrics, val-idating the effectiveness of unifying deep interest mining with generative recommendation. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 

• We identify the “ Shallow Interest ” problem in existing generative recommendation methods and propose DeepIn-terestGR , a novel framework that integrates deep interest mining into the generative recommendation pipeline. 

• We introduce three key components: MLIM for multi-LLM interest extraction, RLDI for interest quality control, and 

IEID for interest-enhanced item tokenization. 

• We design an Interest-Aware Reward mechanism that leverages mined deep interests as supervision signals for reinforcement learning, enabling more personalized recom-mendations. 

• Extensive experiments demonstrate that DeepInterestGR 

consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across multiple metrics and datasets. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Sequential Recommendation 

Sequential recommendation predicts the next item based on user behavior sequences. Early methods used Markov chains [ 21 ] and RNNs [ 6 ], while attention-based models like SASRec [ 11 ] and BERT4Rec [ 22 ]achieved strong performance through self-attention mechanisms. Recent works explored self-supervised learning [ 33 ] and graph-based methods [ 27 ]. However, these embedding-based approaches allocate most parameters to large embedding tables, limiting scala-bility and semantic understanding beyond collaborative signals [ 8]. 

## 2.2 Generative Recommendation 

Generative recommendation represents a paradigm shift from tradi-tional embedding-based methods by reformulating item prediction as a sequence generation problem. The core idea is to compress items into discrete Semantic IDs (SIDs) through quantization tech-niques, then use autoregressive Transformers to generate SID se-quences [9, 14, 15]. TIGER [ 20 ] pioneered this approach by using RQ-VAE to quan-tize text embeddings of item metadata into hierarchical SID tokens, then training a Transformer to autoregressively generate these to-kens. This design enables scaling behaviors more characteristic of language models, where increased depth and capacity translate to consistent performance gains. LC-Rec [ 32 ] further improved SID-language alignment through multi-task learning, enabling LLMs to understand the semantics behind discrete SID symbols. VQ-Rec [ 7 ]explored vector-quantized representations for transferable sequen-tial recommendation. Recent works have focused on enhancing SID construction and training strategies. EAGER [ 26 ] proposed a two-stream architecture with behavior-semantic collaboration. LETTER [ 25 ] introduced learnable tokenizers for LLM-based generative recommendation. CoST [ 34 ] leveraged contrastive learning for semantic tokenization. Industrial deployments such as HSTU [ 28 ] demonstrated trillion-parameter sequential transducers for generative recommendations at scale. However, existing generative recommendation methods primar-ily encode items using surface-level textual features such as titles and descriptions [ 7, 20 , 32 ]. This shallow encoding fails to capture the latent, semantically rich interests underlying user interactions, limiting both personalization depth and recommendation inter-pretability. 

## 2.3 LLM for Recommendation 

The remarkable success of Large Language Models (LLMs) has inspired their application to recommender systems, leveraging their world knowledge and reasoning capabilities for enhanced semantic understanding [30]. P5 [ 5] pioneered the unified prompt-based paradigm, casting various recommendation tasks ( e.g., rating prediction, sequential recommendation, explanation generation) into a text-to-text format. This work demonstrated that a single pretrained language model can handle diverse recommendation scenarios through appropriate prompting. RecFormer [ 13 ] showed that language representations alone are sufficient for effective sequential recommendation, using item titles as the primary feature. Subsequent works explored different strategies for LLM-recommendation integration. IDGenRec [ 23 ] proposed textual ID learning to align LLMs with recommendation semantics. GPTRec [ 19 ] adapted GPT-style models for generative sequential recommendation. UniSRec [ 8]pursued universal sequence representation learning for cross-domain recommendation. More recently, reinforcement learning techniques have been applied to optimize LLM-based recommenders, typically using rule-based or collaborative signals as reward functions [16]. ## 3 Method 

In this section, we present the DeepInterestGR framework in detail. We first formalize the problem setting and introduce the notation used throughout this paper (Section 3.1). We then describe the generative recommendation paradigm that serves as the foundation of our approach (Section 3.2). Subsequently, we present our core contributions: Multi-LLM Interest Mining (MLIM) for extracting deep interests (Section 3.3), Interest-Enhanced Item Discretization (IEID) for enriching item representations (Section 3.4), and our two-stage training pipeline with Interest-Aware Reward (Section 3.5).

## 3.3 Multi-LLM Interest Mining (MLIM) 

The core innovation of DeepInterestGR lies in systematically min-ing deep, semantically rich interests from user-item interactions using multiple frontier Large Language Models. Unlike existing methods that rely solely on surface-level textual features, MLIM leverages the world knowledge and reasoning capabilities of LLMs to extract interpretable interest representations through Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting. 

Motivation. Frontier LLMs accessed via online APIs (such as GPT-4o, Gemini-1.5-Pro, Kimi, and Grok) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding context, reasoning about user intent, and generating semantically coherent descriptions [ 30 ]. We hy-pothesize that these models can infer latent user interests from item metadata that go far beyond what surface-level features can capture. For example, given a purchase of “noise-canceling head-phones”, an LLM can reason about underlying interests such as “focus-oriented work style”, “frequent traveler”, or “audio quality enthusiast”—insights that are impossible to extract from the product title alone.

## 4 Experiments 

In this section, we empirically evaluate the effectiveness of the pro-posed DeepInterestGR framework. We aim to answer the following research questions: 

• RQ1 : How does DeepInterestGR perform compared to state-of-the-art baselines (traditional, generative, and LLM-based)? 

• RQ2 : What is the contribution of each core component (MLIM, IEID, Interest-Aware Reward)? 

• RQ3 : How do different LLMs compare in deep interest min-ing quality? 

• RQ4 : Does multi-modal interest mining improve over text-only mining? 

• RQ5 : How does the Interest-Aware Reward compare to other reward strategies? 

• RQ6 : How well does DeepInterestGR generalize across do-mains? 

## 4.1 Experimental Setup 

Datasets. We conduct experiments on three real-world public datasets from Amazon Product Reviews [ 18 ], which are widely recognized benchmarks in sequential recommendation research. Specifically, we use data from three subcategories: Beauty , Sports and Outdoors (Sports), and Musical Instruments (Instruments). Following prior work [ 7, 20 , 33 ], we apply the 5-core filtering proto-col, excluding users and items with fewer than five interactions to retain meaningful behavioral sequences. We adopt the widely used leave-last-out evaluation protocol [ 11 , 20 ], where the last item in each sequence is reserved for testing and the second-to-last item for validation. The detailed dataset statistics are provided in Table 2. 

Baselines. We compare DeepInterestGR with the following repre-sentative baselines, categorized into four groups: 

Traditional Sequential Models: 

• GRU4Rec [ 6]: An RNN-based approach using GRU for session-based recommendation. 

• Caser [ 24 ]: A CNN-based method that captures sequential patterns using horizontal and vertical convolutional filters. 

• HGN [17 ]: A hierarchical gating network that applies gating mechanisms to RNN-based models. 

Transformer-based Models: 

• SASRec [11 ]: A self-attentive sequential model using unidi-rectional Transformer decoder. 

• BERT4Rec [ 22 ]: A bidirectional Transformer encoder trained with masked item prediction. 

• S3-Rec [ 33 ]: A self-supervised approach that pretrains se-quence representations via mutual information. 

• FDSA [ 29 ]: A feature-aware model that processes item ID and feature sequences through separate self-attention blocks. 

Generative Recommendation Models: 

• TIGER [20 ]: A pioneering generative method that uses RQ-VAE to quantize text embeddings into semantic IDs for au-toregressive generation. 

• LC-Rec [ 32 ]: A language-aligned approach that enables LLMs to understandu SID through mlti-task learning. 

• HSTU [28 ]: A hierarchical sequential transduction unit de-signed for large-scale generative recommendation. 

• MiniOneRec : An open-source generative recommendation framework that validates scaling laws in recsys.

Evaluation Metrics. Following standard evaluation protocols [ 11 ,20 ], we adopt two widely recognized metrics: Hit Rate (HR@K) 

and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@K) ,reporting results at cutoffs of 𝐾 ∈ { 5, 10 }. For generative recommen-dation methods utilizing beam search, the beam size is consistently fixed at 20. 

Implementation Details. All experiments are conducted on a cluster equipped with NVIDIA A100 GPUs. We adopt Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct as the backbone model for autoregressive SID generation. For interest embedding, we use Qwen3-Embedding-4B to encode deep interest texts into semantic vectors. The item indexing scheme uses RQ-VAE with a fixed SID length of 4 and 256 codebook entries per layer. For Multi-LLM Interest Mining (MLIM), we leverage multiple frontier LLMs accessed via online APIs along with their multi-modal variants to generate diverse deep interest descriptions through Chain-of-Thought prompting. For RLDI labeling, we employ Qwen2-7B-Chat as a separate zero-shot classifier to assign quality labels to mined interests—note this is distinct from the generation backbone (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct) to avoid conflating the two roles. During the SFT phase, the model is fully fine-tuned using the LLaMA-Factory framework with a learning rate of 3 × 10 −4 for 3 epochs. For reinforcement learning optimization with GRPO, we use a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5, training batch size of 256, and KL-loss coefficient of 0.001 for 2 epochs. The rollout configuration sets temperature to 0.5 and samples 10 responses per query.

## 4.5 Transferability (RQ6) 

To evaluate the generalization capability of DeepInterestGR, we conduct cross-domain experiments where models are trained on one dataset and tested on another. Results are presented in Table 7. DeepInterestGR demonstrates substantially better cross-domain generalization, achieving 24.8% and 27.3% average improvements in HR@10 and N@10 respectively. This superior transferability stems from our deep interest mining approach: while shallow textual features ( e.g., product titles) are domain-specific, the underlying user interests ( e.g., “quality-conscious”, “trend-following”) transfer across domains. The mined deep interests capture these universal preference patterns, enabling more robust generalization. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We identified the “Shallow Interest” problem in generative recom-mendation and proposed DeepInterestGR, a framework integrating deep interest mining into the SID-based generation pipeline. Our ap-proach introduces three innovations: (1) Multi-LLM Interest Mining (MLIM) leveraging frontier LLMs with Chain-of-Thought prompt-ing; (2) Interest-Enhanced Item Discretization (IEID) encoding in-terests into SID tokens; and (3) Interest-Aware Reward providing semantic supervision for RL. Experiments on three Amazon bench-marks demonstrate 9.2%–15.1% improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, with superior cross-domain generalization confirming that deep interests capture transferable user preference patterns.
