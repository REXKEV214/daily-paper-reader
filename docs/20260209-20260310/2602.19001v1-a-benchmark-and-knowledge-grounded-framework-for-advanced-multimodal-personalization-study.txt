## Abstract 

The powerful reasoning of modern Vision Language Models open a new frontier for advanced personalization study. However, progress in this area is critically hampered by the lack of suitable benchmarks. To address this gap, we introduce Life-Bench, a comprehensive, synthetically gen-erated multimodal benchmark built on simulated user dig-ital footprints. Life-Bench features over 16 , 000 questions evaluating a wide spectrum of capabilities, from persona understanding to complex reasoning over historical data. These capabilities expand far beyond prior benchmarks, re-flecting the critical demands essential for real-world ap-plications. Furthermore, we propose LifeGraph, an end-to-end framework that organizes personal context into a knowledge graph to facilitate structured retrieval and rea-soning. Our experiments on Life-Bench reveal that exist-ing methods falter significantly on complex personalized tasks, exposing a large performance headroom, especially in relational, temporal and aggregative reasoning. While LifeGraph closes this gap by leveraging structured knowl-edge and demonstrates a promising direction, these ad-vanced personalization tasks remains a critical open chal-lenge, motivating new research in this area. 

## 1. Introduction 

Recent advancements in Vision Language Models (VLMs) have achieved state-of-the-art performance on numerous general tasks, demonstrating powerful capabilities in com-prehending and reasoning on the open world [1, 11, 15, 38]. This success naturally spurs growing interests in personaliz-ing these powerful, general-purpose models [34, 35, 47, 52]. The sophisticated reasoning capabilities of modern VLMs raise users’ expectation to address real-world personalized tasks, such as reasoning on a user’s evolving life history, complex social relationships and long-term temporal pat-terns, which are rarely reflected in traditional personaliza-tion studies [13, 19, 20, 29, 50]. We term this emerging re-search direction which focuses on adapting generative mod-els for these complex, dynamic and deeply contextual tasks as advanced personalization study .A key challenge in advanced personalization is that an individual’s private context is inherently isolated from the general knowledge in pre-trained models. To bridge this information gap, two primary paradigms have emerged. One paradigm is training-based adaptation, such as finetun-ing on personal data [2, 3, 31]. However, these methods face scalability challenges and difficulty integrating new information. Another increasingly prominent paradigm is retrieval-enhanced personalization. By externalizing per-sonal knowledge and retrieving relevant information at in-ference time [12, 18, 34, 48], this training-free approach offers a highly scalable and flexible solution. This architec-ture is well-suited for large, evolving multimodal personal data whose volume can easily exceed model context lim-its. Decoupling personal data from the model also makes it adaptable for diverse applications, including resource-constrained on-device scenarios [42]. Building benchmarks that reflect the complexity of real-world personalization scenarios is crucial for progress in advanced personalization. While there are text-only bench-marks for such tasks like personal historical document re-trieval [23, 35], preference and persona [14, 52], the mul-timodal nature of personal data has been overlooked. Ex-isting multimodal personalization benchmarks have made valuable contributions but primarily focus on the founda-tional tasks of personal concept identification [2, 12, 18, 21] and personal preference [21]. However, real-world person-alization demands broader capabilities, such as reasoning over an individual’s history or social relationships. The ab-sence of a comprehensive benchmark for these more com-plex scenarios limits the development of advanced multi-modal personalization methods. Early methods in this area, such as MyVLM [2], Yo’LLaVA [31] and MC-LLaVA [3], rely on training-based strategies like finetuning or adapter-based tuning to instill this personal knowledge. 2.2. Multimodal Personalization Benchmarks 

The development of effective advanced personalization methods is closely tied to the availability of strong bench-marks. While significant progress has been made in text-only personalization benchmarks [9, 14, 22, 35, 52], bench-marks for the more complex multimodal understanding are still at the early stages. Existing multimodal personaliza-tion benchmarks have centered on concept identification and preference learning. For instance, MyVLM [2] and Yo’LLaVA [31] introduced datasets of personal objects and people for recognition tasks. MC-LLAVA [3] extended this to multi-concept scenarios, while PerVA [12] provided larger sets of visual concepts. Subsequently, MMPB [21] introduced an extensive benchmark of 111 concepts, fo-cusing on evaluating VLM consistency and preference-handling in multi-turn dialogues. These benchmarks have been instrumental in developing models that can recognize concepts and understand related user preference. However, real-world personalization extends beyond identifying concepts and preferences. It requires richer sce-narios [10, 46, 50], like reasoning about personal history, event sequences, and evolving relationships, that existing benchmarks does not cover. In this work, Life-Bench is de-signed to fill this critical gap, introducing tasks to represent personal historical and complex logical reasoning that de-mand deeper personalized understanding. 

2.3. Personal Knowledge Graph 

Personal Knowledge Graph (PKG), structured representa-tions of individual’s entities and relationships to power per-sonalized applications, has been a significant research area for decades [4, 36, 43]. With the advent of Large Language Models, the interaction between these two technologies has advanced rapidly [33, 34, 48]. On one hand, LLMs auto-mate PKG construction by interpreting natural language to build and update the graph [48]. On the other hand, struc-tured knowledge in PKGs provides factual grounding to LLMs, enabling personalized response generation through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) [25, 33, 34]. However, this research has largely been confined to tex-tual knowledge, leaving a critical gap in handling personal multimodal data. Extending this paradigm to multimodal contexts to integrate and reason over personal visual data, is a significant but underexplored research opportunity. 

## 3. Life-Bench 

3.1. Benchmark Overview 

To mirror real-world personalization scenario, we introduce the term Vaccount (short for virtual account). Each Vac-count simulates a user’s digital footprint and consists of two components: (1) 3 to 5 concepts including the user and their relatives, forming a small social network within the vaccount. Each concept is defined by a representative im-age and a brief persona. (2) Historical images where each image is annotated with a date and a short, social-media style description. Images from the same event share the same date and description. This Vaccount design aligns our benchmark with realistic setting, and enable complex rea-soning tasks that requires retrieving and reasoning over so-cial relationship and historical data. 3.1.1. Categories 

To ensure our benchmark is comprehensive and reflects real-world use cases, we design Life-Bench to assess a broad spectrum of reasoning capabilities moving beyond prior work [2, 12, 18, 31]. These include not only foun-dational tasks like concept identification and preference study, but also advanced reasoning over relationships, de-tailed events and scenes, temporal sequences, and aggre-gated historical patterns (Tab. 1). To systematically eval-uate this wide range of abilities, we structure our bench-mark tasks into two overarching categories. The first, Re-lational Concept Identification , assesses the crucial skills of reasoning over personal concepts and relationships. The second, Historical Retrieval and Understanding , targets the more complex, dynamic reasoning required to make sense of a user’s evolving multimodal history. The distribution of tasks across these categories is shown in Fig. 2, and con-crete examples of all tasks in Fig. 1. 

Relational Concept Identification. This category evalu-ates a model’s foundational ability to recognize and reason about personal concepts and relationships. The tasks re-quire the model to ground named concepts within a Vac-count and understand their attributes or actions. A key challenge introduced in our benchmark is relational rea-soning. Questions are designed with varying difficulty over relations, from easy direct queries about a concept to medium level multi-hop ( e.g ., “aunt’s dog”) relational con-cept queries that requires resolving a chain of social rela-tionships. As examples shown in Fig. 1, 3 tasks in this category assess a model’s capability to identify concepts, resolve multi-hop relation chain, analyze their presence and attributes, and perform concept-centric visual reasoning. 

Historical Retrieval and Understanding. This category assesses a model’s capability to query and reason over a user’s entire Vaccount history, a critical area for advanced personalization that remains underexplored in multimodal research. We structure the 7 tasks in this category into two distinct subcategories, based on the required granularity of personal context. Four tasks under Event-centric subcat-egory evaluates the ability to reason over a specific event from the user’s context ( e.g ., images from a single date) and answer detailed questions about that isolation context. Three tasks under Complex Logical subcategory demand a broader, more holistic understanding. These tasks require aggregating information across multiple dates and events, to infer personalization patterns, such as personas and pref-erences, activity frequency, or complex relational-temporal sequences. As examples in Fig. 1, 7 tasks are classified as medium or hard level difficulty to reflect their inherent com-plexity and multi-hop retrieval requirements. 

3.2. Benchmark Construction 

All data in Life-Bench, including images, personas, and his-torical contexts, is synthetically generated. This approach is chosen for two primary reasons. Primarily, it fundamen-tally addresses the privacy concerns inherent in using real data. Second, as a training-free evaluation benchmark, the synthetic pipeline allows us to craft complex, consistent and controllable scenarios specifically designed for model evaluation. Our data generation pipeline is powered by ad-vanced generative models, including Gemini 2.5 Pro [11] and Gemini 2.5 Flash Image [17]. To ensure data integrity, we applied a comprehensive quality control process at each stage. Below we describe this data generation pipeline. 

Seeds Preparation. The first step of our data generation pipeline is to prepare three types of text-based seeds: Vac-count, concept, and event seeds. First, we generate 50 Vac-count seeds using Gemini 2.5 Pro [11]. Each Vaccount seed is an outline for a virtual account, specifying a primary con-4cept’s name, age, career, a social network of 3 to 5 relatives, and a brief life story. Next, to source realistic scenarios, we leverage captions of images derived from a CC-licensed subset of the YFCC100M personal photo dataset [40]. We use Gemini 2.5 Pro to generate descriptive captions for these images. The captions are then partitioned into two classes. Captions describing a single, clear subject (person or animal) are classified to concept seeds . The remaining captions are grouped by date for each account, then Gemini 2.5 Pro is prompted to synthesize them into narrative event descriptions, forming the event seeds . Only the text cap-tions are used as seeds in our pipeline; no source images from YFCC100M are incorporated into the final dataset. 

Vaccount Data Generation. The data for each Vaccount is generated through a multi-step process. First, we establish the Vaccount concepts by selecting a Vaccount seed and us-ing Gemini 2.5 Pro to assign corresponding concept seeds. The model then generates detailed personas and visual de-scriptions for each individual. Then we perform a refine-ment step to revise the descriptions to ensure logical consis-tency ( e.g ., shared facial features among family members). Using these refined descriptions, we generate a portrait im-age for each concept with Gemini 2.5 Flash Image [17]. Next, we construct historical data for each Vaccount. Gemini 2.5 Pro selects a series of events from event seeds and rewrites them into coherent narratives that align with the established Vaccount personas. Meanwhile, a synthetic date stamp is assigned to each event. For each event, the model selects and refines a set of descriptive image cap-tions from the event seed to complete the story and explic-itly name the involved concepts. Finally, to generate his-torical images and maintain visual consistency of concepts, the image generation [17] is conditioned on both the refined captions and the portrait image of relevant concepts. This multi-step process ensures generated contents are logically coherent, and centered on unique Vaccount personas. 

Question-Answer Generation. After generating Vaccount data, we then proceed to create the question-answer (QA) pairs. For each task, we provide Gemini 2.5 Pro with the rel-evant Vaccount context ( e.g ., social relationships, historical images) and a set of predefined question templates to gen-erate question-answer pairs. A key advantage of our syn-thetic pipeline is that the generation process itself provides ground-truth context. For example, the textual captions used to generate an image serve as a factual basis for creat-ing related visual questions. We provide this source infor-mation as ground truth metadata directly to the model dur-ing QA generation to ensure the initial accuracy of the an-swers. This step produces QA pairs of tasks, each grounded in the factual context of its Vaccount. 

Quality Control and Verification. To guarantee a high-quality benchmark, we perform quality control and verfica-tion from three perspectives: • Image fidelity and consistency: We generate 3 candidate images for each image. Then we use Gemini 2.5 Pro en-semble vote for the best candidate based on realism and consistency. Low quality images without selected candi-dates are filtered out. This is followed by a manual review by authors and regenerated unqualified images. • QA Correctness: We use a two-step process to verify QA pairs. First, each generated QA pair undergoes several rounds of verification by Gemini 2.5 Pro to filter out ques-tions that are factually incorrect, ambiguous or unanswer-able, using the relative ground-truth context as references. A final manual verification is then performed. • Data Diversity: To ensure data diversity and mitigate gen-erative model bias, our entire process is seeded with tex-tual scenarios derived from the large-scale YFCC100M dataset [40]. This approach grounds our benchmark in re-alistic situations while fully preserving privacy through a multi-step rewriting process. 

## 4. LifeGraph for Personalization 

4.1. Retrieval-Enhanced VLM Personalization 

Retrieval-enhanced personalization provides a powerful, training-free framework for adapting VLMs. The core prin-ciple is to externalize a user’s personal knowledge and dy-namically retrieve only the most relevant information at in-ference time. This approach is crucial as a user’s rich, mul-timodal life history is often too vast to fit within a model’s finite context window. It also offers the scalability and efficiency required for practical applications, especially in resource-constrained scenarios ( e.g ., on-device [42]). This paradigm effectively grounds model reasoning in private, evolving data without overhead of continuous fine-tuning. We provide a formal, approach-agnostic definition for retrieval-enhanced VLM personalization. Let F de-note a Vision-Language Model, which in a general non-personalized setting maps a query x to a response y =

F(x). For personalization, we introduce a user-specific per-sonal knowledge space P and a personalized retriever R.Given a query x, the retriever R extracts a set of relevant context from P , denoted by Cx = R(x, P ). Then the per-sonalized response is conditioned on both the original query 

x and the retrieved context Cx. Thus, retrieval-based per-sonalization system can be represented as: 

y = F(x, C x) where Cx = R(x, P ) (1) 

4.2. Personal Knowledge Graph Solution 

To handle the complex, structured nature of personal data, the personal knowledge graph (PKG) [4, 6, 36, 43] offers a powerful framework. To develop this solution, we first pro-vide a formal definition of the personal knowledge graph, then introduce LifeGraph in Sec. 4.3 and theoretical discus-sion of its effectiveness in Sec. 4.4. 5Formally, we define a personal knowledge graph as G =(N, T, E ) where N is a set of node entities, E is a set of edge relations, T ⊆ N × E × N is the set of triples. Each triple (u, r, v ) ∈ T represents a personal fact. Under this definition, the retrieval system R becomes a graph retrieval. Given a query x, a graph retriever RG searches G to ex-tract relevant context Cx, which is typically a set of factual triples, written as Cx ⊆ T .

4.3. LifeGraph 

We introduce LifeGraph, an end-to-end personal knowledge graph solution for advanced personalization. LifeGraph consists of two main components: a semi-automated graph construction approach, and a graph retrieval enhanced per-sonalized reasoning algorithm. We outline the core design principles below, with comprehensive implementation de-tails provided in the appendix. 

LifeGraph construction. Inspired by recent works that use generative models to assist with knowledge graph construc-tion [16, 44], we leverage VLMs to build the LifeGraph. To ensure a high-quality and expressive graph, we incor-porate several key designs: (1) Hybrid Schema. We use a semi-structured schema. Node entity types are predefined to ensure consistency, while relations are open-ended, allow-ing the model to capture the diverse, nuanced connections present in personal data. (2) Two-step conversational con-struction. We structure the graph construction in two main steps. The first step extracts concepts and relationships to form a stable personal social network scaffold. The second step then process dynamic historical events and intergrates them into the graph. Each step employs a two-turn conver-sational VLM prompt to first identify candidate entities then validate and formalize the factual triples. (3) Graph to mul-timodal source indexing. To preserve the full fidelity of per-sonal data, node entities and edge relations are indexed to the source data. This allows retrieving to fetch original rich multimodal context on demand, complementing the struc-tured facts from a LifeGraph. (4) N-ary relation represen-tation. To model complex n-ary facts [27, 41] that involve more than two entities, we format relations as hyper-edges with auxiliary attributes. This allows us to capture richer, 

n-ary facts without losing factual integrity. 

LifeGraph Retrieval To query the LifeGraph, we em-ploy a graph retrieval algorithm based on the Think-on-Graph [28, 37] framework, which integrates LLM-driven reasoning with an iterative beam search for graph traver-sal, to expand paths by retrieving relevant entities and rela-tions at each step. We adapt the original Think-on-Graph for the personalized, multimodal context of LifeGraph with two key modifications. First, given the smaller and denser nature of personal LifeGraph, we consolidate the original two-step entity and relation retrieval into a single, unified

4.4. Structural Properties and Retrieval Efficiency 

Personal knowledge graphs, akin to many real-world net-works, often exhibit the properties of a scale-free random graph [8, 30]. This structure is characterized by a power-law degree distribution [5, 30], where most nodes have low de-grees while a few “hub” nodes serve as highly connected en-tities. We empirically verify this property in our constructed LifeGraph, as shown in Fig. 3, confirming its structural sim-ilarity to scale-free graph. This structure has profound implication for its storage and retrieval efficiency. To store m facts in a graph, the number of nodes is generally bounded within (√m, 2m]. In the specific case of a scale free graph, the number of nodes is approximately m + c for some constant c [7]. More crit-ically, such graphs are expected to have a small diameter that scales logarithmically with the number of nodes, ap-proximately log( m) [7]. This “small-world” property en-sures that the path length between any two nodes is short. Consequently, a graph retriever RG can theoretically find a path within log( m)/2 hops. While retrieval cost is linear with traversal depth, this logarithmic scaling of the graph diameter ensures this depth remains small. This makes the PKG an expressive and computationally efficient substrate for complex, multi-hop personalization queries.

## 8. LifeGraph Details 

This section provide details of the LifeGraph design. 

8.1. LifeGraph Construction 

LifeGraph is implemented as a directed graph. This struc-ture is essential for accurately representing personal facts, where relations are often asymmetric. 

Hybrid Schema. We employ a semi-structured schema for the graph, where node entity types are predefined and the edge relations are open-ended. This design choice is motivated by two key objectives: (1) Consistency and Graph Quality: Predefined entity types enforces a consistent graph structure. This constraint guides the VLMs during the automated graph construction process to ensure a high-quality and logically sound knowledge graph. (2) Flexibil-ity and Expressiveness: Open-ended edge relations provide the flexibility to capture the diverse and nuanced informa-tion in different personal histories and scenarios. This al-lows the graph to represent a wide range of specific contexts and scenarios without being limited by a rigid, predefined set of relations. We define the following six entity types: • PersonAnimal : Represents core personal concepts. • Event : Represents a specific occurrence in the user’s his-tory, typically associated with a date and one or more im-ages. • Date : Represents a specific date or timestamp. • Location : Represents a geographical place associated with an Event or PersonAnimal . • Activity : Represents an action or occasion occurring 2Structure of a LifeGraph Figure 8. Graph structure of a constructred LifeGraph. 

within an Event. • Object : Any other descriptive facts related to a concept or event will be covered by Object. In this schema, the PersonAnimal and Event entities serve as the primary ‘hub’ nodes. Most other entities are con-nected to these hubs. This design creates a richly inter-connected knowledge structure centered on key individu-als and historical events. The explicit linking of Event and Date nodes further enables reasoning over the user’s life as a chronological trajectory. 

Two-Step Conversational Construction. The graph con-struction process is structured into two sequential steps to ensure the integrity and quality of the resulting knowledge graph. The first step extracts the primary PersonAnimal entities and their static, foundational relationships. This establishes a stable social network structure that serves as the core of the Personal Knowledge Graph. The second step processes the dynamic historical events and integrates them into the pre-built scaffold. The initial social-network LifeGraph serves as contextual input, alongside the multi-modal historical data, which is processed in batches. To optimize this, we employ a specialized bin-packing algo-rithm that clusters relevant images together. Specifically, we group historical images chronologically and adopt a greedy bin-packing strategy to consolidate sequential events into batches. This algorithm restricts each batch size to the VLM’s effective reasoning capacity rather than filling the entire context window, which is a good balance between graph construction efficiency and maintaining the stability and quality of the graph. This step enables the model to extract historical facts in a personalized manner, grounding the new information in the user’s established concepts and relationships. This decoupled approach is crucial for qual-ity control. By first establishing an accurate representation of the social network, we ensure the core structure of Life-Graph is consistent before integrating the more complex and varied information from historical data. Inspired by multi-turn conversational knowledge graph construction methods [32], we design a two-turn conversa-tional LifeGraph construction process. The first turn identi-fies candidate entities from the source multimodal data. We instruct the VLM to extract candidate entities, strictly lim-iting them to the predefined entity types. The second turn formalizes these candidates into structured factual triples. Based on the conversation history, the model generates triples to comprehensively describe the source data. Cru-cially, entities in this step are constrained to the candidate set established in the first turn. This strictly anchors graph relations to verified entities and enables expressing complex 

n-ary relations as edge attributes. 

Graph to Multimodal Source Indexing. To preserve the full fidelity of the source data, entities and relations within LifeGraph maintain an index to the original multimodal context. During construction, the VLM is prompted to cite the source image IDs that provide factual support for each extracted triple. These references are stored as attributes within the corresponding nodes and edges. This index-ing allows the retrieval algorithm to fetch the original rich multimodal context on demand, complementing the graph’s structured facts. 

N-ary Relation Representation. To model complex n-ary facts [27, 41], we identify a primary binary relation and encode all additional information as (key, value) at-tributes on the corresponding edge, forming what we term a ‘hyper-edge’. This property graph approach allows us to represent multi-faceted facts within a simple triple struc-ture, preserving factual integrity without creating interme-diate nodes. For instance, a n-ary fact “In Christmas Party event, David and Rylen are decrocating the Christmas tree using red ornaments” can be express as “Christmas Party event - hasActivity <attendee: [David, Rylen], object: red ornaments > - Decorate Christmas Tree”. The complete construction process results in a richly in-terconnected personal knowledge graph. ## 9. Discussion 

Nature of Scale-free. The analysis of LifeGraph’s struc-tural properties in Sec. 4.4 is based on established theorems from the study of scale-free random graphs [5, 7, 8, 30]. These properties, such as the power-law degree distribu-tion and graph diameter, are direct applications of clas-sical graph theory. For a comprehensive theoretical un-derstanding of this domain, we recommend these related works [5, 7, 8, 30], in particular [7] for diameter study. 

Backbone model selection. We selected Gemma 312B [39] as the standardized backbone model for all eval-uated methods. This decision was guided by two primary factors. First, many real-world personalization scenarios, particularly on-device applications, require computation-ally efficient and adaptable models. This makes small, open-source VLMs a practical necessity to balance perfor-mance with resource constraints. Second, standardizing on a smaller model ensures a fair and direct comparison with baseline methods (RAP [18], R2P [12]), which were also designed around similarly-sized backbones.
