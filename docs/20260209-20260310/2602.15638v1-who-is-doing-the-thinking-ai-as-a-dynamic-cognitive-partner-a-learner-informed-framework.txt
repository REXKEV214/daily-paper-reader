Abstract 

Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly embedded in education, and there remains a need to 

explain how students themselves conceptuali se AI’s role in their thinking , that is how they are 

thinking of using AI in their learning. This study proposes a framework positioning AI as a dynamic 

cognitive partner whose function shifts across different learning situations. Using qualitative 

analysis of written responses from 133 secondary students in Hong Kong following the 

completion of an AI literacy course,  we identified nine interrelated dimensions through which 

learners described AI as partnering with their cognition: (1) conceptual scaffolding for difficult 

ideas, (2) feedback and error detection, (3) idea stimulation, (4) cognitive organi sation, (5) 

adaptive tutoring support, (6) metacognitive monitoring support, (7) task and cognitive load 

regulation, (8) learning continuity support beyond classroom boundaries and (9) explanation 

reframing through representational flexibility during moment s of being “stuck” or overwhelmed .

Across the dimensions, students distinguished between productive support that extends 

understanding and unproductive reliance that replaces cognitive effort , indicating situational 

awareness of when AI should and s hould not be used . Grounded in sociocultural, distributed 

cognition, self -regulated learning, and cognitive load perspectives, the framework clarifies how 

AI becomes integrated into learners’ cognitive activity while also illuminating the boundary 

between cognitive extensi on and substitution.

Introduction 

Across educational contexts, AI systems are increasingly used for tutoring, feedback generation, 

adaptive learning, content organi sation, and well -being support  (Chan, 2025a ; Demartini et al. ,

2024; Meyer et al., 2024; Zong & Yang, 2025 ). Recent studie s highlight both the opportunities 

and challenges AI introduces into learning environments with concerns regarding overreliance, 

cognitive offloading, and diminished learner agency ( Chan & Hu, 2023; Giannakos et al., 2025 ). 

As g enerative AI (GenAI) can interact with learners during the act of thinking itself, AI is no longer 

only providing outputs ; it is participating in cognition in action ( Essel et al., 2024 ). While existing research questions ha ve examined AI’s instructional effectiveness, system design, 

ethical considerations, learners’ acceptability and AI literacy development (Chan , 2025 b; Chiu, 

2026; Ng et al., 2024 ), a key question remains underexplored: 

How do learners themselves cognitively conceptuali se AI’s role within their own thinking 

processes? 

Put differently, when students use AI to learn, how do they position the system in the learning 

process , as a resource they actively steer to support their reasoning, as a tutor that guides them 

step -by -step, or as an answer generator that completes work with minimal learner input? 

Understanding this question is critical because learners’ mental models of AI shape how the 

technology is used, regulated, and integrated into learning activities ( Liu et al., 2025 ). If AI is 

perceived as a shortcut or answer -provider, it may lead to cognitive outsourcing. If it is viewed 

as a support for thinking, it may function as scaffolding that enhances self -regulated learning 

(SRL) and conceptual development (Wu & Chiu, 2025). Thus, AI’s educational impact depends 

not only on system capability and outputs but also on how learners position AI within their 

cognitive processes. 

The present study addresses this by analy sing student written responses in which learners 

describe how AI supports their learning. These texts capture learners’ own perspectives and 

experiences on moments of explanation, feedback, organi sation, adaptation, monitoring, and 

persistence after their AI literacy course . The analysis focuses on how learners portray AI’s 

participation in their cognitive activity across different learning situations. 

From the findings , the study derives a framework conceptuali sing AI as a Dynamic Cognitive 

Partner. The term dynamic reflects that AI’s role is not static; its function shifts according to 

learner needs, task demands, and stages of understanding. It also captures AI’s interactive, 

personalised, and adaptive nature: AI systems respond to learner input, adjust explanations, 

vary representations, and modify levels of support in real time. The term cognitive partner 

emphasi ses that AI’s involvement occurs at th e level of thinking processes , supporting 

reasoning, sensemaking, monitoring, and regulation rather than solely at the level of content 

delivery or administrative assistance. 

By grounding the framework in learner perspectives and linking it to established theories of 

mediated cognition and self -regulated learning, the study offers a process -oriented model for 

understanding AI’s role in human learning activity. This model contributes to educational 

research by clarifying how AI functions within learners’ cognitive syste ms and by providing a 

conceptual basis for examining when AI supports learning and when it risks reducing cognitive 

engagement. 

Theoretical Foundations for conceptualising AI as a Dynamic Cognitive Partner 

Positioning AI as a Dynamic Cognitive Partner requires moving beyond descriptions of AI as a 

“tool” that delivers content or automates tasks, and instead treating AI as part of the learner’s 

cognitive activity system, a partner that learners recruit to think with, not only to get answers. 

This section synthesises theory and AI -in -education research to explain how a learner -informed 

framework is grounded in (a) Sociocultural theories of mediated learning, (b) Activity Theory (c) 

Distributed Cognition and Ext ended Mind Thesis , ( d) Self -Regulated Learning, and ( e) Cognitive 

load and Cognitive offloading. Together, these perspectives explain both the promise of AI as 

cognitive support and the risk that AI can displace learner processing if used as a substitute for 

thinking. Sociocultural Perspective and ZPD : Tool -mediated learning and the “more knowledgeable 

other” 

From a sociocultural perspective, learning is not only an internal cognitive process; Vygotsky 

(1978) argued that human thinking does not develop in isolation but is mediated through 

interaction and participation in socially organised activity with cultura l tools that are created and 

shared within a culture. These mediational means can include physical tools such as books, 

calculators, or digital technologies, as well as psychological tools such as language, numbers, 

diagrams, and problem -solving strategies , all of which shape how an individual reason s, learn s,

and make s sense of the world. Crucially, mediation is not merely mechanical assistance: a 

mediational resource can reorganise what the learner is able to do, what counts as a next step, 

and how meaning is constructed. In addition, Vygotskian ’s Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): 

the distance between what learners can do independently and what they can do with guidance 

(Vygotsky, 1978). Traditionally, this guidance is provided by a teacher, peer, or exp ert. In the 

context of AI, students increasingly describe AI as scaffolding (Wood, Bruner, & Ross, 1976), 

providing “explanations,” “steps,” and “ways of understanding,” suggesting that learners are 

treat ing AI outputs as a form of contingent support especially when facing conceptual difficulty 

(Cai et al, 2025), AI becomes the “more knowledgeable others” in the ZPD .

In learning sciences, scaffolding is typically characterised by contingency (support adjusted to 

need), fading (support reduced as competence grows), and transfer of responsibility to the 

learner (van de Pol et al., 2010) . AI systems can approximate contingency through interactive 

dialogue and iterative prompting; however, the risks are also clear: scaffolds can become 

“crutches” if they do not fade or if the learner does not internalise strategies. In other words, 

sociocu ltural theory does not assume that tools automatically enhance learning; benefits 

depend on whether learners appropriate the tool for sensemaking and internalise the mediated 

practices. 

Within AI -in -education research, this tension is reflected in work documenting both 

opportunities and challenges (Zhai et al., 2024 ). Students’ reported experiences with ge nAI 

likewise include using AI to clarify ideas and support learning, alongside concerns about 

accuracy and temptation to outsource work (Chan & Colloton , 202 4). These findings resonate 

with a mediated -learning view: AI may act as a resource for conceptual advancement within the 

learner’s ZPD, but whether it supports development depends on how learners engage with it. 

Activity Theory: Cognitive mediation in learning activity 

A key conceptual move is shifting the lens from “AI as a tool” to “AI as cognitive mediation.” It is 

reasonable to be cautious with the phrase mediational tool , because AI is not only a static 

artifact (like a textbook); it can behave like an interactive partner through conversation, iterative 

refinement, and responsive explanation (Guo et al., 2025; Casheekar et al., 2024 ). This aligns 

more closely with the idea of mediated action rather than passive tool use.  Activity theory 

(Engeström, 1987 ) strengthens this point by emphasising that learning occurs within goal -

directed activity systems, where tools mediate the relationship between a subject (learner) and 

an object (learning goal), under rules, community, and division of labour.  Within such a system, 

AI does not merely deliver content; it reshapes how the activity unfolds. 

For example, when a student uses an AI chatbot while writing an essay, the student (subject) is 

working toward producing an argument (object). AI mediates this activity by summarising 

readings, suggesting structures, and providing feedback on drafts. At the same time, the rules of 

the classroom (e.g., marking rubrics, academic integrity expectations), the community (teachers and peers), and the division of labour (AI suggesting, student deciding and composing) 

all influence how AI is used. If the student asks AI to brainstorm ideas, the system supports idea 

generation; if the student requests step -by -step clarification of a concept, AI supports 

conceptual scaffold ing; if the student uses AI to check grammar, it functions as a feedback 

partner. The AI’s role therefore shifts depending on the moment -to -moment demands of the task 

(Li et al., 2025) .

When students use AI, they do not simply “access information”; they reorganise the learning 

activity itself. They offload certain sub -tasks (e.g., summarising), open new cognitive pathways 

(e.g., rapid brainstorming), and alter temporal rhythms (e.g., imme diate feedback instead of 

delayed teacher marking). These changes help explain why learners describe AI roles as 

dynamic, situational and adaptive: AI’s contribution varies depending on task type, time 

pressure, confidence level, and the availability of hu man support (Chan & Hu, 2023; Bai & Wang, 

2025 ). In this sense, AI participates in the mediation of thinking within activity, rather than 

functioning as a neutral external aid. 

Distributed Cognition and Extended Mind Thesis : the re -location of thinking 

Distributed cognition perspectives argue that cognition is not confined to an individual mind; it 

is distributed across people, artifacts, and environments (Hutchins, 1995). In education, this 

view has been used to explain how learners think with diagrams, calculators, notes, digital 

systems, and peers. AI fits naturally into this tradition: students can use AI to transform messy 

information into structured representations (outlines, flashcards), to generate alternative 

explanations, or to externalise inter mediate reasoning steps (Sousa & Cardoso, 2025 ). In 

distributed cognition terms, AI becomes part of the learner’s cognitive system, helping 

coordinate processes such as remembering, organising, checking, and sensemaking. 

The extended mind thesis similarly argues that external resources can become functionally 

integrated into cognition when they are reliably available, easily accessible, and tightly coupled 

with the user’s goals (Clark & Chalmers, 1998). Contemporary AI sys tems, always available, 

conversational, multi -purpose demonstrates supports the theoretical angle of the “cognitive 

partner” framing: if learners repeatedly use AI to scaffold explanations, test understanding, and 

regulate progress, AI is not simply a peripheral tool but a functional component of learners’ 

cognitive workflow. 

However, distributed/extended cognition accounts also sharpen the central risk: when external 

resources become too powerful or too convenient (Rivera -Novoa & Duarte Arias, 2025 ), learners 

may rely on them in ways that reduce internal processing. This is not inherently negative ,

offloading routine tasks can free cognitive resources for deeper work but it becomes 

problematic when learners offload the very processes that constitute learning (e.g., generating 

explanations, monitoring comprehension, and practici ng retrieval) (Gerlich, 2025 ).

Self -Regulated Learning : AI as a regulation partner 

Self -regulated learning (SRL) conceptualises learning as cycles of planning, monitoring, strategy 

use, and reflection (Zimmerman, 200 2). AI can support each phase: learners may plan with AI 

(study schedules, task breakdown), monitor with AI (feedback, error spotting), execute 

strategies with AI (practice questions, explanations), and reflect with AI (summaries of 

weaknesses, revision sug gestions). Importantly, SRL theory distinguishes between having 

support available and using support strategically (Karaben ick & Berger, 2013 ). Learners who know when and who to seek help, how to evaluate it, and when to persist independently are more 

likely to benefit. 

Empirical work on AI chatbots and SRL is consistent with this framing. For example, studies 

suggest chatbot -supported learning can relate to motivation and perceived support, with 

teacher support shaping how students engage with AI tools (Chiu et al., 202 3). Research also 

indicates that learners’ needs satisfaction and prior knowledge interact with SRL processes 

when learning with AI chatbots (Xia et al., 2023). These findings indicate that AI’s effects whether 

positive or negative are mediated by learners’ regulatory capacity. 

A key SRL mechanism is metacognitive monitoring: learners must judge what they know, identify 

gaps, and select appropriate strategies. AI can assist monitoring through feedback, explanations, 

and comparison across solutions; yet AI can also degrade monitor ing if learners accept outputs 

uncritically. This dual potential aligns with the idea that AI can either extend cognition or 

outsource it. 

Cognitive Load Theory : The boundary between load and offload 

Cognitive load theory (CLT) provides a complementary explanation for why learners want AI 

support and why over -reliance can happen. CLT argues that working memory is limited and 

learning is constrained when tasks impose excessive load (Sweller, 19 94 ). AI can reduce load by 

summarising, chunking tasks, generating worked examples, and translating complex language 

into simpler forms. From an instructional standpoint, such supports can be beneficial when they 

reduce extraneous load (unnecessary complexit y) and preserve capacity for germane 

processing (sensemaking that contributes to learning). 

At the same time, CLT warns that when supports do too much of the cognitive work, learners 

may not engage in the processing needed for schema construction. Integrative frameworks 

connecting cognitive load and SRL emphasise that effective learning requires not just reduced 

load, but learners’ active regulation of attention, strategy, and effort (de Bruin et al., 2020). AI 

can help regulate load and sustain progress, but the learner’s role as the epistemic agent must 

remain intact. 

When students are busy, stressed, or time -pressured, they may shift from using AI as a scaffold 

to using it as a shortcut , reducing effortful processing such as generating explanations, checking 

reasoning, or practicing retrieval. SRL and CLT together predict this vulnerability: under time 

pressure and high workload, learners may prioritise efficiency over learning quality, especially if 

the system provides fluent answers quickly. A concern of “metacognitive laziness” (or 

metacognitive sloth) (Fan et al., 2025). 

The Present Study 

Taken together, sociocultural theory, activity theory, distributed cognition, self -regulated 

learning, and cognitive load theory converge on a common insight: learning with AI is not simply 

a matter of access to information, but a matter of how AI becomes integrated into the learner’s 

cognitive system. Across these perspectives, AI can function as scaffolding within the learner’s 

Zone of Proximal Development, as a mediating artifact in goal -directed activity, as an external 

component of distributed cognitio n, and as a resource for planning, monitoring, and regulating 

effort. At the same time, these theories jointly predict a central tension: the same features that 

make AI a powerful support for understanding can also make it a mechanism for cognitive 

substit ution when learners offload sensemaking, monitoring, or strategy use to the system. Thus, AI’s educational value is not inherent in the technology itself but emerges from the patterns of 

interaction between learner, task, and AI within specific learning situations. 

What remains underdeveloped in existing work is a learner -centred account of how students 

themselves perceive and position AI within these cognitive processes. While theoretical and 

empirical studies describe AI’s affordances and effects, fewer studies rec onstruct how learners 

articulate AI’s role in moments of difficulty, idea generation, organisation, regulation, and 

persistence. Understanding these learner conceptualisations is critical because they shape 

when AI is used as a scaffold, when it becomes a shortcut, and how responsibility for thinking is 

negotiated. The present study therefore turns to student s’ written responses to examine how 

learners describe AI’s participation in their thinking. From these accounts, we derive a learner -

informed framework that conceptualises AI as a Dynamic Cognitive Partner , not a static tool, but 

an adaptive form of cognitive mediation whose role shifts across tasks, needs, and stages of 

understanding. 

Methodology 

Participants and Context 

Participants were 133 secondary school students (aged 15 –17) from Hong Kong who had 

completed a 30 -hour online AI literacy course prior to data collection. The course provided 

foundational knowledge about artificial intelligence, introduced common AI tools used in 

education, and gave students guided opportunities to apply AI in learning -related tasks. 

Throughout the course, students engaged with AI systems for explanation, feedback, 

organi sation, and content generation, while also discussing ethical use, li mitations, and risks of 

overreliance. This ensured that participants had both conceptual understanding of AI and 

practical experience using AI tools in learning contexts before producing their written responses. 

Data Source 

The dataset comprised of a non -graded component - student written response s produced as 

final part of AI literacy course . Students were instructed to write in English (approximately 400 –

800 words) describing how AI could support their learning. The task prompted students to share 

their perception and experience in the following areas: 

• What AI is and how it may be used in education 

• Useful AI tools and how they support learning (e.g., tutoring, feedback, organi sation) 

• How AI may change the roles of teachers and students 

• Challenges or risks of using AI (e.g., inaccuracy, overreliance, integrity) 

• Personal experiences —how and when the student uses AI to support learning 

Students were not describing tools but articulating their own understandings of AI’s role in 

thinking, studying, and problem solving. Importantly, many students also discussed situations 

where AI might lead to overreliance, reduced effort, or diminished cri tical thinking, providing 

data on both supportive and problematic forms of AI engagement. 

Data Analysis 

Texts were analy sed using qualitative content analysis with both inductive and theory -informed 

elements.  The focus of analysis was not on specific AI platforms, but on the cognitive functions 

students attributed to AI. 

Two researchers independently coded all texts. The first cycle of coding used open coding to 

mark segments where students described: • what AI helps them do cognitively (e.g., explain, check, organi se, give ideas), 

• when they turn to AI (e.g., when stuck, overloaded, unsure), and 

• how they positioned their own role relative to AI (e.g., thinking partner vs. shortcut). 

Codes were then compared and clustered into function -oriented categories such as conceptual 

explanation, feedback, organi sation, adaptation, monitoring, and persistence support. During 

this stage, segments describing risks of AI use, including overreliance, cognitive offloading, or 

loss of independent thinking, were also coded to capture learners’ awareness of boundaries 

betw een support and substitution. 

In the second cycle, the researchers refined and consolidated categories through constant 

comparison across the dataset, ensuring that themes represented distinct forms of AI –learner 

cognitive interaction. Disagreements in coding were resolved through disc ussion until 

consensus was achieved. The final outcome was a set of nine interrelated dimensions 

representing ways students described AI as participating in their thinking processes. 

These dimensions collectively form the AI as a Dynamic Cognitive Partner framework, grounded 

in learner perspectives while interpreted through theories of mediated learning and self -

regulated learning. 

Ethical approval was obtained prior to the study. Participation followed school guidelines, and 

student submissions were de -identified before analysis. All data were stored securely, and no 

identifying information is reported. 

Findings: AI as a Dynamic Cognitive Partner Across Learning Processes 

Analysis of students’ written responses reveals that learners conceptuali se AI not as a single 

instructional tool, but as a dynamic cognitive partner whose role shifts across learning 

situations. Across nine interrelated dimensions, students describe AI as participating in 

explanation, feedback, idea generation, organi sation, personali sation, monitoring, workload 

regulation, continuity and persistence of learning, representational reframing. Importantly, 

within each dimension, students articulate a boundary between supportive use (AI extending 

cognition) and substitutive use (AI replacing cognition). This dual positioning is theoretically 

illuminated by sociocultural theory, activity theory, distributed cognition, SRL and CLT . Table 1 

shows the dimensions and its cognitive functions. 

Dimension 1: Conceptual Scaffolding 

Conceptual Scaffolding  is when AI helps learners understand difficult ideas by breaking 

concepts into manageable steps, modelling reasoning, and explaining in ways that support the 

learner’s own meaning -making.  Students most frequently positioned AI as a conceptual 

scaffold that supports understanding of difficult ideas, closely aligning with sociocultural theory 

and the notion of mediation within the ZPD . Learners described AI as helping them access 

explanations that would otherwise be beyond their indepen dent capability. For example, one 

student explained: “Photomath (AI tool) allows you to input your equation and obtain a step -by -

step explanation and solution. Its step -by -step simplification allows you to follow how the 

equation is solved easily, helping you to understand complicated equations you can’t solve.” 

Another noted: “AI would not just only give out simple answers, but give me long and detailed 

explanations for each step. It really allows me to learn and clear my questions.” These findings 

illustr ate AI functioning as a scaffold in the Vygotskian sense , providing structured support while 

preserving learner engagement in meaning -making. Students emphasi sed iterative clarification 

(“ ask it to explain it to you again and again ”), suggesting contingent support characteristic of effective scaffolding. From an activity theory perspective, AI here mediates the relationship 

between the learner (subject) and conceptual understanding (object) by restructuring how 

explanation is accessed during problem -solving activity. 

Students also framed AI as an external cognitive aid consistent with distributed cognition, where 

reasoning is supported by representations outside the mind. The step -by -step breakdowns 

reduce extraneous cognitive load (CLT), allowing working memory resour ces to focus on sense -

making rather than decoding complexity. This, in turn, supports self -regulated learning, as 

students described using AI explanations to monitor understanding and close gaps. 

However, learners also articulated a clear boundary where scaffolding shifts into cognitive 

substitution. One student warned: “AI can be detrimental for students if they become overly 

dependent on it for answers, leading to a lack of critical thinking and problem solving skills.” 

Another stated: “Instead of using their brains to think about how to solve that equation, they'll 

just paste the equation (answer) from ChatGPT.” Here, AI ceases to mediate learning and instead 

replaces the learner’s cognitive work , aligning with students’ observation that “ learning 

becomes passive when students let AI think for them. ” In sociocultural terms, mediation is no 

longer internali sed. From a CLT perspective, germane cognitive load , necessary for schema 

construction is bypassed. In SRL terms, regulation shifts from learner -driven to AI -driven. 

Thus, within this dimension, students distinguish between AI as scaffold (extending cognition) 

and AI as shortcut (replacing cognition), demonstrating sophisticated awareness of the 

boundary between support and dependency. 

Dimension 2: Feedback & Error Detection 

Cognition occurs when AI helps learners improve by identifying mistakes, giving corrective 

suggestions, and providing immediate information that guides revision.  Students frequently 

positioned AI as a formative feedback partner, closely aligned with SRL theory, particularly 

processes of self -monitoring and performance calibration. Learners described AI tools as 

helping them see errors they would otherwise miss. One student shared: “Mostly, I use 

Grammarly and Quillbot (AI tools) to check my grammar once I finish my draft. Those grammar 

checker apps can make me reali se the tiny mistakes that I have made while writing my essays.” 

Another explained: “After drafting my essays, I seek feedback from ChatGPT, asking it to evaluate 

my work and provide suggestions for improvement.” Here, AI acts as an externali sed evaluative 

lens, supporting metacognitive awareness , a core SRL mechanism. Immediate feedback 

reduces the delay between action and reflection, strengthening regulation loops. From a 

distributed cognition perspective, evaluative judgment is partially offloaded into the system, but 

still interpreted by the lea rner. Within activity theory, AI reshapes the feedback syste m in the 

learning activity by inserting a tool that accelerates the feedback cycle. Students emphasi sed 

that this process “ helps me refine my writing… and deepens my understanding ,” suggesting 

feedback supports conceptual restructuring, not just surface correction. 

AI feedback also reduces extraneous cognitive load by highlighting errors directly, allowing 

learners to allocate working memory to revision strategies rather than error detection itself. 

However, students also recogni sed a shift from regulation support to regulation replacement. 

One cautioned: “A caveat of using an AI chatbot is that the content generated may not always be 

accurate and not always trustworthy.” Others noted: “Students can just type in what they want 

and let AI do the work for them.” When le arners accept AI judgments uncritically, monitoring 

becomes outsourced. In SRL terms, internal evaluation is replaced by external authority. 

Socioculturally, mediation is not internali sed. Students described this as a move toward passivity where “ Students may lose the ability to think for themselves. ” Thus, AI feedback 

operates as metacognitive scaffold when learners remain evaluative agents, but becomes 

cognitive substitution when judgment is ceded entirely. 

Dimension 3: Idea Stimulation 

Idea stimulation is when AI helps learners generate, expand, or explore ideas by offering prompts, 

alternative perspectives, or starting points for thinking.  Many s tudents described AI as a 

generator of initial ideas, positioning it as a tool that stimulates rather than replaces thinking. 

This aligns with sociocultural theory, where tools mediate higher mental functions, and with 

activity theory, where AI becomes a c ognitive artifact that reshapes how learners begin tasks. 

One student wrote: “AI is best for crea tivity blocks… It can generate thousands of ideas, and in 

collaboration with our amazing human brain, millions of answers can be sought by just a single 

prompt.” These accounts show AI functioning as a divergent thinking prompt, widening the 

problem space. From a distributed cognition perspective, idea generation is shared between 

learner and system, expanding associative reach. Importantly, several learners mainta ined 

epistemic ownership: “AI gives ideas but I still develop them myself.” “I sometimes use A I to 

generate layouts or mild suggestions… but I don’t rely on it to complete all my work.” 

Here, AI reduces the initiation barrier without replacing creative construction, supporting 

productive cognitive load (CLT) by freeing resources for elaboration rather than blank -page 

anxiety. It also supports self -regulated learning by enabling strategic help -seeking at early 

planning stages. 

However, students clearly articulated the risk of idea outsourcing: “Students may become overly 

dependent on AI tools, which could hinder their ability to think critically.” 

and “Students just ask AI to create layouts instead of struggling productively.” In these cases, 

generative struggle , essential for creative cognition is bypassed. Socioculturally, mediation is 

not internali sed; instead, the tool becomes the thinker. Learners warned that “ creativity could 

be suppressed if AI always gives ideas first. ” T hus, AI brainstorming operates as a creative 

scaffold when learners remain authors of development, but becomes substitutive cognition 

when ideation itself is delegated to the system. 

Dimension 4: Cognitive Organi sation 

Cognitive organisation is when AI helps learners structure knowledge by summari sing 

information, creating outlines, highlighting key points, or organi sing content into clearer forms .

Students frequently described AI as helping them manage complexity rather than merely retrieve 

answers. Many students focused on summarising, outlining, and restructuring content: “AI can 

distil complex chapters into key points, making it easier for me to grasp essential concepts 

during revision.” “It can generate step -by -step guides… and even give us summarisations of 

notes.” “AI summaries help me manage information overload when my notes are too much.” 

Here AI functions as an external cognitive organi ser, closely aligned with distributed cognition. 

The structuring of knowledge is partially offloaded into the tool (e.g., outlines, flashcards, 

condensed notes), allowing learners to focus working memory on meaning -making rather than 

information management. From a cognitive load theory perspective, AI reduces extraneous load 

(overwhelming volume), potentially freeing capacity for germane processing , understanding 

relationships and key ideas. 

This support also appears in study strategies: “I use AI to create flashcards and quizzes… It’s a 

quick and effective method of studying.” Such use reflects self -regulated learning, where students strategically employ tools to plan and organi se learning tasks. Within activity theory, AI 

reshapes how learners engage with the object (content), altering the representational form of 

knowledge while keeping the learner in the activity. 

However, students simultaneously recognised a boundary. Several expressed concern that 

summarisation could become over -compression : “If AI summari ses everything, I might not 

process deeply myself.” “Students rely on AI notes instead of making their own.” “Information is 

consumed quickly but not retained.” 

When learners skip the act of organi sing , which is itself a learning process , AI shifts from scaffold 

to shortcut. The sociocultural process of internalising structure through active reconstruction is 

replaced by passive reception. Thus, organi sation that supports understanding can become 

compression that undermines it. 

Dimension 5: Adaptive Tutoring Support 

Adaptive Tutoring Support is when AI adjusts explanations, practice, and difficulty based on a 

learner’s level, providing personali sed instructional support.  Students often portrayed AI as 

something that adjusts itself to them, not the other way around. Their descriptions show AI 

acting like a responsive tutor that matches difficulty, pace, and explanation style: Some 

explained “By informing Code Helper… of my current coding knowledge, I can receive exercises 

that match my skill level, allowing me to progre ssively challenge myself.” Another student 

mentioned “AI can be very useful in personali sing a student’s learning experience… match each 

students’ level of understanding and… provide customi sed learning materials.” And another 

student shared that “AI platforms adapt lessons to my pace and explain again when I don’t 

understand.” 

This reflects sociocultural theory and the idea of support within the ZPD , learners use AI when a 

task is just beyond independent ability, and AI provides contingent guidance. The dynamic 

nature adjusting difficulty, repeating explanations , also aligns with activity theory, where the tool 

(AI) mediates the subject –object relationship differently depending on task demands. From a 

cognitive load perspective, adaptation prevents overload by calibrating challenge. It also 

supports self -regulated learning, as st udents choose when to seek help and use AI to extend 

their competence. 

Yet students drew a clear social boundary , some students mentioned that “The position of 

independent learning and teaching by teachers should never be replaced.” And 

“AI should support teachers, not replace them.” “Whereas a chatbot doesn’t. It has no 

emotions…” 

Here, the risk is not cognitive difficulty but misplacement of authority. Learners worry AI tutoring 

could displace human mentorship, weakening the social and relational dimensions of learning 

emphasised in sociocultural and activity perspectives. Thus, ad aptive tutoring is valued as 

cognitive extension but only when it supplements, rather than substitutes for, human teaching. 

Dimension 6: Metacognitive Monitoring Support 

In this dimension, students described AI as helping them see themselves as learners.  AI helps 

learners become aware of their strengths, weaknesses, progress, and learning patterns.  Rather 

than giving answers, AI made performance gaps visible: “AI can analy se a student’s learning 

patterns and preferences… track progress… identifying areas where a student excels and where 

they struggle.” Another student mentioned that “Using the power of AI, teachers can use it to monitor the learning patterns of students, and make personali sed adjustments to suit the needs 

of each individual student. It would be certainly easier to monitor 30 students with an AI 

companion than mere sheets of paper. ”

These findings function as an external mirror that supports monitoring and evaluation phases of 

regulation. Learners use AI -generated feedback to recalibrate effort, revise strategies, and plan 

next steps. From a distributed cognition perspective, metacognitive work is p artly offloaded i nto 

the tool: progress tracking, pattern detection, and performance comparison become shared 

between learner and AI. AI becomes a mediating artifact reshaping how learners engage with the 

object of activity (improvement), making invisible patterns visible. By clarifying weaknesses, AI 

may also reduce cognitive load, freeing working memory from trying to self -diagnose errors. 

However, students were cautious about over -reliance , “It is essential for students to know the 

limits and understand the ethicality of using artificial intelligence.” And another student 

proclaims “AI assumes I understand if I don’t ask questions — teachers can see from my 

behavior.” Here, the risk is illusory understanding. If learners passively accept AI analytics 

without reflective engagement, monitoring becomes mechanical rather than metacognitive. 

Socioculturally, this also weakens teacher –student diagnostic interaction. Thus, AI extends 

cognition when it prompts reflection, but risks replacing cognition if learners outsource self -

awareness instead of developing it. 

Dimension 7: Task & Cognitive Load Regulation 

In this dimension, cognitive load regulation is  when AI helps learners manage workload by 

breaking tasks into steps, planning study sequences, and reducing feelings of overwhelm. 

Students positioned AI as a tool that helps them manage the demands of learning rather than 

directly supplying answers. Several described how AI helped structure work , “AI programs like 

Trello (AI tool) can help me plan out my study time, remind me of impending deadlines, and divide 

complicated jobs into smaller, more manageable chunks.” And another student mentioned “AI 

reminders help me stay organi sed and manage deadlines.” These uses resonate strongly with 

Cognitive Load Theory. By chunking tasks and orga ni sing steps, AI reduces extraneous load 

(planning, remembering deadlines) so working memory can focus on learning itself. Within Self -

Regulated Learning, this reflects planning and time -management regulation, where AI supports 

executive control processes. Fr om a distributed cognition lens, scheduling, sequencing, and 

reminders are partially externali sed into the tool. Activity theory further explains this as a 

reorgani sation of the activity system: the tool reshapes how learners coordinate actions toward 

the object (task completion). Socioculturally, AI here supports participation by lowering barriers 

to engagement rather than replacing thinking. 

Yet students also recogni sed a boundary , “Students can also ask AI… to help them finish their 

homework… and they cannot learn in the process.” And “Because it is convenient, it is easy to 

over rely on it.” The risk appears when regulation shifts from managing work to avoiding effort. 

Instead of scaffolding persistence, AI can become a shortcut that bypasses productive struggle. 

In this substitutive positioning, load reduction turns into cognitive offloading of the core task 

itself. Thus, AI extends cognitio n when it supports task management, but risks replacing 

cognition when learners use it to escape the thinking that tasks are designed to provoke. 

Dimension 8: Learning Continuity Support 

Students frequently described AI as a resource that keeps learning going beyond classroom 

boundaries.  This dimension occurs when AI provides learning assistance beyond classroom 

time, allowing learners to access explanations and help whenever needed.  This positioning highlights AI as a continuity scaffold , as students mentioned “When I don’t have anyone to teach 

me, AI helps. It is very efficient and convenient” , “AI is available anywhere and anytime so I can 

still not miss materials” and “AI is available when teachers are not.” 

From a distributed cognition perspective, cognitive support is no longer tied to a single physical 

setting; assistance is distributed across time, space, and tool. Sociocultural theory frames this 

as expanded participation in learning practices , AI becomes an always -available mediational 

means that allows learners to remain engaged in activity even when traditional social supports 

are absent. Through activity theory, the tool reshapes the temporal structure of the learning 

activity system, extendi ng the sub ject –object interaction beyond school hours. In self -regulated 

learning, AI supports help -seeking and persistence, enabling learners to sustain learning cycles 

independently. This reflects effort regulation, learners use AI to manage frustration and sustain 

task engagement. It may also reduce overload (cognitive load theory) when immediate 

clarification prevents confusion from accumulating. However, students simultaneously 

articulated relational and developmental risks , “Students may stop asking teachers … Teacher –

student relationships weaken” , “Less human interaction harms social development” and 

“Because it is convenient, it is easy to over rely on it.” Here, continuity risks becoming isolation. 

Instead of extending the learning community, AI can narrow interaction to human –machine 

exchange. Socioculturally, this weakens dialogic learning; in activity terms, community 

elements shrink. Thus, AI extends cog nition when it supplements human networks, but risks 

replacing socially mediated learning when it becomes the primary partner, displacing teacher –

student dialogue rather than extending it. 

Dimension 9: Explanation Reframing 

In this dimension, students portrayed AI as a tool that reshapes how ideas are presented, 

allowing the same concept to be encountered through multiple cognitive entry points for 

example via simpler language, multi -modal or alternative examples to support understanding. 

Learners described asking AI to simplify material, rephrase explanations, and adjust the level of 

complexity, such as requesting that content be “explained as if I were a 10 -year -old,” or using AI 

to turn dense paragraphs into “easier to read” language. Others noted that AI “explains in 

different ways until I understand,” provides examples that clarify structure, and even helps 

overcome language barriers through translation. 

From a distributed cognition perspective, these reformulations act as external supports that 

help learners make sense of ideas. In other words, the AI is not just giving information, it 

becomes part of the way the learner is thinking and understanding the topic. Sociocultural 

theory frames this as mediation through language tools: AI reshapes symbolic resources that 

learners use to internali se understanding. In cognitive load theory, simplified phrasing and 

alternative examples may reduce extraneous load, a llowing working memory to focus on core 

concepts. From an activity theory perspective, the tool alters how the learner engages with the 

object (knowledge), transforming the pathway of understanding. Within self -regulated learning, 

learners actively seek re framing when comprehension breaks down, showing strategic 

regulation of understanding. Yet students were also aware that simplification can distort. They 

noted AI explanations are “ not always accurate ,” sometimes contain “ errors ,” or produce “ weird 

steps a nd wrong answers in math ,” requiring teacher verification. Risk emerges when reframing 

becomes over -simplification , learners may accept surface -level clarity without deep processing. 

Socioculturally, dialogic sense -making with teachers can be replaced by passive acceptance of 

machine explanations. Thus, AI extends cognition when reframing supports comprehension and internali sation, but risks replacing cognition when simplification leads to illusion of 

understanding without conceptual depth.

Figure 1: AI as Dynamic Cognitive Partner Framework Discussion 

The framework’s contribution is not simply that it “lists” nine learner -described uses of AI, but 

that it reconceptualises what counts as AI’s educational role and where learning is located when 

learners study with GenAI systems. First, it shifts the unit of analysis from tool categories (e.g., 

tutor, chatbot, grader) toward learner -attributed cognitive functions, aligning with learning -

sciences traditions that treat technologies as mediational means whose meaning emerges in 

use rather than in design labels (Vygotsky, 1978). Second, it theorises AI use as dynamic role 

shifting across phases of activity, consistent with activity theory’s claim that tools reconfigure 

action in relation to goals, rules, and division of labour (Engeström, 1987), and with SRL’s phase 

models in which supports are recruited differently during planning, monitoring, and reflection 

(Zimmerman, 2000). Third, and most importantly, it foregrounds a support –substitution 

boundary as a core theoretical problem: the same AI interaction can either extend cognition 

(scaffolding, reorganising representation s, reducing extraneous load) or replace cognition 

(outsourcing sense -making, metacognitive laziness and AI dependent thinker ). This boundary 

reframes “overreliance” from a purely ethical or policy issue into a psychological and 

interactional phenomenon: a shift in epistemic agency and responsibility for thinking within 

mediated activity. In doing so, the framework integrates so ciocultural mediation, distributed 

cognition, SRL, and cognitive load theory into a coherent account of how AI becomes part of 

learn ers’ cognitive systems while also creating conditions for cognitive offloading that may 

undermine learning. 

Implication of the Framework 

A deeper theoretical contribution of the Dynamic Cognitive Partner framework is that it provides 

a bridging model between established learning theories and the distinctive interactional 

properties of Gen AI. Much prior AI -in -education work either (a) evaluates outcomes 

(achievement, satisfaction, time saved , acceptance ), (b) categorises systems by instructional 

function (tutor, recommender, automated scoring), or (c) focuses on ethics and governance. 

Those approaches are necessary, but they often under -theor ise a key shift introduced by GenAI: 

learners are no longer only receiving instruction from a system; they are thinking with it in real 

time through dialogue, iterative prompting, and rapid re -representation. This framework 

contributes by explaining this shift at the level where learning actually happens: moment -to -

moment cognitive activity under task demands and constraint. 

Figure 1 operationalises the Dynamic Cognitive Partner claim by depicting AI as an agent that 

enters learners’ thinking through dynamic role shifts rather than a single, fixed “tool role.” At the 

top, Learner Cognition anchors the framework in students’ re asoning and self -regulated learning, 

while the central box specifies the nine AI cognitive functions that learners reported recruiting 

across study situations (e.g., conceptual scaffolding, feedback/error detection, idea stimulation, 

organisation, adaptive tutoring, metacognitive monitoring, task/load regulation, learning 

continuity, and explanation reframing). Critically, the framework adds an evaluative boundary 

condition: each function can be enacted along a continuum from cognitive extension (“AI 

suppor ts thinking”) to cognitive substitution (“AI replaces thinking”), depending on learner 

positioning, that is, whether the learner retains epistemic agency for sense -making, evaluation, 

and decision -making, or delegates these core processes to AI. This bound ary reframes 

“overreliance” as a situated shift in the division of cognitive labour, not merely the amount of AI 

use. The theoretical foundations at the base clarify why the same interaction can be productive 

or harmful: sociocultural mediation and scaffol ding explain contingent support; activity theory 

explains shifting roles within goal -directed activity systems; distributed cognition explains external representation and coordination; SRL explains phase -specific regulation demands; and 

cognitive load theory explains when load reduction becomes offloading. Practically, the 

framework can be used (a) by educators to design AI -supported tasks that p reserve learner 

agency by pairing each function with prompts for justification, comparison, and reflection; (b) 

by learners as a self -check to identify when AI is extending versus substituting their thinking; and 

(c) by researchers as an analytic coding sc heme for characterising AI –learner partnerships 

across contexts. 

1.  From “what the tool is” to “what the learner does with it” (a mediational turn) 

The first theoretical move is a mediational redefinition of AI’s role. Sociocultural theory argues 

that higher mental functions develop through mediated action with cultural tools and signs 

(Vygotsky, 1978). Crucially, the “role” of a tool is not fixed by its material form; it is enacted in 

activity. This aligns with activity theory that agency is distributed across people and mediational 

means, and that the same artifact can enable different forms of action depending on how it is 

taken up. By organising AI use into learner -attributed cognitive roles (scaffolding, monitoring, 

organisation, persistence support, etc.), the framework operationalises this insight for the GenAI 

era: AI is theorised not as a “tutor” in the abstract but as a mediational means recru ited for 

specific cognitive functions. This matters because “AI tutor” or “chatbot” categories can 

obscure important distinctions: a chatbot can function as a conceptual scaffold in one moment 

and as a shortcut in another. The framework therefore pushes th eory and research away from 

platform labels toward the psychological function of interaction, which is closer to how 

mediation and learning are theorised in the learning sciences. 

This also clarifies why seemingly similar “AI use” can produce different learning outcomes in 

prior research: effects will vary depending on whether AI was used for sense -making (supportive 

mediation) versus answer acquisition (substitutive offloading). In other words, the framework 

provides a theoretical vocabulary for explaining heterogeneity that outcome -only studies often 

report but cannot interpret. 

2.  Dynamic role shifting as an activity -theoretic and SRL phenomenon 

A second contribution is that it treats variability in AI use as theoretically meaningful rather than 

noise. Activity theory holds that tools mediate subject –object relations inside activity systems 

shaped by rules, community, and division of labour (Enges tröm, 1987). Generative AI intensifies 

this because it can rapidly take on multiple functions within the same activity (e.g., a learner 

writing an essay can use AI to generate an outline, then ask for counterarguments, then check 

grammar, then request cita tions). The framework as “dynamic partner” claim is essentially an 

activity -theoretic claim: AI reconfigures the learning activity by enabling micro -transitions 

between sub -goals, thereby changing the temporal organisation of action. 

This also links tightly with self -regulated learning. SRL models conceptualise learning as cyclical 

phases , forethought/planning, performance/monitoring, reflection/adaptation (Zimmerman, 

2000). Each phase has different regulatory demands, and learners strategically recruit supports 

differently across phases. The framework’s dimensions map naturally onto these p hase 

demands (e.g., idea stimulation and task chunking as planning supports; feedback and 

monitoring as performance supports; reflection through ide ntifying weak points). The deeper 

insight is that GenAI is not only a “resource”; it can become a co -regulator, shaping learners’ 

strategy selection, effort regulation, and monitoring frequency. That expands SRL theory into a 

modern “regulation ecology” in which an AI agent can supply prompts, evaluations, and 

representations that alter the learner’s internal regulatory cycle. At the same time, the framework highlights a theoretical risk: when AI supplies too much 

regulation (e.g., it identifies weaknesses, decides next steps, evaluates correctness), the learner 

may shift from self -regulation toward delegated regulation, weakening metacognitive skil l

development. This is a sharper, more theoretically grounded reframing than the broad claim that 

“students rely too much on AI.” It identifies which part of regulation is displaced (monitoring, 

evaluation, planning) and thereby provid es leverage for both research and design. 

3.  Clarifying the boundary between cognitive extension and cognitive substitution 

The third and most distinctive theoretical contribution is that it centres the support –substitution 

boundary as a hinge concept connecting multiple theories that are too often discussed 

separately. 

From distributed cognition, the core claim is that cognitive work can be distributed across 

artifacts, representations, and social partners (Hutchins, 1995). From the extended mind thesis, 

external resources can become functionally integrated into thinking when they are reliably 

available and tightly coupled to the user’s goals (Clark & Chalmers, 1998). GenAI is unusually 

“extendable” because it is conversational, always available, and can generate bespoke 

representations on demand. The framework advances t hese perspectives by specifying how 

learners experience the extension: not only memory support (like notes) but explanation 

generation, monitoring cues, and motivational bridges. 

However, distributed/extended cognition has always carried an implicit question: when does 

distributing cognition stop being productive and start undermining learning? Education depends 

on internal changes , schema construction, conceptual reorganisation, strategy acquisition , not 

only task completion. The framework makes this boundary visible empirically and theoretically. 

It shows that learners themselves articulate the difference between using AI to support sense -

making versus using AI to avoid sense -maki ng. That boundary is the point where mediation fails 

to become internalised in Vygotskian terms, and where the division of labour shifts so far toward 

the tool that the learner’s epistemic agency is reduced. 

Cognitive load theory helps explain why this boundary is so slippery. CLT distinguishes 

extraneous load (wasteful processing) from germane load (processing that contributes to 

learning) (Sweller, 1994). AI can reduce extraneous load by simplifying language , organising 

information, and providing worked examples. But it can also reduce germane load if learners 

skip explanation generation, error analysis, or retrieval effort , processes that are difficult 

precisely because they build durable knowledge structure s. The framework therefore provides a 

theoretical mechanism for what students described as “information consumed quickly but not 

retained”: AI may optimise fluency and speed while inadvertently reducing the cognitive work 

required for schema formation. Thi s is not a claim that AI is inherently harmful; it is a theory -

driven account of a specific failure mode: cognitive bypass. 

4.  Introducing “learner positioning toward AI” as an epistemic -agency construct 

Another deep contribution is the implicit construct the findings surface: learner positioning 

toward AI. In the literature, “trust calibration,” “AI literacy,” and “help -seeking” are often treated 

separately. The framework unifies them through a positioning lens: learners decide whether AI 

is an assistant to their cognition or a replacement for their cognition. This is fundamentally a 

question of epistemic agency , who is responsible for generating, validating, and owning 

knowledge. The framework thus offers a way to theorise overreliance not simply as “too much 

usage” but as a shift in epistemic stance: from learner -as -author to learner -as -consumer. This matters because it suggests new research predictions: substitutive positioning should 

correlate with weaker transfer, poorer metacognitive calibration, and lower resilience when AI is 

unavailable (e.g., exams), whereas supportive positioning should co rrelate with improved 

strategy repertoire and stronger conceptual understanding. It also provides a design lever: 

systems can be built to nudge learners toward supportive positioning (prompting justification, 

comparison, reflection) rather than substitutiv e positioning (instant final answers). 

5.  A coherent integrative model that the field currently lacks 

Finally, the framework’s theoretical value lies in integration. Sociocultural theory explains 

mediation and internalisation; activity theory explains goal -directed systems and division of 

labour; distributed cognition explains external representation and c oordination; SRL explains 

regulation cycles; CLT explains capacity constraints and offloading risks. Many papers reference 

one or two of these lenses, but few provide a coherent model that can simultaneously explain 

why AI feels empowering, why it changes learning practices, and why it can undermine learning 

when mispositioned. The framework functions as that integrative model , anchored in learner 

accounts rather than imposed categories , making it especially valuable for a field that is 

currently fragmented across tool types, outcomes, and policy debates. 

Conclusion 

This study reconceptuali ses AI’s educational role through a learner -informed lens, positioning 

AI not as a static instructional tool but as a dynamic cognitive partner whose function shifts 

across learning situations. Analysis of students’ written accounts shows that learners perceive 

AI as participating directly in thinking processes , explaining, organi sing, stimulating ideas, 

monitoring understanding, regulating workload, and sustaining learning beyond classroom 

contexts. Crucially, students themselves articulated a boundary between cognitive extension 

and cognitive substitution, demonstrating awareness that AI can either support understanding 

and self -regulation or replace effortful cognitive work .

The framework contributes theoretically by relocating AI from the periphery of instruction to the 

core of cognitive activity. It integrates sociocultural mediation, distributed cognition, activity 

theory, self -regulated learning, and cognitive load perspec tives into a unified account of how AI 

becomes part of learners’ cognitive systems. At the same time, it clarifies that AI’s educational 

value is not inherent in the technology but emerges from how learners position AI within their 

activity as a scaffold f or sense -making or as a shortcut that bypasses it. By foregrounding this 

support –substitution boundary, the framework reframes overreliance as a cognitive and 

interactional phenomenon rather than solely an ethical issue. 

Several limitations should be considered. The study relies on self -reported written responses 

rather than measuring learning outcomes , which capture learners’ perceptions and experiences 

of AI use rather than direct observation of behavior. Students may describe ideali sed or socially 

desirable practices, and their accounts may not fully represent the exact moment of cognitive 

processes during actual AI interaction. 

Finally, AI systems evolve rapidly. The framework captures patterns of mediation characteristic 

of current conversational and generative systems, but specific interaction forms may change as 

technologies develop. 

Future research should move from conceptual description toward design and intervention 

studies that support learners in maintaining epistemic agency when working with AI. One 

promising direction is the development of structured classroom implementations th at help learners become more aware of when they are engaging in productive cognitive effort and when 

they are offloading core thinking processes. Such supports could include reflective prompts, 

metacognitive checklists, or activity routines that encourage learners to pause, evaluate their 

understanding, and justify AI -supported decisions. These forms of implementation would not 

restrict AI use, but instead cultivate learners’ ability to regulate how AI is integrated into their 

cognitive work. 

In addition, future studies should investigate how different learner positionings toward AI relate 

to learning outcomes, transfer, and long -term strategy development. Comparative research 

across age groups, subject domains, and cultural contexts would furt her refine the framework. 

Finally, combining self -report data with learning analytics, screen capture, or think -aloud 

protocols could provide richer insight into the real -time dynamics of human –AI cognitive 

partnerships.
