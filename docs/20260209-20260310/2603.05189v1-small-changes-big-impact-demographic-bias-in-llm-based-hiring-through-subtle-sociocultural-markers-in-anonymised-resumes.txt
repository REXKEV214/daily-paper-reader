Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increas-ingly deployed in resume screening pipelines. Although explicit PII (e.g., names) is com-monly redacted, resumes typically retain subtle sociocultural markers (languages, co-curricular activities, volunteering, hobbies) that can act as demographic proxies. We introduce a general-isable stress-test framework for hiring fairness, instantiated in the Singapore context: 100 neu-tral job-aligned resumes are augmented into 

4100 variants spanning four ethnicities and two genders, differing only in job-irrelevant markers. We evaluate 18 LLMs in two realis-tic settings: (i) Direct Comparison (1v1) and (ii) Score & Shortlist (top-scoring rate), each with and without rationale prompting. Even without explicit identifiers, models recover de-mographic attributes with high F1 and exhibit systematic disparities, with models favouring markers associated with Chinese and Caucasian males. Ablations show language markers suf-fice for ethnicity inference, whereas gender re-lies on hobbies and activities. Furthermore, prompting for explanations tends to amplify bias. Our findings suggest that seemingly in-nocuous markers surviving anonymisation can materially skew automated hiring outcomes. 

1 Introduction 

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into recruitment pipelines promises unprecedented efficiency and scalability, yet simultaneously raises critical fairness concerns that demand rigorous in-vestigation (Chen, 2023; Fabris et al., 2025). As or-ganisations increasingly deploy AI-powered tools for resume screening, candidate ranking, and pre-liminary assessments, the potential for these sys-tems to encode and amplify demographic biases has become a pressing societal issue (Mehrabi et al., 2022; Gallegos et al., 2024). The consequences of biased hiring algorithms extend beyond individ-ual candidates to perpetuate systemic inequalities in employment, reinforcing historical patterns of discrimination (Yarger et al., 2020). While extensive prior research has documented LLM biases against explicit demographic indi-cators, including names (Nghiem et al., 2024; Armstrong et al., 2024; Iso et al., 2025), gender pronouns (Kotek et al., 2023), and racial identi-fiers (Armstrong et al., 2024), real-world hiring systems increasingly employ anonymisation tech-niques to redact Personally Identifiable Informa-tion (PII) (Asthana et al., 2025; Manzanares-Salor and Sánchez, 2026; Lo et al., 2025). This practice, while well-intentioned, may provide a false sense of security: candidates’ resumes remain rich with subtle, ostensibly job-irrelevant sociocultural mark-ers that can implicitly signal race, gender, and so-cioeconomic status (Parasurama and Sedoc, 2022; Madera et al., 2025). These markers (spoken lan-guages, co-curricular activities, volunteering expe-riences, hobbies) constitute sociocultural proxies 

that survive conventional anonymisation. Our study investigates whether these subtle markers alone are sufficient to trigger demographic bias in state-of-the-art LLMs deployed in hiring contexts. We develop a generalisable experimen-tal framework applicable to any cultural setting, which we instantiate within the Singapore con-text for several methodologically important rea-sons. Singapore’s multiethnic, multilingual society, comprising Chinese (74.3%), Malay (13.5%), In-dian (9.0%), and other ethnic groups, including Caucasians (Department of Statistics Singapore, 2020), provides a natural laboratory with clearly distinguishable sociocultural markers across demo-graphic groups (Miner, 2023). Furthermore, Sin-gapore’s recent AI safety evaluations have high-lighted the inadequacy of Western-centric bias test-ing (Chia, 2025), underscoring the need for region-specific bias audits (IMDA and Humane Intelli-gence, 2025). Research Questions. We organise our investiga-tion around three core research questions: RQ1 (Demographic Recoverability): Can LLMs in-fer gender and ethnicity from anonymised resumes containing only sociocultural markers, and which marker categories drive this inference? RQ2 (Out-come Bias): How do demographic markers alter hiring outcomes under two complementary evalu-ation paradigms: (a) Direct Comparison (a 1v1 forced-choice between two candidates) and (b) 

Score & Shortlist (independent scoring of a pool to find top-scoring candidates), and how sensitive are these outcomes to prompt variations? RQ3 (Demographic Disparities): Which demographic groups receive the most and least favourable treat-ment, and how do these disparities manifest across models and settings? 

Contributions. Our work makes three key con-tributions: (1) We introduce a generalisable eval-uation framework (Figure 1) comprising 4100 re-sumes with controlled demographic markers that di-agnoses hiring biases in LLMs via Direct Compar-ison (1v1) and Score & Shortlist , with prompt sen-sitivity analysis that surface distinct bias pathways. (2) We provide empirical evidence that LLMs can infer demographic stereotypes from subtle markers (language suffices for ethnicity; hobbies/activities drive gender inference) and that these inferences can propagate to hiring outcomes. (3) We demon-strate that prompting for decision rationale, as an interpretability and debiasing strategy, neither guar-antees nor systematically improves fairness and can paradoxically amplify bias in certain models. 

2 Related Work 

Bias in Large Language Models. The study of bias in NLP systems has evolved from foundational work on word embeddings (May et al., 2019; Guo and Caliskan, 2021) to comprehensive audits of modern LLMs. Researchers have documented per-vasive biases across gender (Kotek et al., 2023; Anantaprayoon et al., 2024), race (Nangia et al., 2020; Nadeem et al., 2021), and intersectional di-mensions (Kumar et al., 2024; Wan and Chang, 2024). Recent surveys provide taxonomies of bias sources, evaluation metrics, and mitigation strate-gies (Gallegos et al., 2024; Ferrara, 2023). A particularly relevant strand of research ex-amines implicit versus explicit biases in LLMs. Bai et al. (2024) demonstrate that models pass-ing explicit fairness tests can still harbour implicit biases detectable through indirect evaluation. Li et al. (2025) show that LLM agents reveal biases through their decisions rather than explicit state-ments, with more advanced models sometimes ex-hibiting greater implicit bias despite reduced ex-plicit bias. This distinction is critical, as it suggests that surface-level safeguards may be insufficient, motivating our investigation into biases triggered by subtle, non-explicit signals. 

Bias in AI-Assisted Hiring. Algorithmic hiring tools have attracted significant scrutiny for their potential to perpetuate employment discrimination (Chen, 2023; Fabris et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2024; Qu et al., 2025). Armstrong et al. (2024) conducted a comprehensive audit of GPT-3.5, finding system-atic race and gender biases in resume assessment and generation tasks. Iso et al. (2025) evaluated LLMs on job-resume matching, discovering that while recent models have reduced explicit gender and race biases, implicit biases concerning educa-tional background remain significant. Nghiem et al. (2024) and Wilson and Caliskan (2025) both found significant preferences for White-associated pro-files over Black-associated counterparts. However, these studies largely rely on demographic pertur-bation via explicit PII (names), which overlooks the fact that such identifiers can be easily redacted. We instead examine a more resilient source of bias by asking whether LLMs are influenced by socio-cultural proxies (e.g., hobbies, languages); implicit signals that persist even after anonymisation. 

Cultural and Sociocultural Bias. Beyond individual-level demographics, researchers have documented cultural biases in LLMs that favour Western, English-speaking perspectives (Tao et al., 2024; Qu and Wang, 2024). Within the hiring domain, Rao et al. (2025) found systematic bi-ases against Indian interview transcripts compared to UK ones, with disparities linked to linguistic features rather than explicit demographic signals. These findings (that bias can be triggered by sub-tle, culturally-linked patterns) directly motivate our decision to instantiate a generalisable framework within the diverse Singapore context. The sensitivity of LLMs to persona assignments and prompt variations has emerged as a related concern. Gupta et al. (2023) demonstrated that persona-assigned LLMs exhibit implicit reasoning biases. Tan and Lee (2025) revealed that demo-graphic prompts significantly impact response qual-ity across power-disparate scenarios. Our investi-gation of rationale prompting as a potential debi-asing strategy (Yogarajan et al., 2025) contributes to this literature by examining whether explanation requirements alter bias patterns in hiring contexts. 

3 Methodology 

Our experimental framework comprises four se-quential stages: (1) job description curation and neutral resume generation, (2) demographic marker injection, (3) bias evaluation under dual settings, and (4) demographic recoverability and ablation analysis. This design ensures that any observed disparities can be attributed solely to the injected sociocultural markers, as all job-relevant qualifica-tions remain constant across demographic variants. 

3.1 Job Descriptions and Resume Generation 

We curated 100 structured job descriptions span-ning diverse industries including engineering , fi-nance , healthcare , retail , and services , sourced from major Singaporean job portals and corporate career pages 1 to ensure ecological validity. We stan-dardised job descriptions to specify required quali-fications, experience levels, and core competencies, controlling for stylistic variation while preserving authentic role requirements. For each job description, we generated asingle “neutral” baseline resume using a state-of-the-art LLM ( gemini-2.5-pro ), instructed to produce realistic candidates with minor, plausible weaknesses (slightly less experience than required, or missing one secondary skill). These controlled imperfections prevent ceiling ef-fects: For example, if baselines scored 100/100, marker injection could only reveal disadvan-tages, precluding detection of demographic-specific advantages . Each neutral resume con-tains placeholder fields in an “Additional Infor-mation” section: [LANGUAGES] , [ACTIVITIES] ,

[VOLUNTEERING] , and [HOBBIES] . These place-holders serve as injection points for demographic markers while ensuring that the core job-relevant content (education, work experience, skills) re-mains identical across all variants. Examples of job descriptions and generated baseline resumes can be found in Appendix B.

Languages (linguistic repertoires that correlate with ethnicity), Co-curricular Activities (CCAs)

3.3 Models Evaluated 

We evaluated 18 state-of-the-art LLMs spanning proprietary and open-weight model families, as de-tailed in Table 2 (Appendix A). This selection spans parameter scales from 8B to 235B+ and includes models from providers such as OpenAI, Google, Meta, DeepSeek, Alibaba, xAI, Mistral, and An-thropic, enabling analysis of how model character-istics influence bias patterns. 

3.4 Evaluation Settings 

We utilise two complementary evaluation settings that attempt to mirror realistic deployment scenar-ios for LLM-based hiring tools. 

3.4.1 Setting A: Direct Comparison (1v1) 

This setting simulates a head-to-head candidate comparison: recruiters or AI systems must choose 

> 2These markers are intentionally drawn from common (and reductive) sociocultural stereotypes: they are not norma-tive claims about any group, and individuals of any ethnic-ity/gender may plausibly exhibit any marker.

between two applicants. For each demographic-augmented resume, we construct a comparison task against its neutral baseline (same job, iden-tical job-relevant qualifications). To control for position bias, we run each comparison twice with positions swapped (A=augmented, B=neutral; then A=neutral, B=augmented) and average the results. The LLM judge outputs a verdict: A (prefers first resume), B (prefers second resume), or Tie .We convert verdicts to points (win=1.0, tie=0.5, loss=0.0) and compute per-comparison win-rates as the mean across both positions. The ideal win-rate is 0.5 for every demographic group: since augmented and neutral resumes are equivalent in job-relevant content, a fair evaluator should show no systematic preference. Deviation from 0.5 in-dicates bias induced by the presence (or absence) of demographic-associated sociocultural markers. However, this setting inherently frames evaluation as a choice between two candidates, which may not reflect real-world hiring workflows where multi-ple candidates are evaluated independently (Cohen et al., 2025) from a large pool of applicants. 

3.4.2 Setting B: Score & Shortlist 

This setting simulates shortlisting based on scoring and ranking, reflecting hiring workflows where a pool of candidates is scored independently and top candidates are shortlisted. Each resume is scored on a 1 to 100 scale against its job description. For each job, we identify all resumes achieving the maximum score (ties permitted) out of the 40 

augmented and 1 neutral resumes. We then com-pute top-score appearance rates for each demo-graphic group across all jobs. The ideal appear-ance rate is 100% : if demographic markers are truly job-irrelevant, all 41 resumes for a given job should tie at the maximum score. Disparities in appearance rates quantify opportunity loss due to demographic bias (Iso et al., 2025). 

3.4.3 Prompt Sensitivity Analysis 

Stakeholders deploying AI hiring systems increas-ingly demand interpretability that spans multi-stage decision pipelines and supports transparency, con-testability, and auditability (Schumann et al., 2020). Rationale-augmented outputs provide a seemingly accessible form of explanation, though recent work demonstrates that chain-of-thought explanations can be unfaithful, rationalising biased decisions without revealing their true causes (Turpin et al., 2023). This tension motivates our comparison of prompts with and without rationale requirements. Each evaluation setting comprises two prompt variants: No rationale: The prompt requests only the verdict or score. With rationale: The prompt requests a 2–3 sentence explanation before the ver-dict or score (e.g., “Explain your reasoning in 2–3 sentences, then provide your verdict.”). This design tests whether chain-of-thought style prompting, often advocated as a means to im-prove reasoning quality (Gandhi et al., 2023) and interpretability, affects bias magnitude or direc-tion. Temperature is set to 0 for all experiments to maximise reproducibility; small-scale experiments confirmed that temperature=0 yields stable results with largely minimal variance across repeated runs (see Appendix D for prompt templates and Ap-pendix F.2 for temperature stability analysis). 

3.5 Model-Level Bias Metrics 

We employ two model-level metrics: 

Normalised Demographic Performance Dis-parity: The gap between the highest- and lowest-performing demographic groups g, normalised to lie in [0 , 1] . For Direct Comparison (1v1) 

setting, this is the range of group win-rates: 

max g WR g − min g WR g (since WR ∈ [0 , 1] ). For Score & Shortlist setting, this is the range of top-score appearance (in %): (max g TopTier g −

min g TopTier g)/100 .

Normalised Deviation from Ideal: How far the macro-average is from the ideal, normalised to lie in [0 , 1] . For Direct Comparison (1v1) setting, the ideal win-rate is 0.5 and we compute |WR −

0.5|/0.5. For Score & Shortlist setting, the ideal top-score appearance is 100% and we compute 

|TopTier − 100 |/100 .The choice of ideal values reflects our core as-sumption that the injected sociocultural markers (languages, activities, volunteering, hobbies) are 

job-irrelevant and should not affect hiring suit-ability. For the first setting, an ideal win-rate of 0.5 means augmented resumes are selected at the same rate as neutral baselines, indicating that demo-graphic markers confer neither advantage nor disad-vantage. For the second setting, an ideal top-score appearance (the proportion of resumes achieving the maximum score per job after independent scor-ing) of 100% means all 41 variants for a given job tie at the maximum score. We assume that in prac-tice, hiring systems often shortlist only top-scoring candidates; any deviation from 100% top-score appearance indicates that demographic markers in-duced score variation where none should exist. 

3.6 Demographic Recoverability and Ablation 

To validate that injected markers are effective demo-graphic proxies, we conduct recoverability experi-ments: LLMs are prompted to classify the gender and ethnicity of candidates based solely on resume content. High accuracy indicates that markers en-code recoverable demographic stereotypes. We further conduct ablation studies to iden-tify which marker categories drive demographic inference. Starting from fully-marked resumes, we progressively remove marker categories (hob-bies →volunteering →activities →languages) and measure the impact on recoverability F1 scores for gender and ethnicity separately. This reveals the relative importance of each marker type for different demographic dimensions. 

Human Validation To validate realism and pro-vide a human baseline, we ran a small study with 32 volunteer annotators: a brief resume quality check, followed by a five-step progressive reve-lation protocol (Step 0: no “Additional Informa-tion”; Steps 1–3: three marker categories revealed in random order; Step 4: all markers revealed, with languages always last; Appendix G). 

4 Experimental Results 

We present results drawing on comprehensive eval-uations across 18 models, two evaluation settings, and two prompt variants.

LLMs recover demographic at-tributes with high fidelity from sociocultural mark-ers alone, even in the absence of names or explicit identifiers. Across tested models (Appendix F.1), ethnicity classification achieves macro-F1 scores ranging from 0.925 to 0.997 , while gender classifi-cation achieves F1 scores of 0.911 to 0.980 . These results confirm that the injected markers function as effective demographic proxies, validating the core premise of our experimental framework.

Effect of Rationale Prompting (Positive = Improvement) 

Figure 4: Per-model effect of rationale prompting on bias metrics. Positive values indicate an improvement with rationale prompting. Results show model-to-model heterogeneity, suggesting that rationale prompting is an unreliable and model-specific debiasing intervention. 

Aggregate bias differs substantially by evalua-tion setting. Figure 3 highlights that seemingly reasonable hiring paradigms induce different fair-ness expectations, and can deviate from their re-spective ideals by very different amounts. Direct Comparison (1v1) is ideally indifferent between augmented resumes with job-irrelevant details and its neutral baseline (win-rate of 0.5), while Score & Shortlist is ideally invariant in scores, so that all 

41 resumes for a job tie for the maximum score (100% max-score shortlist rate). In practice, Score & Shortlist departs far more from its ideal: mean deviation increases from 0.201 (Direct Compari-son, no rationale) to 0.702 (Score & Shortlist, no rationale), and from 0.279 to 0.759 with rationale. By contrast, mean demographic disparity is similar across settings ( 0.063 to 0.072 ). 

Rationale prompting is not a consistent miti-gation. Figure 3 indicates that rationale prompt-ing increases both disparity and deviation on aver-age in both settings ( Direct Comparison : disparity 

0.065 → 0.068 , deviation 0.201 → 0.279 ; Score & Shortlist : disparity 0.063 → 0.072 , deviation 

0.702 → 0.759 ). Figure 4 further shows that the di-rection and magnitude of this effect vary by model, with some models improving and others worsening, suggesting that prompting should not be treated as a reliable debiasing intervention. 

Bias landscape shows no clear model-family or parameter-scale correlations. Figure 5 situates each model by (i) normalised demographic per-formance disparity (how far the best and worst demographic groups are apart) and (ii) normalised deviation from the setting-specific ideal . Two pat-terns stand out. Firstly, models can have small group gaps yet still be far from ideal : in Score & Shortlist , several models sit at low disparity but high deviation, consistent with small score shifts changing who attains the maximum score and thus enters the shortlist. Secondly, neither model family nor parameter scale appears to guarantee robust-ness: within the same family (e.g., Gemini, Qwen, DeepSeek), variants spread widely across the land-scape, and larger variants can remain far from ideal. 

Direct Comparison (1v1) contains a tighter cluster near the origin (including gemini-2.5-flash , llama-4-maverick , and qwen3-235b-a22b-2507 ), whereas 

Score & Shortlist shifts most models upward (high deviation), with only a small subset (e.g., gemma-3-12b-it and mistral-small-3.2-24b-instruct ) with results remaining comparatively closer to the ideal. Figure 13 in Appendix E provides a complementary one-dimensional ranked summary. 

4.3 RQ3: Demographic Disparities Aggregate rankings and the primacy of ethnicity. 

Across both evaluation settings, Chinese Male is most frequently top-ranked ( Direct Comparison :average rank 1.47 ; Score & Shortlist : 2.25 ; Fig-ure 6), while ethnicity largely determines the over-all ordering: Chinese and Caucasian groups consis-tently rank highest while Indian and Malay groups tend to rank lowest. While the specific bottom-ranked group varies ( Indian candidates fare worst in Direct Comparison (6.31 –6.50 ), whereas Malay Female drops lowest in Score & Shortlist (6.64 )), the systemic penalty against minority ethnicities remains robust across both evaluation paradigms. 

Male advantage within ethnic tiers. Gender functions as a secondary ranking factor nested within ethnicity, exhibiting a systematic male ad-vantage. In Score & Shortlist , males outrank fe-males across every ethnic group, with rank gaps ranging from 0.79 to 1.15 . This male premium persists in Direct Comparison for Chinese, Cau-casian, and Indian candidates, with the sole ex-ception of the Malay group (where females rank higher). Overall, male-coded markers (e.g., tech-nical hobbies, competitive sports) consistently el-evate candidates above their female counterparts within the same ethnic category. 

Intersectional Patterns. The lowest ranks are disproportionately occupied by minority women 

(Malay Female and Indian Female), providing em-pirical validation for theories of intersectional dis-advantage (Guo and Caliskan, 2021; Wilson and Caliskan, 2025). This suggests that LLM-powered hiring systems can encode compounded biases against intersectional identities that are distinct and more severe than biases exhibited against their con-stituent demographic attributes in isolation. 

5 Discussion 

Implications for Hiring Practices. Our find-ings demonstrate that anonymisation limited to explicit PII is insufficient for ensuring fair AI-assisted hiring. Sociolinguistic cues that routinely survive redaction (such as language and culturally-relevant resume content) enable implicit demo-graphic inference and can propagate into outcome disparities. Organisations that seek to deploy debi-ased LLM-based hiring tools should: (1) Conduct demographic stress-tests using controlled resume variants prior to deployment; (2) Prefer models with low disparity and deviation from ideal, vali-dated on demographically representative test sets; (3) Recognise that certain settings like rankings derived from scores can amplify small prefer-ence differences into binary inclusion/exclusion decisions; (4) Implement ongoing monitoring and auditing to detect bias drift as models are updated. 0.05 0.00 0.05 0.10 0.15

The Limits of Prompting for Explanations. In practice, prompting for explanations is typically used for interpretability or to elicit more careful reasoning, rather than as an explicit fairness inter-vention. In our setting, this did not reliably im-prove fairness. On average across models, requir-ing brief rationales increases deviation from the ideal in both Direct Comparison (1v1) (deviation 

0.198 → 0.288 , disparity 0.063 → 0.066 ) and 

Score & Shortlist (deviation 0.730 → 0.772 , dis-parity 0.062 → 0.071 ) (Figures 3 and 4). This pat-tern is consistent with prior works which assert that post-hoc explanations can preserve or even amplify stereotypes rather than correcting them (Turpin et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). Effective mitiga-tion likely requires more than prompt-engineering, such as model-level interventions (data curation, fine-tuning, or alignment) or system-level controls (calibration and post-hoc ranking constraints). 

Generalisability of the Framework. While our empirical results are grounded in the Singapore context, the experimental framework generalises to any setting where demographic groups exhibit distinguishable sociocultural patterns. The method-ology requires: (1) defining demographic baskets relevant to the target context; (2) developing cul-turally authentic job-irrelevant marker variations through community consultation; (3) adapting job descriptions to local labour markets. We encourage researchers to replicate our approach in diverse cul-tural contexts to build a comparative understanding of how LLM hiring biases vary across societies. 

6 Conclusion 

We show that subtle sociocultural markers sur-viving anonymisation enable LLMs to infer de-mographic attributes and can induce measurable disparities in automated hiring outcomes. Eval-uating 18 models across dual settings (win-rate and scoring-to-ranking), we find: (1) languages primarily drive ethnicity inference whilst hob-bies/activities drive gender inference; (2) Chinese and Caucasian males are systematically favoured, whilst Malay and Indian females experience com-pounded intersectional disadvantage; (3) rationale prompting does not reliably reduce bias and can amplify it in certain models; (4) substantial hetero-geneity across models necessitates per-deployment audits. Practitioners should combine stress-testing with demographic variants, careful model selection, ongoing bias monitoring, and policy safeguards to prevent demographic harm in LLM-assisted hiring. Our framework is flexible and can be extended to diverse cultural contexts worldwide. Limitations 

We interpret our findings within the specific context of Singapore’s demographic landscape. While the experimental framework is transferable, the empir-ical results reflect local sociocultural dynamics and may differ in regions with alternative ethnic com-positions. Furthermore, our current analysis opera-tionalises gender as binary to establish a baseline for measurement; we acknowledge that this simpli-fies the spectrum of gender identities and suggest that future research extends to non-binary frame-works. Methodologically, we utilise controlled, LLM-generated resumes to ensure comparability. While we introduced realistic weaknesses to mimic authentic candidates, these documents provide a standardised approximation rather than the full un-structured variability of human-authored resumes. Similarly, our demographic markers were selected to function as distinct stress-tests; consequently, the magnitude of observed disparities reflects a re-sponse to clear sociocultural signals, which may differ from the response to potentially subtler mark-ers in naturally occurring data. Finally, our eval-uation captures single-turn decision-making at a specific point in time; given the rapid update cycles of large language models, behaviour in interactive workflows or future model versions may evolve. 

Ethical Considerations 

We deliberately inject stereotypical demographic cues into synthetic resumes to surface vulnerabil-ities in ostensibly anonymised hiring workflows. This adversarial testing approach is intended to 

identify bias so that it can be mitigated, not to provide a blueprint for discriminatory deployment. The demographic variations we develop encode cultural stereotypes intentionally; we acknowledge the representational harms inherent in stereotyping and emphasise that our markers are designed as bias probes rather than accurate characterisations of demographic groups. Our study uses “Caucasian” in experiments. We recognise this term has problematic historical ori-gins in outdated racial classification systems (Pol-lock, 2008). Contemporary demographic reporting uses White” as a more neutral descriptor. We re-tained “Caucasian” to ensure consistency across our prompts and experimental instruments; future research should adopt updated terminology. Addi-tionally, our framework occasionally uses “Tamil” and “Indian” interchangeably when referring to Singapore’s broader Indian ethnic category. Whilst Tamil represents one specific South Asian group, our markers capture stereotypical associations with the wider Indian demographic as defined in Sin-gapore’s multiethnic context (Jain, 2021). This oversimplification does not reflect the rich diversity within South Asian communities. Our findings should inform model selection, au-diting practices, and policy frameworks for fair AI hiring; they should not be used to justify or enable discriminatory practices. Deployment of AI hir-ing tools must comply with applicable employment law, anti-discrimination regulations, and emerging AI governance frameworks.

Resume Generation Prompt 

Goal: Create a single, complete, and realistic resume for a fictional candidate applying for the job described below. 

Core Principles: 1. Plausibility & Controlled Weaknesses: The candidate’s profile must adhere to ALL of the following constraints to introduce specific, realistic weaknesses. DO NOT create a “superstar” candidate. • The candidate’s total years of experience should be less than what is explicitly required. • The candidate should explicitly lack experience in one of the key secondary skills or tools mentioned. • The candidate’s most recent role should be in a related but different industry, highlighting transferable skills but no direct experience in the target industry. 

2. Content Alignment & Realism: 

• Use the names of real, plausible Singaporean or international companies for the work history. • To ensure variety, create a career path that includes a mix of company types. 

3. Official Singaporean Structure: The resume must have the following structure. DO NOT add any additional field: • Contact Information (Use placeholders like “[Full Name]”, “[Phone Number]”, “[Email Address]”) • Career Summary • Core Competencies • Work Experience • Education • Additional Information
