# Abstract 

Preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) is the domi-nant framework for aligning AI systems to human preferences, but its application to speech remains underexplored. We present a controlled cross-modal study of human and synthetic pref-erence annotations, comparing text and audio evaluations of identical semantic content across 100 prompts. Audio prefer-ences prove as reliable as text, with inter-rater agreement reach-ing good levels (ICC(2,k) ≈ .80) at ∼9 raters—the first ICC-based reliability characterization in the preference annotation literature for either modality. However, modality reshapes how people judge: audio raters exhibit narrower decision thresholds, reduced length bias, and more user-oriented evaluation crite-ria, with near-chance cross-modality agreement. Synthetic rat-ings further align with human judgments and predict inter-rater agreement, supporting their use both for triaging ambiguous pairs and as full replacements for human annotations. 

Index Terms : preference alignment, RLHF, audio language models, annotation reliability, cross-modal evaluation, text-to-speech, human preference data 1

# 1. Introduction 

Conversational audio models have seen rapid development and deployment in recent years. These systems aim to provide natural, responsive interactions aligned with user expectations, yet research on aligning audio models to human preferences remains sparse [1]. Preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) offers a path forward, particularly for qualities like naturalness [2, 3], emotional expressiveness [4], and conver-sational appropriateness [5], where vocal delivery may shape perception alongside semantic content. Current preference alignment research centers on text, fol-lowing a standard pipeline: collect pairwise human or AI pref-erences, train a reward model, and optimize via reinforcement learning algorithms [6]. This approach has revealed biases such as annotators’ tendency to prefer longer responses [7]. In ad-dition, text-based annotations introduce uncontrolled variables: attention allocation and reading order go untracked. Audio, by contrast, delivers information at a controlled and recordable pace, but cannot be presented side-by-side, so pre-sentation order becomes an explicit variable. This affords re-searchers significantly more signals to consider during annota-tion with the caveat of potentially novel and more significant order effects. Despite these additional signals, much of the ex-isting PbRL work for speech relies on Text-To-Speech (TTS)-generated audio with assumed labels [1].

In this work, we investigate the practical implications of data collection methods for PbRL. Specifically, we ask: 1. Is audio preference data reliable? 2. How does modality affect preference ratings? 3. Can synthetic ratings substitute or augment human ratings? We find audio preference ratings to be as reliable as text preferences but behaviorally distinct, introducing biases such as recency effects, while showing reduced susceptibility to length effects. This paper is also the first to report Intraclass Corre-lation Coefficients (ICC) for preference labels in either modal-ity, showing that a single annotator yields poor reliability, three yield moderate, and nine yield good agreement. Cross-modality agreement is near chance, with preference shifts varying by prompt rather than reflecting a uniform directional bias, sug-gesting that text data converted to audio via TTS may not be appropriate for audio preference pipelines. Lastly, synthetic rat-ings can be used to replace or triage ambiguous response pairs, reducing annotation costs without sacrificing label quality. 

# 2. Background 

2.1. Preference-Based Reinforcement Learning 

Preference-Based Reinforcement Learning is a framework in which a reward function is learned from human preferences rather than being explicitly specified. Given pairs (or sets) of trajectory segments or outputs, annotators indicate which they prefer, and these comparisons are used to train a reward model [8]. This reward model then serves as the optimiza-tion objective for a reinforcement learning agent. PbRL has been prominently applied to align Large Language Models with human values through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), where preference data is collected by hav-ing humans or LLMs compare candidate responses to the same query [6]. Standard PbRL pipelines typically rely on binary pair-wise comparisons modeled with Bradley–Terry reward formu-lations [8]. While effective, binary labels discard information about preference strength and are susceptible to well-known or-der and position effects [9], which are difficult to diagnose with-out additional modeling assumptions or counterbalancing. Recent work suggests that richer scalar feedback can yield more informative reward signals than coarse comparisons [10], and continuous rating paradigms such as PRISM’s visual analog scale [11] permit more expressive analyses of annotator agree-ment, including ICC and Krippendorff’s α. However, the prac-tical trade-offs between binary and continuous annotation— including cognitive demands, calibration behavior, and implica-tions for downstream reward modeling — remain inadequately characterized, particularly for audio settings where sequential

presentation already complicates preference elicitation. Several known biases arise from this type of fine-tuning, in-cluding length bias [7], position bias [12], and reward overopti-mization [13]. Specifically, longer responses tend to win more frequently regardless of semantic content, and the first pre-sented response is also more likely to be preferred. In audio, less work has investigated the biases that may arise from annotating speech and how they relate to those found in text-based preference labeling [1]. Many works convert text datasets to audio using TTS [14], implicitly assuming that data suitable for text is also suitable for speech. However, whether preferences and biases transfer across modalities remains an open question. 

2.2. Modality’s Effect on Judgments 

Existing work on annotation biases in the speech literature has largely focused on quality assessment, cataloguing biases and proposing mitigations [15]. Explicit cross-modality compar-isons of preference judgments remain rare. Most similar to this work, Cho et al. [16] demonstrated that preferences differ by modality and used this finding to build speech-adapted mod-els, but their analysis did not extend to reliability scaling, order effects, decision thresholds, or synthetic-human alignment— dimensions we address here. More broadly, speech processing is strictly linear and im-poses higher cognitive load than reading [16], and users tend to anthropomorphize audio interfaces more readily than text-based ones [17], suggesting that evaluation criteria may shift across modalities. 

2.3. Reliability and Agreement in Preference Annotations 

Prior work on preference annotation reliability has generally re-ported only single-rater pairwise agreement—e.g., 73–77% for text summarization [18], 63% for helpful and harmless annota-tions [19], and 60% for music [20]. While intuitive, these per-centages are not directly comparable across studies and, crit-ically, do not address how agreement scales with the number of raters. Intraclass correlation coefficients and Krippendorff’s 

α offer richer characterizations by partitioning variance across raters and stimuli, and by accommodating different measure-ment scales, respectively [21]. To our knowledge, no prior work has used these metrics to characterize the number of raters needed to reach specific reliability thresholds in preference an-notation for either text or audio. 

2.4. The PRISM Dataset 

PRISM [11] is a preference dataset mapping the sociodemo-graphics and stated preferences of 1,500 diverse participants from 75 countries to their contextual preferences in 8,011 live conversations with 21 LLMs. Participants rate each model response on a 1–100 visual analog scale (VAS) without vis-ible numeric values to avoid anchoring. Conversations span three conditions—unguided, values-guided, and controversy-guided—to diversify prompts along the objective–subjective spectrum. Crucially, the person who initiates and steers each conversation is also the one who rates the responses, coupling the evaluative context with the interactive context. We use PRISM’s unguided conversations as our source material, adopt-ing its VAS rating methodology and leveraging its original in-teractive ratings as a baseline for comparison with our external annotations. 

# 3. Methods 

We converted a subset of the PRISM [11] text preference dataset to spoken audio using Kokoro [22] for preference solicitation in the audio modality. In addition to the audio-to-audio judgments, we collected text-to-text comparisons matched in semantic con-tent. To control for potential confounds introduced by audio fi-delity, participants also evaluated the quality of each audio sam-ple. This study was approved as minimal-risk research by the authors’ Institutional Review Board. 

3.1. Converting Text to Audio 

We sub-selected ”unguided” conversations to maintain a more objective dataset, other conversations were intended to be con-troversial. From the 3,113 unguided conversations, we ran-domly selected 100 interactions. To ensure variability in re-sponse lengths, 25% (25/100) of the interactions were drawn from the top 10th percentile of absolute character length dif-ferences between responses (i.e., a minimum difference of 449 characters). The rest (75/100) were drawn from the lower 90th percentile. This yielded 200 audio clips (2 model responses × 100 interactions). Audio clips were also screened so that only innocuous content was presented to participants, removing clips that could be construed as offensive, discriminatory, or emo-tionally distressing. Each interaction was passed through the current state-of-the-art open-source text-to-speech model, kokoro [22], as benchmarked by TTS-Arena [23], to generate audio samples. We used kokoro’s default parameters, with the voice set to ’af heart’ selected for its neutral, clear delivery and kept this consistent across all samples. All audio snippets were manu-ally reviewed for artifacts or mispronunciations that could neg-atively impact quality to focus on semantic content. Low au-dio quality samples were replaced, and replacements were re-evaluated following the same selection criteria. We provide documentation of initial randomization, documented removals, followed by iterative replacements at the following link. Re-moval reasons indicated by row color include: yellow = poor audio quality, orange = other language, green = empty output, and red = harmful content. For each interaction, a history of the conversation up to that point in text is shown to the user. The interface can be seen in Figure 1. Participants are then asked to rate the responses from 1 (Terrible) to 100 (Perfect) using a slider that doesn’t present the numerical value to avoid anchoring, as done in the origi-nal PRISM paper [11]. Each participant rates 20 interactions presented in a balanced block random order with an attention check at the beginning (21 total). The attention check paired a coherent response with a clearly nonsensical one; participants who preferred the nonsensical response were excluded. 

Figure 1: Interface for the Audio Task of preference selection. It includes conversation history, most recent query and two re-sponses. Raters must rate each response from 1-100 on re-sponse quality (the content of the answer) and audio quality (the clarity and naturalness of speech). We also collect discrete preferences with the option to tie and optional free-response reasoning justification. 

Participants are randomly assigned to either text or audio response modality to prevent cross-condition contamination. Those in the audio condition also rate audio quality using the same 1-100 scale. Binary preference data, with the option to tie, are presented to respondents at the end. An optional text box asking raters why they preferred one response over the other is also presented to participants for qualitative analysis. We present responses sequentially since there is no way to present audio side-by-side, as can be done with text to minimize order effects, and aim to compare primacy or recency effects cross-modality. Audio responses must be played in full on the first listen— participants cannot skip ahead or scrub through the timeline. After the initial playback, participants can replay the audio as many times as needed, ensuring parity with the text condition where responses can be re-read at will. In addition to explicit ratings, we track behavioral and tem-poral indicators. We log: (1) total decision time (prompt onset to rating submission), (2) deliberation time in audio condition (time from completion of last audio listen to rating submission), (3) intermediate rating changes to capture preference reversals between responses during the evaluation process, (4) number of audio replays per response, (5) total session duration, and (6) completion rates across modalities to assess dropout pat-terns. For participants who change their ratings before final submission, we track both the number of reversals and, in the audio condition, whether reversals occurred after fully listening to both clips. We also track presentation and ordering effects by recording: which response appears first on screen and the sequential position (1-20) of each rating within a participant’s session to assess fatigue effects. Finally, we record stimulus properties including: charac-ter count and number of conversational turns in the history for each interaction, response length (character count), and abso-lute character length difference between paired responses. Note, character length is linearly correlated with audio length, so we view these as interchangeable analysis signals. These metrics enable us to examine relationships between stimulus character-istics and inter-rater agreement patterns across modalities. 

3.2.3. Synthetic Ratings 

In addition to human ratings, we collected synthetic ratings (10 per prompt per modality) to investigate alignment between syn-thetic and human signals, and to identify interactions where model responses are clearly differentiated versus ambiguous. We generate synthetic ratings (1–100) for text, audio, and audio-quality using GPT-4o for text [24] and GPT-4o-Audio-Preview for audio. We select GPT-4o-Audio-Preview since it is an end-to-end audio model and should present more insights than a speech-to-text (STT) model that uses an LLM and then converts responses back to speech. Such a model would only evaluate TTS and STT accuracy relative to LLM responses. We used default API parameters. These synthetic ratings serve as pre-computed AI scores that allow us to test relationships between real and synthetic in-teractions. Specifically, we’re interested in using synthetic sig-nals to either augment human annotations or possible replace them as a whole for Reinforcement Learning from Artificial In-telligence Feedback (RLAIF) applications. 

3.3. Participants and Exclusions 

We recruited 106 participants (53 per modality) from Pro-lific’s [25] US population, compensating them at a rate of $12 per hour. 7/53 (13.2%) participants in the audio condition and 5/53 (9.4%) in the text condition failed the attention check, leav-ing 46 and 48 participants, respectively. Of those who passed, 4 in the text condition did not complete the survey but we re-tain the trials they did complete, yielding a final sample of 94 participants. This resulted in each audio clip being evaluated by 8–10 participants. We break down the demographics of these 94 participants in Table 1.

# 5. Discussion 

This study asked three questions about preference data collec-tion for speech: (1) whether audio preferences are reliable, (2) 

how modality shapes preference judgments, and (3) whether synthetic ratings can supplement human annotation. Our re-sults paint a consistent picture: audio and text preference data are comparably reliable in aggregate, yet the two modalities produce meaningfully different evaluation dynamics . Raters listening to audio operated with narrower decision thresholds, showed reduced sensitivity to response length, and described their preferences in terms oriented toward the user’s needs rather than the content’s depth. These differences did not manifest as a uniform directional shift—modality did not consistently favor one response over another—but instead interacted with specific prompts to re-shape which response was preferred. In other words, modal-ity changed how people judged, not whether they could judge reliably. Synthetic ratings complemented these human signals, identifying where annotators were likely to agree and offering viable proxy scores in both modalities. Together, these findings suggest that audio preference annotation is a qualitatively dif-ferent evaluative process —one that may better approximate in-teractive text evaluation, and could thus serve as a cross-modal proxy for preference pipelines where collecting in-conversation ratings is impractical. 

5.1. Audio Preference Reliability Singular Ratings Are Noisy; Aggregated Ratings Are Not. 

We interpret ICC values in line with Koo et al.’s [21] conserva-tive thresholds as shown in Figure 4. At k = 1 , agreement is poor (ICC(2,1) = .333 audio, .295 text), but aggregating to the observed ∼9 raters yields good agreement (ICC(2, k) = .821, .788), with diminishing returns beyond that point. Prior work on preference annotation reliability has gener-ally reported only single-rater pairwise agreement—e.g., 73– 77% for text summarization [18], 63% agreement between an-notators (helpful and harmless) [19], and 60% agreement be-tween annotator and research team preferences (music) [20]— but these percentages are not directly comparable to ICC and, critically, do not address how agreement scales with the number of raters. Alternative approaches hoping to collect one-off rat-ings rely on continuous ratings as a signal of decisiveness [10]. To our knowledge, our work is the first to systematically characterize the number of raters needed to reach specific reli-ability thresholds in the preference annotation literature. Prac-tically, collecting 3 ratings per stimulus moves agreement from poor to moderate at a 3 × increase in annotation cost. Reaching good agreement requires approximately 9 raters—a 9 × increase that may or may not be justified depending on the sensitivity and stakes of the downstream task. 

Decision Thresholds Are Smaller in Audio than Text. 

Raters exhibited larger rating gaps between preferred and non-preferred responses in text ( M = 41 .7) than in audio ( M =27 .9; rrb = .303 , p < . 001 ). This compressed margin matters for pipelines that convert continuous ratings into binary pref-erence labels: a fixed binarization threshold calibrated on text data may misclassify audio pairs as ties or reverse preferences, inflating label noise for reward model training. We recommend calibrating thresholds per modality or, where possible, retaining the continuous signal to preserve preference strength informa-tion. Recency Bias Must Be Handled Carefully. Audio preference labels present unique problems in how they are perceived rela-tive to text. In text, preferences are often presented side-by-side to better account for order effects [9]. For long audio clips, this presentation order becomes increasingly problematic since they can’t be visually compared like text can. Our results confirm a strong recency bias, with second-presented items rated signifi-cantly more favorably ( b = −2.61 , p = .012 ). Notably, this effect did not differ by modality ( b = −0.51 ,

p = .732 ), indicating that recency bias is a general problem of sequential presentation rather than an audio-specific phe-nomenon. As employed in this study, counterbalanced presen-tation order across multiple raters can mitigate this bias at the aggregate level, though it inflates disagreement at the individ-ual level—likely explaining why our nominal Krippendorff’s α

is close to 0. Re-listening to the first clip after hearing both has also been proposed [15], and indeed roughly one quarter of au-dio trials in our study (214/920, 23.3%) involved participants voluntarily re-listening to the first clip after completing the sec-ond, suggesting a natural inclination to revisit earlier stimuli when making comparative judgments—though at the cost of in-creased session time. Binary ratings are especially susceptible to recency effects as there is no way to adjust for order effects post-hoc. We suggest using continuous ratings to better handle order effects when collecting audio preferences. 

Longer Rating Sessions Are More Efficient Without Loss of Data Quality. Despite audio trials taking nearly twice as long as text trials (136 vs. 79 sec), participants rated both conditions as equally difficult ( p = .868 ), suggesting that the additional time reflects the inherent pacing of audio rather than increased cognitive demand. Critically, ratings remained stable across tri-als in both modalities ( p = .960 ) with no modality-specific drift ( p = .236 ), even as completion times decreased signifi-cantly (audio: 8.6%; text: 16.0% per SD of trial number). This combination of practice-driven efficiency gains and rating sta-bility implies that longer annotation sessions are feasible with-out sacrificing data quality, offering a practical path to reducing per-annotation costs. While time-on-task effects and cognitive fatigue are well documented in sustained attention and crowdsourcing con-texts [26, 27], to our knowledge no prior work has examined fatigue or practice effects specifically for preference annotation in either modality. Our results suggest that sessions of approx-imately one hour are viable for both text and audio preference collection, though future work should identify the boundary at which rating quality begins to degrade. 

5.2. Cross-Modality Considerations Text Preferences Do Not Generalize to Audio Preferences. 

At a zero threshold, cross-modality agreement on the preferred response was only 53% (binomial test vs. 50%, p = .31 ), not reaching statistical significance. Agreement rose with in-creasingly decisive thresholds (Figure 3), suggesting that text and audio modalities converge, but only when preferences are strong. A mixed-effects analysis confirmed that this divergence is prompt-specific rather than reflecting a uniform directional shift: the fixed effect of modality was not significant ( β = 1 .71 ,

p = .458 ), but adding random slopes for modality by prompt significantly improved model fit ( χ2(2) = 22 .1, p < . 001 ;SD = 12 .93 ). Given the near-zero fixed effect, this random slope magnitude implies that modality could swing preferences by nearly half a standard deviation in either direction depend-ing on the prompt. Our TF-IDF analysis of written justifications offers one interpretation consistent with this heterogeneity: audio raters more frequently referenced “user” and “help” ( p < .001 ,

p = .002 ), while text raters emphasized “detail” and “re-sponse” ( p < . 001 for both). These lexical differences are compatible with audio evaluation promoting a more holistic, user-oriented frame— does this response serve the person? —while text evaluation encourages analytic attention to content depth and specificity. This would align with evidence that users anthropomorphize audio interfaces more readily than text-based ones [17], potentially shifting evaluation from scrutiniz-ing what was said to judging how well the response addressed the user’s needs. Prompts where these two evaluative frames diverge in which response they favor would naturally produce the modality-dependent preferences we observe. 

Audio Is Less Susceptible to Length Bias. Length bias is a well-documented confound in text preference annotation [28], and our results replicate this: longer text responses predicted a roughly 40% larger positive rating increase compared to longer audio responses ( b = 4 .92 vs. b = 3 .52 ; interaction p = .045 ). The fixed pacing of audio likely constrains the salience of length as a heuristic—listeners cannot skim or visually gauge response size the way readers can. This reduced length sensitivity has practical implications: audio-based annotation may offer a more content-focused signal for training reward models, particularly in domains where length and quality are easily confounded. This extends the findings of Cho et al. [16], who showed that excessive verbosity is penalized more heavily in audio than in text. Notably, Cho et al. also observed that their best-performing model generated longer responses than the base model while still being preferred, reinforcing that length bias in audio is moderated rather than reversed. Our per-stimulus es-timates, drawn from approximately 9 raters at good ICC-based reliability, allow us to quantify this attenuation more precisely. One speculative but promising use case of this finding is using audio-derived labels as a complementary signal to text annotations—for example, upweighting audio-based re-ward scores to counteract length bias in text-trained reward models. This would come at the cost of longer annotation ses-sions (approximately 2 × per trial), and whether the debiasing benefit justifies the additional expense remains an open empiri-cal question. 

5.3. Synthetic Rating Applications AI Ratings Identify Easy vs. Ambiguous Prompts. When AI ratings distinguished between two responses, human raters also tended to agree more with each other ( β = 0 .014 , p < . 001 ), and this relationship held across both modalities with no signif-icant interaction ( p = .15 ). Non-parametric checks confirmed robustness (audio: r = .23 , p = .02 ; text: r = .38 , p < . 001 ). This finding has immediate practical value: synthetic rat-ings can serve as a pre-screening signal to triage prompts be-fore human annotation . Prompts where AI models strongly differentiate between responses likely do not require large an-notator pools, while prompts where AI ratings converge may warrant additional human raters to resolve genuine ambiguity. Such adaptive sampling could substantially reduce annotation costs without sacrificing label quality on the prompts that mat-ter most. To our knowledge, this relationship between AI dis-criminability and human inter-rater reliability has not been pre-viously reported, in part because ICC is rarely computed in the preference annotation literature. 

AI Ratings Are Viable Proxies, With a Modality Caveat. 

AI–human absolute error was significantly higher in the text condition than in the audio condition ( ¯d = 2 .02 , p = .035 ). While this margin is modest on a 100-point scale, it is notable given that audio raters operate with smaller decision thresholds (M = 27 .9 vs. 41 .7)—a 2-point discrepancy represents a pro-portionally larger share of the signal that distinguishes preferred from non-preferred responses in audio. Prior work has reported approximately 80% agreement be-tween GPT-4o and human judges for text-based evaluation [29], and RLAIF approaches have shown that AI-generated prefer-ences can substitute for human labels in text domains with min-imal performance loss [30]. Our results extend these findings to audio, suggesting that synthetic ratings are viable proxies in both modalities—though the tighter AI-human alignment in au-dio warrants further investigation into whether end-to-end au-dio models capture evaluative signals not available in text-based pipelines. Audio Annotation May Better Approximate Interactive Evaluation. Among all external rating conditions, human-audio ratings aligned most closely with the original PRISM rat-ings, in which the person who initiated the conversation also rated the responses ( ¯d = 1 .15 , p = .374 ). By contrast, human-text ( ¯d = 8 .03 , p < . 001 ), AI-text ( ¯d = −1.84 , p = .015 ), and AI-audio ( ¯d = −9.18 , p < .001 ) ratings all diverged significantly. The non-significant result for human-audio pre-cludes strong claims, but it is suggestive: the temporal pacing and sequential processing demands of audio may more closely approximate the evaluative context of a real-time conversational interaction in the text modality. If this pattern holds, it would have practical implications for preference data collection— audio-based annotation by external raters may serve as a closer proxy for actual user satisfaction than the text-based third-party annotation pipelines for text-based interactions .

5.4. Implications for the PbRL Pipeline 

Taken together, our findings suggest that the standard text-based preference collection pipeline may not straightforwardly extend to speech. The near-chance cross-modality agreement (53%) and prompt-dependent modality effects indicate that text pref-erences cannot be assumed to serve as reliable proxies for au-dio preferences—a critical consideration for systems that train reward models on text annotations and deploy them to score speech outputs. Practitioners designing PbRL pipelines for audio should ac-count for several factors. First , continuous ratings should be preferred over binary labels to enable post-hoc correction of the recency bias we observe in sequential presentation. Second ,approximately 9 raters per stimulus are needed to achieve good aggregate reliability, though synthetic pre-screening can reduce this cost by identifying prompts where fewer annotators suf-fice. Third , audio annotation’s reduced susceptibility to length bias suggests that audio-derived reward signals may produce less biased reward models than their text counterparts, poten-tially justifying the roughly 2× increase in annotation time per trial. Fourth , the closer alignment between human-audio rat-ings and original interactive ratings raises the possibility that audio-based annotation pipelines may better approximate real user satisfaction than text-based alternatives—though this find-ing requires replication with audio-native data before informing production systems. 

# 6. Limitations 

Converting text-based interactions to audio via TTS is currently the standard approach in the field for generating speech pref-erence data, and our findings demonstrate that meaningful and reliable annotations can be collected from such stimuli. That said, TTS-rendered speech lacks paralinguistic nuances present in natural speech — such as emotional prosody, hesitation, and background noise — that may influence preference judgments in real-world audio interactions. Future work should investigate whether these findings replicate with audio-native datasets that capture these features, or with text prompts specifically written for spoken delivery rather than adapted from text conversations. Additionally, presenting conversation history and the user prompt in text for the audio condition likely introduces some noise; however, having users listen to full conversations in audio would be impractical. Retaining longer conversation histories also allows us to investigate whether the depth of prior context influences preference annotations. To strictly control for confounds, we utilized a single static voice profile ( af heart ). However, this limits our ability to generalize findings across different speaker identities. We do not assess how variable voice characteristics (e.g., gender, ac-cent, pitch) might interact with semantic content to influence annotator preference or agreement rates. Our recruitment was limited to 106 participants from the US population. While this controlled for cultural variability in preference, it limits the generalizability of our findings. The patterns we observed may differ in non-Western populations or among non-native English speakers, who may process audio stimuli with different cognitive loads than native speakers. Finally, our protocol actively removed interactions contain-ing potentially harmful or offensive content. While necessary for participant well-being, this filtering excludes ”safety” and ”refusal” scenarios from our analysis. Consequently, we cannot determine if the audio modality amplifies or dampens human sensitivity to toxic content compared to text. 

# 7. Conclusion 

This study examined the reliability and characteristics of pref-erence judgments across text and audio modalities. We find that audio preference annotations are reliable in aggregate and ex-hibit agreement levels comparable to text when sufficient raters are pooled. However, modality meaningfully shapes evaluation behavior. Preferences did not shift uniformly between text and audio, but instead varied by prompt, indicating that modality in-fluences how responses are judged rather than which responses are preferred overall. Audio judgments showed narrower deci-sion thresholds and reduced sensitivity to response length, sug-gesting that speech-based evaluation may attenuate known text biases such as length effects. We further show that synthetic ratings align with human judgments across modalities and predict human inter-rater relia-bility, highlighting their potential utility for adaptive annotation strategies. These results suggest that text-derived preferences cannot be assumed to generalize directly to speech outputs and that modality should be treated as a first-class consideration in preference-based reinforcement learning pipelines. Future work should validate these findings using audio-native datasets and more diverse speech conditions to better characterize how vocal and paralinguistic factors interact with human preference for-mation. As conversational AI systems increasingly operate across both text and speech modalities, establishing modality-appropriate annotation frameworks will be essential to ensuring that alignment research produces systems that genuinely serve users in the contexts they actually use them.
